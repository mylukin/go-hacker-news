## Ntsc-rs – open-source video emulation of analog TV and VHS artifacts

- 原文链接: [Ntsc-rs – open-source video emulation of analog TV and VHS artifacts](https://ntsc.rs/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48428025)

ntsc-rs is a new open-source tool that copies the look of old analog TVs and VHS tapes. It uses real math to show TV and tape glitches, instead of just using simple filters or color tricks.

The tool is based on real NTSC and VHS signal processing, using ideas from other open projects like composite-video-simulator and ntscQT. ntsc-rs is written in Rust, which helps it run very fast. It uses modern computer features like multithreading and SIMD, so it can make the effect in real time, even on big, high-quality videos.

You can use ntsc-rs in many ways. There is a standalone app, a web app, and plugins for popular video editors like After Effects, Premiere, DaVinci Resolve, Hitfilm, and Vegas. This makes it easy for video makers to add old TV looks to their projects. The goal is to make the VHS effect look much more real than the simple filters you see in other software.

In the Hacker News comments, many people are happy to see such a real-looking VHS effect. Some users remember watching VHS tapes or old TV and say this tool brings back strong memories. Others are surprised how well the effect works, even on clear modern videos. A few developers talk about the technical side, saying Rust is a good choice for fast video tools.

Some users ask about using ntsc-rs on different computers, like Windows or Linux, and how easy it is to add to their video editing setup. Others discuss how the open-source nature helps them learn about old video technology. A few comment that while VHS looks bad compared to digital, the effect gives a nice, nostalgic feeling for music videos or art projects. Not everyone thinks we need more VHS looks, but most agree the project shows great attention to detail and is fun for experimenting.

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48427643)

Meta said that thousands of Instagram accounts were hacked because hackers tricked its AI chatbot. The chatbot was supposed to help people get back into their accounts, but hackers used it to take over accounts instead.

The problem was with how the chatbot checked emails for password resets. If someone asked to reset a password and gave a new email, the chatbot would send the reset link there, even if that email did not belong to the real account owner. This let hackers get control of many accounts, sometimes including contact info and private messages. Meta now says at least 20,225 people were affected, and the attack started in April. Most of these accounts did not have two-factor authentication turned on, which would have helped stop the hack. The bug was inside the chatbot’s code, and Meta says it is now fixed. They have turned off the chatbot for now and are checking other chatbots to make sure this does not happen again. Meta says it does not know if hackers used the stolen information for other bad things. The company is telling people to reset their passwords and use safer ways to log in.

In the comments, some people said this shows why you should always turn on two-factor authentication. Others were surprised that the chatbot would send a reset link to an email that was not even linked to the account. A few people blamed Meta for not testing the chatbot well enough before letting people use it. Some commenters worried about what other AI tools could be tricked like this in the future. There was talk about how cutting staff at Meta may have led to more mistakes. Others said that big companies often rush to add AI but do not think about security. Some people felt sorry for users who lost their accounts and said companies should be more careful. A few wondered if hackers targeted public figures or just regular users. Some commenters joked about how easy the hack was, since the chatbot just did what it was told. Lastly, some said that AI is not ready for important jobs like account recovery if it can be fooled this way.

## Zeroserve: A zero-config web server you can script with eBPF

- 原文链接: [Zeroserve: A zero-config web server you can script with eBPF](https://su3.io/posts/introducing-zeroserve)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48425723)

zeroserve is a new web server that lets you serve a website from a single file, with no setup needed, and lets you add scripts using eBPF. It is made to be fast, simple, and more flexible than nginx or Caddy, especially if you want to customize how requests are handled.

The main idea is that you put your website in a tar file, and zeroserve serves it directly, without unpacking. When you want to update the site, you just swap the tarball and reload—no downtime. All server settings and logic are in eBPF scripts, written in C, which run on every request. These scripts can do things like check headers, add authentication, change responses, or send requests to another server. 

The server uses modern features: HTTP/2, TLS 1.3, and all I/O is done with io_uring for speed. Zeroserve is single-threaded per process, but you can run many processes on one machine for scaling. The eBPF scripts are safe—they run in userspace, sandboxed so they can’t mess with other parts of the server, and are preemptible, so one slow script won’t block everything else.

Benchmarks show zeroserve is faster than nginx and Caddy for small and medium files, and for many proxy requests. For very large files or responses, nginx is still faster. Memory use is low—more than nginx but less than Caddy. The scripting model is different: instead of a config file and extra plugins, all logic is just code—no split between config and scripting.

The article also compares scripting in zeroserve (eBPF) to nginx+Lua. With the right settings, zeroserve’s eBPF scripts are faster than Lua scripts in nginx, especially for middleware and dynamic responses.

In the comments, many people like the simple packaging and atomic deploys using a single tarball. Some praise the “program-as-configuration” idea, saying it’s easier to read and reason about one script than a mix of config and plugins. A few think using eBPF is clever, but worry about the need to write C for config, which might be hard for some users. Some ask if scripting in Rust or another safer language could be supported in the future.

Others share concerns about security, since eBPF and JIT code can be risky if there are bugs. Some users say that for large file serving or heavy proxying, nginx's performance and stability are still better, but zeroserve’s speed for small files and requests is impressive. A few people love the hot-reload feature, noting that it makes updates very smooth.

There are also questions about compatibility with existing tools, and if zeroserve could replace nginx or Caddy in production environments. Some think it’s a great fit for static sites with a bit of dynamic logic, while others say it might be too new or niche for bigger setups. Overall, the response is positive, with most seeing zeroserve as an interesting and fast new option, especially for developers who want more control and less config hassle.

## Universal Memory Protocol – a shared format for agent memory

- 原文链接: [Universal Memory Protocol – a shared format for agent memory](https://universalmemoryprotocol.io/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48428796)

Universal Memory Protocol (UMP) is a new standard that lets AI agents share and use memory across different apps, vendors, and sessions. Today, every AI tool keeps its own memory, so you can’t easily move notes or knowledge from one agent to another—UMP wants to fix this.

The article explains that there are already standards for agents to call tools (MCP) and talk to each other (A2A), but not for sharing memory. UMP creates a single, portable shape for memory, using simple operations like recall, remember, revise, forget, and get. You can use UMP with local files, SQL databases, Redis, vector stores, and even markdown files, so you don’t have to change where you keep your notes or data. There are SDKs for TypeScript and HTTP APIs for other languages. UMP is just a protocol—it doesn’t force you to use a certain database or engine. It keeps your data in typed, signed JSON records, using W3C PROV and DIDs for trust and provenance.

You can import your old agent files, Obsidian notes, or project markdown into UMP, and the protocol keeps the source info. UMP standardizes the way memory is stored, accessed, and trusted, but lets each tool decide how to process or rank the data. It uses a “supersede-never-delete” model—old facts are never erased, only closed and linked to updates. All records are signed by the user, not the vendor, so you keep ownership. The protocol is designed to prevent prompt injection and requires a security pipeline. It also supports consent and redaction, so your data can stay private or be deleted if you want.

In the Hacker News comments, many people like the idea of portable, user-owned memory for AI agents. Some users think this could help stop vendor lock-in and make switching tools easier. Others worry that standardizing memory may be hard because different tools have different needs and ways to store information. A few are excited about using UMP with personal knowledge bases like Obsidian or Notion, while some say the protocol sounds complex and wonder if it will really be adopted. There are questions about security and how to avoid leaking private data between agents. Some developers appreciate that UMP doesn’t force you to use a special database or engine. Others point out that while the protocol is open, the real challenge is making apps actually support it. A few mention that “supersede-never-delete” could make data messy over time, but others say it helps with tracking changes and trust. Some commenters hope UMP will make it easier to build AI apps that work well together; others want to see more real-world examples. Overall, the discussion is hopeful but cautious, with people waiting to see if UMP can deliver on its promises.

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48424605)

Nvidia wants to make a very powerful CPU system for Windows PCs. This new chip has 128 GB of shared memory and up to 6,144 CUDA cores for fast graphics and AI work.

The CPU part has 10 strong cores (performance cores) and 10 energy-saving ones (efficiency cores). The main CPU cores use Cortex-X925, which is a new and fast design. These cores support SVE2 instructions, which help with certain types of math, but some say AMD’s AVX-512 is better. The big change here is the 128 GB of unified memory. Shared memory means the CPU and GPU both use the same pool, instead of having separate memory. Apple started this idea with its Apple Silicon chips, and now others are following. Shared memory is not as fast as special GPU memory, but it is cheaper and still fast enough for many jobs, like running AI models on your own computer. Not everyone will need to run AI models at home, but for those who do, this machine could help. Gamers might also like it, because it should be good for playing new video games. The article wonders what Intel and AMD will do to respond to Nvidia’s chip. AMD’s chips support AVX-512, which is strong for heavy math, but Intel is not making it available for most people yet.

In the comments, some people are excited about the new chip and think it could change how we use computers. Others are not sure if 128 GB of shared memory is really needed for most people. A few say that AI models at home are still rare, so maybe this is only for a small group. Some discuss how SVE2 and AVX-512 compare, with many agreeing that AVX-512 is stronger but harder to use. Others point out that Apple has already shown how good unified memory can be, so it makes sense that Nvidia copies this. A few worry that shared memory could slow down certain games or apps, since GPU memory is usually faster. Some hope this will push Intel and AMD to make their own chips better. Others say that price will decide if people buy this, since big memory and lots of cores can be expensive. There are also people who just want better battery life, not more power. ## Benchmarks in Leipzig

- 原文链接: [Benchmarks in Leipzig](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.05818)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48425247)

This article is about a group of mathematicians who made a set of hard math questions to test how well AI language models can solve them. They did this work during a workshop in Leipzig, Germany, and called it the Leipzig Benchmark.

The team wrote 100 research-level math questions, each with a known answer. These questions cover topics like algebra, combinatorics, and geometry. First, five top AI models tried the questions once. At this stage, 41 questions were not solved by any model. Next, three models tried each question 20 times. After this, only 16 questions were left unsolved. In the final stage, two strong models each tried three times, and just 2 questions were left unsolved. This shows that AI math skills are getting much better. The article also shares tables of results and includes all the questions in an appendix. The project took about six weeks, and most of the work was done in a three-day workshop. The authors wanted to make a fair and hard test for AI, different from school-level math. They hope this benchmark will help future AI research.

In the comments, some people were surprised by how well the AI models performed on tough math problems. Others wondered if repeated tries help the models “get lucky” and find the right answer by chance. Some users asked about the types of questions and if they really reflect true research problems. A few pointed out that real math is more than just answering questions—it’s about finding new ideas and proofs. There was also discussion about how AI can help humans, maybe by checking work or giving hints, but it is not ready to replace mathematicians. Some commenters wanted to see how smaller or open-source models would do on these questions. Others warned about overfitting, where AI could get better just at this test but not at math in general. A few users were hopeful, saying these results could lead to better tools for students and researchers. Some remained skeptical, noting that deep understanding and creativity are still hard for AI. Overall, people found the benchmark useful, but many agreed there is still a long way to go for AI in math.
