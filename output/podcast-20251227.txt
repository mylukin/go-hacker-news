Moving on, we have a fascinating story about retrocomputing. Someone got Windows 2, Word, and Excel running on an old Apricot PC/Xi—a rare British computer from the 1980s that is not IBM PC-compatible. The Apricot uses an Intel 8086 chip and 3.5” floppy disks, but couldn’t run standard IBM PC software, so it never became popular.

The author explains that Windows 1 had already been ported to the Apricot, but it was limited. Porting Windows 2 was much harder, mainly because the drivers for Windows 1 were only available in a combined boot file, not as separate drivers needed for Windows 2. The author tried to write simple drivers and even created a tool to extract and patch the old drivers into a format that worked for Windows 2. After a lot of debugging, including writing a new system driver and fixing the mouse and timer, they finally got Windows 2 running with working graphics, along with Word, Excel, and some games.

The article gives a tour of the system and hardware. The author thanks their fiancée and other experts for help.

In the comments, people praise the author’s dedication and skill. Many are surprised anyone would put so much effort into such an obscure machine, but respect the passion. Some share their own stories about rare computers, and others talk about the technical challenges. There’s also talk about the history of the Apricot, its advanced features, and why lack of IBM compatibility doomed it. Some debate whether this is “tech archaeology” or useful work, but most agree it’s impressive and inspiring.

Now, let’s look at the problem of keeping clocks in sync across many computers. Each computer has its own clock, and small differences in hardware, temperature, or age mean that clocks drift apart over time. This can cause big problems: for example, file changes might be ignored if the server’s clock is ahead, or databases might put actions in the wrong order—critical for things like bank transactions. Debugging is also harder if logs from different machines don’t match up.

There are several ways to sync clocks. Cristian’s algorithm uses a central server, but network delays make it hard to be exact. The Berkeley algorithm averages times from all computers, adjusting clocks slowly to avoid jumps. The most common system is NTP, which uses layers of servers to spread time from atomic clocks or GPS, usually accurate to a few milliseconds, but network and OS delays can hurt precision.

Some jobs need even better accuracy, like financial trading or science experiments. These may use PTP, which needs special hardware. Sometimes, instead of matching the real-world clock, computers use logical clocks—like Lamport timestamps or vector clocks—to order events. Google’s Spanner uses TrueTime, which returns a time interval, not a single number, to make sure events are ordered correctly. CockroachDB uses hybrid logical clocks to get many benefits without special hardware.

The article says there is no perfect solution—choose based on your accuracy needs, cost, and complexity. Always plan for small errors, because perfect clock sync is impossible.

Readers shared many stories about clock drift causing problems, leap seconds crashing systems, and the high cost or difficulty of using GPS or PTP. Some discuss logical clocks and tips for safer software, like checking for time jumps. Others say most apps don’t need microsecond accuracy, but understanding the problem is important. Many agree every developer working on distributed systems should learn more about time sync, since time bugs are hard to find and fix.

NCAR is the top weather and climate research center in the U.S. It has helped invent tools to warn planes about dangerous winds, measure hurricane strength, and improve weather forecasts. If NCAR closes, many people will lose access to valuable weather and climate information, and it would be hard to rebuild later. Weather and climate affect everyone, and weaker science could put more people at risk from storms and disasters.

Now, let’s cover new security problems in GnuPG, the popular tool for PGP encryption and signing. A researcher found many serious bugs—some let attackers change signed messages or make fake signatures, others allow recovery of the original message, or let attackers add subkeys, inject trusted comments, or confuse users with strange formats. The comments are full of strong opinions. Some are shocked that such a trusted tool has so many problems, and worry about projects that depend on it for software updates or email security. Many agree the GnuPG team needs to fix these fast, and that everyone should watch for updates.

Finally, here’s a story about side projects and ownership. In the 1990s, a software developer at Apple made a note-taking program called “Antler Notes.” Apple wanted to buy it, but then realized they might already own it since the developer was an employee. At first, it looked like the developer would have to give it away for free, which was frustrating. In the end, some people at Apple arranged a bonus for him, though not officially tied to the program. The writer says this made him feel “alienated from his labor,” losing control over what he created.

The article explains that, by law, companies own anything employees make that relates to their work, even if made at home on personal time, unless the law or contract says otherwise. In California, Section 2870 says companies can’t claim things made entirely on your own time and resources, unless they relate to the company’s business or your job. But what “related to the company’s business” means is often unclear.

The writer’s advice: talk to your company’s legal team before starting side projects, and list all your old and future project ideas when you join a company. Some people list even vague ideas with code names to protect themselves, though this may not always work.

In the comments, many share their own stories of companies claiming side projects. Some say companies are unfair, others say the law is clear and employees need to be careful. Some advise always getting legal advice or avoiding side projects when working for big tech companies. Others say it’s best to work for companies that respect outside work. There’s also discussion about how rules are different in other countries. Most agree it’s a tough problem for developers who want to build things outside of work.
