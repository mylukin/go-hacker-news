The tasks are not simple puzzles. Instead, the AI must figure out what to do using its own experience in each new world. It does not get answers or hints in natural language. A perfect score means the AI can solve every task as fast as a human. The main things ARC-AGI-3 looks for are: learning new skills quickly, planning many steps ahead, and adjusting to new information as it appears. All the tasks can be solved by people, but they are made so that AI cannot just memorize solutions. The benchmark is interactive, not static—so it tests if the AI can keep learning and adapting over time, not just get the final answer right.

The design is meant to be fair: humans can play the games easily, and AIs cannot cheat by using hidden knowledge. The system also lets people watch replays of how each agent works, so you can see the choices and thinking step by step. There are tools for developers to connect their AI to the platform, and clear guides for building and testing agents. Everything is open and ready for people to try—both humans and machines.

## Earthquake scientists reveal how overplowing weakens soil at experimental farm

- 原文链接: [Earthquake scientists reveal how overplowing weakens soil at experimental farm](https://www.washington.edu/news/2026/03/19/earthquake-scientists-reveal-how-overplowing-weakens-soil-at-experimental-farm/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47517606)

This article talks about how scientists studied the effects of overplowing (tilling) soil at a farm in the UK. They wanted to see how tilling changes the way soil holds water, using special earthquake sensors.

The scientists put fiber optic cables in the ground to measure how the soil moved and how sound traveled through it. They looked at different farm rows: some had never been tilled, some were tilled a little, and some a lot. They also changed how much tractors pressed down on the soil to see how compaction made a difference. The main finding is that tilling and tractor pressure break the tiny channels in the soil that help it soak up water, making the ground less like a sponge. Instead of helping water reach plant roots, overplowing causes water to sit on top, creating mud and making the soil crusty. This can lead to more erosion and flooding. The study used a method called distributed acoustic sensing (DAS), which is cheap and gives lots of data quickly. When soil is wet, sound moves slower through it, so the scientists could tell how much water the soil held by measuring seismic velocity. Their method worked well for tracking soil moisture over time and could help farmers know when fields are at risk of flooding. It could also help scientists make better models for weather and earthquake risks.

In the Hacker News comments, some readers agreed that overplowing is a big problem for soil health. Many liked the use of earthquake tools for farm science and thought it was a clever idea. A few farmers in the comments shared that no-till farming works well for them, and they see less erosion and better water use. Some commenters were surprised that tilling, which is supposed to help plants, actually hurts the soil over time. Others pointed out that switching to no-till methods can be hard for some crops or in some climates. There were also questions about how easy it is for normal farmers to use these high-tech tools. Some readers wanted to know if the method could be used on big farms or in other countries. Others said that while the science is clear, changing old habits in farming is slow. A few people mentioned similar studies from the past and said it's good to see new technology confirming older ideas. Some commenters worried about the cost for small farms, but others said the sensors are getting cheaper. There was also talk about how soil health connects to climate change and food security. Overall, commenters liked the research and hoped it would help push for better farming methods.

## Quantization from the Ground Up

- 原文链接: [Quantization from the Ground Up](https://ngrok.com/blog/quantization)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47519295)

This article explains how quantization makes large language models (LLMs) much smaller and faster so you can run them on your own laptop. The author shows that even huge models with billions of parameters can be compressed to a quarter of their size with only a small drop in quality.

LLMs are big because they have billions or even trillions of parameters (also called weights), which are numbers that decide how the model works. These numbers are usually stored as 32-bit floats, which are accurate but take up a lot of memory. But most of these numbers are actually very close to zero, and don't need so much precision. If we use smaller floats (like 16-bit or even 8-bit), the model still works well. We can also use integers with a scaling trick—this is what quantization does.

There are different ways to quantize. Symmetric quantization squishes the numbers into a smaller range around zero, but sometimes wastes space. Asymmetric quantization shifts and rescales the numbers to use the available space better, reducing the error. In practice, quantization is done in small blocks (not the whole model at once) to avoid problems with outlier numbers.

Tests show that 8-bit quantization gives almost the same results as the original model, and 4-bit quantization is still pretty good (about 90% as accurate). If you go down to 2-bit, the model becomes much worse. Quantized models run faster, too, because there's less data to move around. The article explains how to measure the quality of quantized models using things like perplexity, KL divergence, and real benchmarks. For many tasks, the answers from quantized models are just as good as the original.

From the comments, some people are excited about how easy it is to run strong models on normal computers. They like having more control and privacy. Others warn that quantization can sometimes cause strange errors, especially for tasks that need high accuracy, or when using very small numbers of bits. A few readers point out that quantization is not the only way to make models smaller—there are also methods like pruning and distillation.

Some developers share tips about which quantization settings work best for different hardware. There are also questions about why 2-bit models are sometimes slower than 4-bit, and some suggest possible reasons like hardware limitations or the way the software is written. A couple of commenters say that quantization-aware training (training the model with quantization in mind) can give even better results, but it's more complex. 

Overall, most people agree that quantization is a great tool to make LLMs more practical for everyone, as long as you pick the right level and check that the quality is good enough for your needs.

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47516650)

Ensu is a new offline app from Ente that lets you run a large language model (LLM) directly on your own device, without sending your data to big tech companies. The team believes that LLMs are too important to leave in the hands of big companies, and they want to give everyone privacy and control.

The article explains that even people who dislike LLMs know the technology is here to stay. For fans of LLMs, the main problems today are privacy, censorship, and losing control when using cloud-based models like ChatGPT. The authors are worried that big tech can use LLMs to control or manipulate people. 

Local LLMs have not been as strong as cloud models, but the gap is getting smaller. The team at Ente has experience bringing advanced features, like local face recognition, to devices before, so they feel ready to do the same for LLMs. They say that small models are now good enough for many tasks, and the most important thing is reaching a point where these models are useful and private for everyone.

Ensu is a chat app that works like ChatGPT but keeps everything on your device. There is no cost, and your data stays private. Syncing and backup across devices with end-to-end encryption will come soon. Ensu is not as powerful as ChatGPT yet, but can already help with personal thoughts, book discussions, and offline chatting. The app is open source, runs on many platforms, and supports images. The core is written in Rust, and the team wants feedback to decide what features to build next.

Some future ideas include making Ensu more like a personal notebook with smart suggestions, using it as a smart launcher on your phone, or turning it into a real personal agent that remembers your choices and tasks. Syncing is not enabled yet because the team wants to wait for feedback before making big changes.
