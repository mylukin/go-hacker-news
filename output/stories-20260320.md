The carrier was moving from the Baltic Sea to the Middle East after new conflicts in the region. The group included three frigates and a support vessel. The article warns that apps like Strava can be a risk for military security if users do not turn on privacy settings. While the ship’s general route was public knowledge, Strava gave away the exact time and place.

## Attention Residuals

- 原文链接: [Attention Residuals](https://github.com/MoonshotAI/Attention-Residuals)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47458595)

This article is about a new way to connect layers inside Transformer models, called Attention Residuals (AttnRes). The idea is to help each layer in a Transformer use earlier information more smartly, instead of just adding all previous outputs together.

Normally, Transformers use "residual connections" where every layer simply adds its output to the outputs from before. This works, but as the model gets deeper, the effect of each layer gets weaker, and the numbers can get too big. AttnRes changes this by letting each layer pick which earlier information is most important, using attention. Each layer learns to focus on the right parts of the earlier outputs, instead of treating them all the same. 

There are two main versions: Full AttnRes and Block AttnRes. Full AttnRes lets every layer look at all previous layers, but this uses a lot of memory for big models. Block AttnRes solves this by grouping layers into blocks. It uses normal connections inside each block, but attention between blocks. This method keeps memory use low while still improving results.

The article shows some code for how Block AttnRes works in PyTorch. The results section says that models using AttnRes do better than normal ones on many benchmarks, like MMLU, TriviaQA, and HumanEval (for code). The biggest improvements are for tasks that need more reasoning or coding skills. Also, training is more stable: the numbers inside the model don't get too big, and learning happens more evenly across all layers.

In the Hacker News comments, some people are excited about this new idea, saying it could help make bigger or smarter models. A few users like that it's a "drop-in replacement," meaning you can use it without changing much code. Others ask if AttnRes could slow down training or make models harder to debug, since now every layer can see many earlier outputs. Some are curious about how much memory Block AttnRes really saves, and if it works as well for very large models. A few people mention that other research has tried to fix similar problems, but think this approach looks simple and practical. One commenter wonders if this will become the new standard for deep Transformers. Another warns that results on benchmarks don’t always mean real-world improvements, so more testing is needed. Overall, the community seems interested and ready to try this method in their own projects.

## The Los Angeles Aqueduct Is Wild

- 原文链接: [The Los Angeles Aqueduct Is Wild](https://practical.engineering/blog/2026/3/17/the-los-angeles-aqueduct-is-wild)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47416543)

The article talks about the Los Angeles Aqueduct, a huge system that brings water from the Sierra Nevada mountains down to Los Angeles, over 300 miles away. It explains how the aqueduct was built, how it works using only gravity, and why it is both an impressive and controversial project.

The aqueduct starts at the Owens River, where most of the water is taken from its natural path into a canal. This spot is much higher than Los Angeles, allowing the water to flow downhill the whole way without pumps. The canal needs a constant gentle slope, which was hard to build on the rough land. The first part of the canal is open, but after the Alabama Gates, it is lined with concrete to stop water from leaking into the ground. The Alabama Gates also let workers send extra water back to the river if the canal gets too full.

Taking water from Owens Valley caused big problems for people living there. Many farmers and ranchers lost water for their land and became very angry. Some even broke parts of the aqueduct, an event called the California Water Wars. The loss of water also dried up Owens Lake, making huge dust storms that were dangerous to breathe.

The aqueduct has to cross many hills and valleys, sometimes going underground in big pipes called inverted siphons. These are expensive and needed special steel shipped from far away. The aqueduct also has several reservoirs along the way, like Haiwee and Bouquet Canyon, to store water in case something goes wrong. There are two main aqueducts now, one built in the early 1900s and another in the 1970s to carry even more water.

The water is used to make electricity in hydroelectric power plants as it comes into Los Angeles. Before reaching the city, the water is cleaned and stored in the Los Angeles Reservoir, which is covered with millions of black plastic balls to stop sunlight and keep the water safe. Over time, LA has also pulled water from Mono Lake, but this dried it up too, causing more lawsuits and new rules to protect the environment.

The article says the aqueduct was an amazing engineering job, but also caused a lot of damage. It hurt farmers, native people, and wildlife. Now, climate change is making the water supply less certain, with less snow in the mountains some years. The writer says future engineers should learn from the past, thinking about nature, people, and long-term effects, not just building big projects fast.

In the comments, many people are amazed by the aqueduct’s size and the way it uses only gravity. Some say it shows how bold and risky early 1900s engineering was. Others talk about the harm done to Owens Valley, saying LA took water unfairly and left the valley dry and dusty. A few mention the movie “Chinatown,” which is based on these events.

Some commenters discuss the technical details, like how hard it is to keep such a long canal working, and the clever use of inverted siphons and hydro power. A few worry about what will happen as climate change makes water supplies less reliable. Others praise the article for explaining the history and design clearly, saying more people should learn about where their water comes from.

There are also people who think the aqueduct was a necessary step for LA to grow into a big city, even if it was harsh. Some wish there had been better planning and more respect for the people who lost water. Many agree that future water projects need to be more careful with nature and local communities. Overall, the comments show both respect for the engineering and sadness for the damage done.

## NumKong: 2'000 Mixed Precision Kernels for All

- 原文链接: [NumKong: 2'000 Mixed Precision Kernels for All](https://ashvardanian.com/posts/numkong/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47459447)

NumKong is a new open-source library with over 2,000 kernels for fast math on many types of numbers, including very small and mixed-precision types. The author stopped work on an older project, SimSIMD, and now focuses on NumKong, which is bigger and works in seven programming languages.

NumKong supports many hardware types, like Intel AMX, Arm SME, and RISC-V, and can run on CPUs, GPUs, and even browsers using WebAssembly. It handles many number formats, from high-precision (Float118) to tiny 4-bit floats and ints, and is tested for both speed and accuracy. For normal tasks like matrix multiplication, NumKong is not always the fastest, but it often gives more accurate results than NumPy or PyTorch, especially with complicated or very small number types. The library also provides special tricks and optimizations: for example, how to do fast integer dot products using bit tricks, or how to make reductions (like sum or min) work well on strided or packed data. It can process geospatial math, align 3D shapes, and do complex AI math—all much faster than older libraries like GeoPy or BioPython.

A big focus is on supporting many platforms and languages. NumKong works with Python, C, C++, Rust, Swift, JavaScript, Go, and can be used in machine learning, search engines, or even in browsers. The author describes how different CPUs have unique features and how NumKong takes advantage of them for better speed or precision. For example, Apple’s M4 chips are very good for some mixed-precision tasks; Intel and AMD are adding new features for these workloads too.

NumKong also tries to avoid hidden costs: it does not make hidden memory allocations or start threads without asking. You control memory and threading, which is important for big servers or special hardware. The code is modular, and you can use just the kernels you need. There are many examples in Python, C++, and Rust, showing how to use the library for common AI tasks, like vector search or batched dot products.

From the Hacker News comments, some users are impressed by the huge scope and multi-language support. They like how NumKong supports new number types and works on many platforms, even in the browser. Others are curious about real-world use cases and if the extra precision is worth the speed tradeoff for most people. Some wish the documentation was easier, as the technical details can be very deep. There are questions about how easy it is to install and use in production, and if it really replaces older libraries like OpenBLAS or cuBLAS for most users. A few worry that such a big project might be hard to maintain or keep up with new CPUs. Some developers appreciate the open-source nature and the clear benchmarks, while others are more cautious, wanting to see more adoption and long-term support. Overall, many agree that supporting more number types and platforms is important as AI and data science grow, and NumKong could fill a gap that “classic” libraries have missed.

## Parallel Perl – autoparallelizing interpreter with JIT

- 原文链接: [Parallel Perl – autoparallelizing interpreter with JIT](https://perl.petamem.com/gpw2026/perl-mit-ai-gpw2026.html#/4/1/1)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47424592)

This article talks about “Parallel Perl” (pperl), a new Perl 5 interpreter written in Rust, with help from AI tools and agents. The author, Richard Jelinek, shares how he used Perl and AI to build smart, off-grid homes, then explains how pperl adds new features to Perl.

The author first describes his smart home projects, using Perl to control solar power, heating, lighting, and sensors in houses. He tried existing Perl home automation tools like MisterHouse and FHEM but found them outdated or hard to use. So, he built his own system called WHIP, using modern hardware (STM32 microcontrollers, CAN bus, Raspberry Pis) and lots of Perl code for automation, sensors, and integrations.

AI helped write and test much of this Perl code, and even built tools like an SNMP MIB parser and gRPC bindings for Perl. The author says AI is now good at writing code, but humans still need to guide the process and decide what to build.

The main focus is pperl, a new Perl interpreter/platform. Many past attempts to rewrite Perl for better speed or features failed, but pperl tries a new path: 
- It aims for high compatibility with Perl 5.42, but is written in Rust for safety and speed.
- It uses Rust’s Rayon library to automatically run loops (like map/grep/for) in parallel.
- It has just-in-time (JIT) compilation via Cranelift, making “hot” code run much faster.
- It can call C libraries directly (Auto-FFI), without XS or extra compilation.
- It supports pre-compiled “bytecode cache” files for faster startup.
- It can run as a daemon (background server), so scripts start instantly.

Benchmarks show big speed-ups—sometimes over 400 times faster than Perl 5 for some tasks. The JIT compiler and parallelization work together for major gains, especially in numeric and loop-heavy code.

The author also shares small technical details, like fixing subtle bugs for maximum compatibility, and notes that pperl is best for smaller scripts or new code, since some edge cases may differ from Perl 5.

In the Hacker News comments, many people are excited and impressed by the project. Some say it’s amazing to see Perl get a fast, modern interpreter. Others discuss the challenges of keeping full compatibility with Perl 5, which is famous for odd edge cases. A few express doubt about how easy it will be for pperl to support all of Perl’s features, especially for big, old codebases. Some point out that Perl’s ecosystem has slowed down, and wonder if pperl might help bring new life or if it’s too late.

Other commenters talk about the technical details—how using Rust for safety and Rayon for parallelism sounds great; some are surprised by the speedups from the JIT. A few Perl fans say they want to try pperl for their own scripts, especially if it “just works” with little change. On the other hand, a few worry that Perl’s strong reliance on CPAN modules, sometimes with C extensions (XS), might be hard to support in pperl.

Some users compare pperl to projects in other languages, like PyPy for Python or GraalVM for Java, and ask if it could do for Perl what those projects did for their languages. Others suggest that if pperl really works, it could make Perl scripts useful again for automation, data tasks, or as a scripting shell. Finally, a few say they wish more languages got this kind of “second life” with modern interpreters, and thank the author for pushing Perl forward.
