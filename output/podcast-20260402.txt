First, Google has released Gemma 4, a new family of open AI models based on Gemini 3 research. Gemma 4 comes in several sizes: from small E2B and E4B models for mobile and IoT devices, to larger 26B and 31B models for personal computers and servers. The small models can run offline on devices like phones and Raspberry Pi, supporting real-time audio and visual processing. The bigger models are made for coding assistants, agent-based workflows, and advanced reasoning, and they are optimized for consumer GPUs so developers can run them locally.

Gemma 4 is strong in many areas. It can handle tasks in 140 languages, work with text and images, and help build autonomous agents. Developers can fine-tune the models using popular frameworks. Google says Gemma 4 scores high on benchmarks for language, coding, math, and scientific knowledge. The 31B model, for example, beats older models on multilingual Q&A, coding, and math tests. Safety is a focus—Google says the models go through strong security checks and are safe for enterprise use. The weights are available for download on Hugging Face, Ollama, Kaggle, and Docker, and you can run or train the models on Google Cloud, Keras, or Google Kubernetes Engine.

On Hacker News, people are happy to see Google release open models. Many like that small models can run offline and are good for edge devices. Some note the license is not fully open source, so there are limits for commercial use. A few want even smaller models. Some compare Gemma 4 to Llama and Mistral, debating which is faster or smarter. There are also worries about possible tracking, but others trust Google’s reputation. Overall, the community is interested and plans to test Gemma 4 for coding, research, and running on their own hardware.

Next, Tailscale fixed a problem with its menu bar icon disappearing behind the notch on newer MacBooks. Apple does not let developers control where icons show up in the menu bar, and there’s no warning when icons get hidden. Tailscale first tried a pop-up warning, but it was not perfect—the message could appear at the wrong times. The real solution was to add a new windowed interface for macOS. Now, users can open Tailscale from the Dock or with Spotlight, not just the menu bar. The new app lets you search devices, check status, copy IPs, send files, and more. There’s even a mini player and a product tour for new users. The menu bar app still exists, but the windowed app makes Tailscale easier to use, especially if your menu bar is crowded.

In the comments, users were surprised Apple has not fixed the menu bar problem. Many shared their own struggles with lost icons and use third-party apps like Bartender to manage crowded menus. Some praised Tailscale’s quick warning pop-up and liked that the team shared code for it. The new windowed interface is popular, and some wonder if other apps will follow. People also wish for a more customizable menu bar or an overflow menu. Not everyone likes the notch, but some have gotten used to it. Overall, users appreciate that Tailscale listened to feedback and kept improving the app.

George Goble, known as “GHG,” has died at age 73 in West Lafayette, Indiana. George was an early computer expert who helped shape modern computing, especially at Purdue University. He worked on the first dual-CPU Unix system, created early computer networking, and helped connect DEC VAX systems. He even streamed one of the first live web videos of a solar eclipse and checked email on a beach using a laptop and cell phone, long before smartphones. He worked with Dennis Ritchie, Ken Thompson, and Bill Joy, and received awards for his work. George also worked on refrigeration and invented a way to make hydrogen from water. He became famous for lighting a barbecue grill in three seconds with liquid oxygen, which won him the Ig Nobel Prize.

George was known for his humor, kindness, and helping friends. Many on Hacker News remember his clever ideas, patience, and sense of fun. Stories about his fast BBQ lighting and early networking experiments are popular. Some thanked him for his work on refrigeration, which helped the environment. People say he made science fun and showed what was possible with curiosity and a little risk. The community feels lucky to have learned from his life and stories, and many say, “Rest in peace, GHG.”

NASA’s Artemis II mission is sending astronauts around the moon, and for the first time, they have a real toilet onboard. Earlier missions used plastic bags and tubes, which were messy and hard to use. The new Universal Waste Management System, or UWMS, took over ten years to design. It lets astronauts pee and poop at the same time, works for both men and women, and gives privacy with a door. The toilet is made from 3D-printed titanium, is strong but light, and uses handles to help astronauts stay steady in microgravity. Older toilets sometimes let waste float around if something went wrong.

The UWMS was first tested on the ISS in 2020 and installed in 2021. Now, a special version is on the Orion capsule for Artemis II. NASA hopes this toilet will work for future moon and Mars trips. In the comments, many joke about space toilets but agree it’s important—a bad toilet can ruin a mission. Some remember old Apollo stories about floating poop. People are happy NASA now includes women’s needs in the design. There’s respect for NASA engineers who solved such a hard problem. Overall, the mood is light, but many agree this is real progress for space travel.

Alibaba has released Qwen3.6-Plus, a new AI model for real-world tasks like coding, web development, and image and video understanding. The model is now available through an API, and it’s much better than the older Qwen3.5, especially at solving complex coding problems. Qwen3.6-Plus has a big context window and is good at “agentic coding”—acting almost like a coding assistant that can plan and finish tasks. It’s strong in reasoning, memory, and making decisions, and beats or matches popular AI models like Claude, Kimi, and Gemini on many tests.

Qwen3.6-Plus is also strong with images and videos, able to understand charts, find people in pictures, or create slideshows. Developers can use a new feature called “preserve_thinking” to help the AI remember its thought process. The model works with popular coding tools and is moving closer to being a real “agent” that can see, understand, plan, and act.

On Hacker News, many are impressed by the technical progress and large context window. Some are excited to try the new model for code review or project planning. Others wonder about real-world performance and ask about pricing, privacy, and whether smaller, open-source versions will come out. There is debate about whether an AI can really replace a human coder or planner. Some are wary of lock-in to Alibaba’s cloud, while others compare Qwen3.6-Plus to models from OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google. Overall, people see it as a big step forward, especially for coding and planning, and are either excited to use it or waiting to see real-world results.

Next, a writer shares a lesson: good ideas should not need many lies to be accepted. He uses business and politics as examples—especially the Iraq War. He learned in business school that companies tried to hide stock option costs, which showed the options were not as good as claimed. He says if a war was really a good idea, leaders would not have needed to lie so much about weapons of mass destruction. He explains that when someone lies once, you should not trust their future predictions. In business, if you do not check if forecasts match reality, bad forecasts become common. The same is true in politics. He ends by saying that people who lie often get a reputation, and then nobody believes them, even when they tell the truth.

In the comments, many people agree: if an idea needs tricks and lies, it’s probably a bad idea. Some share their own stories. Others note that sometimes even good ideas need to be “sold” with some exaggeration. Some discuss how hard it is to know when someone is lying or just mistaken. Many focus on the importance of checking facts and doing audits. A few joke about business school lessons being just expensive common sense.

A former Microsoft engineer wrote about big mistakes inside the Azure cloud team. He says these mistakes almost made Microsoft lose OpenAI as a customer and hurt trust with the US government. The author joined the Azure Core team and was shocked to see a plan to move many Windows features to a small chip made for Linux, with little memory and power. He learned the current system already used too many resources and did not scale well. He also found out there were 173 different agents running on Azure nodes, and no one could explain what they all did.

He worried that all these agents could make Azure fragile. He tried to warn leaders, but says he was ignored. He hints at more problems, like wasted efforts, communication failures, and features promised before they were ready.

On Hacker News, many agree with the author’s worries. Some share their own stories about failed projects or problems in big companies. Others think the article makes sense, especially about too many agents making things fragile. Some defend Microsoft, saying it’s not easy to run a giant cloud. There are jokes about tech companies loving complexity. Overall, people show both agreement and doubt, and many say they would be more careful if they depended on Azure.

AMD has released Lemonade, a new open source server that lets you run AI models for text, images, and speech on your own computer using your GPU or NPU. Lemonade is small—only 2MB—and works on Windows, Linux, and macOS. You can install it quickly, and it auto-detects your hardware. It can run different AI engines and models at the same time, and the built-in app makes it easy to download and switch models. It follows the OpenAI API standard, so developers can use Lemonade as a drop-in replacement. The latest release brings easier package management, better integration with Hugging Face, and faster support for new models.

In the comments, many are excited about running big AI models locally, without sending data to the cloud. Some like that Lemonade is open source and private. Others wonder if their hardware is good enough, while some are happy that Lemonade works on different platforms. Developers mention how easy it is to switch from cloud APIs to Lemonade. There are questions about support for older GPUs and laptops. Some people hope Lemonade will add more features for advanced users. Overall, most comments are positive and show excitement about making local AI more accessible.

Finally, LinkedIn is using hidden code to check which browser extensions you have installed when you visit their site. This scan happens without telling users, and the data is sent to LinkedIn and outside companies. The article says this is illegal under EU law, because LinkedIn collects personal information, such as religious or political views or job-hunting status, just by seeing which extensions you use. LinkedIn also scans for competitor extensions and shares your data with third-parties, including a cybersecurity firm. None of this is mentioned in LinkedIn’s privacy policy. The group behind the article, Fairlinked, wants people to support legal action to stop this.

On Hacker News, people are shocked and angry, calling this a big privacy violation. Some say many big sites do similar things. Others worry it’s unsafe to use browser extensions. Some think legal action is the only way to stop this. There are questions about whether this is illegal everywhere or just in the EU. Some are frustrated that LinkedIn does not warn users or let them opt out. A few suggest technical ways to block the scanning, but agree it’s not easy for most users.
