## The Anatomy of an AI-Native Org

- 原文链接: [The Anatomy of an AI-Native Org](https://ajeygore.in/content/the-anatomy-of-an-ai-native-org)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48622815)

This article talks about how AI is changing the way companies are organized, especially in tech. It explains that most software companies had a similar structure for the last 30 years, with a few people at the top deciding the purpose (“why”), a middle group planning what to build (“what”), and many people at the bottom doing the actual work (“how”).

The author says the middle layer was mostly about translation—turning business ideas into specs, specs into tickets, tickets into code, and so on. Many frameworks, meetings, and manager roles were built around this translation process. But now, with AI agents doing much of this translation work faster and cheaper, the need for so many people in the middle is going away.

AI did not just replace certain job titles. Instead, it replaced the translation type of work, which was common for roles like project managers, engineering managers, and even some engineers. The hardest and most creative work (“why” and “what”) is actually getting more important, while the simple “how” tasks are being handled by AI.

Managers who only coordinate without adding value are at risk. Some managers are trying to keep old rituals to show they are needed, but others are moving back to hands-on work, like designing or coding. The author says managers now must contribute to important decisions or system designs, not just run meetings.

In the new team, there are fewer people overall. The top group still sets the vision. The middle group (now bigger) makes key decisions about what to build and what is good. The bottom group (smaller) does the hardest technical work, like building systems that AI can safely use. AI agents do most of the repetitive or translation tasks.

Being “hands-on” now means being close to the real work and making big decisions, not just approving tickets or running meetings. The author says companies will hire fewer people, and the roles will be different. Engineers should focus on the things AI cannot do, like defining quality or solving tough problems.

People in the comments have mixed feelings. Some agree that AI will shrink the middle layer, making teams smaller and more focused. Others worry that removing too many coordinators could cause chaos, as someone still needs to make sure everyone works together. A few point out that not all translation work can be automated, especially when things are unclear or messy. Some say that “why” and “what” work is much harder to scale and needs real human judgment. Others feel worried that many current jobs could disappear or change a lot, and wonder how companies will help people adapt. One commenter thinks this change will make work more interesting for those who stay, but harder for those who liked routine tasks. Another person says that even with AI, bad decisions at the top could still hurt the company, just faster. Overall, many agree that teams will look very different soon, and everyone will need to learn new skills or become more flexible.

## JSON-LD Explained for Personal Websites

- 原文链接: [JSON-LD Explained for Personal Websites](https://hawksley.dev/blog/json-ld-explained-for-personal-websites/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48621517)

This article explains how to use JSON-LD, a way to add structured data to your personal website so that search engines and web crawlers can better understand your site. The author shows how using JSON-LD can help your site appear in search results with better previews and possibly improve your SEO.

The article first explains the basics of JSON-LD. You add a special `<script type="application/ld+json">` in your website’s `<head>`. Inside this script, you write your site’s information in JSON format, following the rules from schema.org. This helps web crawlers know what kind of content is on each page. The author gives examples for different types of nodes, like `WebSite`, `WebPage`, `Person`, `ProfilePage`, `SoftwareApplication`, `BreadcrumbList`, `CollectionPage`, `Blog`, and `BlogPosting`. Each node has some required fields, such as `@type` (what it is), `@id` (a unique URL), and other properties like `name`, `description`, and `image`. For a personal site, you usually need to add `WebSite`, `ProfilePage`, and `Person` nodes to the root page. If you have a blog, you also add `Blog` and `BlogPosting` nodes for each post. The author shares many copy-paste-ready code blocks for each type and gives tips on which properties are important or optional. They also talk about linking nodes together using IDs, and about best practices, such as making sure your IDs are unique and using the right node types for each page.

Many people in the comments are skeptical about the real benefits of JSON-LD today. Some say that Google now just uses your site’s data to train AI models, and shows answers above your site in search results, so these structured data tags may not help much with getting more visitors. Others remember adding microdata years ago, but only saw Google use it to keep users on their search page. Some think making your site more understandable for search engines is still useful, especially for sites that want to appear as sources in LLMs (AI models), or for business sites that want to show up on maps or in rich search results. There are jokes about making fake pages just for LLMs to see, or hiding real info from scrapers. Some people say adding JSON-LD is extra work, since you already have to make your HTML semantic, but others point out that semantic HTML and JSON-LD have different goals: HTML helps people and browsers, JSON-LD helps machines know things like who wrote a page, what products you sell, or what a blog post is about. A few commenters prefer older formats like RDFa or microdata, but admit JSON-LD is now easiest to use and works best with search engines. Some say it is fine to duplicate some information between HTML and JSON-LD, because it is easier to maintain. Finally, a few readers think you should only add the most important metadata and skip the rest unless you have a good reason.

## Everything Is Logarithms

- 原文链接: [Everything Is Logarithms](https://alexkritchevsky.com/2026/05/25/everything-is-logarithms.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48622626)

This article says that many things in math are like logarithms, even if we don’t usually think about them that way. The author tries to show how ideas from different areas—like vectors, derivatives, and dimensions—can all be thought of using the idea of logarithms.

First, the author explains that a logarithm usually has a base, but you can think of a “baseless” logarithm as an abstract object. You only get a number when you compare two baseless logarithms (like dividing one by another), which is like changing units in physics. This is similar to how, in vectors, you need a reference point to measure a displacement. 

Next, the article says that logarithms act a lot like vectors. Both can be written in different “units” or coordinate systems, and changing the base of a logarithm is like changing the basis of a vector. There are also “projections” in both areas, like p-adic valuations in number theory, that pick out parts of the structure—just like picking a component of a vector.

The author then flips the idea, showing that vectors can be seen as logarithms of translation operators, especially in differential geometry. There are also connections between logarithms and derivatives: the natural log can be written as a kind of limit, similar to how we define derivatives. The dimension function from linear algebra (which counts the size of a basis) also acts like a logarithm, because it turns multiplication in vector spaces into addition in dimensions.

The article keeps going, showing that even bases in vector spaces and certain combinatoric ideas can be thought of in terms of logarithms. The main message is that these connections are not just tricks—they might hint at a deep, unified idea in mathematics. The author feels that math hides these patterns under different names and notations, making things look more different than they really are.

In the comment section, there are not many replies yet, but the top comment points out that the article is very abstract and hard to follow, but also “fun and thought-provoking.” Some readers say they like seeing math ideas linked together in new ways, even if they don’t fully understand all the details. One person hopes for more concrete examples, saying that the article could help students if it was more practical. Another commenter wonders if these connections could help make teaching math easier, by using the same ideas in different topics.

A few users mention similar ideas they have seen in physics or category theory, agreeing that math often repeats the same patterns. Others are unsure if treating everything as a logarithm is helpful, or if it’s “just wordplay.” One reader suggests that the idea of “units” in math is important and more people should talk about it. There is also a suggestion that this kind of thinking might help with understanding programming concepts, like types or data structures, in a new way.

Overall, the comments show both excitement and confusion. Some people enjoy the big-picture thinking, while others want more clear, simple examples. Most agree that looking for patterns across different areas of math is a good way to discover new ideas.

## Prefer duplication over the wrong abstraction (2016)

- 原文链接: [Prefer duplication over the wrong abstraction (2016)](https://sandimetz.com/blog/2016/1/20/the-wrong-abstraction)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48620090)

This article talks about why copying code is sometimes better than making the wrong abstraction. The author explains that trying to remove duplicate code too early can make things worse if the abstraction chosen does not fit future needs.

The main idea is that when you see repeated code, it is tempting to pull it out and put it in a shared function or class. At first, this feels clean and smart. But as the program changes, new requirements often do not fit the old abstraction. Developers start adding more parameters and special cases to the shared code. Over time, the abstraction becomes full of conditionals, flags, and exceptions. This makes the code hard to read and easy to break.

The author says when you notice this happening, it is often best to "go backward." That means: bring the duplicated code back into each place that uses it, delete any extra parts that are not needed, and let each spot have its own simple version. This helps you see what is really the same and what is different, making it easier to find a better abstraction later, if one really exists.

A big problem is the "sunk cost fallacy"—developers feel they must keep a complicated abstraction because so much time was spent building it. But keeping bad abstractions makes code harder to change. The author suggests: do not be afraid to remove abstractions that no longer fit. Wait until you truly understand the problem before making a new shared function or class.

In the comments, many people agree with the article but stress the need to balance. Some say "single source of truth" is important—if having copies means bugs can appear in one place but not another, that is risky. Others point out that sometimes it is not clear if two pieces of code really do the same thing, or just look similar. They agree that premature abstraction often causes more trouble than a little duplication.

Some commenters share stories where over-abstraction made codebases very hard to work with. Others say too much duplication leads to messy maintenance, especially in big teams. A few suggest waiting until code is repeated three times before making it shared. Many note that every project is different, and there is no simple rule.

Some think modern tools or even AI might make dealing with duplication easier. Others warn that LLMs (AI code generators) might make duplication worse if not managed. Several people mention that good abstractions are still valuable, but finding them takes time and real experience.

Overall, the main lesson is: do not rush to abstract code. Wait until the need is clear, and do not be afraid to undo a bad abstraction. Both duplication and abstraction have risks, and choosing between them is an art, not a science.

## Stop wasting tokens and re explaining your project between sessions

- 原文链接: [Stop wasting tokens and re explaining your project between sessions](https://github.com/raiyanyahya/recall)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48622590)

This article is about a tool called Recall that helps you save and summarize your Claude Code sessions locally, so you don’t have to keep explaining your project every time you start a new session. Recall works completely offline and doesn’t send any data to external servers, keeping your information private.

Recall solves the problem of “cold starts” with Claude Code, where every new session forgets your project’s context. Instead of sending your whole project summary to the AI each time (which costs money and uses up tokens), Recall keeps a record of your work on your own computer. It creates two files: `history.md` records everything you do, and `context.md` gives a short summary of your project, including goals, files touched, and next steps. This summary is built by a Python script using TF-IDF and TextRank algorithms—no large language model calls, no internet needed.

You can control Recall using simple commands like `/recall:save` to update your summary, `/recall:show` to display the current summary, and `/recall:log` to see your session history. The tool is easy to install and requires no special setup; it runs as soon as you load the plugin. You can adjust settings in a config file, like how many sentences the summary uses or if you want to include git info.

Recall is careful about privacy and security. It never uses API keys or sends data out, and tries to remove secrets before saving logs. It also stops anyone from changing where files are saved, so nothing leaves your project folder. If you want to share team memory, you can commit the `.recall/` folder to git, but it’s ignored by default.

In the comments, people liked the idea of saving tokens and time by not repeating themselves between AI sessions. Some users said they often have to explain their whole project again and again, so this tool could help them a lot. A few people wondered about security, especially if the summary or logs might leak secrets, but they liked that Recall tries to scrub these out. Others asked if it works with other AI tools or only with Claude Code, and discussed possible ways to use or improve it. One commenter suggested adding encryption for even more privacy. Some people said the offline, no-API design is a big plus compared to cloud-based memory tools. There were also questions about how well the summarizer works for large projects, and if it can handle very long histories. Overall, most users were interested in trying Recall, especially for solo or small-team coding.

## (How to Write a (Lisp) Interpreter (In Python)) (2010)

- 原文链接: [(How to Write a (Lisp) Interpreter (In Python)) (2010)](https://norvig.com/lispy.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48619831)

This article shows how to write a simple interpreter for the Lisp language Scheme, using Python. The writer, Peter Norvig, explains how you can build your own small Scheme-like language, called Lispy, and why this is a good way to learn about programming languages and computers.

He starts by explaining the basics of Scheme: its syntax (how you write code) is simple, relying mainly on parentheses and just a few keywords. Unlike other languages, everything in Scheme is an expression, and there’s no difference between statements and expressions. Numbers and symbols are atomic (meaning, simple and not split up), and all other things are lists. The first thing in a list decides what happens—if it is a keyword like "if," it's a special command, but if it's a function, it's a function call. The article compares Scheme to Java and Python, which have many more keywords and syntax rules.

Norvig builds up the language step by step. First, he creates a simple calculator version of Lispy that can handle numbers, variables, if-conditions, definitions, and function calls. He explains how to turn code into tokens (small pieces), how to parse (analyze) these tokens into a tree structure, and how to evaluate (run) the code. In Python, this means turning Scheme code into lists and numbers, then running them using Python functions.

He shows how to build an environment, which is a set of variable names and their values. This lets you define new variables or functions. He writes the core eval function, which looks at the kind of expression and runs the right logic—getting a variable, doing math, making a decision with "if", defining something new, or calling a function.

The article then adds more features to Lispy, like quoting expressions (not evaluating them), assigning new values to variables, and creating user-defined functions with "lambda". To support local variables, he updates the environment code so that variables can be looked up in nested scopes. He uses Python classes to do this, making it so functions can remember the environment in which they were created.

Norvig finishes by showing Lispy in action: defining functions, using recursion (calling a function from inside itself), and doing more complex things like the Fibonacci sequence. He explains that, although Lispy is small and simple, it is fast enough for learning, but it lacks many features of full Scheme. Still, building Lispy is a powerful way to understand how interpreters work.

Now, looking at the comments, people share many views. Some say building a Lisp interpreter is an eye-opening project and recommend doing it in different ways or languages. Others mention MAL (Make-A-Lisp), a project that helps people build Lisps in many languages, saying it’s more language-neutral and beginner-friendly compared to Norvig’s Python version. There’s a discussion about Lisp's heavy use of parentheses—some think it’s hard to read, while others say you get used to it and even enjoy its simplicity with the right formatting. One person points out that Lisp’s syntax lets you keep the interpreter code small, unlike more complex languages with infix operators.

Some commenters talk about the learning value: they say even if AI or someone else can write an interpreter better, doing it yourself is still very useful for understanding programming. Others worry that, as AI gets better, tutorials and learning resources may become less important, but some disagree and say old resources still matter because they help teach both humans and machines.

There’s also nostalgia—some people remember when programming was just for fun, not always for profit. People reflect on why they build things: sometimes it’s for the process, not just the result. A few share their own Lisp projects, or mention that building a Lisp is a good way to test a new language or to learn about programming in general.

In short, the article and comments show that writing a Lisp interpreter is both a simple and deep way to learn about programming, and that the experience is valuable whether or not you use AI or read old books. The community finds both the technical process and the learning journey rewarding, no matter how technology changes.

## The minimum viable unit of saleable software

- 原文链接: [The minimum viable unit of saleable software](https://brandur.org/minimum-viable-unit)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48620342)

The article talks about when it makes sense to buy software versus building your own, especially now that AI tools can help build things faster and cheaper. The author gives an example where a company replaced Jira (which cost $400/month) with their own tool, made using an AI, but questions if this really saves money or time in the long run.

The main idea is that even with AI, building good software takes work. You have to spend time tweaking the AI’s output, testing it, and fixing bugs. Maintenance is always needed, and a human has to check and manage the tool. The author uses numbers to show that if an engineer makes $200,000 a year (about $96 per hour), they can’t spend more than about four hours a month on their own Jira clone before it costs more than just paying for Jira. Even if you’re really fast, it takes years to break even after the first build. For expensive tools, like Salesforce at $25,000/month for 50 users, building your own might make more sense since you could hire engineers for the same money. The author calls this the “zone of viability”—when software is priced right, it’s better to buy than build, even with AI. For his own project, River, he hopes the price and features are good enough that people will buy instead of building their own.

In the comments, some people agree that building is easier now, but it still takes a lot of time and motivation to finish and maintain a project, especially after the first burst of excitement. Others say that AI can make coding faster, but making the software easy to use and well-designed is still hard. Some mention that changing requirements and specs mean code often needs to be rewritten, and AI doesn’t help much with understanding what users really want. There are technical suggestions too, like using different models for better results.

On the business side, people say buying is usually easier and safer, especially for companies that want to avoid the risk and work of maintaining their own tools. Some warn that trying to rebuild big tools like Jira or Google Docs is much harder than it seems, even with AI. Others discuss how buying software can be slowed down by company rules and bureaucracy, and sometimes small purchases take more time to approve than they’re worth. Some think developers often want to build their own tools because it’s fun or they want control, but as they get more experience, they prefer to buy and save time.

A few people say the real value is in software that helps the business make money, not just save it. Some think community-built software can be great, but others warn about risks like too many features or lack of direction. In the end, most agree that build vs. buy is still a tough choice, but AI has only made the line move a bit—not erased it.
