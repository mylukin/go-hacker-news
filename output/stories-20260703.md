## Espionage Against the European Parliament

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48779683)

This article is about spyware attacks on members of the European Parliament, especially Stelios Kouloglou, who was part of a committee investigating spyware abuse. Kouloglou was hacked with Pegasus spyware while working on the PEGA Committee, and his phone was infected during important committee activities.

Pegasus is a powerful spyware made by NSO Group, often sold to governments. Once on a phone, it can read messages, listen to calls, and access private information. The Citizen Lab, a research group, found that Kouloglou’s phone was infected in October 2022 and again in March 2023. These hacks happened during busy times for the PEGA Committee, like when they were writing reports and preparing for country visits. The investigation does not claim that the Greek government was responsible, but thinks the attacker could be someone who is allowed to spy in more than one European country. The same operator might also have targeted Russian and Belarusian journalists in Europe.

Kouloglou was a journalist and politician from Greece. He worked on the PEGA Committee, which was created to look into the use of Pegasus and similar spyware in the EU. The committee wanted to find out how governments were using spyware against activists, journalists, and politicians. While working as a Member of European Parliament (MEP), Kouloglou kept reporting for his news site and stayed active in public debates about spyware.

When his phone was hacked, Kouloglou was in the hospital and met with another journalist, Thanasis Koukakis, who had also been a victim of spyware. This means private medical and committee information could have been stolen. Apple sent warning notifications to Kouloglou about the spyware, but he did not remember seeing them at the time.

Other MEPs have been targeted with Pegasus in the past, and there are worries that more members might have been hacked without knowing. The article suggests that all members and staff who worked on the PEGA Committee should get their devices checked for spyware.

The report does not point to a specific country as the attacker but says this kind of hacking is dangerous for democracy. It could have let the attackers see secret discussions inside the European Parliament and influence their work. The article calls for stronger investigations, better device screening, and more support for MEPs to protect against spyware.

In the comment section, many readers are shocked that even high-level officials are not safe from spyware. Some say this shows how hard it is to stay secure, even with Apple’s warning systems. Others think that Pegasus and similar tools are too powerful and should be banned or controlled more strictly. Some readers ask how such spyware can bypass security on modern phones and wonder if anyone can really be protected. Others point out that governments and private companies need to work together to fight these threats. There are also comments about privacy, with people saying that if even lawmakers are targets, ordinary citizens must be at risk too. Some discuss whether the EU is doing enough to protect its members and call for more action and transparency. A few suggest that politicians should use special phones or only communicate in person for sensitive matters. Some readers are worried about the future, saying that as spyware gets more advanced, attacks like this will only become more common. ## SearXNG: A free internet metasearch engine

- 原文链接: [SearXNG: A free internet metasearch engine](https://github.com/searxng/searxng)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48779454)

SearXNG is a free and open-source metasearch engine that lets people search the internet without being tracked or profiled. It works by collecting results from many different search engines and databases, and then showing them in one place.

The project is available on GitHub and uses the AGPL-3.0 license, which is a strong open-source license. SearXNG is written mainly in Python with some other code in Shell, HTML, and TypeScript. Anyone can install SearXNG on their own server by following the installation guide, and there are also many public instances run by volunteers. The project focuses on privacy and does not store user data or create user profiles. If you want to change how it works, you can edit the settings, and there is a configuration guide to help. SearXNG can search across sites like Google, Bing, DuckDuckGo, Yahoo, and more, all at once. It is possible to contribute to the project, and there is a community chat room for users and developers. Many people use SearXNG because they do not like big companies tracking their searches. The website and documentation are well-organized, making it easy to get started.

From the comment section, many users say they like SearXNG because it helps protect their privacy online. Some people run their own SearXNG servers so they can control their own search experience. Others use public SearXNG instances but worry these can be blocked by search providers like Google. A few users say SearXNG results are not always as good or fast as Google, but they accept this for better privacy. There are tips in the comments about setting up SearXNG behind Tor or a VPN for extra protection. Some users discuss problems with search engines changing their APIs, which can break SearXNG until it is updated. People also mention that running a public SearXNG server can be hard because of high traffic and the risk of being blocked. Some suggest using SearXNG together with other private search tools for the best results. A few users warn that you still need to trust the server you use if it is not your own. Others say they wish more people knew about SearXNG and privacy tools in general. Overall, the comments show a strong interest in privacy and open-source search.

## FreeBSD ate my RAM

- 原文链接: [FreeBSD ate my RAM](https://crocidb.com/post/freebsd-ate-my-ram/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48778757)

This article is about someone moving their server from Ubuntu to FreeBSD and then being surprised by how FreeBSD reports RAM usage. The author wanted to understand why different tools showed different amounts of “used” and “free” memory.

The main point is that modern operating systems, like FreeBSD and Linux, use all the RAM they can to cache files from disk, so the system runs faster. Even if RAM looks “used,” much of it is just cache and can be freed when needed. FreeBSD breaks memory into types: “active” (in use right now), “inactive” (not used recently), “laundry” (about to go to swap), “wired” (locked by the system or kernel), and “free” (really unused). FreeBSD also uses “ARC” cache if you use the ZFS file system, which takes a lot of RAM for fast file access. Tools like `top`, `htop`, `fastfetch`, and `btop` all try to show memory stats, but they use different rules (heuristics) to decide what counts as “used” or “free.” For example, `btop` on FreeBSD was wrong because it used 32-bit numbers, so it wrapped around at 4 GB, making results look silly if you had more RAM. Also, it tried to read a memory value that was always zero, because it’s just a dummy for old programs. The author wrote fixes and patches for both `btop` and `htop` to handle ARC cache and memory reporting better. Testing these changes meant running lots of FreeBSD versions and trying different file systems and memory use cases. The author learned a lot, made some code contributions, and shared how tricky it is to report memory usage clearly.

In the comments, many people said they had similar confusion when moving to FreeBSD or even on Linux, because modern systems always try to use free RAM for cache. Some users explained that “unused RAM is wasted RAM,” so seeing low “free” memory is good, not bad. Others shared that different tools on Linux and BSD can be misleading, and that sysadmins should know how the OS really works. A few people pointed out that ZFS ARC cache is especially tricky, since it’s very aggressive and uses a lot of RAM, but will shrink if programs need more. Someone else joked that the real problem is “bad RAM reporting tools,” not the OS itself. There were also comments about how swap works, and why it’s normal to see some swap used even if you have plenty of RAM. One person warned that people new to BSD or ZFS might panic when looking at RAM numbers, but there’s no need to worry. Some users thanked the author for fixing the tools and documenting their findings, while others remembered having to read OS internals just to figure out why “free” memory seemed so low. Overall, the discussion showed that memory reporting is hard, and it’s better to learn how your system manages RAM instead of trusting one tool’s numbers.

## Costco is the anti-Amazon

- 原文链接: [Costco is the anti-Amazon](https://phenomenalworld.org/analysis/the-anti-amazon/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48776044)

The article says Amazon and Costco have very different ways of selling things. Amazon focuses on giving customers many choices and fast home delivery, while Costco limits choices and wants people to shop in person. Amazon uses complex logistics to deliver almost anything to your door, but this makes their business complicated and expensive. Costco, on the other hand, only sells about 4,000 items in each store (much fewer than Amazon or Walmart), so shopping is simpler and less stressful.

Costco’s small selection means they can work closely with suppliers and check each product for good quality. They don’t always have the cheapest items, but they often have the best price for what they sell. Their business model also helps move products quickly, so they get paid fast and don’t need to pressure suppliers for slow payments like Amazon sometimes does. Costco’s stores are busy, and shopping there is not always easy, but members like the experience and keep coming back—over 90% renew their memberships each year.

Costco spends little money on advertising, relying on word of mouth. Their costs for things like running stores and paying staff are much lower than Amazon’s delivery expenses. Because they have low overhead, they can pay workers more. For example, Costco pays higher average wages than Walmart or Amazon, and their workers stay with the company longer—only 6% leave each year, compared to much higher rates at other retailers. This simple model also means less stress and fewer strict rules for employees.

The article says Amazon’s way of delivering small single items to homes is not efficient for society. It uses more trucks, fuel, and makes logistics complicated. Costco’s way—selling in bulk to people who visit the store—is cheaper and better for the community. Their simple warehouses don’t use lots of automation or conveyor belts; workers just move big pallets from one truck to another.

Some people say Amazon is more “modern,” but the author thinks Costco’s simple way is better for society. Amazon is good for special needs, like delivering medicine quickly to someone who can’t go to the store, but for most shopping, Costco’s model works better and is easier to run.

In the comments, many readers agree that Costco offers good value and a simple shopping experience. Some say they like that Costco makes choices easy—less time comparing products and less confusion. Others point out that Costco pays workers well and treats them better than many big companies. A few people mention that shopping at Costco feels like being part of a club, and that makes them feel loyal.

However, some commenters say Costco’s model doesn’t work for everyone. For example, people who don’t drive, live alone, or have small homes may not want to buy in bulk. Some say Amazon’s delivery is more helpful for people with disabilities or busy schedules. Others discuss how Amazon’s fast delivery is impressive but maybe not good for the environment, while Costco’s model is more sustainable.

A few also note that Costco’s stores are often outside city centers, so people without cars can’t use them easily. Some think both companies have strengths and should keep improving. In the end, many agree that having different choices for shopping is good, and each person can pick what fits their life.

## Applied Category Theory Course (2018)

- 原文链接: [Applied Category Theory Course (2018)](https://math.ucr.edu/home/baez/act_course/index.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48779723)

This is a free online course called "Applied Category Theory," taught by John Baez, based on a well-known book by Fong and Spivak. The course has many web pages, each one a lecture, and explains how category theory can be used in real problems.

The course starts with basic ideas like ordered sets and logic. Early lectures talk about preorders, Galois connections, adjoints, and how logic fits with sets and partitions. Then, the course moves to resource theories—how you can use category theory to understand things like chemistry, manufacturing, and even pricing and scheduling. It talks about special kinds of categories, like monoidal preorders and enriched categories, and gives real-world examples. Later, it covers databases, showing how categories help design and transform them. There are lessons on graphs, functors, natural transformations, and special tools like Kan extensions. The last big topic is collaborative design, where the course explains how teams can use these ideas to work better together. Here, you learn about feasibility relations, cost, feedback, and special diagrams for working with categories.

Many people in the comments liked this course. Some said that John Baez and the book make a hard subject much easier to understand. Others found the links and structure very helpful, especially for self-study. A few people shared that they had trouble with category theory before but this course finally "clicked" for them. Some thought the ideas were still very abstract, and not all examples felt practical. Others pointed out that learning category theory is slow at first, but it helps you see connections everywhere in math and programming. There were comments about the course being a good match for people interested in functional programming, databases, and systems design. One person wished there were more exercises or code examples. Another said that the real value is thinking about relationships and structure, not just solving math problems. Some users warned that the course gets hard in later chapters, but it is worth the effort. A few even said that after learning this, they started to see category theory ideas in their daily work.
