## An entire Herculaneum scroll has been read for the first time

- 原文链接: [An entire Herculaneum scroll has been read for the first time](https://scrollprize.org/firstscroll)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48675179)

A team has read an entire Herculaneum scroll, sealed since 79 AD, for the first time ever—without opening it. They used X-ray scanning and machine learning to “unwrap” the fragile scroll virtually and reveal its ancient Greek text.

The scroll, called PHerc. 1667, was too delicate to open by hand. Past attempts damaged its outer layers, but the inner core survived. The researchers scanned the scroll using high-resolution X-rays. The scans showed thin, rolled-up sheets inside the lump of carbonized papyrus. Using computers, they mapped out the scroll’s shape, flattened it, and enhanced the faint ink using AI. This allowed them to read the scroll’s surface in full, about 1.4 meters long, with around 22 columns of text.

The text is a philosophical work about ethics, likely written by a Stoic philosopher in the 2nd century BC. It talks about human nature, wisdom, and happiness. The name Aristocreon appears, linking it to a famous Stoic thinker, Chrysippus. The scroll’s text is damaged in places, but many lines are readable for the first time in almost 2,000 years.

The same method was used on other scrolls. On one, higher-resolution scans made the ink directly visible inside the rolled-up papyrus. On another, they found the scroll’s title and author: Philodemus, On Gods, Book 8. These are big steps for ancient studies.

All the data, code, and results are free for anyone to use, thanks to the Vesuvius Challenge—a public project that brought together scientists and hobbyists worldwide. Many people on the team started as contest participants and later joined the core group.

## Un-0: Generating Images with Coupled Oscillators

- 原文链接: [Un-0: Generating Images with Coupled Oscillators](https://unconv.ai/blog/introducing-un-0-generating-images-with-coupled-oscillators/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48679007)

This article talks about Un-0, a new way to make images with a system of coupled oscillators instead of standard deep neural networks. The creators believe this can make AI much more energy efficient, maybe even 1,000 times better than current computers.

Un-0 works by setting up many simple units called oscillators, which are like metronomes. Each oscillator has a phase, and they all influence each other. The strength of their connections and their natural speeds are the main things the model learns. To make an image, you start all the oscillators in random positions, pick a class (like "cat"), run the system for a while, and then use the final positions as a hidden code for a decoder that makes the image.

The team tested Un-0 on datasets like CIFAR-10 and ImageNet 64x64. Their best model got a FID score of 6.74 on ImageNet 64x64, which is similar to early versions of popular models like GANs and diffusion models. Most of the model’s size comes from the oscillators, while the decoder is a small part. They did experiments to see if the oscillators really help. When they removed or froze the oscillator part, the image quality got worse, showing that the oscillators really do useful work.

The article also explains that the system is inspired by the brain, where oscillations help with tasks. The model can be built with real hardware in the future to use the physics directly. They share model weights and code, encouraging others to try new ideas using physical systems for AI.

## IBM debuts sub-1 nanometer chip technology

- 原文链接: [IBM debuts sub-1 nanometer chip technology](https://newsroom.ibm.com/2026-06-25-ibm-debuts-worlds-first-sub-1-nanometer-chip-technology)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48674967)

IBM has announced a new chip technology that is smaller than 1 nanometer, using a new 3D “nanostack” design. This chip can fit around 100 billion transistors on a chip as small as your fingernail, which is almost double what their 2 nm chip could do.

The main new thing is the nanostack architecture. Instead of laying out transistors flat, IBM stacks them in layers, a bit like having a tall building instead of a flat one. This helps save space and lets the chip work faster and use less energy. The chip is said to be up to 50% faster or 70% more energy efficient than IBM’s older 2 nm chips. This should help with things like AI, cloud computing, and new types of gadgets.

IBM says that nanostack lets them use different materials in each layer, so each part of the chip can be optimized for its job. They have tested the design and shown that it works in real circuits. The new design also improves how memory is stored on the chip, making it 40% more efficient, which is important for AI and big data tasks.

The chip uses new ways to build and connect the parts, and IBM worked with other companies and new tools, like high-precision EUV lithography, to make this happen. They say this new way of building chips will let the industry keep making smaller and better chips for at least another ten years. IBM has a long history in chip research, and this is their latest big step.

## Zig's new bitCast semantics and LLVM back end improvements

- 原文链接: [Zig's new bitCast semantics and LLVM back end improvements](https://ziglang.org/devlog/2026/#2026-06-25)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48673825)

Zig just changed how the `@bitCast` function works and made improvements to its LLVM backend. Before, Zig stored small custom integer types (like u4, i13) in memory the same way LLVM does, but this led to problems: missed optimizations and bugs. The new change stores these types as standard integer sizes (like i8, i16) in memory, only using the custom size when needed. This matches how C handles similar types and helps the optimizer work better.

The `@bitCast` function also got a new meaning. In the past, it just reinterpreted the bytes of one type as another, which depended on how the computer stored the data (for example, the order of bytes could change on different machines). Now, `@bitCast` is defined by the logical ordering of bits, not the physical memory. For example, casting an array of two u8s to a u16 always puts the first element in the least-significant bits, no matter the CPU’s endianness. This change makes `@bitCast` work the same everywhere and avoids undefined or dangerous behavior.

Another benefit is that the compiler can now use a “Legalize” pass to turn hard `@bitCast` operations into simple ones, making compilation easier and more reliable. The new rules also allow some interesting tricks, like turning arrays of small integers into vectors of bits or other types, which could be handy for some low-level work.

Besides this, the update blocks casting to and from vectors of pointers (which was unsafe) and allows casting enums using `@bitCast`. All these changes will be explained in the next Zig release notes.

Performance also improved: by changing how integer types are handled, some code runs about 5% faster, even inside the Zig compiler itself.

Commenters on Hacker News are mostly happy with these changes. Some think the new `@bitCast` semantics are easier to understand and safer. A few people worry that updating old code to work with the new rules might be hard, especially if they depended on the old, memory-based behavior. Others are glad that `@bitCast` is now the same on all platforms, so they don’t have to worry about endianness bugs. There are also comments praising the performance gains and how the change copies good ideas from how C handles custom integer sizes. A few users ask for more clear examples in the documentation to help people migrate their code. Some are excited to try the new features and see how it helps with writing safer, more portable Zig code. Overall, most reactions are positive, with some requests for better migration tips and extra docs.

## An oral history of Bank Python (2021)

- 原文链接: [An oral history of Bank Python (2021)](https://calpaterson.com/bank-python.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48678645)

This article talks about a special form of Python used inside big investment banks, called “Bank Python,” and looks at a fictional system named Minerva. In banks, this version of Python is very different from the normal Python most programmers use, and it comes with its own tools and ways of working.

Minerva is built around a big database called Barbara, which stores almost everything as Python objects—trades, market data, and even the code itself. There is no normal file system; instead, scripts read and write their data directly to Barbara. Barbara is simple but strong, using Python’s pickle and zip to store things, and can combine data from several “rings” or namespaces using overlays.

Investment banks have to calculate the value of complex financial instruments, often built from other instruments. The Dagger system in Minerva keeps track of how these values depend on each other, like a big graph. When one value changes, Dagger can automatically update all related values—helping banks know what something is worth at any time.

Jobs, like daily reports or updates, are run by Walpole, a single job runner for the whole bank. Walpole makes it easy to deploy and schedule jobs, mainly by editing a simple config file. This is much easier than using modern cloud systems, which need special skills.

For handling tables of data, Minerva uses MnTable—a home-grown table library, instead of popular open-source tools like pandas. MnTable is designed for “medium” sized data, like gigabytes of trades, and is used everywhere in Minerva.

Working with Minerva is not always easy. New people need a long time to learn the system, and it is so different from open-source Python that skills may not transfer back. The system is closed, with little code shared outside, and it even has its own in-house IDE and web framework. Over time, Minerva and outside Python become more and more different.

Many are surprised by how much banks have changed Python to suit their needs and how “Bank Python” has grown apart from open-source Python. A few people wonder if the closed nature of these systems hurts the developers’ skills and makes it hard to leave banking for other jobs.

Others point out similarities to working in other big companies with their own tools and platforms, saying this “monoculture” is common in industries that value control and security. ## Parallel Parentheses Matching

- 原文链接: [Parallel Parentheses Matching](https://williamdue.github.io/blog/parallel-parentheses-matching)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48678623)

This article talks about how to check if parentheses in a string are balanced, and how to do this using parallel algorithms. First, it explains the simple case: you have only one type of parenthesis, like ‘(’ and ‘)’, and you want to know if every opening has a matching closing. The classic way uses a stack: push for ‘(’, pop for ‘)’, and if the stack is empty at the end, the string is balanced.

The article shows a Haskell example for this. It then explains that you can solve the same problem in parallel if you map ‘(’ to +1 and ‘)’ to -1, then do a prefix sum (scan) over the array. If at any point the sum goes negative, or the sum isn’t zero at the end, the parentheses do not match. The article gives a Futhark code sample for this approach.

It also talks about making this even more efficient by combining the sum and the minimum value in a single reduce operation, using a monoid structure. However, this fast parallel solution works only with one kind of parenthesis. If you have more types, like ‘{[()]}’, it gets much harder.

For the general case, the article explains a method that computes the nesting depth of each parenthesis, then uses a stable sort to match pairs. But sorting can be expensive. Another method uses a special reduction tree (like a binary tree in an array) to help find matching pairs efficiently in parallel. This method uses a trick called the "previous smaller or equal" problem, which is well-studied.

In the end, the article shows that problems usually solved with stacks can be parallelized using these techniques, and references parser generators that use similar ideas.

People in the comments discuss these methods. Some say the stack approach is easy and good for most real code, but the parallel methods are interesting for teaching and big data. Others ask about handling many different types of brackets, and if this is useful for real programming languages. A few point out that parallelizing stack-like problems is tricky and may not be faster unless the input is very big.

One commenter likes the map-reduce style for showing how to frame problems differently, while another says the reduction tree approach is clever but hard to understand at first. Some people share links to similar algorithms in other languages, and ask about real-world uses, like in compilers or code editors.

A few are worried about edge cases, like overflow in integer sums, or Unicode brackets. Others debate if the stable sort method is practical, since sorting is slow. One person suggests that for short strings, the simple stack is still best.

Overall, readers like learning about parallel algorithms, but think simple solutions are best for most tasks. Still, they enjoy seeing how deeper computer science ideas can change the way we look at old problems.

Romanians have called themselves “Roman” for a long time to show they are different from their neighbors. Next, it talks about the Romansh people in Switzerland. The Urums are also Greek descendants, but their name comes from the Arabic word for Rome. These groups have been split up and mixed with other peoples over time, but they still have connections to their Roman past.

There are also some “honorable mentions.” The Romani people, sometimes called “Gypsies,” have names that sound Roman, but they actually came from India. Vatican City is the last place where Latin, the Roman language, is officially used, but it’s more of a church center than a nation of Romans. And of course, people still live in the city of Rome today, surrounded by old Roman buildings.
