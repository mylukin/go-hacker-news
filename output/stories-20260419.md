## The Bromine Chokepoint: How Strife Could Halt Production of World’s Memory Chips

- 原文链接: [The Bromine Chokepoint: How Strife Could Halt Production of World’s Memory Chips](https://warontherocks.com/cogs-of-war/the-bromine-chokepoint-how-strife-in-the-middle-east-could-halt-production-of-the-worlds-memory-chips/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47826100)

The article explains that almost all of the world’s memory chips depend on a special chemical called hydrogen bromide, which is made from bromine mostly extracted in Israel. Because of the war between Israel and Iran, Israel’s bromine production is at risk, and there is no quick way to replace it if it stops. Bromine is used to etch tiny parts in DRAM and NAND chips, which are found in everything from smartphones to servers and military equipment.

South Korea imports 97.5% of its bromine from Israel, and there are very few facilities in the world that can turn raw bromine into the ultra-pure hydrogen bromide needed for chips. These facilities take years to build, and others outside Israel are already running at full capacity for their own customers. If Israeli production stops, memory chip makers like Samsung and SK hynix would quickly run out of the chemical, leading to shortages of chips everywhere. This would raise prices for electronics, especially in poorer countries, and could even affect military systems that depend on these chips.

The article says there is no substitute for bromine in this process, and the existing supply chains are very fragile. The only quick actions possible now are building up small reserves or making supply contracts, but these only help for a few months. The real fix—building new purification plants in other countries—would take years. The author suggests that governments in South Korea, the US, and Israel need to work together to build backup supply and protect existing plants before a missile strike makes the problem worse.

In the Hacker News comments, many readers are surprised that such a small detail—bromine supply—could threaten the whole tech world. Some say this is another example of hidden weak spots in global supply chains, like what happened with semiconductors during the pandemic. Others think the article might be alarmist and wonder if there are really no backup plans or substitutes at all. Some engineers add that building new chemical plants is much slower and harder than most people think, so the warning is serious.

A few commenters discuss how wars in one place can have big effects on technology everywhere, and that many governments do not plan for these “black swan” events. Some readers suggest that tech companies should have seen this risk and invested in more diverse supply chains long ago. Others ask if using less memory or older chips could help for a while, but experts reply that new devices and AI need even more memory now. Finally, some say this shows why countries are starting to treat tech supply as a national security issue, and that we may see more government action soon.

Ayer describes being very sick with pneumonia, leaving the hospital too early, and having a relapse. He got worse, was taken to the hospital again, and then his heart stopped beating for a short time. After doctors revived him, he slowly recovered, surprising the medical staff with his quick mental return. During the time when he was dead, Ayer remembers seeing a very bright and painful red light, which he felt was controlling the universe. He imagined beings in charge of space and time, and he tried, but failed, to communicate with them. He also thought about fixing space by working on time, but nothing worked, and the vision ended suddenly.

Ayer knows these experiences could just be his brain acting strangely from lack of oxygen or medical problems. He talks about the idea that even when the heart stops, the brain might still work for a little while, which might explain such visions. He also discusses philosophical questions about life after death, personal identity, and whether memories and experiences make up the “self.” Ayer mentions that even if there was an afterlife, it would not mean that God exists—he remains a convinced atheist. He shares stories of other philosophers who believed in some kind of afterlife but were also not religious, and he jokes about how strange philosophical hopes for the afterlife can be.

Others discuss the philosophical side, wondering if these experiences challenge strict materialist views or if they just show how complex the brain is. ## Swiss authorities want to reduce dependency on Microsoft

- 原文链接: [Swiss authorities want to reduce dependency on Microsoft](https://www.swissinfo.ch/eng/swiss-ai/swiss-authorities-want-to-reduce-dependency-on-microsoft/91280532)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47827383)

Swiss government leaders want to use less Microsoft software in their offices. They have made this decision even though Microsoft 365 was just installed on over 50,000 work computers in the country. The main reasons are worries about data security and the high cost of Microsoft licenses. In the last ten years, Swiss authorities spent more than 1.1 billion Swiss francs on Microsoft software. Some people inside the government did not want to change and called new ideas “tinkering.” But now, a study shows switching to open-source software is possible.

Germany is an example for Switzerland. One region in Germany, Schleswig-Holstein, has already moved its administration to open-source programs. Open-source software is free to use and can be changed by anyone, so the country would not need to depend on big tech companies. There is also a legal problem: a US law lets American officials ask for data from US tech companies, even if the data is stored in another country. This means Swiss data on Microsoft servers could be seen by US authorities, and Swiss officials would not always know who is looking at it or why.

In the Hacker News comments, some people support the Swiss decision. They say it is good for a country to control its own data and not trust foreign companies. Others warn that moving away from Microsoft could be very hard and expensive. They remind everyone that open-source software needs workers who know how to use and fix it. Some users say Germany’s move to open-source is slow and difficult, with many problems. Others think more countries should try to be independent and not rely on just one company. A few users say Microsoft products are easy to use and work well together, so leaving them could cause trouble for daily work. But some think the risk to privacy is too big, and that is more important than cost or comfort. There is also a debate about whether open-source software is really safer, or if it just changes who you trust. Some say governments should build their own software, but others reply this is costly and not always better. Finally, many agree that it is good for governments to have a plan, so they are not trapped by one company.

## The seven programming ur-languages (2022)

- 原文链接: [The seven programming ur-languages (2022)](https://madhadron.com/programming/seven_ur_languages.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47822486)

This article talks about the idea that most programming languages come from just seven main “ur-languages.” These ur-languages are like big families, and learning them helps you understand many other languages. The seven ur-languages are ALGOL, Lisp, ML, Self, Forth, APL, and Prolog.

ALGOL is the base for most popular languages today, like C, Java, Python, and JavaScript. It’s about writing code in steps, with loops and functions. Lisp is known for its brackets and list structure; it lets you change the language itself using macros. ML is a functional language where functions are very important, and you use recursion instead of loops. Self is about objects sending messages to each other, and it inspired languages like Smalltalk and JavaScript. Forth is a stack-based language, where you push and pop numbers and words onto a stack, kind of like old calculators. APL is all about arrays and uses special symbols for operations, making it very short but hard to read. Prolog is about facts and logic; you tell the computer what is true, and it figures out answers by searching.

The author explains that learning one language from each family can help you learn others more easily. For example, if you know Java, learning C# or Python is not hard because they all come from ALGOL. But learning Prolog or Forth will feel very different. The article also gives a short history for each ur-language and suggests which modern language to try if you want to learn its style, like Haskell for ML or PLT Racket for Lisp.

In the comments, some people really like this way of thinking. They say it helps them see connections between languages and makes it easier to try new ones. Others point out that there are more language families, or that some languages mix ideas from several ur-languages. A few readers argue that the list is a bit too simple, and there are other important styles, like dataflow languages or assembly. Some people share their own stories of learning languages like Forth or Prolog and how it changed how they think about programming. Others ask about SQL, which the author says is like Prolog, but many think SQL is very different in practice. Some comments suggest that learning very different languages, even if you never use them for work, helps you think in new ways. There is debate about whether you really need to learn all these languages, but most agree that knowing more than one style is useful.

## Game devs explain the tricks involved with letting you pause a game

- 原文链接: [Game devs explain the tricks involved with letting you pause a game](https://kotaku.com/video-game-devs-explain-how-pausing-works-and-sometimes-it-gets-weird-2000686339)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47793161)

This article talks about how video game developers make the pause function work in games. Pausing seems simple to players, but for developers, it can be strange and sometimes tricky.

Many developers use tools in game engines to pause a game, often by setting the game speed to zero or almost zero. For example, one developer said he slowed the game to a tiny fraction of normal speed so the pause would work better in Unity. Another developer used a very slow speed so testers could still fly around during pause. Some just stop the game time, but make sure the menu still works.

There are different kinds of pauses. If you open a menu, unplug a controller, or use a system button, each might need a different pause style. Sometimes, games even have seven types of pause! This can cause problems if the different pause types conflict or if developers forget to handle every situation.

One clever trick is to take a screenshot when you pause. The game then shows this picture as the background for the pause menu. This can save memory and let the game do things in the background, like not rendering enemies. Some developers even move the player to an empty room while paused and return them after unpausing. Sometimes, there’s a small delay because the screenshot needs to be taken without the user interface showing.

Pausing is not the hardest feature, but it still needs care. If done wrong, like having every game object check the pause state every frame, it can slow the game down. With experience, developers learn to handle pausing better, usually by letting only one main object check if the game is paused.

In the comments, some readers were surprised that pausing is more complex behind the scenes than it looks. Others shared their own experiences, saying they also made mistakes with pausing in their first games. A few people noted that online games often don’t really pause, which frustrates them. Some developers agreed that handling many pause types is messy, especially with technical rules from game consoles. Others liked the screenshot trick, calling it clever or “hacky,” but said it works well. Some readers wondered if games should always allow pausing, even during special parts like cutscenes or boss fights. A few worried about cheats made possible by pausing, while others felt every game should let you pause, no matter what. Many agreed that pausing is a small but very important part of game design that deserves more attention.
