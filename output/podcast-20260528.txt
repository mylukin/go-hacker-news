Our next article says you do not need a special workflow system like Temporal or Airflow—just use Postgres to manage durable workflows. The main idea is that your app servers write workflow steps to a Postgres table, check for new tasks, and save results. If a server crashes, another can pick up using the saved steps. Postgres locks help make sure only one server works on a task at a time. This method means fewer moving parts, and you can handle scaling, reliability, and backups using Postgres tools.

In the comments, many developers like the simplicity and say Postgres is reliable and works well for small and medium workloads. Others warn that for very large or complex workflows, Postgres can become a bottleneck, and tools like Temporal are better. Some people note that you have to be careful with locking and transactions to avoid deadlocks. There’s debate about “reinventing the wheel”—some prefer ready-made solutions, while others like the control and simplicity of this approach. Overall, readers agree that using Postgres for workflows can work well, but you need to think about your own needs and limits.

Our next story is about Nick Winans, who created the nice!nano, a wireless keyboard microcontroller, while in college. Nick wanted a wireless keyboard with long battery life, but existing boards were too big, too expensive, or not open source. So he made his own using KiCad, and it worked—his keyboard lasted weeks on one battery. After posting the project online, demand grew fast. Nick ran a group buy and sold 1,000 units in seven hours. He worked with the ZMK firmware creator to improve wireless support. Later, clones of his product appeared, but people still bought the real nice!nano because the quality was better. Over the years, the board sold more than 50,000 units.

In the comments, many praised Nick’s open sharing and hard work, saying nice!nano made wireless DIY keyboards easier. Next, we have news about the future of Raspberry Pi computers. The Raspberry Pi 6 is not coming soon—maybe in early 2028—because of a worldwide shortage of DRAM chips. The new model will focus on being faster, with a better CPU, but not many new features. There will not be a special AI chip; the CPU will handle AI tasks. Cheap models like the Pi Zero 2W are hard to make because old parts are hard to find and new parts cost more. The old Pi 3B is still popular and sells almost a million units each year. The Pico microcontroller still uses micro USB, but USB-C may come in the future. Raspberry Pi now sells more microcontrollers than small computers, partly because of rising prices.

Hacker News readers are disappointed about the long wait for Pi 6 but understand the chip shortage. Some wish for more features, but many agree that cost is a big problem. Others say that the long life and good support for old models is impressive, and that Raspberry Pi should focus on reliability and software. Some worry that higher prices will make it harder for schools or hobbyists to buy Pis, but many feel the team is doing its best in a tough market.

Our last story is about a short online game called “Continue? Y/N” that shows the problem of “permission fatigue” with AI helpers. In the game, you have 60 seconds before your next meeting, and an AI helper asks you to quickly approve or deny several commands. The idea is that too many permission requests make people stop reading and just click “approve,” which is risky. The game warns that real attacks can happen if users get used to approving everything without thinking.

In the comments, some people think the game is a good way to show the problem. Others say this is not just an AI issue—we already see it with software updates and cookie popups. There are ideas for better user training, smarter permission systems, or “default deny” rules. Some warn that AI tools may ask for even more permissions in the future, making the problem worse. Overall, most agree that permission fatigue is a real and growing challenge for both AI and normal software.
