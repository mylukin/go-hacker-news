Hello everyone, this is the 2026-03-11 episode of Hacker News Daily Podcast. Today, we bring you a rich mix of stories covering software, AI, internet culture, and more.

First, let’s talk about the rules for using Hacker News. There’s a new article explaining how to post, comment, and behave on the site. The main idea is to only share things that make smart people curious, not just news about politics or celebrities. Use the original source and don’t change story titles for clicks. If you post a video or PDF, add a warning in the title. Don’t post just to promote yourself—share because it’s interesting. In comments, always be kind and curious. Don’t be rude or negative, and focus on ideas, not people. AI-written or AI-edited comments are not allowed; Hacker News wants real people talking. If you see rule-breaking, flag it for moderators instead of replying angrily. Use *asterisks* for emphasis, not ALL CAPS. Don’t complain about small things or vote counts, and email moderators for site problems.

Most commenters support these rules. They say the focus on curiosity and kindness keeps the site special. Many agree that banning AI comments is important for real conversation. Some wish politics could be discussed more, but others say it usually leads to fights. There’s debate about self-promotion—some say it’s fine if the post is truly interesting. The “no Reddit” rule made a few people laugh. Overall, most agree the rules help keep Hacker News friendly, though a few worry being too strict might make people afraid to share.

This leads to another article about the “dead Internet” theory—the idea that much of the web is now filled with AI bots, not real people. The author shares stories about fake-sounding job replies, bots posting bogus comments on Reddit, and AI-generated posts flooding LinkedIn and GitHub. On Hacker News, new rules try to fight this trend. Many commenters agree the Internet feels less “alive.” They say AI makes it hard to trust what you see online. Some hope new rules and tools can help, but others believe fighting bots is a losing battle. A few miss the old, more personal Internet and now spend less time online. Some point out that AI can be useful, but only if we can clearly see what is human and what is not.

Moving on to JavaScript: after nine years of hard work, the new Temporal API will be part of the ES2026 standard. Temporal replaces the old Date object, which was full of bugs and confusion, with clear types and safe, immutable data. It handles time zones, calendars, and date math much better. Teams from Microsoft, Google, Mozilla, and more built a shared Rust library to help all browsers support Temporal. It’s already live in Firefox, Chrome, and Edge. In the comments, developers are very happy: no more fighting with Date or relying on heavy libraries like Moment.js. Some worry about learning the new API, but most agree it’s worth it. Many thank the teams for working together and hope other languages learn from JavaScript’s long struggle with time.

On the AI front, there’s a big story about Anthropic, an AI company in a fight with the U.S. government. The government wants Anthropic’s AI for mass surveillance and autonomous weapons, but Anthropic refuses. The article asks: who should control AI? The government, the company, the user, or maybe even the AI itself? Some say government control is needed for security, but others fear too much power in either government or big tech. There’s worry that with AI, both surveillance and government pressure will get stronger. Most commenters agree this is a hard question with no easy answers, and hope for more debate and better laws before AI becomes too powerful.

Let’s talk about WebAssembly, or Wasm. Even after years of progress, Wasm is still hard to use on the web. You have to write “glue code” in JavaScript, and calling web APIs from Wasm is complex. This keeps most developers using JavaScript, and only big projects choose Wasm. The upcoming WebAssembly Component Model could make Wasm much easier, letting code from any language use web APIs directly. Many commenters are excited, but some are worried about complexity and slow progress in browsers. There is hope, but also caution: Wasm may never be as simple as JavaScript, but making it more “first-class” is important for the web’s future.

In company news, Google has finished buying Wiz, a cloud security startup. Wiz is known for finding big security problems and building easy-to-use tools, including an AI Security Platform. Now with Google, Wiz plans to use Google’s AI, like Gemini, to help customers build safely and quickly. They promise to keep supporting all clouds, not just Google Cloud. Some people are worried Wiz might lose independence or become too complex, but others think Google’s resources will help Wiz grow. Many praise Wiz for real security work and hope the service stays good.

Next, a new tool called Site Spy is getting attention. Site Spy watches web pages for changes and sends updates through RSS, email, or push notifications. It shows exactly what changed, lets you track just part of a page, and supports AI agents for summaries. There’s a free plan for up to five sites, and paid plans for more. Many users find Site Spy useful for tracking prices, tickets, or official news. Some like the visual diff, while others ask about privacy and handling tricky sites. There’s also interest in open-source alternatives and an API. Overall, comments are positive and people are eager to try it.

Now, let’s look at Meticulous, a small London startup working on frontend testing. They offer a tool that can automatically test large codebases, without using common AI models. Dropbox and Notion are already paying customers. Meticulous says their tool covers every possible case, helping teams catch more bugs. The team is small but strong, with engineers from top tech companies. Commenters are impressed by the mission but want more technical details. Some are skeptical about the bold claims and worry full automation might miss important bugs. Others are excited to see a company not just chasing AI trends and hope Meticulous can really change how we build software.

Switching to science, there’s a worrying story about large-scale scientific fraud. “Paper mills” are businesses selling fake or low-quality research papers, and they’re growing fast. They work with brokers and sometimes even journal editors to push these papers into the scientific record. The problem is getting worse, with fake papers growing faster than real ones. Some fields now have up to 4% of papers retracted. The main cause is pressure to publish for jobs and funding. Commenters are shocked at how big the problem is and say it happens everywhere, not just in certain countries. Some think the only fix is to change how science is rewarded, but that’s very hard. There’s hope that AI or better reporting can help, but others worry fake science will be hard to spot, even for machines. Many agree we need better ways to fight and punish fraud.

Finally, a study looks at AI-generated code patches, or PRs, that pass SWE-bench tests. The finding: about half of these AI PRs would still be rejected by real maintainers, even after passing all tests. Reasons include poor code quality, breaking other parts, or not really solving the problem. Even human-written PRs were only re-accepted about 68% of the time, showing how subjective code review can be. The main lesson: passing automated tests is not enough for real-world use. Many commenters agree, saying code review is about more than “does it work?”—it’s about maintainability, clarity, and fitting the project. Some are optimistic that AI will get better with feedback, but for now, test scores alone do not mean AI is ready to replace human coders.
