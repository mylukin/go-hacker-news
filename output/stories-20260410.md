## Chimpanzees in Uganda locked in eight-year 'civil war', say researchers

- 原文链接: [Chimpanzees in Uganda locked in eight-year 'civil war', say researchers](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cr71lkzv49po)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47722333)

Chimpanzees in Uganda’s Kibale National Park have been fighting each other for eight years in what scientists call a “civil war.” The large Ngogo chimp group split into two — Western and Central — and since 2018, fights have led to 24 deaths, including 17 infants. Before, these chimps lived peacefully and even held hands, but now they attack and sometimes kill former friends. Researchers think the split happened because of three main reasons: many adult chimps died in 2014, a new alpha male took over in 2015, and a respiratory disease killed more chimps in 2017. These events broke social ties and trust between subgroups. After the split, Western chimps started attacking Central chimps more and more. The violence surprised scientists, as the two groups had lived together for decades. The study suggests that fighting between groups is not only about land or food but also about changes in relationships and group size. The researchers say this can help us understand how human wars and group conflicts might start, even without things like politics or religion.

A few people discussed how animal behavior can sometimes look very human, with shifting alliances and fights for power. Some users worried about what this means for chimpanzee safety and conservation. Others thought the findings might help explain why humans also sometimes fight over new group identities. A few commenters wondered if humans are really so different from our closest animal relatives. There were also jokes about “chimp politics” and how similar the story sounds to human history. Some wanted to know if researchers should step in to stop the fighting, but others said it is important to let nature take its course. One comment said this shows that violence is not just a human problem but something deeper in our shared past. Another user found the research hopeful, saying understanding animal conflict could help us avoid future wars. Overall, the story made people think about both nature and human society.

## 1D Chess

- 原文链接: [1D Chess](https://rowan441.github.io/1dchess/chess.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47719740)

This article introduces a simple version of chess called 1D-Chess, played in one straight line instead of a square board. You play as white against an AI, using only three types of pieces: King, Knight, and Rook.

In 1D-Chess, the King can move one space in either direction. The Knight jumps two spaces forward or backward, skipping over any pieces. The Rook can move any number of spaces in a straight line, left or right. The goal is the same as regular chess: checkmate the enemy King by putting it in a position where it is attacked and cannot escape.

The article also explains when games can end in a draw. This happens if a player is not in check but has no moves left (stalemate), if the same position is repeated three times (threefold repetition), or if only Kings are left on the board, making checkmate impossible. The author mentions a possible forced win for White and gives a hint for the best moves to try.

1D-Chess was first described by Martin Gardner in a 1980 issue of Scientific American. The article links to his original column for people who want to read more.

In the comment section, many people are interested and surprised by how tricky 1D-Chess is, even though it looks simple. Some users say it’s fun to play and a good way to practice thinking differently about chess. Others wonder if the game is always a win for White, or if there are possible draws with perfect play from both sides. A few commenters discuss other chess variants, saying that trying new forms can help you understand the original game better.

Some users point out that the Knight in 1D-Chess is much more powerful than in standard chess, because it can jump over pieces so easily. Others like the clear and simple rules, and a few mention that this version could be good for teaching beginners. There are also some jokes about playing “zero-dimensional chess” next. A handful of people mention Martin Gardner’s influence on math and games, saying it’s nice to see his ideas still inspiring new versions today. Overall, most commenters agree that 1D-Chess is a clever twist on a classic game, and they enjoy trying it out.

## Watgo – A WebAssembly Toolkit for Go

- 原文链接: [Watgo – A WebAssembly Toolkit for Go](https://eli.thegreenplace.net/2026/watgo-a-webassembly-toolkit-for-go/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47722262)

This article talks about “watgo,” a new toolkit written in Go for working with WebAssembly. The toolkit lets people read and write WebAssembly code using only Go, with no extra software needed.

watgo can take WebAssembly Text (WAT) files, check if they are correct, and turn them into the binary WebAssembly (WASM) format. It can also do the reverse—read binary WASM and show it as text. There is a command-line tool, so you can use it from your terminal, and there is also a Go programming interface if you want to use it in your own Go programs. The main part of watgo is called “wasmir.” This is a way to see and change the inside of a WebAssembly module. When you use watgo, it changes some details from the text into a more direct or “flat” format, to make things simple for checking and running the code.

The article gives an example: you can write a short Go program that uses watgo to look at a small WAT file, count how many times certain operations appear, and print the results. watgo also comes with a lot of tests. It uses the official WebAssembly test suite, which has many examples and checks for errors. Because of this, the creator feels confident that watgo works well. The testing tool runs the code using Node.js. The author also keeps a collection of sample programs to help with more testing.

In the comment section, many people are happy to see a WebAssembly toolkit made in pure Go. Some say they have wanted something like this for a long time, because other tools like WABT or wasm-tools require C++ or Rust. A few users point out that watgo does not yet support every feature that wasm-tools has, but most agree it covers the basics well. Some developers share that they use Go for backend work and are excited to try watgo for WebAssembly projects. Others ask if the author will make the internal textformat parser public, since it could be useful for more projects. There are questions about performance, but the author explains that for most use cases, watgo is fast enough. A few people say they had trouble testing with wazero, and are glad the author chose Node.js for the test harness. Some comments suggest possible new features, like better support for new WebAssembly proposals. Overall, the community seems interested and supportive, with many looking forward to future updates.

## Show HN: FluidCAD – Parametric CAD with JavaScript

- 原文链接: [Show HN: FluidCAD – Parametric CAD with JavaScript](https://fluidcad.io/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47721997)

FluidCAD is a new parametric CAD tool that lets you design 3D models by writing JavaScript code. You type code, and you see the 3D shape update right away. In FluidCAD, you use simple commands to make sketches, extrude them, add fillets, and create shells. For example, you can draw a circle, extrude it into 3D, round the edges, and hollow it out—all with a few lines of code.

You can go back through your modeling steps at any time and change things without breaking your model. If you like working with your mouse, you can also drag to shape objects in the 3D view, then lock in those changes with code. There are tools for repeating, mirroring, and rotating shapes, so you can build complex patterns from simple pieces. FluidCAD supports importing and exporting STEP files, so you can use your designs in other CAD programs.

The workflow is like traditional CAD: you sketch, extrude, and add features in order. But FluidCAD tries to make the code simple to read and write—operations like extrude or fillet usually guess what you want to do with your last shape, so you don’t need to repeat yourself. You can also reference faces, edges, or corners of your shapes without doing lots of math.

To start with FluidCAD, you install it using npm, set up a project, and add an extension to your VS Code editor. Then you’re ready to write code and see 3D models appear.

## Helium Is Hard to Replace

- 原文链接: [Helium Is Hard to Replace](https://www.construction-physics.com/p/helium-is-hard-to-replace)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47719274)

This article explains why helium is hard to replace in many industries, especially now that supply is disrupted because of the war in Iran and the closure of the Strait of Hormuz. Helium is mostly collected as a byproduct of natural gas, and Qatar and the US produce most of the world’s supply; with the supply chain broken, prices have gone up and there are shortages.

Helium is special because of its unique properties. It is very light, doesn’t react with other elements, and has a lower boiling point than any other element. This means it can cool things to almost absolute zero, which is important for many technologies. On Earth, helium is rare because it escapes into space, so we get it only from underground gas pockets formed over millions of years. The US and Qatar together make about two-thirds of the world’s helium; other producers include Russia, Algeria, Canada, China, and Poland.

Helium is used in many ways. MRI machines need liquid helium to keep magnets cold enough to work. Most MRI machines today still need helium, though they use less than before because of better designs. The semiconductor industry uses even more helium, mostly for cooling, cleaning, and as a non-reactive gas in making computer chips. This use is growing as newer chip-making machines need it. The fiber optics industry also needs helium to make cables without bubbles. Aerospace uses helium to clean fuel tanks, and NASA is the biggest user in the US. Helium is used as a lifting gas in blimps and balloons, in scientific research and big machines like the Large Hadron Collider, for welding, and as part of breathing gas for deep-sea divers.

Sometimes, other gases can replace helium, like argon for welding or hydrogen for balloons—but these substitutes have problems, such as being heavier or dangerous. Recycling helium can help, and some industries, like MRI machines and aerospace, now use much less helium thanks to recycling. Still, in many cases, nothing works as well as helium.

In the Hacker News comments, some people worry that selling off the US helium reserve was a mistake, as it leaves the country and the world more exposed to shortages. Others say that recycling helium should be done much more, especially in labs and hospitals, where a lot is wasted. Some commenters point out that hydrogen could replace helium for balloons, but it is flammable, which makes it risky. A few are hopeful about high-temperature superconductors, which may one day remove the need for so much helium in MRI machines and other devices.

Some users say governments should treat helium more like oil and keep reserves for emergencies, since it takes millions of years to form. Others mention that helium is used for fun things like party balloons, and they question if that is a good use of a rare resource. There are also voices who believe that, like past shortages, high prices will push new technology and better recycling, making the problem less serious over time. But many agree—helium is hard to replace, and losing access causes big problems for science, medicine, and industry.

## What is RISC-V and why it matters to Canonical

- 原文链接: [What is RISC-V and why it matters to Canonical](https://ubuntu.com/blog/risc-v-101-what-is-it-and-what-does-it-mean-for-canonical)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47691538)

RISC-V is an open standard for how CPUs understand and run instructions, and the article explains why this matters for Canonical, the company behind Ubuntu. Most CPUs use rules from companies like Intel or ARM, but RISC-V is free for anyone to use and change.

The article says RISC-V is growing fast, and soon more developers can use it for Linux projects. RISC-V started in 2010 and is now managed by a group that includes people from companies, universities, and the open-source community. Unlike other CPU designs, anyone can make a RISC-V chip, and they can use the design for free or sell their own versions.

Big companies like Qualcomm and NVIDIA already use RISC-V in their products. Google also uses RISC-V in the OpenTitan project, making a secure, open-source CPU for Chromebooks and data centers. One special thing about RISC-V is that it is easy to add new features or custom instructions, which helps for things like AI, machine learning, security, or special hardware. For example, you can make a chip with only the instructions you need, saving space and energy.

The article says the open-source community already gives RISC-V good support in the Linux kernel and tools like GCC and LLVM. Ubuntu has supported RISC-V since 2021, and Canonical promises long-term support for RISC-V in future Ubuntu versions. Canonical also works with chip makers to support their products and gives tools to help companies build their own systems with Ubuntu on RISC-V.

RISC-V is changing the chip industry, making it easier for new ideas and products to appear. Canonical wants RISC-V to be as important as Intel or ARM in its products. They invite people to try RISC-V with Ubuntu for various projects, from small devices to servers.

Hacker News users had many opinions on this. Some are excited about RISC-V because it is open and lets anyone build new chips and products without paying high fees. They say this could lead to more innovation and lower costs. Others wonder if RISC-V’s flexibility might hurt software compatibility, since too many custom versions could make it hard to run the same programs everywhere.

Some users think the software support for RISC-V is not as strong as for Intel or ARM and want to see more mature development tools and applications. A few worry that open hardware might still face legal or political problems, especially if countries or companies try to control the technology. There are also comments about how RISC-V helps with “technology sovereignty,” letting countries or companies avoid relying on foreign chip makers.

Others are happy Canonical supports RISC-V and say this shows the open-source world is ready for it. Some developers shared their own experiences using RISC-V, noting that it’s easier to try out new ideas than with closed platforms. A few are cautious, saying that it will take time before RISC-V chips are as fast or cheap as mainstream CPUs. Still, many agree that RISC-V is a big and positive step for hardware freedom and new business models.

## CPU-Z and HWMonitor compromised

- 原文链接: [CPU-Z and HWMonitor compromised](https://www.theregister.com/2026/04/10/cpuid_site_hijacked/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47717847)

CPUID’s website was hacked for six hours, causing downloads for popular tools like HWMonitor and CPU-Z to serve malware instead of the real software. The attack did not change the actual programs but swapped download links so users got fake installers that included harmful files.

The breach happened through a side API, not the main system. The attackers made the HWMonitor download link point to a file called “HWiNFO_Monitor_Setup.exe,” which was suspicious. The real installers stayed safe and signed, but people who clicked links during the attack could still get infected. The malware came in the form of a fake Windows file (CRYPTBASE.dll) that would then reach out to a remote server for more harmful code. It mostly stayed in memory, using PowerShell to avoid leaving traces on disk. The malware also tried to steal data from browsers, like Chrome passwords, by accessing special Windows functions. Experts say this attack looked similar to an earlier one that targeted FileZilla users, so it may be part of a bigger plan. CPUID says the problem is now fixed, but they haven’t shared how the hackers got in or how many people were affected.

In the comment section, many people were upset and worried. Some said this shows how even trusted sites can be risky if their backend is not secure. Others pointed out that digital signatures on files are not enough if attackers can change download links. Some developers talked about the need for stronger checks, like hashing and verifying downloads. A few users said they now only trust open-source tools or build software themselves. Others asked for better ways to check software before running it, like sandboxing. Some commenters defended CPUID, saying it’s hard for small teams to protect everything, while others blamed them for not having better security. People also discussed how attackers are getting smarter, not just by hacking code but by attacking the ways software is delivered. A few shared tips on how to clean an infected system or check if they were affected. Some said they were lucky to avoid the attack because their antivirus blocked the malware. Many agreed that this is a wake-up call to always be careful, even when downloading from well-known sites.
