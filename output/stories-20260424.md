## My audio interface has SSH enabled by default

- 原文链接: [My audio interface has SSH enabled by default](https://hhh.hn/rodecaster-duo-fw/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47894747)

This article talks about a Rodecaster Duo audio interface that had SSH turned on by default. The writer wanted to update the firmware and decided to look closer at how the update process works.

They found the firmware dumped as a simple gzipped tarball on their Mac. When the update failed, they explored the device files and found that SSH was enabled, but only allowed public key authentication. The default SSH keys were included, and the firmware didn’t use any signature checks, meaning you could flash any firmware you wanted. The author switched to a Windows PC, used USB packet capture tools, and figured out how the Rodecaster app sends specific “M” and “U” commands to start and finish the update process. This works by sending one command to enter update mode, copying the update files to the device, and sending another command to start the flashing. The process is simple: connect, send the command, copy files, change permissions, unmount, send the next command, and the device updates its firmware.

The writer created custom firmware to add their own SSH keys and allow password authentication, making it easy to log in to the device. They were surprised by how easy it was to replace the firmware and liked that they could really “own” the hardware. However, they wondered why SSH was enabled by default and why default keys were used. They tried to contact Rode about this but got no response.

In the comments, some people were worried about security risks. They didn’t like that SSH was enabled with default keys and no password, because someone on the same network might get in. Others pointed out that having open firmware is nice for people who like to tinker and learn. A few users said this is common for hardware, and sometimes companies leave these “back doors” for their own support use. Some readers felt that not having signature checks is risky, while others liked the freedom to install custom firmware. There were questions about whether most users would even notice or care. One commenter said this is a good reminder to check new devices for open ports or services. Another thought Rode should give a better way to report security problems. Some people shared tips for making devices safer at home, like using firewalls. Overall, the discussion showed both concern for security and excitement for hacking and learning.

The mummy was one of several uncovered by a Spanish-Egyptian group. Some of the mummies had gold or copper tongues placed in their mouths, and some had gold leaf on them. The tomb also had wooden coffins, but many were in bad condition because people stole from the tomb long ago. In another part of the cemetery, the team found stone burial chambers with the remains of adults, a baby, and even cats wrapped in cloth. They also found small statues made of clay and bronze, showing gods like Harpocrates and Cupid. All these things help experts learn more about how people buried their dead during Greek and Roman times in Egypt.

## Sabotaging projects by overthinking, scope creep, and structural diffing

- 原文链接: [Sabotaging projects by overthinking, scope creep, and structural diffing](https://kevinlynagh.com/newsletter/2026_04_overthinking/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47890799)

The article talks about how overthinking and letting your project’s scope get too big can stop you from finishing things. The author shares personal stories about starting projects, sometimes finishing them quickly by just doing what’s needed, and other times getting stuck by researching too much or trying to add too many features.

One example is a simple woodworking project: the author and a friend had fun making a kitchen shelf in one weekend because their main goal was just to hang out and build something useful. They didn’t worry about making it perfect or doing lots of research. But with tech projects, like finding a better tool for code “diffing” (comparing two versions of code), the author fell into the trap of looking at many tools and reading about complex solutions. Instead of building a small tool to solve their problem, they spent hours researching, which delayed real progress.

The author says it’s important to know your own “success criteria”—what you really want out of the project. If your goal is clear and simple, you can finish things quickly and have fun. But if your goals are fuzzy, it’s easy to get lost and never finish. The author lists other personal projects (like making new programming languages) that have stalled because the goals were not clear.

There’s also a story about using AI to code a search tool. The author started simple but got distracted by extra features suggested by a library, leading to wasted time. In the end, the extra work wasn’t needed, showing how easy it is for scope to creep and slow down a project.

The article looks at different code diff tools (like difftastic, semanticdiff, and gumtree), explaining their strengths and weaknesses. The author just wants a simple way to see changes in code, but most tools are too complex or not a good fit. So, the plan is to build a small, personal tool and not worry about making it perfect or popular.

In the Hacker News comments, many people agree with the idea that overthinking can kill projects. Some say it’s easy to get caught up in research and never start coding. Others point out that scope creep happens a lot, especially when using powerful new tools like AI. A few readers think that some research is still helpful, but you have to know when to stop. One user jokes about “YAGNI” (you ain’t gonna need it) and says most features end up unused. Another says building simple versions first is best because you learn by doing.

Some commenters discuss the technical side of diff tools. They mention how hard it is to match code changes in a smart way, and why most tools still use simple line-by-line comparisons. A few suggest that perfect semantic diffs are almost impossible, so don’t get stuck trying to find the perfect tool.

Others share personal stories about projects that stalled because of unclear goals or too much planning. They say it’s better to ship something simple than to wait forever for the perfect solution. A few people disagree, saying that background research is important for big projects, but most agree that you should keep things small and focused, at least at the start.

Overall, the comments support the article’s advice: know what you want, keep your project’s scope small, and just start building instead of overthinking.

A user on Hacker News found that Claude 4.7, an AI model, is not following “stop hooks”—special signals meant to tell the model when to stop generating text. Stop hooks are important for developers because they help control the output from an AI, making sure it does not go off-topic or output too much text.

The user explains they tried using stop hooks in their API calls to Claude 4.7, but the model ignored them. This means the AI kept talking even after it should have stopped. The problem did not happen with earlier versions of Claude, so this seems new in version 4.7. The post gives specific examples, showing code where “stop” settings should work, but do not. The user tried changing the stop word and using different ways to set it, but nothing worked. They even tried waiting for a new version, but the issue stayed.

Other developers joined the conversation to share their own experiences. Some said they also faced the same problem with Claude 4.7, while others said it works fine for them. A few people suggested that maybe the stop hook logic changed in this version, or that it could be a bug. Some thought it might be a problem with the API, not the model itself. One commenter suggested using a workaround by trimming the text after it is generated, though this is not ideal.

Others pointed out that stop hooks are tricky in language models, and sometimes models ignore them if the stop word is rare or appears in the wrong place. Some said this problem also happens with other AI models, and not just Claude. A few people suggested reporting the issue to Anthropic, the company behind Claude. There was also some discussion about the importance of clear API documentation, and a reminder to always check for updates or known bugs before using new versions in production. Overall, the community was helpful, sharing both technical advice and workarounds.

## Show HN: I've built a nice home server OS

- 原文链接: [Show HN: I've built a nice home server OS](https://lightwhale.asklandd.dk/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47896163)

This article introduces Lightwhale, a new operating system for home servers that runs Docker containers easily. It is made to be simple, reliable, and maintenance-free.

Lightwhale boots from an ISO file straight into Docker, so you don’t need to install or set up anything. The core system is immutable, meaning it can’t be changed or broken by accident or by malware. Your data and custom settings are stored on a different device, making backups easy and the system more secure. Lightwhale is lightweight and uses few resources, so it works well even on older or low-end hardware. It’s designed to be plug-and-play: download the ISO, write it to a USB, boot your server, and you’re ready to run Docker containers. By default, the system is read-only, but you can enable persistence so your changes and data stay after reboot. The system starts up quickly, using a simple and classic Linux init system. The idea is to remove the pain of updates, package managers, and system clutter. If something goes wrong, a reboot restores the system to its original state. Only a few folders—like /etc, /var, and /home—are writable, keeping the rest locked and safe. All Docker data is saved on the data filesystem, which can be on a hard drive, SSD, or just in memory. Lightwhale also tries to protect your privacy and collects no personal information by default.

In the Hacker News comments, many users like the idea of an immutable, container-focused server OS. They say it reminds them of projects like RancherOS, Flatcar, or NixOS, but they appreciate the focus on simplicity and home use. Some people are happy to see a system that boots straight into Docker without extra setup. Others point out that the “live boot” and “no maintenance” ideas are great for small home servers, test labs, or for people who don’t want to worry about updates breaking things. A few users ask about support for ARM devices or Raspberry Pi, but the project only works on x86-64 for now. Some people wish they could install more software directly, but the creator explains that this would break the purpose of the system. Others mention the importance of changing the default password before putting the server online. There are questions about persistence and how safe the data is if the USB or disk fails; replies explain that the immutable system is easy to replace, and only user data needs careful backup. Some worry about missing their favorite tools like nano or wget, but most accept the minimal approach. A few users discuss using Lightwhale for education or teaching Docker basics. Overall, the community is positive, finds the project useful, and is glad to see a new simple home server OS on Hacker News.

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47893779)

This paper says that a true scientific theory for deep learning is beginning to form. The authors review different research areas that are helping us understand how deep learning works at a basic level. One area looks at simple, ideal situations that teach us about how training works in real-world systems. Another area uses math to study deep learning when things like the number of layers or data points get very large. They talk about finding simple rules that explain things we can measure, like how loss or accuracy changes over time. They also want to understand which settings (like hyperparameters) really matter and which can be simplified. Some researchers look for behaviors that appear in many different neural networks, not just one kind. 

All these studies focus on how training changes the network, and they try to make predictions that can be tested. The authors call this new way of thinking “learning mechanics,” saying it is like the mechanics in physics but for learning. They say this approach can work together with other ways of understanding deep learning, like seeing it as a way of handling information or statistics. They respond to people who think a theory of deep learning is impossible or useless, arguing that a theory is coming and will be helpful. They end by pointing to open questions and advice for new researchers.

In the comments, some people are hopeful and say that theory is important for progress. Others are more careful, saying deep learning is very complex and a full theory may be far away. A few users point out that even small, simple models are hard to fully understand, so big networks will be even harder. Some think that theory will only explain the average case, not every detail. Others write that past fields, like physics, took a long time to build their theories, so patience is needed. One commenter worries that new models change so fast that any theory will quickly become outdated. But another replies that even if models change, basic rules can still help. Some users are excited by the idea of “learning mechanics,” while others prefer older views, like looking at deep learning as statistics. Finally, a few say that more theory could help us build safer and more reliable neural networks.
