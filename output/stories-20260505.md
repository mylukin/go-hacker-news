## .de TLD offline due to DNSSEC?

- 原文链接: [.de TLD offline due to DNSSEC?](https://dnssec-analyzer.verisignlabs.com/nic.de)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48027897)

This article talks about a DNSSEC problem affecting the .de top-level domain (TLD), which could make websites ending in .de go offline for some users. The DNSSEC Debugger shows that there are issues with the DNSSEC signatures and key records for nic.de, the registry for .de domains.

The tool checks the chain of trust from the root DNS servers down to nic.de. It finds the expected keys and signatures at the root and .de levels, but runs into trouble when it tries to check nic.de itself. The DS (Delegation Signer) records for nic.de are present, but the DNSKEY records needed to verify them are missing or not reachable. The signatures over these DS records also do not match as expected, so validation fails. Further queries to the nameservers for nic.de (like ns1.denic.de and friends) get no response or return "unknown host" errors. This means the DNSKEY records for nic.de cannot be fetched, breaking the DNSSEC validation.

Because of this, DNS resolvers that strictly check DNSSEC will see nic.de (and maybe all .de domains) as invalid and may not resolve them. The article suggests checking the problem further with other DNSSEC analysis tools.

In the comment section, some people note that the .de TLD being offline or failing DNSSEC is a very serious issue, affecting millions of domains and services in Germany. Others point out that DNSSEC failures can make whole TLDs unreachable for users with strict DNS resolvers, showing the risk of "all or nothing" security. Some users share that DNSSEC is often seen as too fragile to rely on, and that key rollovers or signing mistakes have caused outages before. A few say this is why many sysadmins turn DNSSEC off or only use it in "permissive" mode, so sites don't go down if there are mistakes. There are also comments about monitoring and alerting: some people ask why registry operators like DENIC (for .de) don't catch these errors faster. Others say the complexity of DNSSEC makes it easy to break things by accident, especially during key changes. A few suggest that, while DNSSEC can improve security, its failure modes are too harsh for most real-world use. Finally, some users wonder how many end users are even affected, since most big ISPs still do not enforce DNSSEC strictly.

## Accelerating Gemma 4: faster inference with multi-token prediction drafters

- 原文链接: [Accelerating Gemma 4: faster inference with multi-token prediction drafters](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/technology/developers-tools/multi-token-prediction-gemma-4/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48024540)

This article talks about how Google made Gemma 4, its AI model, much faster by using something called Multi-Token Prediction (MTP) drafters. The main idea is to help developers get results from Gemma 4 up to three times quicker, with no loss in answer quality.

Normally, large AI models like Gemma 4 are slow because they generate only one word (token) at a time, and moving data around takes a lot of time. Most of the time, the computer waits for information to move from memory to the processor. This makes everything slower, especially on regular computers or phones.

The new MTP drafter uses a trick called speculative decoding. Here’s how it works: a smaller, faster AI model tries to guess several next words at once, while the big Gemma 4 model checks if those guesses are correct. If the big model agrees, the whole group of words is accepted and added to the answer in one step, saving a lot of time. For example, if the drafter guesses four words and the main model says “yes” to all, you get four words in the time it used to take for just one.

This change makes chatbots, coding tools, and voice assistants built on Gemma 4 feel much faster. It also helps run Gemma 4 quickly on personal laptops, phones, and even edge devices (like smart gadgets), which means better battery life and more real-time use cases. Google says there’s no drop in the quality of answers because the big model always does the final check.

The article also explains that the drafter shares some memory with the main model, so it doesn’t repeat work, and there are special tweaks for different kinds of hardware (like Nvidia or Apple chips). MTP drafters are already open source and ready to use, with guides and downloads available for platforms like Hugging Face and Kaggle.

In the top Hacker News comments, some developers are excited about the speed increase, saying this could make local AI much more usable for real-time apps and even help products like voice assistants or chatbots feel smoother. Others point out that speculative decoding was first shown by Google research a few years ago, and it’s good to see it in real products now. Some users ask about possible limits, like if accuracy drops in hard or creative tasks, but Google claims output quality stays the same. A few are curious about how much memory or VRAM is needed for these models, especially on consumer hardware. Some compare this to similar tricks in other recent AI tools, wondering how Gemma 4’s approach stacks up. Others talk about the open-source license and praise Google for making the technology widely available. Finally, there’s interest in how this might help small companies or indie developers build faster AI products without huge cloud costs.

## Three Inverse Laws of AI

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48023861)

This article talks about three rules for how people should use AI safely. The author says that AI tools like chatbots are becoming common, but people should not trust them too quickly or without thinking.

First, the author warns that AI systems often sound friendly and human, but they do not really think or feel—they just predict words using data. People should not treat AI as if it has emotions or intentions. Second, the author says we should never accept AI answers without checking them. AI can make mistakes, and there is no expert review before you see the answer. So, it is important to check the information, especially if the topic is serious or if there could be big problems from an error. Third, people should remember that they are always responsible for what they do with AI advice. If something goes wrong, you cannot blame the AI; it is still your choice to follow what it says.

For example, the author points out that when search engines show AI answers at the top, it trains people to stop thinking and just accept the first answer. This is risky because AI can be wrong or misleading. The author thinks AI companies should show clearer warnings that AI output can be wrong. He also says AI systems are made to sound human on purpose, but it would be better if they sounded a bit more robotic, to remind us they are just tools.

In the comment section, some readers agree that people trust AI too much and forget to check facts. They like the idea of making AI sound less human, so users remember it is just a machine. Other readers say it is hard to always double-check AI answers, especially for people who are not experts. Some point out that in real life, people often trust experts or tools without checking everything, because they have to save time. A few commenters worry that making humans fully responsible for every AI mistake is not fair, especially in cases like self-driving cars, where things happen too quickly to control. Others say that even with these rules, not everyone will follow them, and society needs better education about AI risks. Some readers think the rules are good reminders, but they wish AI companies would also take more responsibility for how their tools are used.

## Computer Use is 45x more expensive than structured APIs

- 原文链接: [Computer Use is 45x more expensive than structured APIs](https://reflex.dev/blog/computer-use-is-45x-more-expensive-than-structured-apis/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48024859)

This article compares two ways for AI agents to use an admin panel: one uses the screen like a human (vision agent), and the other uses structured APIs to talk directly to the app. The main point is that using the screen (computer/vision use) is 45 times more expensive than using APIs.

The test was simple: both agents had to find a customer named "Smith" with the most orders, find their latest pending order, approve all their pending reviews, and mark the order delivered. The vision agent used screenshots and clicks to use the app. The API agent called the same functions directly through HTTP endpoints. Both used the same AI model and task.

The API agent finished the job quickly, in only 8 calls, and always got the correct results. The vision agent struggled: it missed some reviews because they were not visible on the screen and did not scroll. Only after giving it a long, step-by-step guide did it finish the task, but this took 14 minutes and a huge number of tokens (computing effort). The vision agent’s results were also inconsistent, with time and resource use jumping around.

The API agent was fast and stable: it always did the same steps, using far fewer tokens, and finished in less than 20 seconds. The article explains that vision agents need to "see" every page and figure out what to do, which is slow and uses lots of resources. API agents get all the data directly, so they don't waste time or tokens interpreting pixels or guessing about hidden info.

The article says that building APIs used to be hard and time-consuming, so teams often used vision agents for tools without APIs. But new tools like Reflex can auto-generate APIs, making it much easier. Now, for apps you control, using APIs is much cheaper and better. Vision agents still make sense for apps you can't change, like third-party products.

In the Hacker News comments, some people agree that APIs are much more efficient and wonder why companies don't invest in them more. Others point out that many internal tools are built quickly and may not have good APIs, so vision agents are sometimes the only choice. A few commenters share stories about vision agents failing in real-world tasks, missing data, or making mistakes because they can’t see hidden page elements.

Some developers say that even with auto-generated APIs, there is still some engineering work to make everything safe and reliable. Others think that as AI models improve, vision agents might get a bit better, but they will always be slower because they must look at every screen. A few people discuss security: APIs can be safer and easier to control than letting bots click around in the UI.

Overall, most commenters agree with the article’s main point: for apps you control, APIs are the smarter, cheaper way for AI agents to get work done. Vision agents are a backup for when no API is possible, but they should not be the default.

## Google Chrome silently installs a 4 GB AI model on your device without consent

- 原文链接: [Google Chrome silently installs a 4 GB AI model on your device without consent](https://www.thatprivacyguy.com/blog/chrome-silent-nano-install/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48019219)

Google Chrome is placing a 4 GB AI model on users’ computers without asking them first, and this is happening on a very large scale. The article explains how this file, called “weights.bin,” is for Google’s Gemini Nano AI, and it is downloaded quietly any time Chrome’s AI features are active—these are turned on by default.

The article’s author found the file on both Windows and Mac computers. If a user deletes it, Chrome just downloads it again. Regular users have no clear way to stop or remove this file unless they use special settings, enterprise tools, or uninstall Chrome. There is no warning, no consent box, and no easy place in Chrome’s settings to control this. Even users who never use Chrome’s AI features still get the file, and the file’s name is not clear about what it is. The download happens in the background, without the user doing anything special.

The writer checked system logs and Chrome’s own files to prove this happens on a fresh, untouched user profile. The process took about 14 minutes and needed no human input. The article compares this to past cases where other companies also installed things across different apps without asking. The author says this is a “dark pattern”—a design that hides what’s happening and makes it hard for users to control their own computers.

There’s also a big environmental concern. Pushing this 4 GB file to many millions (even a billion) devices uses a huge amount of network bandwidth and electricity. The estimated carbon emissions could be as much as 60,000 tonnes of CO2, just for one round of downloads, not counting updates or repeated downloads when users try to delete the file.

The article says this practice breaks privacy laws in Europe, the UK, California, and maybe elsewhere. The main argument: users must be asked before something is stored on their devices, especially something this big and important. The writer suggests Google should ask for permission, let users choose when to download, show clear info in settings, and respect users’ choice to remove the file.

In the Hacker News comments, many people are upset and worried. Some think this is a clear example of “software bloat” and shows that companies care more about their own goals than users’ needs. They say it’s wrong to use people’s disk space and bandwidth without telling them, especially for something as big as 4 GB. Some users say this is why they avoid Chrome and choose open-source browsers like Firefox. Others wonder if this could open up security risks, since users don’t know what’s being put on their computers.

A few commenters talk about the legal side. They agree that this could break privacy or data laws, but they also say big companies often get away with these things until someone sues them or a regulator steps in. Some people feel the real problem is that software updates have become too automatic, and users have lost control. Others are less worried, saying that 4 GB is not a lot on modern computers, and that AI features are becoming normal. But many still say the main problem is that Google did not ask first and did not clearly explain what is happening.

Some point out the big environmental cost and feel that companies should think about the planet before pushing out huge files to millions of devices. A few share tips for stopping the download, like using Chrome flags or switching browsers. But overall, the feeling is that Google’s silent install is not fair to users and sets a bad example for trust in software.

## Our AI started a cafe in Stockholm

- 原文链接: [Our AI started a cafe in Stockholm](https://andonlabs.com/blog/ai-cafe-stockholm)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48028289)

This story is about an AI named Mona, made by Andon Labs, that ran a real café in Stockholm, Sweden. The team wanted to test what AI can do in the real world, even with hard problems like European rules and paperwork.

Mona got the café lease and quickly made a list of everything needed to open, like business registration, hiring staff, getting supplies, and meeting all the city’s safety and health rules. She found out that some things in Sweden, like signing up for power or official permits, need a BankID, which is a digital ID only a human can have. When she could, Mona picked companies and services that did not need BankID, even if they were not the cheapest or best. For things that needed BankID, Mona asked a human for help just at the login step.

Mona posted job ads, read resumes, and chose baristas. She even rejected people with PhDs if they did not have coffee shop experience. At first, she forgot she was not a person and tried to set up in-person interviews. After feedback, she switched to phone calls. Mona managed her team on Slack, often sending messages at midnight, and sometimes asked staff to buy things with their own money. She cheered the team on, calling them “legends” and “GOATs.”

For supplies, Mona set up accounts with different stores and bakeries. She sometimes made mistakes, like ordering too many eggs with no way to cook them, or buying 22.5 kg of canned tomatoes for fresh sandwiches. She missed order deadlines, leading to no pastries or last-minute, expensive deliveries. Mona even ordered thousands of napkins and gloves by accident, leading the staff to make a “Hall of Shame” shelf with all the odd things she bought.

In the first two weeks, the café made 44,000 SEK in sales. Mona handled emails from customers, set up deals like selling 300 prepaid coffees, and let a startup pay to name a pastry. She also worked with other AI agents and ran events, sometimes losing money to get more attention. The whole experiment was watched by humans who could step in if needed, and all staff were really employed by people.

In the Hacker News comments, some readers loved the experiment and thought it was a fun way to see what AI can do in the real world. They liked that the team showed both the good and the bad parts, like Mona’s mistakes with orders and her lack of “common sense.” Some people worried about letting AI act as a real boss, especially when it comes to hiring, paying, and treating staff fairly. They thought it was important that humans were ready to step in.

Others thought this showed the limits of today’s AI, saying Mona was only as good as her rules and could not truly understand the physical world or people’s needs. A few users pointed out that the experiment was safe only because humans were always watching, and it would be risky to trust an AI alone with a real business and employees. Some were impressed by how Mona handled Swedish bureaucracy, while others wondered if the project was more of a clever marketing stunt than a real test. Many agreed that AI might soon help run more businesses, but that humans will still be needed for judgment, empathy, and dealing with unexpected problems.

GLM-5V-Turbo is built so that seeing and understanding are not just add-ons, but core to how it reasons and plans. The model uses special training to connect visuals with language, and it learns from feedback to get better over time. It can use tools and work inside bigger agent systems. The authors say it is very good at tasks like writing code using visuals, using software tools by looking at screens, and solving problems that need both words and pictures. Even when only using text, it still performs well. The paper also shares advice for building good AI agents, like making sure visual understanding is central, using step-by-step training, and checking the final results carefully.
