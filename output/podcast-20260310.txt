First, we say goodbye to Tony Hoare, who has died at age 92. He was famous for inventing the quicksort algorithm—a fast way to sort data that many of us learned in our first computer science classes. But Tony did much more. He helped build program logic, now called Hoare logic, to help people prove their programs work as they should. He worked on the ALGOL language and helped grow software engineering as a field. Friends and colleagues remember Tony as kind and funny. He liked to tell the story of his quicksort “wager,” where he bet his boss sixpence he could write a faster sorting program, and won. He was humble, did what was needed at work even if he had a better idea, and loved movies. Tony once joked that real genius takes years, not just quick thinking like in films. He also wondered if governments have computers much better than we can imagine. The comments under the news are full of respect and sadness. Many thank Tony for quicksort, share memories of meeting or learning from him, and agree he was a true giant whose work will not be forgotten.

Next, we turn to RISC-V hardware and Fedora Linux. A developer writes about building Fedora on RISC-V computers, and how slow the process is compared to other architectures like x86_64 or ARM. Building big packages can take hours, and RISC-V boards often have less memory, so features like LTO are disabled. Some hope new boards like UltraRISC UR-DP1000 and SpacemiT K3 will help, but RISC-V is still behind in speed. The writer often uses QEMU’s virtual cores to build packages, which is sometimes faster than real RISC-V hardware. The plan is to keep working and wait for better hardware. In the comments, many agree RISC-V is not ready for heavy server work yet. Some say this is normal for a new platform and that RISC-V’s openness and simplicity are strengths for learning and research. A few worry that if RISC-V remains slow, developers may lose interest, while others point out that for education or special projects, speed is less important.

Amazon has been facing outages, and they found out that code changes made with AI tools were partly to blame. Now, Amazon says any code change made with AI help must be checked and approved by a senior engineer before going live. The article explains that a bad software update, with help from AI, caused a six-hour outage, and another AI-assisted change broke AWS for 13 hours in China. Amazon leaders now want more careful review for all AI-assisted code, and junior engineers need approval from seniors. Some workers say job cuts are also causing problems, but Amazon says outages are not just about fewer workers. In the comments, many agree that more review is a good idea, since AI tools still make mistakes. Others say this will slow teams down or make engineers afraid to use AI. There are worries about overloading senior engineers with sign-off requests. Some say this shows AI tools are not ready for important systems yet. People agree that it’s better to be careful now, before AI causes bigger outages.

Another story is about building AI agents that write and test code while you sleep. The author finds that letting the same AI write both code and tests is risky, because mistakes can go unnoticed. Hiring more human reviewers does not scale. The best solution is to write detailed acceptance criteria first, then let AI build the feature and use browser agents to check if each part works. For backend changes, API checks are used. The author built a Claude Skill called “verify,” which checks setup, plans tests, runs browser agents, and lets AI judge the results. This process is better at catching real-world bugs, but does not fix mistakes in the original spec. In the comments, some are excited about overnight agents and higher productivity, while others worry that letting AI both write and check code can hide errors. People say writing good acceptance criteria is hard, but like that browser agents can catch bugs normal code review might miss. Some wonder if this will scale for big teams, and worry about losing human understanding of the code. Overall, people find the idea promising, but warn not to trust AI too much.

Next, Yann LeCun, a top AI researcher and former Meta scientist, has started a new company called AMI with $1 billion in funding. LeCun wants to build AI that understands the real world, not just language. He says large language models like ChatGPT are good at making text, but miss real-world understanding. AMI plans to build “world models” that can remember, reason, plan, and interact with real things. The company’s first projects may help improve airplane engines by building digital models. The team is from top labs and is backed by investors like Mark Cuban and Eric Schmidt. LeCun left Meta because he wants to work on world models, not just language models, and wants his work to be open source. He says society should decide how AI is used, not just tech leaders. In the comments, many support LeCun’s idea and like the idea of open source AI. Some worry that $1 billion is not enough, or that building world models is very hard. Others warn about AI risks, but most are hopeful for AI that can do more than just talk.

A post about digital books takes us back to 1992, when The Voyager Company published a digital version of William Gibson’s Sprawl Trilogy for old Macintosh computers using HyperCard. The books tried to feel like real books, with good fonts, bookmarks, and margin notes. This edition has an afterword by Gibson that is hard to find. The files are small and work on old Macs, or on modern computers using emulators. The post gives step-by-step instructions for setup. In the comments, people thank the uploader, share memories about HyperCard and early digital books, and discuss technical tips for running the files today. Some say today’s e-books have lost some of the charm of these early editions. There is both technical interest and nostalgia for the early days of digital reading.

A new tool called RCLI lets you control your Mac and ask questions using your voice, all without the cloud. It runs fully on Apple Silicon and uses MetalRT for fast AI responses. RCLI supports speech-to-text, large language models, and text-to-speech, all together on your Mac. You can do 43 Mac actions by voice and ask questions about your local documents. No data leaves your computer, and there’s low delay. There is a terminal dashboard, and you can switch AI models without restarting. The main engine is proprietary, but the rest is open source. In the comments, many praise the speed and privacy, and like that no internet is needed. Some wish there was support for Windows or Linux, and others want the MetalRT engine to be open source. Most are happy to have a fast, private voice assistant that does not need the cloud.

Debian has been discussing whether to accept AI-generated code, but decided not to make a new rule for now. The debate started with a proposal: if you use AI to write code, you should say so, you must understand and stand by your code, and you should not use AI on private data. Some wanted to define “AI” more clearly, while others said the type of tool did not matter. People worried that AI could mean fewer real people joining Debian, or flood the project with low-quality code. Ethics and legal risks were also discussed, including copyright and the way big AI models are trained. In the comments, some say AI-generated code is just another tool, while others think it is less controlled than older code generators. Many agree that AI can help with good code if used carefully, but can also make it easy to produce lots of junk. For now, Debian will use its normal rules and wait to see how things develop.

There is a new project called ffmpeg-over-ip, which lets you use GPU-accelerated ffmpeg on a remote server, even if your local machine has no GPU. The client sends commands to the server over a TCP port, and the server runs a special version of ffmpeg that reads files from the client. This means you don’t need to set up network drives. The project provides ready-built binaries for Linux, macOS, and Windows, and works with Docker. Security uses HMAC-SHA256. In the comments, many people like this clever solution for media servers and home labs, especially because it avoids complex GPU setups. Some ask about performance and security, and others want more platform support. Most agree it keeps setup simple and makes GPU transcoding much easier.

Finally, we have an article about scientific theories in the age of AI. In the past, simple equations like E=mc² explained many things in science. But some problems, like climate change or the stock market, are too complex for simple models. Now, with AI and big neural networks, we can build models that work, even if we don’t fully understand why. These models are huge—billions of numbers—so no human can remember them. Some people say a good theory should be simple and small, but the basic structure of models like transformers is simple, even if the details are big. Scientists are now trying to study these big models to find new simple rules inside. In the comments, some agree that complex problems need complex models, while others worry we cannot trust what we don’t understand. Some hope that by studying these models, we will find new simple theories in the future. There is excitement about progress, but also caution about trusting models we cannot explain.
