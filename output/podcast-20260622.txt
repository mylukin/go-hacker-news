Next, a technical story about time zones and databases. British Columbia will stay on Pacific Daylight Time all year, starting in 2026. This means clocks won’t “fall back” in November anymore. The article explains how this can cause problems with storing times in Postgres databases, especially when using the default `timestamptz` type. Usually, databases store timestamps in UTC and convert to local time using current time zone rules. If the rules change after you save an appointment, the displayed time can shift by an hour. For example, an appointment saved for 10 AM could show up as 11 AM later, if time zone data gets updated.

The safer way is to use a dual-column pattern: store the user’s local time, the time zone name, and the calculated UTC time. This way, if the time zone rules change, you can fix future UTC times by recalculating from the local time and zone. There’s also RFC 9557, which adds the time zone name to timestamps, but it doesn’t fully solve the problem for future events, so the dual-column method is still best.

If you’ve already updated your time zone data and have appointments that could be wrong, the article gives steps to find affected records, notify users, and update appointments.

In the comments, many users say time zones are always a pain and wish everyone used UTC. Some say local time is needed for things like meetings and deliveries, so the problem can’t be avoided. The dual-column solution is popular, but some worry it adds complexity. Others wish databases made this easier. There are also stories about how companies handle time zones and debates about keeping `tzdata` up to date. Some people joke that time zones still cause more bugs than anything else in programming.

Now, a story about how Japan uses symbols to share information without words. In Japan, family crests called kamon have been used for centuries, and symbols are everywhere: police cars, fire trucks, and trains have their own marks. For drivers, there are four main marks—new driver, elderly driver, hearing-impaired, and disabled driver—each with its own symbol. On public transport, you may see the “Help Mark” for people who may need help, and the “maternity mark” for pregnant women.

These symbols are important because sometimes you can’t see who needs help. Japan’s culture expects people to “read the air,” or understand others without being told, but symbols help when this isn’t possible. They make it easier for everyone to be considerate.

Comments are very positive. Many users like the idea of symbols instead of words, saying it makes life easier for everyone. Some wonder if it would work as well in other countries. A few share stories of seeing these symbols in Japan and finding them helpful. Some worry too many symbols could get confusing, but others note that in Japan, people learn the meanings from daily life. Many think symbols are polite and universal, not needing language to understand.

Next, a privacy story about LG smart TVs. Researchers found that about 2,000 out of 6,000 LG TV apps turn your TV into a “residential proxy,” letting others use your home’s internet connection without you knowing. This makes your home IP address look like the source of someone else’s online activity. Many apps, like screensavers or games, include proxy software to make money in the background. TVs are easy targets because people don’t check them as much as computers.

Some apps are made by proxy companies themselves, just to get into homes. Other TV platforms like Amazon and Roku block these apps, but LG and Samsung do not, so the problem is worse there. The risk is more than just sharing your IP. If the proxy software is not careful, outsiders could reach other devices on your home network. Hackers have used these proxies before to attack home devices.

Researchers checked real app files and found clear proof of proxy software. The proxy companies say they have rules and vetting, but the main problem remains: most users don’t know or control what’s happening.

Comments show surprise and concern. Many say this is a big privacy risk, since people trust their TVs more than computers. Some think banning these apps is the best solution. Others say the real problem is poor rules and warnings from TV makers. Users also worry that consent popups are not clear, and most people just click “yes.” There are fears about criminal abuse if proxy controls fail. Some suggest treating smart TVs like computers—be careful what you install. Many doubt the situation will improve without stronger action from regulators or TV companies.

Now, some AI news. Moebius is a new AI model for image inpainting, with only 0.22 billion parameters, but works as well as models over 10 billion parameters. This makes image editing, like filling in missing parts or removing objects, much faster and possible on normal computers or even phones.

Moebius uses a special block called LλMI and a smart training method called distillation, where a small model learns from a big model but with much less memory and time. It works 15 times faster than big models, taking only 26 milliseconds per step on a single GPU. Moebius matches or beats the best models on natural and portrait photos, and is better at tricky details like faces or textures. The model focuses only on inpainting, making it more efficient.

In the comments, many are impressed by the speed and small size, saying this could bring good editing to phones and laptops. Some want open-source releases and demos, while others are careful, wanting to see more real-world tests. There are questions about hard cases and if the model could be used for other tasks like video editing. Some think specialist models give better results than general ones. There’s also interest in the training method for shrinking models. Many want fast, offline editing without big servers.

Moving to version control news, Oak is a new open-source version control system made for AI agents, not just humans. Oak is faster and more flexible than Git for coding agents like Claude or Codex. It uses a branch-per-session workflow, content-addressed storage, and “lazy mounts” so agents can start editing almost right away. Oak is split into a core library and a command-line tool, both open source. It supports macOS, Linux, and Windows (with extra setup). Oak is made to be easy for scripting, with machine-readable output.

In the comments, some users like the idea of a version control system made for agents and see that Git isn’t always a good fit. Oak’s focus on agent workflows, fast access, and simple branch management matches agent needs. Some are interested in its use of BLAKE3 hashes for speed and security. Others are cautious, wondering if Oak can really replace Git given its huge ecosystem. There are questions about merge conflicts and history rewriting. Some want to see real-world use before switching. Overall, people are curious and see potential, but want more details and time.

Finally, Canada has announced plans to build up to 10 new nuclear reactors in the next 15 years, spending over $100 billion. The goal is to double the electricity grid by 2050 and make Canada a leader in clean energy and uranium exports. Nuclear power is seen as important for steady, low-carbon electricity.
