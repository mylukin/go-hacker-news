First, let’s talk about Claude Design, a new tool for making user interfaces. Many teams use Figma today, but the article says Figma has become too complex and does not always match how code works. When the code changes, it’s hard to update Figma, which causes problems with things like colors and button styles. The author says Figma’s way of storing information may not work well with new AI tools, since AI learns best from code. Claude Design works with real HTML and JavaScript, so it connects design and code more closely. The author thinks the future will have two main types of design tools: one for real code, like Claude Design, and one for free-form art. Figma is stuck in the middle and may lose its place, just like Sketch did before. In the comments, some people agree Figma is now slow and hard to use, while others still like it for quick sketches. Many are excited about new tools that help designers and developers work together with less confusion. Some worry about losing the creative side of design if everything becomes code-first. A few joke that every new tool promises to fix things, but always becomes complex over time.

Next, we visit the past with an article about the B-52 bomber’s Angle Computer, an electromechanical device used for star navigation before GPS. The Angle Computer was part of the Astro Compass system and used gears, arms, and motors to solve math problems about the stars’ positions. Navigators entered star data, and the system helped them find the plane’s heading by tracking stars through a telescope. It could remember up to three stars for better accuracy. The device changed global star positions into local pointing angles using the “navigational triangle,” and moved a pointer inside a sphere to show where to look. The system used a mix of mechanical and electrical parts, since digital computers were too slow in the 1960s. In the comments, people are impressed by the engineering and surprised that such machines worked so well. Some share stories about navigation and how these old problems led to new ideas in computing. There is a sense of respect and nostalgia for these clever solutions before digital tech was common.

Now, let’s look at new technology. NIST scientists have made a chip that can create laser light in almost any color, using tiny circuits on a silicon wafer. The chip is made from layers of special materials, like lithium niobate and tantala, with metal parts to control the light. Each chip can make tens of thousands of different colors, which is important for quantum computers, atomic clocks, and other high-tech devices. This could make these systems smaller, cheaper, and easier to use outside labs. The chips might also help with faster communication between computers, better AI, and nicer virtual reality displays. In the comments, people are excited about the possibilities, asking about how easy it is to control the color, and if the chips can be made in large numbers. Some wonder if lasers like this will become cheap for everyone, or if they will stay in labs for a long time. Overall, there is hope that this will help many areas in science and technology.

Moving on to software, there’s an article about speeding up Ruby’s path handling methods, which is important for big projects that run many tests at once. Tools like Bootsnap help Ruby start faster by caching which files exist. But keeping the cache updated is hard, and scanning directories can be slow, especially on macOS. The author suggests a new method, Dir.scan, coming in Ruby 4.1, which doubles the speed of scanning. They also improved File.join and other path methods to be much faster, mainly by focusing on common cases and avoiding extra checks. In the comments, many developers are happy to see Ruby getting faster, especially for CI pipelines. Some are surprised by how much difference these low-level changes can make. Others talk about the risks of breaking compatibility and like the careful way new methods are added. There is also interest in bringing these ideas to other languages, and jokes about Japanese text encodings in Ruby. People appreciate the open-source process and hope these changes will speed up Rails and other Ruby apps soon.

Next, we have a story about moving a company’s servers from DigitalOcean to Hetzner to save money and get better performance. The company dropped its monthly bill from $1,432 to $233, with a better server. They did the move in six careful steps: install and match software, copy files, set up MySQL replication, lower DNS TTLs, use nginx as a reverse proxy, and then switch DNS. The hardest part was the MySQL upgrade, but they used tools like mydumper and fixed some privilege issues. They tested everything before switching, used scripts to speed up the process, and had zero downtime. In the comments, people praise the clear write-up and share their own stories of moving from cloud to dedicated servers. Some warn that Hetzner support is not as strong, and others talk about the risks of putting everything on one big server. There are also thanks for open-sourcing the scripts, making similar migrations safer for others.

In education, a college instructor at Cornell is fighting AI cheating by having students use manual typewriters in class. The teacher, Grit Matthias Phelps, wants students to write essays and poems by hand, with no screens or spellcheck. Students have to think more before writing, since fixing mistakes is not easy. Some students even needed help from the teacher’s children to use the typewriters. Many students say they focus better and talk more with classmates, instead of searching online. In the comments, some people remember their own typewriter days and say it teaches patience, while others think it may not help much in a digital world. Most agree it’s a creative way to avoid AI cheating and helps students see writing in a new way.

Now, let’s talk about Kdenlive, a free and open-source video editor. The team made big progress in 2025, with new features like automatic masking, background removal, and faster audio waveforms. They fixed many bugs, improved the audio mixer and titler, and made the welcome screen and monitor better. The next release will add monitor mirroring, animated transition previews, easier timeline editing, and more. The team is also working on better color support, playback, OpenFX plugins, subtitles, and a new keyframe timeline. Kdenlive is used worldwide, with over 11.5 million downloads last year, but funding is still low. In the comments, users praise Kdenlive for being powerful and stable, but some want even more bug fixes and polish. There are calls for better documentation and easier onboarding, and many thank the developers for their work.

Let’s move to embedded systems. The article explains how floating point math works on Arm Cortex-M chips, like the nRF52840 and ESP32-S3. There are three ABI options: soft, softfp, and hard. Soft means all math is done in software, hard uses the hardware FPU and special registers, and softfp is a mix of both. Mixing code with different ABIs causes build errors. The article shows how to change settings in Zephyr RTOS and what happens if you try to use the FPU without turning it on. In the comments, many people share their own problems with floating point ABIs, especially when using third-party libraries. Some suggest always using hard ABI if your chip has an FPU, but warn about context switching in multi-threaded code. Others talk about how hard it is to debug these problems and wish for better documentation and tools. Overall, everyone agrees that floating point on embedded Arm chips is tricky and needs care.

Next, MDV is a new tool for writing documents, dashboards, and slides using Markdown with extra features for data and charts. You can add a YAML section for settings, use special blocks for charts, and export as HTML or PDF. Charts are SVG images, so no JavaScript is needed. There is a live preview for VS Code, and the project is open source, running on Node.js 20 or higher. In the comments, people are excited because it could replace tools like Google Slides or simple dashboards. Some like that it’s easy to share documents without JavaScript, while others wonder about the learning curve and custom themes. Many are glad about built-in PDF export and hope for more features, but warn against making it too complex. Most are interested to see how the project grows.

Finally, we remember Michael O. Rabin, a giant in computer science who passed away at 94. Rabin was born in Germany and moved to Israel as a child. He worked at many top universities and made many important discoveries. With Dana Scott, he created nondeterministic automata, a key idea in complexity theory, winning the Turing Award. Rabin also made the Miller–Rabin primality test, the Rabin cryptosystem, and helped invent the Rabin–Karp algorithm. He worked on privacy, security, and many areas in theory. In the comments, people show deep respect and share stories about Rabin’s teaching and kindness. Many say his work is still used every day, and that he changed how we think about computers and algorithms. His influence will last for a very long time.
