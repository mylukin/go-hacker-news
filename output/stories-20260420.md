Apple announced that Tim Cook will step down as CEO and become executive chairman, while John Ternus will take over as CEO starting September 1, 2026. This change comes after a long planning period and was agreed on by the whole Apple board.

Tim Cook has led Apple since 2011. Under his leadership, Apple launched new products like the Apple Watch, AirPods, and Apple Vision Pro, and grew its services business to over $100 billion per year. Cook helped Apple grow from a $350 billion company to a $4 trillion company, with revenue almost four times higher than when he started. He focused on making Apple bigger worldwide, especially in new markets, and pushed for better privacy, more accessibility, and a lower carbon footprint. Cook also made Apple stronger in making its own chips and hardware.

John Ternus started at Apple in 2001 and has been important in designing many Apple products, including iPad, AirPods, and Mac computers. He helped launch new devices like the MacBook Neo and the iPhone 17 series. Ternus led improvements in product durability, repairability, and using eco-friendly materials, like recycled aluminum and 3D-printed titanium. He is seen as someone who knows Apple well and has strong engineering skills.

Arthur Levinson, who led Apple’s board for 15 years, will now be the lead independent director. Both Ternus and Cook said they are grateful for the chance to work together during this transition.

Some pointed out that Cook’s focus on services and privacy was good for users, while others hope Ternus will care more about making devices easier to repair. Overall, people are watching to see if Ternus will change Apple’s direction or continue Cook’s steady style.

## Kimi vendor verifier – verify accuracy of inference providers

- 原文链接: [Kimi vendor verifier – verify accuracy of inference providers](https://www.kimi.com/blog/kimi-vendor-verifier)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47838703)

Kimi has released a tool called Kimi Vendor Verifier (KVV) to help people check if AI models run correctly on different systems. This is important because open-source AI models can behave differently depending on where and how they are used.

The article explains that after making their model open-source, Kimi saw many problems with how people used it. Some users got strange results because they set the wrong parameters, like Temperature or TopP, or because of other technical mistakes. Kimi tried to fix some problems by making their API stricter, but they found many issues still happened when others used third-party systems or ran the model themselves.

To solve this, Kimi made KVV. It is a set of tests and benchmarks to check if a provider’s system runs the model correctly. There are six main benchmarks, such as OCRBench for checking images, AIME2025 for long answers, and tests for coding tasks. Each one checks for different common errors, like wrong parameter settings, issues with image processing, or bugs with memory use.

The tool also helps providers test their systems before letting users try the model. Kimi plans to make the results public, so everyone can see which vendors run the models well. They also work with open-source communities to fix problems at the root, not just find them.

KVV’s tests can take a long time to run, but Kimi has made scripts to help automate and speed up the process. Finally, Kimi invites more vendors to test their systems and welcomes help to make new, lighter tests.

In the comments, some people are happy that Kimi is focusing on trust and quality in open-source AI. They say it is easy to blame a model for mistakes, when the real problem is often in how it is run. Others like that Kimi is making the tests open and public, which can make vendors more honest and careful. Some users share their own stories about getting weird results from AI models and not knowing if it was a model problem or a setup problem.

A few commenters point out that running these checks can be expensive and time-consuming, especially for small companies. Some worry that making everything public could cause vendors to “game” the tests instead of really improving their systems. Others suggest that more benchmarks and easier tests would help smaller vendors join in.

One person mentions that this tool could help not just with Kimi’s models, but with many other AI models too. Another commenter wonders if users will really care about these scores or just choose whatever is cheapest. Some people ask for more technical details about how the tests work, while others are just glad to see someone working on this problem. Overall, most agree that having a way to check if AI models are running as they should is a good step for the community.

## Soul Player C64 – A real transformer running on a 1 MHz Commodore 64

- 原文链接: [Soul Player C64 – A real transformer running on a 1 MHz Commodore 64](https://github.com/gizmo64k/soulplayer-c64)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47839645)

This project is about running a real transformer AI model, like a very tiny version of ChatGPT, on a 1 MHz Commodore 64 computer. The model has about 25,000 parameters and uses real transformer parts: multi-head attention, softmax, and RMS normalization, all written in 6502 assembly code.

The model is small: it uses 2 layers, 4 attention heads, and 32-dimensional embeddings, with a tiny vocabulary of 128 tokens and a 20-token context window. All parameters are stored as 8-bit integers, and the code fits on a floppy disk. It takes about 60 seconds for the C64 to generate one word, so a whole answer takes a few minutes. You train it on your own text data using Python and PyTorch, then export the trained model to run on the C64. The project shares all the code, training scripts, and even test suites to check the math. The C64 version is slow and not very smart, but it proves that transformer models can run on old hardware with tight memory and no math coprocessor. The repo explains how training uses quantization-aware tricks, so the model works even with low-precision math and limited range, and includes fixes for integer softmax to make attention weights work.

You can use the project in an emulator or on real hardware. There are instructions for building everything, training your own chatbot, and testing the model. Most code is in Python, but the core model for the C64 is hand-written assembly.

In the comments, people are amazed this is possible on a Commodore 64. Some say it’s a fun demo, even if the model isn’t useful for real tasks. A few point out that running a transformer on a 64KB, 8-bit machine is a great technical challenge. Some ask about the practical uses, but most agree it’s more for learning and showing what’s possible. Others remember writing much simpler programs on the C64 and are impressed by how much ML code fits in so little space. There are comments on the long wait for each token—some joke that it gives you time to think. A few discuss ways to make it run faster, like using custom hardware or clever coding tricks. Some people want to try it on other old computers, like the Apple II. Others say it’s a good way to understand AI by seeing it in its simplest form. Most agree it’s a great example of mixing old tech with new ideas.

## GitHub's fake star economy

- 原文链接: [GitHub's fake star economy](https://awesomeagents.ai/news/github-fake-stars-investigation/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47831621)

This article talks about fake GitHub stars—how people buy stars to make their projects look more popular, and how this tricks investors. The authors studied 20 repositories and found that many stars are not real, with some projects having up to 76% fake stargazers.

A study from Carnegie Mellon University showed 6 million fake stars across thousands of projects. Most fake stars are found on AI or blockchain projects. People can buy stars for as little as $0.03 each on many websites, or order them on places like Fiverr and Telegram. There are even tools and fake profiles for sale, with some “aged” GitHub accounts costing $5,000. Some sellers offer “non-drop” stars that try to avoid detection by GitHub.

The article explains how to spot fake stars. Real projects have stargazers with active profiles—these users have followers and public repositories. Fake projects have many stargazers with empty profiles, no followers, and few repos. One strong sign is the fork-to-star ratio: in real projects, many people who star a project also fork it (copy it to use or change). In fake projects, the number of forks is very low compared to the number of stars.

VCs (investors) often use star counts to find new startups to fund. Some firms even scrape GitHub for fast-growing projects. This creates a loop—startups buy stars to get noticed by VCs, and VCs keep using stars as a signal, so more startups cheat. Some projects have raised millions after getting lots of stars, even if those stars were fake.

The problem is not just on GitHub. Fake metrics also happen on npm (JavaScript packages), VS Code extensions, and on social media. The law is starting to catch up: the FTC now bans buying fake influence metrics, with big fines for each violation.

GitHub says it bans fake stars and accounts, but enforcement is slow. They remove projects but leave many fake accounts untouched, so sellers can keep working. The article suggests better ways to judge real project popularity, like counting monthly contributors or checking fork-to-star ratios.

In the comments, many people agree that GitHub stars are a bad way to measure real value. Some say they have seen suspicious star growth on projects that don’t seem useful. Others point out that it’s easy to buy fake stars, and they worry about investors being misled. A few developers admit they have been pressured to focus on boosting star counts instead of real work.

Some commenters think the fork-to-star ratio is a helpful tool, but not perfect—projects like tutorials or lists get starred a lot but rarely forked. Others argue that GitHub should do more to stop fake stars, maybe by making their detection public or using better algorithms. A few people defend using stars as a rough signal, saying it’s hard to find any perfect metric for open source popularity.

There are also concerns about the legal risks. Some say startups could get into serious trouble if they raise money based on fake metrics. Others think enforcement will be slow, and that “fake it till you make it” is still common in startups. People also discuss how this trend hurts trust in open source and makes it harder for real projects to stand out.

Finally, some suggest ways for VCs and users to spot real projects: look for active issues, real discussions, and regular contributors—not just big star counts. Many agree that stars are now too easy to fake, so everyone should look deeper before trusting them.

## We got 207 tok/s with Qwen3.5-27B on an RTX 3090

- 原文链接: [We got 207 tok/s with Qwen3.5-27B on an RTX 3090](https://github.com/Luce-Org/lucebox-hub)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47838788)

This article talks about new software called Lucebox, which helps large language models (LLMs) run faster and more efficiently on regular consumer GPUs, like the RTX 3090. The team rewrote the LLM inference code by hand, making it work really well on specific hardware instead of using general-purpose tools.

The first main project is a “megakernel” for the Qwen3.5-0.8B model. It runs all 24 layers in a single CUDA operation, which means fewer delays and better use of the GPU. In tests, it gets much higher speed and better energy use than other tools like llama.cpp or standard PyTorch. For example, the megakernel hits 413 tokens per second and nearly 2 tokens per joule, while others are much slower and use more power.

The second project is DFlash for the bigger Qwen3.5-27B model. It brings a new way of generating text called speculative decoding, which guesses some results, then checks them quickly. This method, along with a tree-checking system (DDTree), lets the model reach up to 207 tokens per second, about 3.5 times faster than normal methods. It even fits a huge 128,000-token context into the 24 GB memory of a single RTX 3090.

The software is open source, and it’s made to help people run strong AI models at home or in small offices, without paying for cloud services or worrying about private data leaving their computers. The team explains that before, making special code for each chip took too long and was too expensive. But now, with the help of AI and better tools, it’s possible to do these deep optimizations faster and share them with everyone.

In the comments, some users are very impressed by the high speed numbers, saying that 207 tokens per second for a 27B model on a 3090 is amazing. Others ask about how hard it is to install and run this software, and if it works on other GPUs like the 4090 or older cards. Several users discuss the benefits of hand-tuned code versus general frameworks, agreeing that this approach can unlock much more power from the hardware but needs careful work. A few worry about compatibility with other models or future updates, while others are excited to see open source code and clear benchmarks. Some mention that consumer GPUs like the 3090 are still expensive, but having local, fast AI is very valuable for privacy and cost savings. There are questions about how much memory is needed and if the code can be adapted to smaller cards. Overall, the community seems excited, with many hoping for more projects like this for different chips and model types.

## ggsql: A Grammar of Graphics for SQL

- 原文链接: [ggsql: A Grammar of Graphics for SQL](https://opensource.posit.co/blog/2026-04-20_ggsql_alpha_release/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47833558)

ggsql is a new tool that lets you make data plots using SQL commands. It brings the “grammar of graphics” to SQL, so SQL users can create rich, structured charts without needing to use R or Python.

The article shows how ggsql works with examples. You can start a plot with the VISUALIZE command, map data columns to axes or colors, and use DRAW to pick the kind of plot, like scatterplots, bar charts, or histograms. The code looks a lot like SQL, so if you know SQL, it feels familiar. You can keep adding layers, like regression lines or labels, step by step. The visual query part starts after the VISUALIZE keyword, and you can also use SCALE to pick colors or LABEL to add titles. ggsql lets you write both the data query and the plot in the same place, and you do not need to use another programming language. The tool also works with big datasets by only fetching what is needed for the plot, making it efficient. It is designed to be easy for large language models (LLMs) to generate code for, and it is simple to add to other tools because it does not need a full runtime like R or Python.

The team behind ggsql says they made it to help SQL users who want to make good plots without exporting data to other tools. They think SQL and the grammar of graphics match well because both are based on clear, modular rules. They also learned a lot from making ggplot2 for R over 18 years and wanted a fresh start. The article promises new features in the future, like interactivity and support for maps, and says ggplot2 will still be supported.

In the comments, many people like the idea and think it will help analysts who work mostly in SQL. Some say the syntax is a bit wordy, but agree it is clear and easy to follow. A few wonder if it will be easy to get used to compared to writing code in R or Python, and if it is as powerful. Some users are excited about using ggsql with big databases, since it only moves small amounts of data needed for the plot. Others discuss how it could work well with AI tools that already write SQL, making it easier to generate plots with natural language. There are also comments asking about limits—like how customizable the plots will be, and if it will support all types of charts. A few people hope it will be easy to connect with their favorite SQL databases and tools like Jupyter or VS Code. Some users are happy that ggsql does not need R or Python installed and think that is great for sharing plots with others. Finally, a few ask if ggsql will inspire changes in ggplot2, and are glad to hear both tools will keep getting updates.

## F-35 is built for the wrong war

- 原文链接: [F-35 is built for the wrong war](https://warontherocks.com/cogs-of-war/the-f-35-is-a-masterpiece-built-for-the-wrong-war/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47839835)

The article says the F-35 fighter jet is a great, high-tech plane, but it was built for the wrong kind of war. The authors compare it to a beautiful violin—amazing on its own, but too expensive and hard to make for a big orchestra.

The F-35 has cost over two trillion dollars and the U.S. plans to buy thousands, but modern wars are changing. Today, countries use cheap, simple drones and missiles in big numbers, like in Ukraine, the Red Sea, and Iran. These are easy to replace if lost. The F-35, on the other hand, is expensive, slow to build, and needs a lot of support and maintenance. It did well in a fast, planned attack against Iran, but that’s not the kind of long, hard war the U.S. might have to fight against a big country like China.

There are two main problems: first, in the Pacific, U.S. air bases are close enough for China to hit them with missiles, so most planes could be destroyed on the ground. Second, the F-35 needs lots of parts, repairs, and people to keep flying, so if the base or supply lines are hit, the jets can’t fly. Spreading out the planes to many bases sounds good, but then it’s harder to get them fuel, parts, and support.

The article notes that tankers and long-range missiles can help, but they are also expensive and easy targets. In a long war, the U.S. would lose F-35s and could not build new ones fast enough. Maintenance is always an issue: about half the F-35s are ready to fly at any time, even in peacetime.

The authors suggest using more unmanned systems, like drones, that are much cheaper, can be built quickly, and do not need as much support or people. These drones can use small, simple bases, and if lost, it’s not a big problem. But drones right now can’t carry as many weapons and don’t have the same sensors as the F-35. Still, a mix of fewer F-35s and more drones could be better for future wars.

The article says this is not just an F-35 problem—the military often wants the best gear, but forgets to think about how to build enough in a real war. The solution is to buy fewer F-35s and more cheap, replaceable systems, saving money and being ready for a bigger fight.

In the comments, some people agree and say the F-35 is too expensive for modern wars. They point to Ukraine, where small drones are making a big difference. Others say the F-35 is still needed for special missions, like attacking strong defenses or gathering important information. A few think the U.S. should keep buying F-35s to keep its edge, but use them alongside drones.

Some users argue that the military is slow to change and likes expensive, complex planes because of politics and big companies. Others wonder if drones will really work in a big war, since we haven’t seen them in a fight on this scale yet. There’s also talk about how hard it is to fix and build new F-35s during a war.

A few commenters say the article is right—no one weapon can do everything, and the U.S. needs a mix. Some worry about putting too much trust in new, untested drone technology. Others think the F-35 should have a smaller role, but not be dropped completely. There is wide agreement that wars are changing, and the U.S. must think about cost, speed, and being able to replace lost gear quickly.
