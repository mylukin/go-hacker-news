First, Thinking Machines has released Inkling, a new open-weights AI model for everyone to use, study, and fine-tune. Inkling is very large, with 975 billion total parameters, and it can handle text, images, and audio. It works with very long inputs—up to one million tokens—and was trained on 45 trillion tokens from text, pictures, sound, and video. There’s also a smaller version, Inkling-Small, with 12 billion active parameters for faster and cheaper jobs.

Inkling is designed to be a generalist. It can reason, code, follow instructions, and work with different types of data. For example, it can build web apps or improve projects step by step, even making a multiplayer game after lots of feedback. The model is not always best in every test, but it balances power, efficiency, and is easy to customize. A special feature is “controllable thinking effort”—you can choose how much time and resources to spend on answers, which is useful for faster or cheaper results. Inkling is strong on audio and visual tasks, and it can say when it is unsure about an answer, helping with trust and safety.

Safety is a focus: Inkling is trained to refuse dangerous requests better than other open models, but not block harmless ones. Benchmarks show it is strong in safety, reasoning, and coding, but some closed models still score higher in some areas. It uses modern techniques like Mixture-of-Experts Transformers and reinforcement learning. As it trained, its answers became more compact and direct. Both Inkling and Inkling-Small are available on the Tinker platform and work with APIs and tools like Hugging Face.

Hacker News users are excited to see a truly open, large, and multimodal model. Many like the detailed benchmarks and the focus on customization. Some users are cautious about the hardware needed to run such a large model, and wonder how Inkling compares to other open models like Llama or Mistral. There’s interest in safety, but some worry that updates could reduce it. A few ask about licensing and “true openness,” and if the model can really be a generalist or will still need expert tuning. Many hope this release will push others to be more open with their own models.

Next, Grok Build is a new tool that lets you build and test software with plain English instructions. You just write what you want, like “Build my project and run the tests,” and Grok Build figures out the commands. It works with languages like Python, JavaScript, and Go, and you don’t need special config files. You can also ask it to show what it will do before anything runs. Grok Build is open source, making it easy for anyone to use or change.

In the comments, people like that Grok Build helps new developers and saves time. Some are worried about trusting AI to run commands on their computer, while others like seeing what will happen before anything is executed. Some developers prefer writing scripts themselves for full control. Users ask if Grok Build works well with big or complex projects, and some want more details on its safety. A few suggest using it as a teaching tool, and many hope it will support more languages in the future.

Now, a big move in online payments: Stripe and Advent International have offered to buy PayPal for over $53 billion, about 28% above PayPal’s recent stock price. The deal has $50 billion in bank backing. If it goes through, Stripe and Advent will share ownership equally. This would combine two payment giants, handling $3.7 trillion in payments a year. Stripe is strong with merchants, PayPal has 430 million consumer accounts and popular products like Venmo. The deal could help Stripe reach more consumers, offer its own digital wallet, and lower fees to Visa and Mastercard by running more payments on its own network. Stripe also wants to use PayPal’s network to push stablecoin payments.

PayPal’s value has dropped a lot since 2021, and it’s been trying to turn things around by splitting into units and cutting costs. Some analysts say the offer is too low and expect a higher bid. There have been other big deals in payments as companies try to keep up with new technology.

In the comments, many are surprised at how much PayPal’s value has fallen. Some think PayPal has lost its edge to Apple Pay and Google Pay. Developers say PayPal’s products are confusing, making Stripe look better. Some worry merging Stripe and PayPal could reduce choices for merchants and consumers. Others wonder what will happen to Venmo, or hope Stripe can improve PayPal’s tech and support. A few warn that private equity deals often lead to layoffs. There are also questions about PayPal’s new CEO, whether he’ll accept the deal, and if regulators will allow such a big merger. Some users are excited about more stablecoin payments, while others note the fast pace of change in payments.

Next, a story about running big AI on old hardware. Someone managed to run Google’s Gemma 4 language model—a 26-billion-parameter model—on a 2013 Xeon server with no GPU. The server cost less than $300 and only supports AVX1 instructions. With help from Claude AI, the author rewrote some C++ code so the software would use slower, older instructions instead of AVX2. After the fix, the server could generate about five tokens per second.

The author shared their patch and steps for others to use big models on old hardware, especially when cloud AI is too expensive or unavailable. In the comments, many are impressed by the technical achievement and the value of reusing old hardware. Some note that it’s not practical for most users, as it’s slow and uses more power. Others enjoy the fun of hacking old machines, but warn that for real work, modern GPUs or cloud APIs are still much faster and easier. Several users say open-source tools make these hacks possible, while closed systems would not allow it.

Turning to games, Duskers 2.0, the sequel to the popular command line space game, was just announced and funded by Stray Signal. The first Duskers is known for its lonely, tense atmosphere and controlling drones with text commands. In Duskers 2.0, you can now help others and face moral choices, making the story deeper. The team wants to keep the things fans loved: exploring dark ships, surviving with little help, and feeling alone, but now with more choices and emotion.

Indie game funding is hard, but the EGG community is helping. Max McGuire, known for Subnautica, is now supporting and writing code for Duskers 2.0. The team hopes to create a new kind of game company where creators help each other.

Comments are excited for the sequel, praising the first game’s style and atmosphere. Some hope the new story will not lose the lonely feeling. Others think moral choices will add depth, while some worry about making things too complex. Many like the new funding model and hope more small devs succeed. Some share how Duskers made them care about their robots, even though they know they are just machines.

Next, “brainless” is a new set of UI components inspired by code from Claude, Codex, and Grok. It makes it fast and simple to add modern, nice-looking elements, like pricing blocks, to web projects. You can add a component with a single command, and it supports features like path suggestions and release notes. The components are open and easy to use, aiming to help developers build apps faster while keeping a modern look.

In the comments, people like the idea of copying AI tool styles and saving time. Some ask if the components work with different frameworks or worry that “brainless” means it’s only for simple projects. Others warn about all apps looking the same and ask for more ways to customize. Some share their own tips and hope for more features and support in the future.

Now, a big question: Are we in an AI bubble, and does it matter? The article says even if there is a bubble and prices later fall, the investments made during the boom can still leave a lasting impact. The author explains that big investments in AI raise company values and build real things like data centers. If enough AI capital is built during the “boom,” the economy can stay at a higher level even after prices correct, giving workers higher wages and capital owners more wealth.

Some Hacker News users agree, saying past bubbles like the dot-com boom left real infrastructure behind. Others warn that not every bubble leaves something useful, and AI might be different because it can replace jobs. There are debates about the effects on workers and whether low interest rates are good or bad. Many users like the idea that even failed hype can build useful things, but remind everyone that real life is messier than models.

This next story is about a type design company that refuses to use AI for any of their work. The writer explains that the history of writing and type is full of human choices, shaped by hands, habits, and even discomfort. AI, in their view, cannot feel or invent new ways to write; it can only mix old ideas. They worry that AI will freeze design, making it harder for small languages and new styles to exist. They believe the world needs new typefaces, and if AI replaces designers, we may lose new ideas forever.

Many comments agree, saying human creativity is special and cannot be copied. Some say AI is just a tool and can help with boring parts, but others worry companies that refuse AI will not survive. There are debates about mixing human and AI work, or if AI will stop new ideas. Some designers value handmade work even if it’s tough to make a living. Others fear AI could make everything look the same, but some hope handmade fonts will become even more valuable. The comments show strong feelings, with people wanting to protect human creativity but also curious about AI’s future.

Finally, Voxatron is a program where you can play and make games using small 3D cubes called voxels. It comes with built-in games and tools to create your own. You can share and play user-made games, and the software costs $19.99 with free updates. There’s also a bundle with PICO-8.

In the comments, many like the cute, creative voxel art and remember playing Voxatron for years. Some compare it to PICO-8, noting the unique 3D world. Others discuss how easy or hard it is to use the devkit, and some want more tutorials. There are questions about exporting games, running on different computers, and hopes for open source in the future. Most agree Voxatron’s style makes it special, and people enjoy the creative freedom it gives.
