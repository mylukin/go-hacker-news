Hello everyone, this is the 2026-03-21 episode of Hacker News Daily Podcast. Today, we bring you a mix of technology, science, and society, with stories about online age checks, open source projects, AI hardware, new discoveries in astronomy, and much more.

First, let’s talk about a big change happening online: new laws that require everyone to prove their age before using websites or apps. These age checks are spreading far beyond adult sites, now reaching social media, games, and even the computer’s operating system itself. The goal is to keep children safe from harmful content and dangers online, like bad videos and online predators. But the article we looked at warns that these laws go much further—they turn the open internet into a place where you must always show proof or ID. Some laws even want your device to share your age with every app, raising serious privacy concerns. Even open-source systems like Linux are feeling pressure to add age fields to user data.

The article argues that there’s a big mistake here: confusing content moderation with guardianship. Blocking bad content is a technical job, but teaching children how to stay safe is personal, something for parents and teachers. These laws, it says, hurt privacy, give more data to big companies, and take power away from families. The article also points out that age checks do not really work well—kids use VPNs and fake accounts to get around them, making the system expensive and hard to use. The writer worries that once age checks are built, they could be expanded to track where you live or your legal status.

In the Hacker News comments, many people agree that these laws threaten privacy and turn the internet into a “checkpoint.” Some developers worry about new security risks from adding things like birthdates to Linux. Others support the laws, saying parents cannot watch kids all the time, and big platforms must help protect children, especially those without good guardians. Some suggest better content filters and local controls instead of big, central age checks. There’s debate about the impact on open-source projects and on people without ID. One user says the real problem is business models that profit by keeping people hooked online, not the lack of ID checks. Overall, the comments show a mix of worry about privacy, doubts about whether the rules will work, and some support for protecting kids in any way possible.

This leads us to a related story: the systemd team in Linux recently decided to remove a new “birthDate” field from user records. Their main reason was privacy and freedom. Storing user ages can create sensitive data and make Linux act like an authority, which many users do not want. There’s also no reliable way to check if a birth date is real without invasive checks. Some legal rules now require age checks, but many Linux groups feel these laws do not fit free and open-source projects. In the comments, most people support the removal, saying it protects privacy and keeps Linux simple. Some worry even an optional field could be misused. There’s also frustration about how heated the debate became, showing how strongly people feel about keeping Linux free of personal data.

Next, we look at a project bringing a fresh approach to deep learning: tinygrad and its new hardware, tinybox. Tinygrad is a simple neural network framework that breaks all operations into just three types, making the code easy to read and learn from. It’s growing fast, with funding and open hiring that welcomes new contributors. The team wants to make deep learning easier and cheaper.

Tinybox is their dedicated AI hardware. It promises big power for the price, with versions that support training massive models with up to 120 billion parameters. The hardware uses strong GPUs, lots of RAM, and fast storage, and is shipped worldwide—but only in fixed configurations to keep costs down. Payment is by wire transfer only.

People in the comments are excited about the simplicity of tinygrad and the power of tinybox. Some say tinygrad is a great learning tool, better than big frameworks for beginners. Others are impressed by the hardware, noting it offers a lot for the price. There are questions about real-world performance, worries about noise and power use, and debates about whether tinygrad can ever replace major frameworks like PyTorch. Many like the open style of hiring, and there are jokes about the business choices. Overall, people like the open, simple approach but are waiting to see if tinygrad and tinybox can handle even bigger projects in the future.

Now for a story about patience in tech. An article reminds us that some things, like growing trees or building trust, simply take time. While the tech world moves fast and loves new tools like AI to speed up work, the writer argues that real value often comes from slow, steady effort—whether building software, communities, or relationships. He shares his own experience working on open-source projects for years, saying the most important things cannot be rushed. In the comments, people agree that modern tech culture can feel too fast and miss deeper, lasting work. Some share stories of projects that only succeeded after years, while others note that sometimes speed is needed, especially for experiments or when money is tight. Still, most agree that the best things in life and work need time, care, and patience.

Let’s move on to some new open-source projects. First is Grafeo, a graph database written in Rust that focuses on speed and small memory use. You can use it as a library or a server, and it supports many query languages—GQL, Cypher, Gremlin, GraphQL, SPARQL, even SQL. Grafeo supports both labeled property graphs and RDF triples, and includes vector search for AI applications. It has bindings for many languages, strong ACID support, and is open source under Apache 2.0. Comments praise the Rust base, wide language support, and modern features, but some want more benchmarks and real-world use cases before trusting it for production.

Another fun project is Termcraft, a 2D sandbox survival game for your terminal, written in Rust. Inspired by classic block games, you play by moving, mining, crafting, and exploring—all using keyboard in the terminal. The world has different areas, and there are systems for health, hunger, weather, and more. It’s early alpha, single-player only for now, but many users are excited about a Minecraft-like game for low-resource computers and for learning Rust. Some discuss the technical challenges, nostalgia for ASCII games, and ideas for future features.

For families and learners, the second edition of "Electronics for Kids" is out, teaching children and adults about electronics with 21 hands-on projects. The book starts from the basics and goes up to building real alarms, motors, and games, all explained in simple language with full-color pictures. Comments show many adults wish they had this book growing up, some plan to use it for themselves or their kids, and most agree that hands-on learning is the best way. There are some worries about finding parts, but others say kits are easy to buy online. The book is praised for being clear, fun, and inspiring for the next generation of makers.

Turning to the world of health and industry, we look at Invisalign, the world’s biggest user of 3D printers. Invisalign makes clear aligners to straighten teeth, and now wants to 3D print the aligners directly. This could make the process faster, cheaper, and less wasteful, but making millions of aligners each day is a big engineering challenge. The company has developed special materials and handles most steps in-house, from scanning teeth to shipping products worldwide. Prices are still high, and some users in the comments wish the savings would go to customers. There are also worries about plastic waste, the results compared to metal braces, and the real effectiveness for all patients. Still, many are impressed by the scale and technology, and hope that 3D printing will help make dental care better and cheaper in the future.

In science news, a strange and very long gamma-ray burst, called GRB 250702B, was seen in July 2025. It lasted seven hours with three separate bursts in a day, much longer than normal gamma-ray bursts. Scientists think a normal star got too close to a rare, medium-sized black hole and was pulled apart, shooting out jets of energy. This may be the first time such a black hole has been seen eating a star in this way. The event is unusual and has excited astronomers, but some want more data before making big conclusions. In the comments, people are amazed by the power of these events, hopeful that new telescopes will catch more, and reminded that science is often slow and full of surprises.

Lastly, a new study from the University of Sydney says cannabis does not help with anxiety, depression, or PTSD, even though many people use it for these problems. The study looked at 54 clinical trials over 45 years and found no real proof of benefit for these mental health issues. In some cases, cannabis may even make things worse. There is stronger proof for its use in certain types of epilepsy, multiple sclerosis, and some pain, but not for mental health. In the comments, some users agree with the findings, while others say personal stories are important too. There are worries that these results could be used to block all cannabis use, debates about its safety compared to alcohol, and calls for more open and unbiased research as laws change.
