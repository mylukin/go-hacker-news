## There are no instances in ATProto

- 原文链接: [There are no instances in ATProto](https://overreacted.io/there-are-no-instances-in-atproto/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48599515)

This article explains that in ATProto, the protocol used by Bluesky, there are no “instances” like in Mastodon or ActivityPub. The author says people often ask “where are all the Bluesky instances?” but this question doesn’t make sense for ATProto. The main point is that ATProto separates hosting (where your data lives) and apps (how you view and interact with data), similar to how blogs and RSS readers work.

In the old days, blogs lived on many servers, and apps like Google Reader just collected posts from everywhere. Social media like Facebook put everything in one box, leading to centralization. Mastodon and ActivityPub tried to decentralize by letting anyone run an “instance”—a mix of hosting, app, and community. Users join an instance, and these talk to each other, but your identity is always tied to your chosen instance. This can cause problems: if your instance goes down, your identity disappears, and fights between admins can break connections.

ATProto does it differently. There are no “instances.” Instead, your data lives on a server you choose (called a PDS), and any app can read from any server. You can swap your hosting without losing your identity, and you can use new or different apps at any time. Apps are just ways to look at the data; they don’t own your data. This system is more flexible and avoids some problems of the “instance” model. You can self-host, try new apps, and move your data easily.

In the comments, some people agree that the article explains the difference well, especially for users coming from Mastodon. Others think the article is too hard on ActivityPub and that both systems have trade-offs. Some say that even if there are no “instances,” you still have to pick a host, which feels similar to picking an instance. A few feel the article focuses too much on technical terms and doesn’t answer practical questions about moderation, censorship, or data control.

There are debates about running parts of the network. Some think running your own “relay” or app server in ATProto is too expensive for most people, so in practice, most users still rely on the main Bluesky servers—making it feel centralized. Others reply that these servers are for developers, not average users, and that the cost is going down. Some comments worry that if Bluesky’s main server goes down, most of the network would still be affected, showing that true decentralization hasn’t arrived yet.

People also discuss moderation. In Mastodon, instance admins can block other instances, while in ATProto, apps can choose their own moderation. If you get banned from one app, you can move to another, but if the big app (like Bluesky) bans you, you lose access to a large audience. Some like that ATProto lets you move your identity and data; others worry about central points of failure, like the PLC directory (which manages identities).

A few say the focus on “counting instances” misses the bigger problem: most people just use what’s easiest, which leads to centralization. Others compare the situation to email or RSS, saying that most users always end up on the biggest provider. Some wish the article explained more about how ATProto solves issues like spam, content moderation, or what happens if the main server is lost. In the end, many agree that both models have strengths and weaknesses, and real decentralization is a hard problem to solve.

There’s also talk about factory work: many tasks are already automated, but some small, tricky jobs are still done by humans because it’s too costly to build special machines for each one. Humanoid robots might help with these “long tail” tasks.

## I used sound waves to make espresso. It could cut coffee‑brewing energy use by ¾

- 原文链接: [I used sound waves to make espresso. It could cut coffee‑brewing energy use by ¾](https://theconversation.com/i-used-sound-waves-to-make-espresso-it-could-cut-coffee-brewing-energy-use-by-75-284929)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48514843)

This article is about making espresso with sound waves instead of hot water, which could cut the energy needed by 75%. Researchers found that using ultrasound at room temperature can make espresso as strong and tasty as the traditional hot method.

The new process uses high-frequency sound waves, called ultrasound, to shake coffee grounds in water. This shaking creates tiny bubbles that pop near the coffee, helping to pull out flavor, aroma, oils, and caffeine very fast. The method uses a metal device (a transducer) that vibrates the filter basket in a regular espresso machine. Instead of hot water, they use room temperature water. They tested different water amounts, grind sizes, and how long to use ultrasound. The best results came from using fine grounds and about two and a half to three minutes of ultrasound.

The researchers compared their “ultrasonic espresso” to traditional espresso by giving both to about 100 regular coffee drinkers in a blind test. People could not tell the difference between the two espressos in taste, smell, or bitterness. For filter coffee, the ultrasound version was actually liked more. The main benefit is the big energy saving, which matters most for companies that make a lot of bottled or ready-to-drink coffee, not so much for home use.

In the comments, some people are excited about the technology, especially for industrial coffee makers who want to save power and make cold drinks. Others are skeptical about the energy savings, pointing out that the research compared two different machines and may not have included all energy costs, like making milk-based drinks hot. Some coffee fans say espresso should be hot and doubt room temperature espresso will catch on for daily drinking, but others note that for products like instant coffee or iced drinks, temperature doesn’t matter. A few wonder if the process could work using milk instead of water.

There’s discussion about taste—some say they only care about the final drink, not how it’s made, while others are curious about how the ultrasound changes extraction. Home coffee lovers debate if this method is useful for them, especially since taste tests used average coffee drinkers, not experts. Some share experiences with uneven extraction and talk about ways to improve espresso at home, like using different grind sizes or tools. Comments also talk about the noise of ultrasound machines and if quieter designs are possible. Finally, some users discuss energy costs in general and how saving power is more important for industry than for home coffee lovers.

## Project Valhalla, Explained: How a Decade of Work Arrives in JDK 28

- 原文链接: [Project Valhalla, Explained: How a Decade of Work Arrives in JDK 28](https://www.jvm-weekly.com/p/project-valhalla-explained-how-a)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48595511)

Project Valhalla is a big change coming to Java in JDK 28. It brings “value classes,” which let you write classes that work like primitives for better performance and memory usage.

The main idea is simple: with value classes, you can make new types (like Point or Money) that act like regular classes, but the JVM can store and use them as if they were plain numbers, not objects with pointers. Normally, in Java, everything except the basic types (like int or double) is a reference. This means if you have an array of Points, you really have an array of pointers to Points scattered in memory, which is slow for big data. With value classes, the JVM can store the data directly, side by side, with no extra headers or pointers. This makes things faster and uses less memory. It also avoids problems where “escape analysis” sometimes helps, but is unpredictable.

The Valhalla project has been in the works for over ten years. There were many failed ideas before this version. The current design is that value classes are declared with a new “value” keyword. They have no identity: two value objects with the same data are “the same” when you use ==. You can’t synchronize on them, and all fields are final. But, even with these changes, value classes can still be null for now—non-nullable types are planned for the future.

Two main optimizations come with value classes: scalarization (where the JVM just passes around the raw fields, not objects) and heap flattening (where arrays or fields store the data directly, not as pointers). This is best when the object is small (like two ints), but if it’s too big, or if it can be null, sometimes the JVM will still use regular objects. In JDK 28, this is a preview feature and not enabled by default.

Another big topic is generics. Right now, Java uses type erasure, which means you can’t get flat, fast storage with generics like List<Point>. That will come in later phases. For now, you get the main benefits with arrays and fields, not with generic collections.

Value classes are not just records; you can have ordinary classes, records, value classes, and value records. The change is deep: it lets Java move from “every object has identity” to “some objects are just values.” This opens the door to future improvements, like denser collections, better generics, and faster math.

People in the comments had a lot to say. Some are excited about the performance and memory improvements. Others worry about new complexity. For example, there’s debate about how == works for value classes—it now checks all fields, not just if two variables point to the same object. This can be confusing, especially if you’re used to old Java behavior, and could cause subtle bugs. Some suggest the “value” keyword should be required everywhere you use a value class, to make code clearer.

Many commenters compared Java’s approach to .NET and C#. Some think Java is just catching up to features C# has had for years, like structs and non-nullable types. Others say making this work in Java is much harder because of backwards compatibility. There’s also debate about Java’s type system and whether not having null safety by default is a mistake.

A few people are unhappy with the slow pace—non-nullable types and full generic support are still “coming soon.” Some feel the article or project overstates the complexity, or that Java’s team is too cautious. Others defend Java’s careful approach, saying stability and compatibility matter most for such a widely used platform.

Some comments talk about the article itself, noting AI-generated images and writing style, and wishing for more human input or clearer visuals. A few argue that the real change will only come once big libraries and frameworks start using value classes, especially after the next LTS release.

Overall, opinions are divided. Some see Valhalla as overdue and a bit behind C#, while others appreciate the care taken to ensure old code keeps working. Most agree that, once finished, this will make Java faster and more flexible for high-performance tasks. The preview in JDK 28 is just the start, and there’s a lot more to come.

## How many of the 170k English words do you know?

- 原文链接: [How many of the 170k English words do you know?](https://vocabowl-870366514258.us-west1.run.app/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48598586)

This article talks about a website that tests how many English words you know, out of about 170,000, using a 100-question quiz. The questions use a method called stratified sampling, picking words from different difficulty levels to estimate your vocabulary.

The test breaks words into bands: basics, intermediate, advanced, expert, and obscure. Each band has a set number of words, and your score in each band helps calculate your estimated vocabulary size. You answer by picking the correct definition from four choices. Many of the words use Latin or Greek roots, which can help if you know other languages. The quiz gives you a total at the end, sometimes with a fun message comparing you to someone like Stephen Fry.

People say the quiz is long, and some get tired before finishing. The user interface is slow, needing many clicks per word, and the buttons can be hard to reach, especially on mobile. Some say it’s too easy to guess the right answer, either by picking the longest option, using word roots, or by process of elimination. Many also complain there’s no “I don’t know” button, so you have to guess, which can inflate your score.

Some users think the distractor answers (the wrong choices) are too obvious or silly, making the quiz less accurate. Others note that the test sometimes gives you the definition of a word using the word itself, which is not helpful. A few say the quiz mixes in very obscure or even made-up words, which aren’t really used in English.

There’s talk about language origins—people who know Romance languages or Latin can guess many hard words. Some users have fun seeing words from fiction or other languages, like “Brobdingnagian” or “weltschmerz.” Others debate what counts as an English word, since English borrows from many languages.

Many think the test should adapt to your level faster, showing harder words if you do well, and easier words if you miss some. Suggestions include using keyboard shortcuts, fewer clicks, and better randomization of answer order. Some wish the test would let you admit when you’re just guessing, or add confidence levels.

People also discuss the scoring method, noting that active vocabulary (words you use) is much smaller than passive vocabulary (words you recognize). Some like the quiz for fun or learning, but think it’s not a perfect way to measure true vocabulary size. Others debate the value of knowing rare words, or the link between vocabulary and intelligence.

In short, most find the quiz fun, but see room for improvement in user experience, answer quality, and accuracy. Many agree that knowing word roots or other languages gives a big advantage. Some feel scores are too high because of easy guessing, and want a way to skip words they don’t know. Others just enjoy seeing strange and rare words pop up.

## A 1976 university experiment spun up the U.S. wind industry

- 原文链接: [A 1976 university experiment spun up the U.S. wind industry](https://spectrum.ieee.org/william-heronemus-wind-energy)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48541777)

This article talks about how a small team at the University of Massachusetts Amherst built a wind turbine in 1976, starting the U.S. wind industry. The project was led by William Heronemus, a Navy veteran and engineering professor, who believed that wind power could help solve America’s energy problems after the 1970s oil crisis.

Heronemus and his students built the first turbine using old car parts and handmade blades. They used the turbine to heat a small house during cold winters, and it worked so well they had to open the doors to cool down. At that time, most people thought wind power was only for small tasks, like pumping water, but Heronemus believed it could create enough electricity for many homes. He gave talks and wrote papers explaining his idea that wind farms—big groups of turbines—could provide a lot of the country’s electricity. He also argued that nuclear power was too risky and expensive, which made some people in the government and energy industry angry.

Heronemus’s ideas were bold. He suggested building thousands of wind turbines on land and even huge floating turbines in the ocean. He inspired many students who later helped start big wind farms, especially in California. The first turbine they built, called the “Wind Furnace,” became very important for training new engineers. Even today, many wind power experts started their careers working with Heronemus or studying at the university’s wind center.

The article also explains how wind power became more popular around the world. The U.S. never supported wind energy as much as Heronemus hoped, but other countries, like those in Europe and Asia, built many wind farms and new types of turbines. Some of the technology ideas from Heronemus’s team, like floating turbines and multiple rotors, are now being tested in places like China.

In the Hacker News comments, one person shared a personal story about wind turbine research in Romania. Their dad worked at a wind institute and saw a big vertical turbine fail because the emergency brakes didn’t work, showing how dangerous early wind experiments could be. The commenter said they didn’t find these stories interesting as a child, but now see how important and exciting this work was. They also joked that their own child might feel the same way about their stories.

Other readers enjoyed hearing this personal memory and pointed out that early wind energy projects faced many challenges and failures. Some people discussed the difference between vertical and horizontal wind turbines, and why horizontal designs became more common. A few commenters talked about how important it is to learn from mistakes in engineering, and how sharing these stories helps everyone understand the risks and progress in new technology. Overall, readers respected the hard work and vision of the early wind energy pioneers, even if their ideas seemed strange at the time.

## A new bill takes aim at government pressure to silence lawful online speech

- 原文链接: [A new bill takes aim at government pressure to silence lawful online speech](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/06/new-bill-takes-aim-government-pressure-silence-lawful-online-speech)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48600950)

A new bill called the JAWBONE Act was introduced in the U.S. Senate to stop government officials from pushing tech companies to silence legal online speech. The bill lets people sue the government if officials try to make companies like Apple, Google, or Meta take down posts or accounts that are protected by the First Amendment.

The article explains that "jawboning" is when the government pressures private companies to censor speech without the public knowing. Sometimes this is done with threats, and it can break free speech rights. The bill would give users a way to fight back in court if the government oversteps. For example, the EFF (Electronic Frontier Foundation) is helping the creator of an app called ICEBlock. ICEBlock let people report immigration enforcement actions, but federal officials threatened the app maker and forced Apple to remove it from the App Store. The EFF is also suing to find out exactly how the government spoke with Apple and other companies in these cases.

The article says not every message from the government to a tech company is bad—sometimes officials share real information to keep people safe. But there is a risk if the government goes too far. The EFF points out that social media companies also have their own free speech rights. They can choose how to moderate content and are not forced to let every kind of speech on their platforms. The Supreme Court has agreed that companies can decide what is allowed on their sites. The EFF hopes this new law will help protect everyday users' rights while letting companies run their platforms as they see fit.

In the comments, many people joked about the creative name of the bill. Some users pointed out that the bill is bipartisan, with support from both a Republican and a Democrat senator. Others worry whether the sponsors really care about free speech in all cases, especially since politicians sometimes support speech when it helps their side.

Some commenters argued that both right and left political groups try to limit speech when it suits them. Others discussed limits to free speech, like not allowing threats or fraud, and debated if more laws are needed or if the First Amendment is enough. People also shared examples from other countries, saying free speech problems are not just in the U.S.

A few users worried that government pressure to remove harmful misinformation—like about vaccines—can cross the line, but others said public health is important and officials should be able to warn companies about dangerous posts. Some said the real problem is when the government threatens companies with punishment if they don’t obey. There was also debate about whether companies should be forced to allow all speech, or if private platforms have the right to ban users as they choose.

Some people brought up famous cases where people were punished for posts or memes, while others doubted that the U.S. limits online speech compared to Europe. Several users were skeptical about the bill’s true purpose, saying it might only be used to protect one side’s speech. Many agreed the issue is hard: balancing free speech, company rights, and public safety is not simple, and the new law could have both good and bad effects.
