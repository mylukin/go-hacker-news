First, Mullvad VPN is rolling out a new fix to fight “exit IP fingerprinting,” a problem where someone could guess which VPN server you are using by your exit IP address. This update is now live on servers in countries like Australia, Canada, Germany, France, Ireland, Norway, Sweden, and the United States. Mullvad says this makes it harder for anyone to track or link your activity across their servers. They are known for caring about privacy, sharing details on their website, and keeping their software open source. More servers will get the fix soon, and you can check the list online. In the comments, many users praise Mullvad for fixing even small privacy problems and being open about their work. Some ask for more technical details and worry about possible slowdowns, while others note that perfect privacy is hard. Many wish other VPN companies were as open, and say Mullvad’s clear communication builds trust. Some remind us that VPNs are just one part of online privacy, and not everyone cares about this level of protection, but it’s good Mullvad keeps improving for all users.

Next, Norway’s National Library is building a large language model for Norwegian using 2 petabytes of Huawei flash storage. No big tech company is making an LLM in Norwegian, so Norway wants AI that knows its own language and culture. The library has the largest collection of Norwegian books, newspapers, and web pages. Since 2005, they digitized 20 petabytes of data, stored in three copies, making about 60 petabytes total. They use this data for their AI, including text, pictures, and audio. Special agreements let them use copyrighted content that private companies cannot get. For the AI pipeline, they use fast Huawei flash storage and clusters of Nvidia GPUs and CPUs to process and clean the data. The cleaned data is sent to the supercomputer Sigma2 Olivia for training. Moving large amounts of data from slow archives to fast AI storage was a big challenge, and the team built their own solutions. They also face tough questions about judging the AI’s quality, because Norwegian has two written forms and many dialects. Deciding who controls and uses the AI is still not clear, and making all the systems work together is ongoing work. In the comments, people praise Norway for building something for their own language and thinking about data sovereignty. Some worry about using Huawei hardware, while others say Norway is being practical. There’s talk about the value of local AI, the technical challenges, costs, and whether this project will inspire others. Many agree local AIs are the future, but small countries may find it hard to afford. Some say using the National Library was a smart move, and many are glad Norway is setting an example for respecting language and culture.

Turning to the US, California is changing a new law so Linux and other open-source operating systems do not have to check a user’s age. The first version of the law wanted all operating systems, even free ones, to ask for your age or birth date and share it with apps and app stores. Open-source systems usually have no company in charge, no user tracking, and no way to check ages. Critics said the rule was too broad and almost impossible for open source, and privacy groups warned it was dangerous. After protests, lawmakers wrote a change saying open-source software is not covered. Most Linux systems like Ubuntu and Fedora will not have to follow the law, but company-tied platforms like SteamOS might. The change is not final yet, but moving forward. In the comments, people are happy for open source and privacy. Some joke that young people will want Linux now. Others ask if running Linux inside Windows avoids the law—one user explains Windows still has to follow the rules. Some think age checks are pointless because users can just lie, and others worry the law is unfair to Windows and MacOS. Some see this as a win for open source, but still have concerns about fairness and logic. Many are frustrated that lawmakers do not understand technology, and some say media should cover these stories more.

On the technical side, there’s an article about why writing C code that works everywhere is very hard, especially with different compilers. The author, who is building a new C compiler, finds that many projects use features or tricks special to compilers like GCC or clang, not just standard C. For example, system libraries like glibc use attributes and macros only understood by GCC. Some headers expect GCC features, making it tough for new compilers. Other projects, like SDL, OpenBSD, and Android’s bionic libc, all have their own tricks or special code. The author lists ways to handle this: try to fix other projects (hard), become popular (rare), patch as you go (easiest), or copy GCC’s features (most practical). In the comments, many agree that portability in C is very hard. Some share their own struggles and say acting like GCC or clang is the only way. Others wish for more standard code, but admit it rarely happens. Many thank the author for sharing these real-world examples and admire the work of small compiler developers. Some say other languages are easier, but C is too old and widespread to change soon.

In developer tools, “gobee” is a new project that lets you write BPF programs in a subset of Go, instead of C. BPF programs run in the Linux kernel and are usually written in C. Gobee translates Go code into C, checks it for errors, and also creates Go bindings so your userspace code can easily talk to the BPF program. It supports many BPF types, knows about kernel helpers, and keeps the generated C readable. You still need clang to compile the code, but gobee helps Go users work with BPF without learning all the details of kernel C. In the comments, many are excited because Go is easier for them, and they like the readable output. Some note you can only use a subset of Go, and you still need to know how BPF works. There are questions about fit with Go workflows, possible bugs, and comparisons with Rust’s Aya. Most agree gobee is a useful bridge for Go users, making BPF work smoother, but not a full replacement for learning kernel programming.

In aviation news, Japan’s JAXA and top universities have tested a new hypersonic ramjet engine that could lead to flights from Tokyo to Los Angeles in just two hours. The ramjet has no moving parts, uses the plane’s speed to compress air, and can fly at five times the speed of sound. The test was on the ground, using a wind tunnel to simulate high altitude. Engineers built a special heat shield to protect the plane’s systems from the extreme heat, and sensors checked the temperatures matched their models. The next step is a real flight test at Mach 5 with a small rocket. If successful, Japan hopes to have passenger planes at these speeds in the 2040s. In the comments, people are excited about the idea, saying it could change travel. Some worry about heat, noise, cost, safety, and the environment. Others point out that government rules and airports will need to change too. Still, many hope to see this new technology in their lifetime.

In data science, there’s an article about Jensen–Shannon divergence (JSD), a way to measure how similar or different two probability distributions are. JSD is based on Kullback–Leibler divergence, but is symmetric and always finite. It is used to compare distributions in areas like machine learning, bioinformatics, and social sciences. JSD is bounded between 0 and 1, and its square root is a true distance metric. There are also tools in Python, Ruby, and R for working with JSD. In the comments, many say they use JSD often, especially in machine learning and natural language processing. Some like its clear results and that it avoids the “infinite” problem of Kullback–Leibler. There’s talk about using JSD in GANs and comparing with other distances like Wasserstein. Some ask for code examples, and others warn about careful handling with small samples. Overall, JSD is seen as a useful and popular tool in tech and data science.
