Let’s start with space. The Artemis II crew took amazing new photos of Earth while heading toward the Moon. NASA shared these high-quality pictures, showing our planet with the blue Atlantic Ocean, a thin atmosphere, and green lights at the poles. Venus even appears as a bright spot. Commander Reid Wiseman’s main photo, called “Hello, World,” captures parts of Africa, Europe, and South America. The crew took these shots after the Orion spacecraft made its final engine move to leave Earth’s orbit, now on a journey of over 200,000 miles to circle the Moon and return—the farthest humans have gone since 1972. They’ll pass behind the Moon on April 6, coming home on April 10.

Another photo shows the “terminator,” the line between day and night on Earth. After the big engine move, the astronauts were excited, taking lots of pictures—even asking how to clean the windows. Wiseman said it was hard to get clear images at first because of the distance, but they soon succeeded.

The community loves iNaturalist. Many users say it helped them discover rare species and meet new people. The app is easy to use and works well for families and kids, making walks more interesting. Some use it for school or science projects, praising the image recognition and the community’s help. There are a few privacy concerns about sharing locations of rare species, but most feel the benefits are much greater. Some want more features or offline support, but overall, iNaturalist is respected for making learning about nature fun and helping science.

Moving on to networking, there’s an article about turning a regular Linux computer into a router. It lists seven key changes: enabling IP forwarding, creating a network bridge, setting up firewall rules with nftables, enabling connection tracking, configuring NAT and masquerading, running a DHCP and DNS server with dnsmasq, and using hostapd for WiFi access. The article explains how packets move through the Linux networking stack and how all these tools work together. With these steps, a Linux system can act just like a home router, supporting both wired and wireless devices.

Hacker News commenters shared their thoughts. Some find building a Linux router fun and educational, especially with single-board computers like the Raspberry Pi. Others like the control and security this setup offers compared to cheap routers. However, they warn that you need to manage updates and solve problems yourself. Some mention performance limits, especially with many users or high speeds. Tips include using OpenWRT or pfSense to make setup easier. Many learned networking by building routers, while others prefer ready-made solutions. There were also discussions about nftables versus iptables, and some suggested adding extra features like intrusion detection. Most agree that building your own router is a great learning experience, but you need to understand both the benefits and the challenges.

Next is an article about improving an AI documentation assistant. The team first used Retrieval-Augmented Generation, or RAG, but it struggled with answers needing information from many places. They wanted the assistant to work like a developer searching files with commands such as grep, ls, or cat. Running real filesystems in sandboxes was too slow and expensive, so they built ChromaFs—a virtual filesystem that uses their Chroma database. This lets UNIX-like commands quickly search and list documents, with sessions now starting in just 100 milliseconds and almost no extra cost.

ChromaFs connects bash commands to database queries, supports access control, and caches results for speed. It’s read-only, making it safe. For searches, it combines database queries and in-memory filtering. Now, the assistant serves many users fast and efficiently. Hacker News users praised the creative solution, liked the use of database queries as a filesystem, and discussed edge cases and scaling. Some shared their own virtual filesystem projects, while others asked about security and the chance to add write support. Overall, people thought this was a clever way to make an AI assistant faster and cheaper.

Let’s talk about TinyGo, a special Go compiler for small devices and WebAssembly. TinyGo is based on LLVM and supports over 100 microcontroller boards, like the BBC micro:bit and Arduino Uno, plus WebAssembly for browsers and edge computing. It lets you use Go where regular Go is too heavy.

There’s an example of making an LED blink with TinyGo, and the website offers a playground to test code online. Top comments show excitement for Go on embedded systems and WebAssembly. Some users like that they can use a familiar language for hardware projects, though not all Go features are supported. Others compare TinyGo to MicroPython or CircuitPython, finding Go faster or more robust for their needs. There’s talk about TinyGo’s small binary sizes and some warnings that not all Go libraries will work, so simpler code is better. Debugging and tool support are improving, and overall, people see TinyGo as making embedded programming easier and more accessible.

Finally, we have a story about Samsung Magician, a disk utility for managing Samsung SSDs. On Mac, uninstalling it is extremely difficult. There’s no simple uninstall, just a hidden script that fails to remove most files. The author had to manually find and delete many files, even disabling System Integrity Protection to remove protected kernel extensions. The process took 18 steps and two reboots.

The app itself is large and complicated, with hundreds of PNGs for a simple spinning circle, running on the heavy Electron framework. In the comments, people were shocked by the uninstall process and shared similar stories with other vendor software. Some explained that Electron makes building and updating apps easier, but at a cost. There were calls for Samsung to provide a real uninstaller. Many said this kind of experience is a sign of poor software design and a lack of respect for users.
