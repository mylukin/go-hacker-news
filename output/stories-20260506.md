## Valve releases Steam Controller CAD files under Creative Commons license

- 原文链接: [Valve releases Steam Controller CAD files under Creative Commons license](https://www.digitalfoundry.net/news/2026/05/valve-releases-steam-controller-cad-files-under-creative-commons-license)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48037555)

Valve has released the CAD files for its new Steam Controller, and they are now free to use under a Creative Commons license. This means anyone can download the design files and use them to make their own add-ons or changes, as long as it is not for commercial business.

The files include the outer shell shape of both the controller and its “Puck” accessory, and come in popular formats like .STP and .STL. There are also engineering diagrams that show which parts must not be covered, so the controller works well. These files let people 3D print things like new grips, stands, skins, or even phone holders for the controller. Valve has done this before for other hardware, like the Steam Deck and the Index VR set, so this is a welcome move but not a surprise. The license is “non-commercial,” so if a company wants to sell accessories, they must talk to Valve first. The license also asks that anyone who makes changes shares their work and gives credit to Valve. The article suggests that even just making a simple smartphone clip could be a cool project for many users.

In the Hacker News comments, people are mostly happy with Valve’s move. Many think it is great to see a big company support makers and modders. Some say this helps the community make the controller better and keeps old hardware useful, even after Valve stops selling it. Others talk about how open files like these can help with repairs or customization, and how this supports the “right to repair” movement. A few worry that the “non-commercial” rule is too strict, and it would be nice if small shops could sell their custom parts. Some users also remember that Valve has shared CAD files before and hope more companies do the same. Overall, people see this as a positive step for both hobbyists and hardware fans.

## From Supabase to Clerk to Better Auth

- 原文链接: [From Supabase to Clerk to Better Auth](https://blog.val.town/better-auth)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48038827)

This article is about Val Town’s journey moving from using Supabase for authentication, then Clerk, and finally to Better Auth. The author explains why each change happened and what problems they faced along the way.

At first, Val Town used Supabase for both their database and authentication. Later, they wanted more control and switched to Render for the database and Clerk for authentication. Clerk is a big company and is very popular, but Val Town ran into problems. The main issue was that Clerk tried to handle both users and sessions, but it did not work well for them. For example, Clerk limited how many times you could ask for user data—only five requests per second for all users. This was a big problem for Val Town, which is a social site where you often need to see many users’ details at once. They had to create extra steps to copy user data from Clerk into their own database, which made things more complex and sometimes buggy.

Another problem was that Clerk was a single point of failure. If Clerk was down, nobody could use the site, even those who were already logged in. Clerk had outages that lasted a long time, making Val Town’s site unusable. The author says that if one critical system goes down, the whole site goes down with it. There were also smaller bugs and issues that took a lot of work to solve.

Even with these problems, switching away from Clerk was not easy. Clerk worked well for some simple apps and had good tools for developers. The alternatives were not perfect—many open-source options were old or not reliable, and other services had the same risk as Clerk.

Finally, Val Town switched to Better Auth. Better Auth is open source and lets them control their own data and sessions. If Better Auth’s company has problems, Val Town’s site will still work because they run it themselves. The author also liked that Better Auth’s dashboard is smart, and their paid service does not control sessions, so there is less risk. They mention AuthKit from WorkOS as another good choice, but open source was most important to them.

The Hacker News comments have many opinions. Some users agree with the author, saying it’s risky to let a third party control your users or sessions, and they share similar stories about outages and problems with big auth providers. Others point out that Clerk and Supabase are good if your app is simple, or you do not need social features, and that most companies never reach the scale where these problems matter. A few people defend Clerk, saying the company is improving and the rate limits are there for security. Some suggest building your own authentication is risky unless you have a lot of security experience. Others say open-source solutions often need a lot of work to keep secure. A few comments mention how hard it is to balance control, security, and ease of use, and how every solution has trade-offs. Some users are excited about Better Auth and share tips on using it, while others are still watching to see how it works out long-term. Overall, most agree there is no perfect answer, and teams must choose the best fit for their needs.

- 原文链接: [Vibe coding and agentic engineering are getting closer than I'd like](https://simonwillison.net/2026/May/6/vibe-coding-and-agentic-engineering/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48037128)

This article talks about how AI tools are changing the way people write code, and how two styles—“vibe coding” and “agentic engineering”—are starting to blend together. The author explains that vibe coding is when someone uses AI to make code without really understanding or checking it, while agentic engineering is when an experienced engineer uses AI tools carefully to help build strong, safe software.

The author used to think these two styles were very different, but now he finds himself mixing them. He says vibe coding is okay for personal tools, but it’s risky and wrong if you are writing software for others, because mistakes can hurt more people. Agentic engineering is better for important work because it uses AI, but with human experience and care for things like security and quality.

The problem is, now AI tools are so good that even experienced engineers stop checking every line of code, even for big projects. This makes the author feel worried and guilty. He compares it to using code from another team at work: you trust it because of their reputation, but AI does not have a reputation or responsibility. Still, the AI often does a very good job, so it becomes easy to trust it—maybe too much.

The author also says it’s now hard to judge if a software project is really good. AI can make a project with lots of code, tests, and nice documentation very fast, but that doesn’t mean it’s been used or tested in real life. He thinks it’s more important to know if someone has actually used the software and found it useful over time.

Another big point is that writing code is now faster, so old ways of planning and designing software might not fit anymore. If it’s cheap to rebuild things, maybe we don’t need to spend months on design before coding. Even with all these changes, the author is not afraid for his job. He says making good software is still very hard, and AI only helps people who already have experience.

In the comments, many readers agree that AI coding tools make things faster, but they worry about people not checking the code carefully. Some think that trusting AI too much could lead to big mistakes, especially in important systems. Others say that using AI for small, personal projects is fine, but for real products, you still need humans to check and test everything.

Some people point out that using code you haven’t checked is not new—many teams already trust code from other teams or open-source projects. But with AI, the speed and scale are much bigger, so the risks grow. A few readers note that AI can help experienced engineers do more, but it won’t replace the need for deep understanding and judgment.

Others discuss how companies might use AI to build more software faster, but customers will still want products that are proven to work. Some are hopeful that these tools will let teams try new ideas quickly and focus on real problems. Still, many agree that the main risk is getting too comfortable and trusting AI without enough checks, which could cause problems in the future.

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48039362)

Google has announced Google Cloud Fraud Defense, which is an upgrade to their reCAPTCHA tool. This new platform is made to help websites and businesses stop online fraud from both humans and AI agents.

The article explains that the web is changing because of AI agents—programs that act and make decisions by themselves. These agents can help users shop or do other tasks, but they also make it easier for bad actors to commit fraud. Google says old ways of stopping bots are not enough anymore. So, they built Fraud Defense to watch for both human and agent activity, find out who is real, and block fraud. 

Fraud Defense uses dashboards to show how agents and humans act on a site. It works with new industry standards like Web Bot Auth and SPIFEE to better tell if traffic is good or bad. There is a ‘policy engine’ that lets businesses set rules to allow or block certain users or agents. If the system is unsure if someone is real, it can show a QR code challenge that a human must scan with a phone—something AI cannot do easily. 

reCAPTCHA will still be a part of this new system. Current users do not have to change anything; pricing and site keys stay the same. Google claims this new tool can cut account takeovers by 51% and make websites safer without annoying users. The article also says Fraud Defense is invisible for most people, so there are fewer annoying puzzles, and only possible threats get blocked or challenged.

In the Hacker News comments, some people are worried about privacy and Google tracking even more user data. Others question if a big company like Google should have so much power over web security. A few users like that Fraud Defense might stop new types of fraud, but they worry about false positives blocking real users or agents. Some developers say the QR code challenge could be hard for users without smartphones, or for people with disabilities. Others ask if open-source or self-hosted options might be better for control and transparency. A few are hopeful that these tools will help businesses fight new, smarter fraud, but there is a clear split between people who trust Google’s solution and those who want more open, less centralized answers.

## Learning the Integral of a Diffusion Model

- 原文链接: [Learning the Integral of a Diffusion Model](https://sander.ai/2026/05/06/flow-maps.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48040002)

This article talks about a new way to make diffusion models faster and more efficient. Diffusion models are used to make things like images or sounds from random noise. Normally, to get a good result, these models need to take many small steps, slowly turning noise into something useful. The process is slow because each step only knows about the current point and noise level.

The main idea in the article is about "flow maps." Instead of taking many tiny steps, flow maps try to learn the whole path from noise to data in one go. This can let you go from pure noise to a final result with fewer steps, maybe even just one. The article explains that this is harder for neural networks to learn, but if it works, it saves a lot of time.

To make flow maps work, the article explains three "consistency rules" that help train these models. These rules are called compositionality, Lagrangian consistency, and Eulerian consistency. Each rule gives a way to check if the flow map works as it should. For example, if you go from point A to B, and then B to C, it should be the same as going straight from A to C. These rules are used to create loss functions, which help the neural network learn the right mapping.

The article also talks about practical problems, like how to actually train these maps. It is more complex than normal diffusion models, and sometimes you need to use tricks like stopping gradients or using special ways to calculate derivatives. There are also different ways to set up the training: you can "distill" from a regular model, train from scratch, or use combinations.

There are many methods and variations being tested. Some use extra teacher models, some use just data, and others use only noise. The article lists many papers and compares their techniques, costs, and results. Flow maps are now being used for faster image and audio generation, and even for new things like reward-based learning where you want to steer the output towards a goal.

In the Hacker News comments, some users are excited about the speed-up potential. They like the idea of making sampling much quicker, especially for big models and for running on smaller devices. Others point out that training flow maps is still hard and can use more resources than regular diffusion models. Some are worried about the extra complexity and whether the results are as good as with classic methods.

A few readers discuss technical details, like which consistency rule is best, or if stopping gradients could hurt the learning. Some mention that the area is confusing, with many new papers and different notations, making it hard for newcomers to follow. There are also questions about how robust these methods are when the data or noise changes, and if the models can generalize to new kinds of data.

Other commenters share their own experiments. Some tried flow maps and found them tricky to get working well, but promising when they do. A couple of people link to open-source code or visual demos. Finally, several users like the article's deep explanations and are happy to see a big summary of the field, while others wish for more simple examples or code snippets to help them start.

## The bottleneck was never the code

- 原文链接: [The bottleneck was never the code](https://www.thetypicalset.com/blog/thoughts-on-coding-agents)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48006967)

The article talks about how new coding agents, like AI tools that write code, have made it much faster and easier for people to turn ideas into working software. The author tested these tools and saw that they can quickly build things if you tell them exactly what you want. But the article says the real problem in software is not writing code—it’s people working together and agreeing on what to build.

The author explains that most big software is made by teams. The hardest part is getting everyone to agree on the details. For years, people tried to make coding faster, but now that code is easy to write, the real bottleneck is making good plans and clear instructions. Managers and team leads now spend more time writing detailed specs and roadmaps because agents need very clear information to do their job. The work has shifted from typing code to writing down what the code should do.

Another point is that when it gets easier to make things, people often build more features—sometimes too many. But users can only handle a few new things at once, so teams need to learn when to say no. Good focus makes better products.

The author also talks about “context,” which means shared understanding about why things were built a certain way. Humans learn this by working together, reading chats, and sharing history. Agents can’t just “pick up” this context—they only know what is written down. So if you don’t record all the important reasons and details, agents might make mistakes.

The company in the article is building tools to help agents read everything—code, comments, tickets, chats—to collect this hidden context. This helps both agents and new team members understand why things work the way they do. Still, not all knowledge can be written down, and some things are only learned by being part of the team.

The article ends by saying the real advantage for companies in the future will not be just having better AI tools. The winners will be the ones whose teams stay organized and keep everyone on the same page, even as they grow. Tools like coding agents make good teams better, but they can also make bad teams worse if they can’t work together. Writing and sharing knowledge will be more important than ever.

Hacker News commenters had many thoughts. Some agree that writing code is not the hard part—making good decisions and explaining them is. Others say that even with agents, understanding what to build is still the real job. A few worry that writing down all context will slow teams down or that too much documentation might be ignored. Some are excited about tools that collect context, saying they could help new people join projects faster. Others point out that human relationships and trust are still key for teams, and that machines can’t replace that. A few people also warn that more features don’t always mean better software, and that focus is important. Some share stories about how their teams struggle more with planning and communication than with coding. Overall, many agree that tools are helpful, but good teamwork and clear goals matter most.

## MIT’s virtual violin offers luthiers a new design tool

- 原文链接: [MIT’s virtual violin offers luthiers a new design tool](https://arstechnica.com/science/2026/05/mits-virtual-violin-offers-luthiers-a-new-design-tool/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48041009)

MIT has made a computer tool that lets violin makers (luthiers) design virtual violins and hear how changes affect the sound. This tool uses physics-based 3D modeling, not just recorded sounds, so luthiers can try out different materials and shapes before building a real instrument.

Traditionally, luthiers learn by experience, picking woods and shapes to get the sound they want. The MIT tool can help them test ideas faster by changing things like wood type or body thickness in a computer model. The model is based on real data from famous old violins, like a 1715 Stradivarius, and breaks the instrument into millions of small cubes. Each cube has details about the material used in that spot, and the program calculates how everything vibrates and creates sound, even including the air inside and around the violin.

The team at MIT used this model to make a virtual violin play simple plucked notes, even playing parts of Bach and “Daisy Bell.” They can’t yet simulate bowing (using the bow on the strings), which is more complex, but they are working on it. This is different from most computer violin sounds, which play back recorded notes. Instead, the MIT model creates sound from scratch using physics.

Many people have tried to find out why old violins like Stradivarius sound so good. Some say it’s the shape, some say it’s the special wood, others think it’s because of the varnish or chemicals used to treat the wood. Studies have looked at the density of the wood, the types of varnish, and even scanned the violins with lasers and CT scans to see how they work.

MIT’s tool builds on this research, letting luthiers experiment without wasting rare materials. The creators say this won’t replace the skill of master violin makers, but it can help them understand more about how violins make sound.

In the comments, some people are excited about using science to help with musical instrument making. They think this tool could help new makers learn faster or try creative ideas. Others are doubtful; they say the art and feel of a violin can’t be replaced by a computer, and that playing and listening in person is still the best way to judge an instrument.

A few commenters worry that too much focus on technology might take away from the magic of handcrafting. Some say that even if the sound is “perfect” by science, it might not feel right to players or listeners. Others mention that every violin is unique, and great music comes from the player, not just the instrument.

Some people point out that this tool could also help with other string instruments, not just violins. A few wonder if, in the future, we could design entirely new instruments or sounds that no one has heard before. There are also comments about how hard it will be to get simulated bowing right, but most agree this is an interesting step in mixing science and music.
