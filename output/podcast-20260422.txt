First, let’s start in Alberta, Canada, where a company is building tractors without any modern technology. These tractors do not have computers, sensors, or high-tech parts. They cost about half the price of normal new tractors. The idea is simple: many farmers do not want or need the latest tech. High-tech tractors are expensive, hard to fix, and often need special tools or software. This new startup makes machines that can be fixed with basic tools, and spare parts are easy to find. Because there are fewer things to break, these tractors are more reliable in tough conditions, and farmers can keep them running for years without big repairs. The company hopes this will help small and medium farmers save money and stay independent from big equipment brands.

Comments on Hacker News are mostly positive. Many like that the tractors are affordable and easy to fix, and remember how old tractors could last for decades. Some farmers say they do not want to pay for features they will never use. But there are worries too. A few people think these simple tractors may not meet new rules about safety or pollution. Some say modern features like GPS or automation can really help on big farms. There are also questions about whether this business can survive, since big companies control the market. Still, most commenters agree: it’s good to give farmers a choice.

Next, Apple released a fix for a serious privacy bug. Before the update, when you got a message on your iPhone or iPad, the text often showed up in a notification. Even if you deleted the message in an app like Signal, a copy of the notification stayed in your phone’s database for up to a month. Police could use special tools to recover these deleted notifications and see messages that should have been erased. This problem became public when the FBI used it to get deleted Signal messages from a suspect’s phone. The president of Signal asked Apple to fix it, saying deleted notifications should not stay. Apple’s update now removes these leftovers, and the fix is also in older iOS versions. Privacy experts were worried, because many people use message deletion to stay safe. If the phone is taken, they want messages truly gone.

Hacker News users were surprised this bug lasted so long. Some talked about how hard it is to really delete data on complex devices. Others pointed out that users often think deleting a message means it’s gone, but copies can remain. Commenters said notification systems are tricky, and some worried about what other data might stick around after “deletion.” Many felt Apple should have fixed this faster, especially since they talk a lot about privacy. Some said this shows why open-source software is good, because you can check how deletion works. There were reminders to keep devices updated and not trust that any deletion is perfect.

Moving on to AI coding tools: a new article looks at how AI models like Copilot, Claude, or GPT often change too much code when fixing bugs. The author calls this “over-editing.” Sometimes, an AI only needs to fix one line, but instead, it rewrites or moves things it should leave alone. This makes code reviews harder and can hide what was actually fixed. The article gives an example where a small bug in a range() call leads GPT-5.4 to rewrite the whole function, adding checks and changing logic that was not needed. The author built a dataset with simple bugs and measured how much code the AI changed. Results show all models over-edit, but Claude Opus 4.6 is more careful. If you tell the model to “make as few changes as possible,” results improve a lot.

Some comments say over-editing happens because models like to “show off” or improve code, even when not asked. Others agree it is better to reward small, simple fixes, but also note that sometimes a bigger rewrite is needed for quality. Many suggest better prompts and tools that show what changed and why. There is hope that with better training and instructions, AI tools will make smaller, safer edits in the future.

Now to a privacy bug in Firefox and Tor Browser. Websites could use a hidden browser feature to track users, even in private mode. The problem came from how IndexedDB databases were listed. The order of the list was not random and could act like a fingerprint. Websites could create and list databases, then check the order to track users across sites—even in private browsing or after using Tor’s “New Identity” button. The fix was easy: just sort the list before giving it to websites. Mozilla fixed this in Firefox 150, and the Tor Project also needs to update.

Hacker News commenters were surprised that such a small detail could break privacy. Some do not fully trust private browsing modes and say browser privacy is always hard. Others praise Mozilla for fixing it quickly. There was talk about how open-source browsers help people find and report bugs, and how privacy needs careful design, even for small leaks.

Next, Qwen3.6-27B is a new open-source AI model focused on coding, with 27 billion parameters. The model is dense, meaning all its parameters are active, so it is easy to run. It works with both text and images and beats much bigger models on coding and reasoning tasks. You can download the model weights and use it with popular coding tools. It also supports features like “preserve_thinking,” which helps in agent-based coding tasks. The main goal is to give developers a strong, open coding AI that is easy to use.

Most commenters are impressed, saying it is a big deal to beat a 397B MoE model with just 27B parameters. Some are happy the weights are open, which is good for research and small teams. Others ask about real-world performance and how well it works with different programming languages. Some share tips for running the model, while others warn about possible overfitting. Overall, many see Qwen3.6-27B as a strong and practical option for coding tasks.

Now, let’s talk about tiny fonts. One article explains how to make a 5x5 pixel font for small screens, like those on microcontrollers. The author says 5x5 is the smallest size where letters are still readable. Smaller fonts, like 3x5 or 2x2, lose too much detail. The 5x5 font is fixed-width, so it is easy to use in code, and uses only 350 bytes of memory—perfect for tiny chips and displays. The article gives a code file and links to other tiny font resources.

In the comments, people praise the work and say 5x5 is a good balance between clarity and size. Some share memories of using similar fonts on old computers. Others suggest tricks to fit more languages or make letters more clear. Many agree that fixed-width is smart for programming, and thank the author for sharing the font.

Next, a creative hack lets you run both Windows 9x and Linux apps together on old PCs, without hardware virtualisation. The author, Hailey, made a system where the Linux kernel works with the Windows 9x kernel, even on very old computers like a 486. It is like the old coLinux project, but for Windows 9x. No special CPU features are needed. The project uses tricks with memory and threads, and only a bit of Windows internals hacking. Networking is not ready yet, but may come later.

Comments are full of praise, calling the project “awesome” and “epic,” even “cursed” in a nice way. Many remember using coLinux or Cygwin and think this is a clever way to save old hardware. Some ask technical questions, and others joke about running it on even stranger setups. Most people love the spirit of the project and want to see what happens next.

Now, let’s review a new book about JG Ballard called "The Illuminated Man." The biography started with Christopher Priest, but after he became sick and died, Nina Allan finished it. The book mixes Ballard’s life story with Priest’s illness and Allan’s grief. Ballard was a famous writer shaped by his hard childhood and strange, strong images in his work. He wrote about the mind, not space, calling it “inner space.” Many say his books predict today’s chaotic world.

Hacker News readers respect Ballard for making science fiction deeper, but opinions are split. Some love his style, others find it too strange and dark. Some debate if the book’s mix of Ballard’s life and the authors’ personal stories is good or distracting. There is talk about Ballard avoiding technology, which seems odd for a sci-fi writer. Most agree his work is powerful and unique.

Next, Flipbook is a new website where every page is a fresh image, made in real time by AI. There is no normal text or links—everything you see is a picture. Sometimes the words in the images look strange, but the creators say this will improve. Flipbook can also animate pages with a live video stream, but this uses a lot of computer power. The idea is to make the web more visual, even like a video game.

Hacker News users found Flipbook creative and futuristic, but some worry it is hard to search or copy information, and not accessible for people with vision problems. Some are curious about the technology and cost of making all these images. Others think it could be fun for exploring, but not for serious work. Most agree it is an interesting experiment with a long way to go.

Finally, a new robot now plays ping-pong very well. The robot uses cameras, sensors, and machine learning to track the ball and move fast. It can play safe or attack, and learns from past games. In tests, it played against both amateur and pro players—and sometimes even won. The team hopes the robot can help people practice and improve their skills.

People in the comments are impressed by the robot’s speed and skill. Some wonder if it could help in other sports or training. Others worry about robots replacing humans, but most are excited to see what robots will do next.
