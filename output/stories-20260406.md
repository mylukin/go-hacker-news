## Show HN: Ghost Pepper – Local hold-to-talk speech-to-text for macOS

- 原文链接: [Show HN: Ghost Pepper – Local hold-to-talk speech-to-text for macOS](https://github.com/matthartman/ghost-pepper)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47666024)

Ghost Pepper is a new speech-to-text app for macOS that works fully local—no internet needed, and your voice data never leaves your computer. You just hold the Control key, talk, then let go to see your words typed out and pasted where you need.

The app uses open-source speech models like WhisperKit for turning your voice into text. There are different models you can pick: some are small and fast for English, others are bigger and support more languages. After your speech is transcribed, a local language model (LLM) cleans up your text by removing “um” and other filler words, or fixing mistakes. You can choose how much clean-up you want, or even change the prompt it uses. Ghost Pepper runs in your menu bar, not your dock, and is set to start at login by default. The app never saves your voice or text to disk, and debug logs only stay in memory. For setup, you download the app, drag it into Applications, and give it microphone and accessibility permissions. You can also build it yourself from source using Xcode. The app is free and open-source, built with several popular AI and Mac tools. For companies, IT admins can pre-approve the permissions using MDM tools.

People on Hacker News had a lot to say about Ghost Pepper. Many are happy to see a speech-to-text tool that does not use the cloud and keeps everything private. Some users say this is great for people worried about privacy or working with sensitive information. Others like that it is fast and doesn’t need internet, unlike Apple’s own dictation or other paid apps. A few commenters wonder how well it works with accents or in noisy places. Some people wish there was Windows or Linux support, or hope for more languages. Others shared that the app is easy to install and use, and the open-source code makes them trust it more. There are questions about how accurate the transcriptions are compared to big cloud services, and some want to know if the app will get new features like shortcuts or custom hotkeys. A few raised worries about the size of the models and how much disk space they take, but others say it is worth it for privacy. Some people are excited to see more local AI tools like this, saying it is a sign that open-source is catching up with big tech. Others just appreciate that it is free and not trying to collect user data.

## Launch HN: Freestyle – Sandboxes for Coding Agents

- 原文链接: [Launch HN: Freestyle – Sandboxes for Coding Agents](https://www.freestyle.sh/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47663147)

Freestyle is a tool that lets you run and manage AI-generated code in special sandboxes, which are like safe spaces for code to run. The main idea is to give coding agents, like AI helpers, a real Linux virtual machine (VM) for their work—these VMs start very fast and can be controlled with an API.

With Freestyle, you can create VMs from code templates, fork VMs to do many tasks at once, and even review code changes using AI. For example, one agent can build an API, another can make the UI, and a third can write tests—all at the same time, in separate VMs. The VMs can be paused to save money, then resumed later. Code is managed using Git repositories, and you get features like webhooks, GitHub sync, and easy deployments. Unlike other sandboxes that use containers, Freestyle gives you full Linux VMs with root access, so you can run almost anything, even other VMs inside them. Security features include user isolation and full networking. The service is free to start, and you only pay when VMs are running, not when they are paused.

In the Hacker News comments, some users are excited about Freestyle’s speed and full-VM approach. They like that you can start a VM in under a second and fork it quickly, which is useful for running many agents. Others compare it to similar tools, like CodeSandbox or GitHub Codespaces, but notice that Freestyle gives more power and deeper control because it’s a real VM, not just a container.

Some people ask about security, since giving root access can be risky. The Freestyle team says they have strong isolation for each user. A few commenters worry about the cost if you need thousands of VMs running at once, but others point out that pausing VMs saves money. There are also questions about how easy it is to connect these VMs to other cloud services, and if it’s possible to use Freestyle for teaching or research. Overall, the community sees Freestyle as a strong tool for advanced users who want to build, test, and deploy AI code agents quickly and safely.

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47667321)

The article is about peptides, which are small chains of amino acids that are important in biology and medicine. The author wants to explain why peptides are interesting and sometimes difficult to work with.

Peptides are like short pieces of proteins. They are smaller than proteins but still do important jobs in the body, like sending signals between cells or fighting germs. Scientists can make peptides in the lab to use as medicine or for research. Making peptides is not always easy because they can stick together, fold the wrong way, or break apart. Some peptides are very helpful for treating diseases, but it is hard to get them into the body because they can be destroyed in the stomach or blood. Scientists are trying to find ways to make peptides last longer and work better, maybe by changing their shape or adding special parts. Some new drugs made from peptides are already helping people, like medicines for diabetes and some cancers. But it takes a lot of work to make peptide drugs safe and useful.

In the comments, some people say peptides are amazing tools for research and medicine. Others talk about how hard it is to make or use peptides because they can break down easily. A few commenters mention that there are many tricks to help peptides survive in the body, but none of them are perfect. Some people share stories about working with peptides in the lab, saying it can be very frustrating. Others are excited about new peptide medicines and think there will be more in the future. One person worries that some people are using peptide supplements without really knowing if they work. Another commenter says peptides are a big topic and there is still a lot to learn. Overall, people agree that peptides are important, but working with them has many challenges.

## A cryptography engineer's perspective on quantum computing timelines

- 原文链接: [A cryptography engineer's perspective on quantum computing timelines](https://words.filippo.io/crqc-timeline/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47662234)

A cryptography engineer says we must switch to quantum-resistant cryptography much sooner than expected, because new research shows quantum computers could break current encryption by 2029. Two new papers from Google and Oratomic lower the amount of quantum power needed to crack 256-bit elliptic curves, which means attacks are possible sooner and with fewer resources.

The article points out that hardware is getting better, quantum algorithms are improving, and error correction is not as big of a problem anymore. The author is not a quantum physicist, but trusts experts who say the risk is now real, and we cannot assume quantum computers will not arrive soon. He says it is dangerous to ignore these warnings, because security is about preparing for even unlikely risks, not just the most probable ones.

He explains that waiting for perfect solutions is no longer an option. We must use the post-quantum tools available now, like ML-KEM and ML-DSA, even if they are not perfect or easy to fit into existing systems. Hybrid approaches that mix old and new crypto should be avoided, as they add complexity and slow things down.

The author also mentions that symmetric encryption (like AES) is still safe with 128-bit keys, and that not all systems will break at once, but big changes are needed soon. Some old systems, like hardware security modules, are not ready for post-quantum keys and may be left behind. He warns that users of modern cryptographic identities and file encryption must act fast, or they could face big problems if quantum computers arrive before they finish updating.

In the Hacker News comments, many readers agree that the timeline for quantum computers is moving up, and that it is wise to prepare. Some say that even if the risk is small, the consequences are so big that we must act now. Others are more skeptical, saying that quantum computers may still be far away, and that rushing could introduce new bugs or weak points in the new systems if we do not take enough time to test.

Some commenters remind everyone that deploying new cryptography is hard and takes years, so starting now is necessary even if the threat is not immediate. A few people are worried about the size and speed of post-quantum cryptography, especially for things like Web certificates and embedded devices. Others point out that the new standards are better tested than before, and that waiting for perfect solutions often means missing the real deadline.

There is also debate about trusting experts versus waiting for proof, and about how much risk is acceptable in security. Some users share stories of slow migrations in the past (like SHA-1 to SHA-256), saying that it is better to accept some pain now than to face a sudden crisis later. Overall, the comments show a mix of urgency, caution, and real-world worry about how to make the switch in practice.

## German police name alleged leaders of GandCrab and REvil ransomware groups

- 原文链接: [German police name alleged leaders of GandCrab and REvil ransomware groups](https://krebsonsecurity.com/2026/04/germany-doxes-unkn-head-of-ru-ransomware-gangs-revil-gandcrab/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47660954)

German police have revealed the identity of "UNKN," the person believed to lead the Russian ransomware groups REvil and GandCrab. The article says his real name is Daniil Maksimovich Shchukin, a 31-year-old from Russia, and he worked with another Russian, Anatoly Kravchuk, to attack many companies and demand ransom.

Shchukin is accused of running two of the biggest ransomware groups, which together caused over 35 million euros in damage in Germany alone between 2019 and 2021. GandCrab and REvil are known for "double extortion": they not only locked up company files but also threatened to leak stolen data unless victims paid extra. The U.S. government found a crypto wallet linked to Shchukin with more than $300,000 made from ransoms. GandCrab first appeared in 2018, paid hackers to break into big companies, and updated their malware often to avoid security tools. In 2019, GandCrab claimed to shut down after earning more than $2 billion.

After GandCrab, REvil started with a big announcement on a Russian forum, showing $1 million in escrow to prove they were serious. Experts believe REvil was a rebranding of GandCrab. "UNKNOWN" (UNKN) even gave an interview about his rise from poverty to becoming a millionaire through hacking. The ransomware gangs ran like real businesses, hiring outside help to improve their malware and make more money. They targeted rich companies, especially those with cyber insurance, because insurance would pay the ransom. In one famous case, REvil hacked an IT company called Kaseya, which affected over 1,500 businesses. The FBI later said they had already broken into REvil’s servers and released a free decryption key to help victims. This caused the gang to fall apart.

Police think Shchukin still lives in Russia. They also found links between Shchukin and an older hacker name, "Ger0in," but nothing directly ties him to the UNKN account on crime forums. Officials compared photos from a birthday party to confirm his identity.

In the comments, some people joke about insurance making ransom payments more likely, saying “free money wants to be free.” Others disagree, pointing out that insurance is not free—higher costs for companies get passed on to customers. There’s some sarcasm and arguments about who really loses when ransom is paid. A few commenters link to old conference videos that discussed Shchukin’s identity years before, showing that some in the security community knew or guessed who he was. Some users point out how fast ransomware groups adapt and how hard it is to catch their leaders if they stay in Russia. There is also debate about whether public naming will actually stop these criminals or just make them more careful. Overall, many are surprised by how organized and business-like these ransomware gangs have become, and there is concern about the effect on regular people and companies.

## What being ripped off taught me

- 原文链接: [What being ripped off taught me](https://belief.horse/notes/what-being-ripped-off-taught-me/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47660286)

This story is about someone who lost $35,000 after working on an AR bus project in China. The writer explains how a friend brought him into the job, which sounded urgent and came with good references.

When he arrived, he saw a big mess: the team was very inexperienced, there was no version control, and the code was a confusing mess. The hardware setup was bad, and the environment was not safe for the equipment. The team did not understand basic AR problems like lens distortion, parallax, or how to make things look real on the bus windows. GPS didn’t work well, and the device setup was dangerous and badly planned.

The writer tried to help by suggesting better ways to work, like using version control, measuring lenses, and improving the render pipeline. He worked long hours, used his own equipment, and even helped manage team conflicts. But the team only wanted flashy demos, not real fixes. The client was often upset because results did not meet expectations. The writer missed time with his family and worked through pain from an injury. He received only a small deposit and trusted that the rest would be paid.

After the project, they never paid him. He tried debt collectors but learned suing was useless because the company could just disappear. In the end, he realized some lessons: contracts may not protect you, trust your gut, and sometimes you cannot help people who refuse real help.

In the Hacker News comments, many people felt sorry for the writer and said this kind of thing happens too often in tech and freelance work. Some said always use progress payments and never trust just a contract, no matter how short the project. Others pointed out that bad teams often don’t know how much they don’t know, and good work is hard to see for clients who don’t understand the details. A few commenters shared their own stories of not getting paid and said it’s important to check if a client has a good history. Some said that being asked to rush or work without clear plans is a red flag. Others talked about how legal protection often does not work for small amounts or if the company can easily close. Finally, many repeated the main advice: always trust your gut and protect yourself, even if it feels rude.

## Issue: Claude Code is unusable for complex engineering tasks with Feb updates

- 原文链接: [Issue: Claude Code is unusable for complex engineering tasks with Feb updates](https://github.com/anthropics/claude-code/issues/42796)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47660925)

This article is about a problem with Claude Code, an AI tool for coding, after a big update in February. The author and their team used Claude for complex engineering tasks, but after the update, Claude started making mistakes and became less helpful.

The main points are:
- Before February, Claude could follow detailed instructions, remember project rules, and make careful changes to code. It did lots of “thinking” before editing files.
- After February, the model’s “thinking” got reduced. This means it stopped checking code carefully and started making quick, careless changes.
- The team tracked this by looking at thousands of sessions, tool calls, and logs. They found that as Claude’s “thinking tokens” (the part that lets it plan and reason) went down, the quality of its work dropped.
- The model started editing files without reading them, making the same mistakes many times, ignoring project rules, and asking for permission to continue instead of working on its own.
- Users had to stop and correct Claude much more often. The AI even admitted its own mistakes, calling its own work “lazy,” “rushed,” or “sloppy.”
- The team built tools to watch for these problems — like scripts that would force Claude to keep working if it tried to stop or dodge responsibility.
- As a result of these problems, the human engineers became frustrated, used more negative words, and stopped trusting Claude with important work.
- The article shows that when Claude did less “thinking,” it used more computer resources, made more requests, and wasted time on corrections — so even if each request was cheaper, the total cost went up a lot.
- The author asks for clear information from Anthropic (Claude’s maker) about how much “thinking” their model is allowed to do, and suggests offering a premium tier for users who need deeper reasoning.
- Time-of-day analysis showed that Claude’s quality now changes during busy hours, which did not happen before.
- The author says that deep thinking is not just a nice feature — it is necessary for serious engineering work.

In the comments, many users agree that AI models sometimes get worse after updates, especially when companies try to save costs by reducing resources. Some say that “thinking depth” is important for all serious AI work, not just coding. Others think that Anthropic should let users pay more for better quality, or at least be honest about changes. A few people share similar stories from using other AI tools, like ChatGPT, and suggest that companies often hide these downgrades. Some users worry about relying on AI for important jobs if quality can drop suddenly. Others point out that tracking tool behavior and user sentiment is smart, and wish more teams did this. There are also comments about how hard it is to balance cost, quality, and user needs, and some defend Anthropic, saying scaling AI is difficult and mistakes happen. Overall, the community wants more transparency and control over how AI models use their “thinking” power.

## Book review: There Is No Antimemetics Division

This article reviews the science fiction book “There Is No Antimemetics Division,” which is based on stories from the SCP Foundation, a website where people write about strange and scary objects. The book’s big idea is about “antimemes”—ideas that erase themselves from people’s minds, making them impossible to remember or fight.

The story follows Marion Wheeler, who leads the Antimemetics Division. Her job is to fight threats that everyone else forgets exist because of their antimemetic nature. To do this, she and her team take special drugs that help them remember things their brains want to forget, but these drugs cause pain and other problems. The enemy in the story is a huge, dangerous creature that kills anyone who fully understands it, so even knowing about it is dangerous. The book’s world is built on the idea that information is more real than physical things, and there are strange creatures that live in the space of ideas, not just in the real world.

The review says the book is scary in a way that feels familiar to people who work with computers or systems, because it is about losing information and not even knowing it is gone—a bit like silent bugs in software or forgotten backups. The main character even erases her own memories to keep the enemy from using them, losing her identity and relationships as a kind of sacrifice.

The story also includes a sad but hopeful idea: even when someone is forgotten, the love they leave behind can be felt, even if people cannot remember why. The book’s style also matches its theme—chapters start in the middle of scenes and information is missing, so readers feel the same confusion as the characters.

In the comments, some people say they love the book and the SCP Foundation, calling the story unique and clever. Others say the idea of antimemes is very interesting and makes them think about how information can be lost in real life, like bugs in systems or things we forget. Some readers like how the book mixes horror with computer science ideas. A few mention that the writing style can be confusing at first, but they think it fits the story’s theme. There are also comments about how the book shows the pain of working in a job where your hard work is invisible, like being an open source maintainer. Not everyone thinks the book is perfect—some say it’s hard to follow or too strange for them. But most agree it is creative, deep, and worth reading if you like weird stories about memory, information, and horror.
