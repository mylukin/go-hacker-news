## First in-utero stem cell therapy for fetal spina bifida repair is safe: study

- 原文链接: [First in-utero stem cell therapy for fetal spina bifida repair is safe: study](https://health.ucdavis.edu/news/headlines/first-ever-in-utero-stem-cell-therapy-for-fetal-spina-bifida-repair-is-safe-study-finds/2026/02)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47218743)

A new study shows that using stem cell therapy during fetal surgery for spina bifida is safe. This is the first time doctors have tried this treatment in unborn babies.

Spina bifida is a birth defect where the spine does not form properly, which can cause life-long problems with movement and organ function. In this treatment, doctors opened the mother’s uterus and placed a patch with stem cells, taken from donated placentas, over the baby’s exposed spinal cord. The hope is that the stem cells help the spinal tissue heal better than surgery alone. The study looked at the first six babies who got this treatment. All surgeries were successful, and there were no problems like infections or strange tissue growth. The wounds healed well, and MRI scans showed signs of improvement in all the babies. None of the babies needed extra surgery for fluid on the brain. Because the results were good, the FDA said the study can continue and include more patients. Researchers will follow these children for six years to see if they have better movement and fewer problems as they grow up.

In the comment section, many people were amazed by the progress in medicine. Some said this could help many families and children in the future. Others were careful, pointing out that this is only the first phase of the trial and that long-term results are not known yet. A few commenters wondered how expensive this treatment might be and if it will be available to everyone. Some people praised the use of stem cells from placentas because it avoids the ethical problems linked to other types of stem cells. There were questions about how the stem cells help the body heal and if they could be used for other birth defects. A few users shared personal stories about friends or family with spina bifida, saying this news gave them hope. Others worried about possible risks we have not seen yet, since the children are still very young. Some commenters hoped this research would lead to more treatments for unborn babies with other conditions. There were also people who thanked the families who joined the trial, saying they are helping future generations. Overall, people were interested, hopeful, but also careful about getting too excited too soon.

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47223620)

British Columbia will stop changing clocks twice a year and will use daylight saving time all year. This means people in the province will not need to “spring forward” or “fall back” anymore.

The government decided this after many people said they do not like changing the clocks. Changing time can make people feel tired or confused. Some studies show time changes can cause more car accidents and health problems. By staying on daylight time, there will be more light in the evenings, but it will be darker in the mornings, especially in winter. The decision depends on nearby places like Washington state and Oregon also stopping time changes, so everyone in the region stays on the same time. British Columbia wants to make sure businesses and travel are not affected by having a different time from neighbors. The new rule will not start right away; it will happen when the neighboring US states also switch. The government says most people want this change, based on a survey. Schools, work, and daily life may feel a bit different at first, but many expect to get used to it.

In the comments, some people are happy and say time changes are annoying and bad for health. Others worry about dark mornings in winter, especially for kids going to school. Some think it is better to stay on standard time all year instead of daylight time. A few people say it is strange to depend on what US states do, but they understand it helps with business and travel. Some remember stories about time changes causing problems, like missed appointments or tired drivers. Others point out that no system is perfect, but stopping the clock changes is a good step. - HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47223863)

This article is about a tool called Pianoterm, which lets you use a USB MIDI piano keyboard to run shell commands on Linux. The tool is for people who want to control their computer using piano keys instead of a regular keyboard.

Pianoterm lets you assign any shell command to any key on your MIDI keyboard. For example, you can make one key play or pause music, another key run your own script, or even control your media player. You set up Pianoterm by editing a simple config file in your home directory. In this file, you choose if a command runs when you press or release a key, and you match each MIDI key number with a command. The tool works with ALSA, which is a common Linux sound system. To set it up, you use commands like `aconnect` to find your MIDI device's port, and `aseqdump` to get the key numbers. To install Pianoterm, you clone the GitHub repo and build it with `make`. It needs a C compiler and ALSA tools.

## LFortran compiles fpm

- 原文链接: [LFortran compiles fpm](https://lfortran.org/blog/2026/02/lfortran-compiles-fpm/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47188014)

LFortran, a new Fortran compiler, can now build and run fpm, the Fortran Package Manager. This is the most complex project LFortran has worked on so far, and it uses many modern Fortran features.

The team began this work in April 2025 and finished in February 2026. Compiling fpm was hard because fpm is not just about math; it works with files, runs other programs, reads environment variables, and parses code. To make LFortran work with fpm, the team had to add support for many Fortran features, like classes, inheritance, arrays of classes, and more. They found and fixed many bugs and missing features during this process.

Now, LFortran is close to its first beta version. The team wants the compiler to handle medium-sized codes (about 10,000 lines) correctly, but right now, it works best with smaller codes (500–1,000 lines). They are fixing the remaining bugs, which are now smaller and easier to handle. The LFortran team also improved how they handle classes and inheritance by learning from Clang, a C++ compiler.

To make sure everything works, LFortran now runs all fpm tests automatically for every code change. They also added support for fpm’s main dependencies, like TOML and JSON libraries. LFortran finds some bugs in user code that other compilers might miss, like using arrays with the wrong shape.

LFortran compiles fpm faster than gfortran on a MacBook Pro—about 17 seconds versus 42 seconds. Most of the time is spent converting LLVM code to a binary. The team hopes to make this step up to 10 times faster in the future. They plan to keep improving the build system and optimize other slow parts.

The next steps are to fix more bugs, support bigger projects, and prepare for a public beta. They also want to add features like coarrays and parameterized types later. The team invites new contributors and thanks their sponsors and volunteers.

In the Hacker News comments, many users are impressed by LFortran’s progress and its ability to compile such a complex project. Some people remember when Fortran was seen as outdated, and they are happy to see modern tools like fpm and LFortran making the language more usable. Others talk about the importance of having a fast compiler with good error messages, and they hope LFortran can help Fortran stay relevant.

Some commenters mention that fpm is making Fortran easier to use, especially for new users, because it handles building and managing dependencies. A few users compare LFortran’s speed and features to other compilers like gfortran and ifort, noting that being faster or easier to use could attract more developers. Some are curious about the missing features (like coarrays) and ask when they will be added.

There are also suggestions about how LFortran could improve, such as better integration with build systems, more checks for user errors, or better multi-core compilation. A few people share stories about working with Fortran in the past and how much the ecosystem has changed. Overall, the comments are positive, with many people cheering on the LFortran team and saying they might try it out soon.
