Hello everyone, this is the 2026-03-23 episode of Hacker News Daily Podcast. Today, we bring you a mix of stories covering AI, open source tools, security, and the changing world of coding and science.

Let’s start with an article about using AI agents, specifically Claude Code, to improve old machine learning projects. The author wanted to see if an AI could update and optimize their research code while they did chores. The method, called “autoresearch,” lets the AI try small changes, test them quickly, and keep only the good ones. The AI operated in a safe, locked-down container, with short experiments to avoid overfitting.

Since the original medical data was missing, the AI used a dataset of Japanese woodblock prints. Over one day, the AI ran 42 experiments, accepting 13 changes and rolling back 29. The main metric, “mean rank” of image-text matches, dropped from 344.68 to 157.43—a big 54% improvement. The best gains came from bug fixes and tuning settings, while big creative changes mostly failed. The sandbox setup was key for safety, but also blocked some actions.

In the comments, people liked the hands-off approach and saw it as a way to let AI do boring work while freeing up human time. Some noted that AI is good at clear, simple tasks, but still struggles with deeper creativity. Many agreed that strict safety was smart, and wondered about letting the AI plan bigger changes. A few worried about overfitting and meaningless changes, but overall, many were inspired to try autoresearch for their own projects.

Next, another article showed how Claude Code helps speed up daily coding work. The author automated pull requests with a tool called `/git-pr`, making PRs faster and less distracting. Server builds were sped up by switching to SWC, letting the author test changes quickly. Claude Code’s preview feature let the AI check UI, so the author only needed to do a final review. By running code on multiple ports, the author could work on many branches at once with no conflicts.

With these changes, the author felt more like a manager of agents, not just a coder. Each fix revealed the next slow step, showing the “theory of constraints” in action. In the comments, readers liked the focus on removing friction and building better tools, not just adding AI. Some shared their own scripts for automating boring tasks. Others worried that too much AI might make developers forget important skills, and wondered if these tricks work for bigger teams. Many liked the idea of managing agents, but a few missed hands-on coding. Several pointed out that investing in good infrastructure makes the biggest difference over time.

Now, let’s look at a surprise from the hardware world. Someone shared a video showing an iPhone 17 Pro running a massive AI language model with 400 billion parameters at 0.6 tokens per second. Normally, such big models need huge servers. This demo amazed many, but also caused doubts: was the full model really on the phone, or was some running in the cloud? People discussed possible tricks like quantization or streaming. Some were excited about the future of on-device AI and privacy, while others pointed out that performance is slow and battery life is a worry. Many asked for technical details and hoped for more proof. Even with doubts, most agreed this is a sign of fast progress in mobile AI.

Changing topics, a technical article explained a hidden problem in regular expressions. When you search for all matches in a string, most regex engines get slow very quickly—often taking time that grows with the square of the text length. Even the fastest libraries, like Rust’s regex or Google’s RE2, have this issue if you want all matches, not just the first. The author’s own library, RE#, solves this with a two-pass method that is close to linear time, though it does not yet support all regex features.

In the comments, many were surprised by the O(n²) cost. Some shared that they had seen slow regex in practice but did not know the cause. There was debate about the trade-offs between speed and correctness. Some wanted better warnings in libraries, while others gave tips like using Aho-Corasick for simple cases. Many were interested in the new RE# approach, but wondered if missing features would slow adoption.

Next, we have a story about LocalStack, a tool for running AWS services locally. LocalStack has archived its main GitHub repo and now requires users to sign up for an account. The free version is now limited to non-commercial use, and the code is read-only. Many developers are unhappy, seeing this as a move away from open source. There are worries about depending on tools that can suddenly change or close. Some understand that the team needs to make money, and point to alternatives like Moto or MinIO, though these are not always as good. A few users asked about forks, while others discussed the risks of trusting closed tools. Most feel disappointed and are looking for better, more open alternatives.

In more positive open-source news, Dune3D is a new parametric 3D CAD app made for designing things like 3D-printed enclosures. The creator was not happy with FreeCAD and Solvespace, so built something new using ideas from PCB design software. Dune3D can import and export STEP files, uses Open CASCADE for geometry, and is released under GPL-3.0. In the comments, many are excited to see a new open CAD tool. Some hope it will be easier than FreeCAD and good for beginners. Others ask about scripting and plugins, and wonder if it can handle big projects. The creator explained that big changes are hard in existing projects, and building new tools is more fun. The community is supportive and curious to see how Dune3D grows.

Turning to security, Trivy, a popular security tool, suffered a major attack. An attacker got access to Trivy’s GitHub Actions, putting malware into 75 out of 76 tags. The malware stole secrets from CI/CD pipelines, sending them to a fake website or uploading them using the victim’s own GitHub account. Only tag 0.35.0 was safe. The attack was well-hidden, copying old commit info and fooling GitHub’s “Immutable” badge. The breach came from leftover access after a past incident.

In the comments, many users were shocked that a trusted tool was hit so hard. People discussed the dangers of using tags instead of commit SHAs in GitHub Actions. There was debate about whether GitHub should block force-pushing tags, and whether the “Immutable” feature is misleading. Some blamed the maintainers, others pointed to the growing threat of supply chain attacks. Many shared scripts to check if they were affected, and called for better education and tooling. The overall mood was serious, with many realizing the risks in automated build systems and open source supply chains.

Now, let’s look at a story from the world of prediction markets. On Polymarket, new accounts made big bets—about $70,000—that the US and Iran would agree to a ceasefire soon. Experts say these bets look like insider trading, since the accounts were new and the timing matched big news events. The odds for a ceasefire jumped from 6% to 24% in a few days.

People worry that prediction markets like Polymarket make it easy for those with secret information to profit from world events, especially wars. The site is also criticized for helping people profit from war and for spreading false news. In the comments, some say prediction markets could help spread real information, while others worry about ethics and the risk of cheating. There is debate about whether stronger rules are needed, and whether governments can use these markets to spot leaks. Some just find it interesting that “following the money” now means watching crypto wallets.

Switching gears, an article discussed how today’s AI helps science, but may also hold it back. AI is very good at making predictions and finding patterns, but not at creating new ideas or ways of thinking. The article uses the example of a map that is so detailed it matches the land exactly—sometimes what we need is not more detail, but a new way to look at things. Big scientific changes, like relativity or evolution, came from thinking differently, not just more data.

In the comments, many agree that AI speeds up what we already do, but is not good at finding new concepts. Some hope AI will surprise us in the future. Others warn that too much AI could make us less creative. There is agreement that both AI and humans are slow to accept new ideas, and that science funding often rewards safe work. Some suggest future AIs should be designed to look for things that don’t fit the usual patterns, but for now, AI is mainly a tool for working within current science.

Finally, let’s look at BIO, a new I/O co-processor made for the Baochip-1x. The creator wanted something like the Raspberry Pi’s PIO, but more open and simpler. The first try at copying PIO used too many hardware resources. BIO was then built using a RISC-V core, making it smaller and more flexible. BIO can handle complex I/O tasks, pause cores for timing, and has more memory than PIO. It is open-source and avoids patent issues. The author gives examples in assembly and C, and explains how to use standard tools like Zig and Rust.

In the comments, people suggested using Zig for better integration and asked about timing details. There was discussion about complex I/O, data rearrangement, and the need for better error handling. Overall, the community is excited about BIO’s flexibility and openness.
