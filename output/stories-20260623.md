## FUTO Swipe – A new swipe typing model

- 原文链接: [FUTO Swipe – A new swipe typing model](https://swipe.futo.tech/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48648619)

FUTO Swipe is a new open-source swipe typing system for Android that is fast, accurate, and works fully offline. The team built it because most good swipe keyboards today are either not private or use closed, unlicensed code.

To make FUTO Swipe, the team collected more than 1 million swipe samples from volunteers using their phones. They used this data to train models and make the swipe typing very good. The dataset is now open and free for anyone to use.

FUTO Swipe uses three types of models. The encoder works for any keyboard layout or language, but is not the most accurate. The ContextLM is a small language model that looks at word order to improve guesses. The decoder is focused on QWERTY English and is the most accurate, but needs special training data. Using all three, FUTO Swipe is almost as good as the best big tech keyboards, with very low error rates.

The models are small and run fast even on cheap phones. Training them was also cheap and used little energy. The project also released a C++ library that lets developers use these models to turn swipe paths into word guesses, using smart search with a dictionary.

FUTO Swipe is free to use under its own license, and the code is open for others to build on. The team hopes people will use it to make new apps or features, and they are working on a research paper to explain more details.

In the comments on Hacker News, some people are excited about an open, private swipe keyboard. They like that it works offline and does not collect user data. Others are happy that the dataset is open and can help researchers and developers build better keyboards.

However, some commenters wonder how easy it will be to use FUTO Swipe with other keyboard layouts or languages, since the best model is only for English QWERTY right now. There are also questions about the licenses—some users prefer more permissive licenses than GPL, and worry it might limit use in commercial projects.

A few developers are interested in porting it to other platforms, like iOS or desktop. Some people mention that open swipe keyboards have been missing on Android, and hope this will help fill the gap. Others talk about the technical details, like the small model size and fast speed, and are impressed it works well even on older phones.

Overall, the Hacker News community sees FUTO Swipe as a positive step for privacy, open source, and better typing tools. Some want more languages and layouts, while others are just happy to see a new, open choice for swipe typing.

## Printing Gaussian Splats

- 原文链接: [Printing Gaussian Splats](https://www.patreon.com/DanyBittel/posts/printing-splats-161333338)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48618481)

This article talks about how an artist used a special 3D printing method to make a model of an insect using “Gaussian splats.” The process started when someone from crysta.ai offered to print the artist’s digital insect model as a physical object.

To make the model, the artist prepared the data in a way that would work for 3D printing. They used spherical harmonics at level 0, which means they chose a simple way to show color, since the printer cannot print colors that change depending on where you look from. The artist also used “linear space” for the files, hoping the print would look more realistic, but this made the process harder for the printing company.

The method involves changing the “Gaussian splat” data into small cubes called voxels, like the blocks in Minecraft. Each voxel can have different colors and levels of transparency. A special 3D printer prints these cubes layer by layer, using different inks, to slowly build the final object. The finished piece looks like a piece of crystal or amber, with the insect “trapped” inside.

The artist was happy with the result, but noticed the print looked a bit too brown and dark, maybe because of the way the files were prepared. Some small mistakes, or “splat artifacts,” could still be seen. The fine details, like the fur on the insect, were also hard to get right because making thin, see-through parts is tricky in 3D printing. When you shine light through the model, you can see inside, which looks interesting, almost like seeing organs.

The printing company is still improving their tools. They have made a test version of a new editor, which lets you move and scale your model before printing. The artist wishes for a better way to preview how the model will look as voxels, and for the ability to import models from other programs, like MagicaVoxel, to make it easier for more artists to use.

In the comments, one person said the print looked beautiful and asked about the price. The artist replied that it’s expensive right now—about $143 for a small 30mm cube—but prices might drop if more people order prints. Another commenter just said the project looked amazing. The general feeling in the comments is excitement about the technology, but some worry about the high cost. People are also interested in new features and easier ways to use the service. Some hope the process will become more open for other artists and cheaper as the company grows. Overall, readers are impressed by the mix of art and modern 3D printing tech.

## Show HN: TikZ Editor – WYSIWYG editor for figures in LaTeX

- 原文链接: [Show HN: TikZ Editor – WYSIWYG editor for figures in LaTeX](https://tikz.dev/editor/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48645437)

TikZ Editor is a new tool that lets you edit TikZ figures for LaTeX using a WYSIWYG (what you see is what you get) editor. It works in your browser or as a desktop app, and is free and open source.

With this editor, you can open your LaTeX files and directly edit figures by dragging, resizing, or adding shapes like nodes, arrows, circles, and rectangles. The TikZ code updates right away as you move things, so you do not need to write or change code by hand. The editor keeps your original formatting, such as spaces and line breaks, and highlights errors if something is wrong. You can use tools to snap, align, group, and distribute elements. There are rulers, guides, zoom, and a magnifying glass to help you fine-tune your work.

The app supports most TikZ features, including loops with \foreach, node labels, pins, and paths that connect to anchors. You can work on many figures at once and open a whole paper to see all its figures. The source panel has syntax highlighting, code folding, and lets you pick colors or change numbers easily. You can also import and export files in formats like SVG, PNG, PDF, and even from PowerPoint or Ipe.

The desktop version adds an AI assistant if you have OpenAI Codex, so you can ask for help editing your figures. The app is written in TypeScript and Rust and uses modern libraries to handle TeX parsing, color conversion, and text rendering. It keeps your code readable and only changes the part you edit, so your style stays the same.

On Hacker News, many users are excited about finally having a true graphical editor for TikZ. Some say this will save them a lot of time when making diagrams for papers, as editing by hand is slow and tricky. Others like that it keeps the code clean and does not force a new format. A few users mention that past tools like TikZiT were limited, and this app feels much more complete.

Some people are curious about how well the parser works for complex or unusual TikZ code. They want to know if the editor can handle custom macros and tricky LaTeX setups. Others are interested in the AI features and wonder if it really helps with figure editing or just gets in the way. There are questions about how well the app works with big documents and if it can export perfect figures for publication.

A few users are worried about privacy, especially when using the web version with sensitive research files. Others wish there were even more features, like better math support or tighter integration with Overleaf. Some developers praise the use of modern tools like Tauri and CodeMirror, while others suggest ways to make the app faster or more robust.

Overall, the response is positive, with many users happy to see an open source project that solves a long-standing problem in LaTeX editing. People share ideas for improvements and hope the project keeps growing. Some even ask how they can contribute or support the creator. The community seems eager to try the tool and see how it helps with their next paper or project.

## F3

- 原文链接: [F3](https://github.com/future-file-format/f3)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48647799)

The article talks about F3, a new open-source tool for working with flash memory. It explains that flash memory, like USB drives and SD cards, can sometimes be fake—they say they have more space than they really do. F3 helps people check if their flash drives are real or fake.

The article describes how F3 works. It writes test files to the flash drive to fill it up. Then it reads back the files to see if the data is correct. If some files are missing or have errors, the drive is probably fake. F3 is fast and simple to use. It works on Linux and Mac. The article gives examples of commands. It shows how to start a test, wait for it to finish, and read the results. The results will tell you if the drive is good or bad. Many people have found fake drives online, so this tool is helpful. The article also mentions that the tool is free and open for anyone to use or change.

In the comment section, some people share stories about buying fake USB drives online. One person says they lost important files because of a bad drive. Another person is happy that F3 is open source and easy to use. Some users compare F3 to another tool called H2testw, which works on Windows. A few commenters warn that fake drives can be dangerous, especially if you put important files on them. Others like that F3 is fast and works on different systems. Someone suggests always testing new drives before using them. Another person says it’s sad that fake drives are still a problem. A few people ask for a version that works on Windows. Some hope for more tools like this. Overall, most commenters think F3 is a great tool and are glad it exists.

## The worthlessness of Vitamin D is mildly exaggerated

- 原文链接: [The worthlessness of Vitamin D is mildly exaggerated](https://dynomight.net/vitamin-d/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48647486)

This article looks at whether vitamin D supplements really help people. For years, many thought vitamin D was like a magic pill for good health, but new studies now say it might only help if you are very low on vitamin D.

The writer explains how vitamin D works in the body. It helps your gut take in calcium from food, which is important for healthy bones. If you do not get enough vitamin D, your body takes calcium from your bones, making them weak. Vitamin D comes from sunlight and food, and your body turns it into different forms, some of which can be stored for later use.

Many studies found that people with more vitamin D seemed to be healthier, with less cancer, heart disease, and other problems. But these were just connections, and later tests where people took vitamin D did not show it actually caused these good effects. Three big trials (WHI, VITAL, D-Health) gave people vitamin D and checked for less disease or death, but found no strong benefit. Sometimes, taking vitamin D even led to more kidney stones.

Some problems with these studies are explained. Many people already had enough vitamin D before the trials. Also, some people in the "no vitamin D" group still took it on their own. This makes it hard to see any true effect. The trials also did not focus on people with very low vitamin D, who might benefit most.

The writer says that while vitamin D is not a magic fix, it still might help people with low or low-normal levels. Evolution gives a clue: people living outdoors in Africa have high vitamin D levels, and humans even evolved lighter skin to make more vitamin D in less sun. This hints that higher vitamin D might be useful.

The article also explains that taking a small pill every day is low-cost, and even a tiny benefit (like living a few months longer) could be worth it. Most people already get some vitamin D from fortified foods like milk or cereal.

In the comment section, many readers agree that vitamin D is not a miracle, but some still take it “just in case.” Some say they feel better or have fewer colds when they take vitamin D, but others see no difference. One person points out that spending time outdoors is the best way to get vitamin D, but this is hard for people living far north or staying indoors. Some worry about overdose or side effects, but most agree that normal supplement doses are safe. A few readers discuss the problems with big health studies, like not focusing on people who might need vitamin D the most. Others say that food fortification is probably a good idea, since deficiency is rare in countries that do this. One commenter says, "If vitamin D helps even a little and costs almost nothing, why not take it?" But another replies, "If it does almost nothing, why bother?" In the end, most comments are open-minded but not very excited—people want better studies, but for now, vitamin D looks helpful only if your levels are low.
