## Fast16: Cyberweapon that predates Stuxnet by five years

- 原文链接: [Fast16: Cyberweapon that predates Stuxnet by five years](https://hackingpassion.com/fast16-pre-stuxnet-cyber-sabotage/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47913855)

The article explains that fast16 is a cyberweapon made in 2005, five years before the famous Stuxnet worm. Unlike Stuxnet, which destroyed machines, fast16 quietly changed math results in scientific and engineering software, making outputs look normal but actually wrong.

Researchers found fast16 after 21 years, hidden in a Windows file named `svcmgmt.exe`. Inside, it had a Lua scripting engine, which was rare for malware back then, and a driver called `fast16.sys`. The malware only ran on old single-core computers and had three parts: a carrier that spreads fast16, a worm that moves through weak network shares, and a kernel driver that tampers with floating-point math in certain programs. It targeted software used for nuclear simulations, building safety, and water modeling, such as LS-DYNA (used by Iranian scientists), PKPM (used in Chinese nuclear reactor analysis), and MOHID (for environmental modeling).

Fast16 was careful to avoid machines with popular antivirus programs at the time. It would secretly spread through networks, copying itself to other computers. The kernel driver would wait until someone logged in before starting to corrupt math routines, but only in programs built with the Intel C++ compiler. It changed calculations without touching the original files, so results looked fine but were incorrect.

The article also links fast16 to the NSA’s Equation Group, based on leaked files that told operators to leave fast16 alone if they found it. Code in fast16 had old Unix version control marks, hinting it was made by experienced, institutional programmers, not hobbyists.

For 21 years, almost no antivirus detected fast16, even while it sat in public malware databases. Researchers only realized its purpose recently. The article ends with warnings: anyone using old engineering software should check results on a clean, separate computer to avoid being tricked by fast16.

Hacker News commenters had a lot to say. Some were shocked that such clever malware could stay hidden for so long, and worried there may be more like it out there. Others discussed how targeting math routines is especially sneaky, as it’s hard to notice wrong results in complex simulations. A few users debated who could have made it, with many pointing to state actors like the NSA, but some saying the evidence isn’t 100% clear.

Several commenters praised the technical skill behind fast16, noting the use of Lua scripting and kernel-level tampering. Others highlighted the “cluster munition” design, where the malware can carry different payloads, and found it both impressive and scary. A few people wondered about the real-world effects—did fast16 actually mess up any big projects, or was it mostly a spy tool?

Some warned that the same tricks could be used in the future, especially as old malware sits undetected for years. A couple of readers questioned why antivirus software missed fast16 for so long, and whether current security tools are any better. There was also debate about ethics, with some feeling uneasy about governments making such powerful and secret attacks.

Finally, a few commenters pointed out the lesson for everyone: always double-check important calculations and never fully trust results from any one system, especially in sensitive work like nuclear science or big engineering projects.

Butterflies in North America are disappearing, and the Western Monarch is in trouble. The article says that butterfly numbers, especially Monarchs, have dropped a lot over the past few decades.

Scientists track Monarchs by counting them at special spots in California during winter. In the 1980s, there were millions, but now there are only thousands left. The article says loss of habitat is a big reason. People build homes and farms where butterflies once lived. There is also less milkweed, which Monarchs need to lay eggs. Pesticides and climate change make things worse, as hot weather and chemicals hurt butterflies and their food.

The article explains how Monarchs migrate long distances, flying from the western U.S. to the California coast. Many don’t survive the trip anymore. Some groups are planting milkweed and making gardens to help. Scientists use tags and community science projects to follow their journeys and learn more.

In the comments, some people are sad and worried about the Monarchs. They say butterflies are important for nature and show if the environment is healthy. Others share stories about seeing fewer butterflies in their gardens. Some blame pesticides and big farms for the decline. A few think climate change is the biggest problem. Some commenters say planting native plants and avoiding chemicals can help. One person warns that planting the wrong kind of milkweed can actually harm Monarchs. Others hope that with more people helping, the butterflies might come back. Some users wonder if it is too late, but many still want to try to save them.

## SWE-bench Verified no longer measures frontier coding capabilities

- 原文链接: [SWE-bench Verified no longer measures frontier coding capabilities](https://openai.com/index/why-we-no-longer-evaluate-swe-bench-verified/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47910388)

OpenAI says that their coding benchmark, SWE-bench Verified, is no longer a good way to measure how advanced code-writing AI models are. They explain this is because the test has become “contaminated,” meaning that AI models have seen the test problems and answers during training, making their scores unreliable.

The article says that after some early progress, model scores on SWE-bench Verified stopped improving much. OpenAI found two main problems: First, many test cases are flawed—over half of the hard problems reject correct solutions because the tests are too strict or too loose. For example, one problem asks for a fix, but the test only accepts a change using an exact function name, so other correct answers fail. Second, the benchmarks come from public open-source projects, so AI models can find the exact answers in their training data. This means the models might just “remember” the answer, not really solve the problem. OpenAI shows that all top AI models, like GPT-5.2, Claude Opus, and Gemini, can sometimes give verbatim answers, including code patches and even exact comments, just from the problem’s ID or a small hint.

OpenAI explains that this “contamination” makes it hard to know if improved scores mean smarter models, or just more memorization. They also note that making perfect test cases is difficult—automated tests often reject good solutions or require extra steps not mentioned in the problem. They tried to fix these issues by reviewing problems with experts, but many issues remain.

As a result, OpenAI has stopped reporting scores for SWE-bench Verified and recommends using a newer benchmark, SWE-bench Pro, which is less contaminated. They also suggest that future benchmarks should use private, original problems not found in public data, and should be reviewed by humans, even though this takes more work.

People in the comments have many views. Some agree with OpenAI and think public benchmarks will always get contaminated as more AI models train on web data. Others say this was obvious from the start, and that OpenAI should have known better. A few think the real problem is that code-writing AI is already close to the limit of what these tasks can measure, so new tests are needed anyway. Some users say automated tests are always tricky—even for humans—because many software bugs don’t have one “right” answer, and real projects often accept many fixes.

There are comments about the need for private benchmarks, but some worry this will make it harder for outsiders and small companies to check AI progress. A few are annoyed that OpenAI made a big push for SWE-bench Verified, only to drop it soon after. Some argue that “contamination” will always be a problem unless training data is tightly controlled, and that this is a growing issue for all AI benchmarks, not just coding ones. Others point out that in real life, developers often Google for answers, so maybe “remembering” is not so bad. Some hope OpenAI and others will share how they build new, cleaner benchmarks, and suggest that open collaboration is key for real progress.

## Clay PCB Tutorial

- 原文链接: [Clay PCB Tutorial](https://feministhackerspaces.cargo.site/Clay-PCB-Tutorial)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47911350)

This article is about making printed circuit boards (PCBs) using natural clay instead of normal plastic and metal materials. The team wanted to find a more ethical way to create hardware, using local, natural, and low-impact materials.

They started by trying to build a microcontroller board for an ATmega328P chip, which is the same chip used in Arduino Uno boards. They had some broken Arduino boards but the chips still worked, so they decided to reuse them. The next challenge was to design a circuit that could take different sensor inputs and control outputs like LEDs or motors.

To make the PCB shape, they used a 3D-printed stamp and recycled plastic. They learned that clay shrinks when it dries and fires, so they had to plan for about 5% shrinkage. For the base, they picked porcelain (china clay) because it’s good for electronics and is strong and insulating.

They wanted to avoid using lots of electricity, so instead of firing the clay in an electric kiln, they learned from a local potter how to fire clay using a wood fire outside. They collected wild clay, cleaned it using a kitchen colander, and mixed it with water to make it soft. After shaping the clay into hexagons with a tile cutter, they pressed in the circuit pattern with their stamp.

The boards had to dry for at least a day outside, and sometimes longer inside. When dry, they sanded the boards and painted the circuit lines with a special silver paint made from recycled jewelry silver. They needed to be careful, because metals like tin or copper melt or lose conductivity in the fire, but silver works well.

After painting, they fired the boards in a wood fire for about 20 minutes, reaching about 700°C, and quickly cooled them in water to test their strength. If the boards survived, they were ready for use as PCBs.

In the Hacker News comments, some people found the idea very creative and liked the focus on ethical, local materials. Others pointed out that using silver and gold is still expensive and not always ethical, so it’s not a perfect solution. A few were skeptical about the reliability and durability of clay PCBs compared to traditional ones, especially for real electronics work. Some liked that the project is open-source and artistic, and said it’s a good way to learn about electronics, materials, and history. Others shared ideas for improving the process, like using different clays or firing methods. There was also debate about how practical this is for large-scale projects, but many agreed it’s a fun and inspiring approach for small, ethical, and creative hardware experiments.
