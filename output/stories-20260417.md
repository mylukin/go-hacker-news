Claude Design is a new tool from Anthropic that helps people make visual things like designs, slides, and prototypes with help from AI. It uses their latest AI model and is now available for paid users to try.

The main idea is to let both experts and beginners create and change designs by just describing what they want. Designers can try many ideas quickly, and people without design skills can still share their visions. You start by telling Claude what you need, and the AI makes a first draft. Then, you can change things by chatting, writing comments, editing directly, or using sliders to adjust how things look. Claude can also use your company’s colors and style automatically if you give it access.

Teams are using Claude Design for making prototypes, wireframes, pitch decks, marketing images, and even advanced things like 3D or voice-powered designs. You can add files, upload pictures, or let Claude read your code and design files to match your brand. Designs can be shared privately or with your team, and you can export them as PDFs, PowerPoints, Canva files, or HTML. When a design is finished, Claude can make a package for developers to use, so the design goes straight to code with no extra work.

The tool also works well with other products like Canva, and it will get more integrations soon. Some teams say it saves a lot of time, turning weeks of work into just one meeting. Others mention it makes hard things, like interactive prototypes, much easier and faster.

## Measuring Claude 4.7's tokenizer costs

- 原文链接: [Measuring Claude 4.7's tokenizer costs](https://www.claudecodecamp.com/p/i-measured-claude-4-7-s-new-tokenizer-here-s-what-it-costs-you)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47807006)

This article looks at how much more it costs to use Claude 4.7’s new tokenizer compared to Claude 4.6. The author tested both versions to see how many tokens the same text would use, since Anthropic said the new tokenizer would use about 1.0 to 1.35 times as many tokens.

The author measured real files and found that Claude 4.7 actually uses up to 1.47 times more tokens with technical documents, and about 1.45 times more for a CLAUDE.md file. Most real-world content sits at the high end of Anthropic’s estimate, not the middle. This means you hit your token limit sooner, and each prompt costs more even though the price per token is the same.

The author ran two sets of tests: one with real content like prompts, blog posts, git logs, and code; another with synthetic samples like English, code, JSON, and even Chinese and Japanese text. For English and code, the new tokenizer uses about 1.3 to 1.45 times more tokens. Non-English content, like Chinese and Japanese, barely changed.

Why did Anthropic change the tokenizer? They say it helps the model follow instructions more closely, especially detailed or strict ones. The author tested this by running 20 prompts designed to check how exactly the model follows instructions. Claude 4.7 passed 90% of strict prompts, compared to 85% for 4.6—a small but real improvement.

But this improvement comes at a cost. The author did the math for a long coding session and found that using Claude 4.7 raises the total session cost by 20–30%. For people who pay for Claude by tokens, or who hit token limits, this means you get less use for the same money.

The article also explains how this affects prompt caching. With more tokens, cache reads and writes use more tokens too, so those costs go up. If you compare logs or costs before and after switching to Claude 4.7, you’ll see a sudden jump.

Some readers argue that, since most input is cached and cache reads are much cheaper, the real dollar cost doesn’t change much unless you often hit cache misses. Others say the key problem is for people with strict token quotas, since you’ll hit your limits faster even if the price per token is the same.

Commenters also discuss whether the small improvement in instruction following is worth the higher cost. Some think +5% is a good trade for people who need precise answers. Others feel the improvement is too small and wish Anthropic had been clearer about the real token increase, especially for code and English-heavy work.

A few people point out that technical users might be surprised, since the top of the “1.0–1.35x” range is more common than expected. There’s some frustration that the most common use cases—like coding—are hit the hardest. Others are glad for the technical detail, since it helps them plan budgets and understand why their costs changed.

Overall, many agree that it’s important to measure real-world impact and not just trust documentation ranges. Some plan to adjust their workflows, while a few worry that rising token costs could add up if this trend continues.

## All 12 moonwalkers had "lunar hay fever" from dust smelling like gunpowder (2018)

- 原文链接: [All 12 moonwalkers had "lunar hay fever" from dust smelling like gunpowder (2018)](https://www.esa.int/Science_Exploration/Human_and_Robotic_Exploration/The_toxic_side_of_the_Moon)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47808913)

The article explains that all 12 Apollo astronauts who walked on the Moon had “lunar hay fever” after breathing in Moon dust, which made their throats sore and eyes water. Lunar dust is very sharp and toxic, and scientists are now studying how dangerous it could be for people.

The dust on the Moon is made of silicate, like the dust miners face on Earth, which can cause lung problems. But Moon dust is even worse because it is not smooth; it is spiky and sharp, almost like tiny bits of glass. The low gravity on the Moon means these small dust pieces can float around longer, making it easier for people to breathe them in. The Apollo astronauts found that the dust stuck to their suits, got everywhere, and even smelled like burnt gunpowder inside their spacecraft. Some of their hay fever symptoms lasted for days.

Scientists are worried because on Earth, small dust particles can slowly be cleaned away by wind and water, but not on the Moon. There, the dust stays sharp and dangerous. Sunlight also charges the dust, making it float above the surface and get into machines and lungs more easily. Tests with fake Moon dust show it can destroy lung and brain cells after long exposure. ESA is running tests with a similar dust from Earth, but making it is hard because grinding it makes the dust less sharp. Even so, Moon dust could also help future explorers, because it can be used to make bricks for shelter or to get oxygen for breathing.

In the Hacker News comments, some people are surprised to learn the dust is so harmful. Others point out that the Apollo missions were lucky not to have bigger health problems from it. A few say we need much better spacesuits and air filters for future Moon trips. Some users compare the Moon dust problem to issues with fine dust in mining or building work on Earth. There are comments about how difficult it will be to keep Moon bases clean, with dust getting everywhere and even hurting electronics. Others wonder if robots could help clean up or if astronauts could change their suits outside before entering habitats. Some are excited about making bricks and oxygen from the dust, but warn that safety must come first. A few share worries about what happens if someone has a strong allergic reaction or lung issue on the Moon. Finally, people agree that understanding Moon dust is key before humans can live there safely for a long time.

## A simplified model of Fil-C

- 原文链接: [A simplified model of Fil-C](https://www.corsix.org/content/simplified-model-of-fil-c)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47810872)

Fil-C is a new way to make C and C++ programs memory safe by changing how pointers work. Instead of letting pointers cause bugs or crashes, Fil-C adds extra checks and uses a garbage collector.

The main idea is that every pointer variable gets a new helper variable called an AllocationRecord. This record keeps information about the memory the pointer points to, like where it starts, how long it is, and some hidden space for more checks. When a pointer moves or is copied, its AllocationRecord moves with it. When you use functions like malloc or free, Fil-C replaces them with special versions that also manage AllocationRecords and extra memory for hidden checks.

When you read or write to a pointer, Fil-C adds code to check if you are inside the correct memory area. It also checks that you do not go past the end, and that you use the pointer in the right way. If a pointer points to another pointer, Fil-C uses the hidden memory (invisible_bytes) to keep track of the new AllocationRecord for the pointer inside memory, not just for local variables.

Fil-C uses a garbage collector to clean up AllocationRecords that are not used anymore. This means if you forget to free memory, the garbage collector will do it for you. The garbage collector also helps when the program is more complex, like when you take addresses of local variables and use them after their normal lifetime. Sometimes, Fil-C will move these variables to the heap so the garbage collector can manage them.

Some standard functions, like memmove, are tricky because they can copy pointers around. Fil-C uses rules to make sure it copies both the visible and invisible parts in a safe way, but this means copying memory one byte at a time is not always the same as copying it all at once.

The article also talks about problems with threads and function pointers, and how Fil-C adds more checks and metadata to keep things safe. There are ideas to make Fil-C faster or use less memory, but safety is always the main goal. The author says Fil-C is most useful if you have a lot of old C or C++ code that is not safe, or if you want to find memory bugs like with AddressSanitizer, or if you need safe compile-time evaluation in languages like Zig.

In the comments, some people liked the clear explanation and said it helps them understand how Fil-C works. Others worried about the performance cost, since all these checks and the garbage collector can make programs much slower. A few mentioned that adding a garbage collector makes C/C++ feel more like Java or Go, and wondered if it is better just to rewrite code in those languages. Some liked that Fil-C can catch pointer bugs even better than tools like AddressSanitizer. Others were unsure about how Fil-C would work with tricky C/C++ features, like pointer arithmetic or unions. There were questions about how well Fil-C works with existing code, or if it would break some old patterns. Some thought the idea of tracking pointer “provenance” was interesting, and liked that Fil-C gives a real example of how it can matter for safety. Finally, a few people asked if Fil-C could be used in teaching, to help students learn about memory safety in C/C++.

## Show HN: Smol machines – subsecond coldstart, portable virtual machines

- 原文链接: [Show HN: Smol machines – subsecond coldstart, portable virtual machines](https://github.com/smol-machines/smolvm)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47808268)

Smolvm is a command-line tool that lets you build and run small, fast, and portable Linux virtual machines (VMs) on macOS and Linux. The main idea is to give you easy hardware isolation for any program, with very quick startup (under 200 ms) and the ability to pack a whole VM into a single file to move between systems.

With smolvm, you can run programs in a real VM that is separated from your main system. By default, there is no network access, but you can allow it for trusted tasks. You can limit network to only certain hosts, making it safer to run untrusted code. The tool lets you build portable executables: for example, you can pack Python into a self-contained binary and run it anywhere without installing Python on the host. Machines can be persistent for development, so anything you install inside stays after restarting. You can also use your host’s SSH agent inside the VM, so private keys do not leave your system. Smolvm supports describing environments in an easy TOML config file (called a Smolfile), which makes setups repeatable and shareable.

Technically, each VM gets its own kernel using Hypervisor.framework on macOS or KVM on Linux. Smolvm uses libkrun and a custom kernel for fast startup, low memory use, and good isolation. You can adjust CPU and memory if you want. Compared to containers, QEMU, or Firecracker, smolvm aims for a mix of speed, isolation, and portability, with native support for macOS and an SDK for embedding in other tools.

People in the comments are excited about how fast and portable smolvm is. Some like that it starts up quicker than normal VMs and has better isolation than containers. Others point out that being able to pack and move machines as single files is very handy, especially for sharing or deploying apps. Some users compare it to Firecracker or Kata containers, and see it as simpler to use on laptops or desktops, especially on macOS. A few wonder about security details and how well it integrates with existing workflows. There is some interest in using smolvm for CI/CD, running untrusted code, or teaching. Some users ask about Windows support or whether GUI apps could run inside. Others warn about possible limits, like volume mounts and network controls, but agree these are fine for many tasks. Overall, most see smolvm as a helpful new tool for developers who want strong isolation with very little setup or wait time.

## Middle schooler finds coin from Troy in Berlin

- 原文链接: [Middle schooler finds coin from Troy in Berlin](https://www.thehistoryblog.com/archives/75848)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47806484)

A 13-year-old in Berlin found a very old coin from Troy, which is now in a museum and is the first Greek antiquity found in the city. The coin was made between 281 and 261 B.C. in Ilion, the Greek name for Troy, and shows the goddess Athena on both sides. The coin is small, made of bronze, and was likely not valuable money, but may have been kept as a charm or used in a ritual.

Archaeologists checked the place where the coin was found and discovered it was a historical site with items from many times, including the Bronze Age, Iron Age, Roman times, and the medieval period. This makes it likely the coin arrived in Berlin hundreds or even thousands of years ago, not recently. Historians think the coin may have traveled to Northern Europe through ancient trade routes like the Amber Road, where people traded goods for amber from the north. The coin’s low value means it was probably not used for a big trade but might have been a souvenir or an offering for the dead.

The city of Troy, called Troy VIII during this coin’s time, had many visitors and a famous temple to the goddess Athena. Even Alexander the Great visited and gave offerings there. The city was attacked many times in history and was finally destroyed by the Romans in 85 B.C. The coin is special because it helps show how ancient objects could travel far from their home.

In the comments, one person explains that Ilion is the Greek name for Troy and connects it to the older Hittite name Wilusa. They mention the Romans liked the story of Troy and said their hero Aeneas came from there. Another point is that the coin might have been carried by travelers or as a souvenir, not for trade. The commenter also talks about different routes that connected southern Europe to the north, and how ancient people moved and traded. Some people think the coin’s journey is a fun mystery and shows how items can move over time. Others are interested in the mix of history found at the site, from many different periods. There is excitement that a young person made such a rare find, and some hope more discoveries like this will help us understand ancient trade and travel.
