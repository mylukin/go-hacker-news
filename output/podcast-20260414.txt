Hello everyone, this is the 2026-04-14 episode of Hacker News Daily Podcast. Today, we have a full lineup of stories for developers, tech fans, and anyone who loves new ideas.

First, let’s talk about Claude Code Routines. This is a feature that lets you save and run tasks in the cloud, even when your laptop is off. You set up a routine with a prompt, your code, and connect it to other tools. You can choose to run it on a schedule, by API call, or when something happens in GitHub, like a new pull request. For example, you could make a routine that checks new GitHub issues every night, tags them, assigns people, and posts a summary to Slack. Or it could check alerts from your monitoring tool and even open a pull request with a possible fix. 

Setting up is easy—you can use the web, the desktop app, or the command-line tool. The web and desktop app let you add all sorts of triggers, but the CLI is just for scheduled tasks so far. Each routine runs as your own user account and is not shared with teammates. You can filter triggers by author, label, or branch, and routines run in cloud environments that you can control, including network access and variables.

There are limits: routines count against your daily usage, unless you pay for more. In the comments, many users liked how easy it makes automation, especially for code reviews or handling alerts. People see value in running tasks even when their own computer is off. Some worry about security, since routines can push code or send messages as your user. Others wish routines could be shared with teams. A few compare this to GitHub Actions or Zapier, but say Claude routines are easier for coding tasks. The main advice: start small, be careful with permissions, and see what it can do for you.

Next, a big win for music fans—a huge collection of rare concert tapes is now online at the Internet Archive, thanks to a Chicago fan named Aadam Jacobs. He recorded over 10,000 concerts since the 1980s, with bands like Nirvana, Sonic Youth, R.E.M., and many more. Volunteers help digitize these old cassettes, clean up the sound, and organize everything. So far, 2,500 tapes are online, including rare shows like a Nirvana concert from 1989 before they were famous.

People on Hacker News love the idea of saving music that could have been lost. They share stories about finding rare music and how much it means to them. Some worry about copyright and wonder if it’s legal to share these tapes, while others note that many bands liked people taping shows, especially in the punk scene. There’s also talk about how hard it is to save old tapes, and how much work goes into making the music sound good. Many hope more people will share their own collections, and most are just happy to listen to the concerts or discover new bands.

Moving on, let’s look at the Orange Pi 6 Plus, a new single-board computer with strong hardware. It has a 12-core ARM CPU, Mali GPU, special NPU for AI, 16GB RAM, and two fast network ports. The hardware is impressive, but the software side is still rough. The reviewer built their own Debian Linux image, which took effort—there were problems with booting, resizing, and getting the GPU and NPU to work. The GPU works well after some work, but the NPU is still hard to use and mostly for vision tasks. The board is fast but uses more power than a Raspberry Pi, and the fan is noisy.

In the comments, users are split. Some are excited by the price and specs, calling it a “Raspberry Pi killer.” Others warn that software support is not as good as on the Raspberry Pi, and worry about power use and noise. People like the dual network ports for home labs, but say they will wait for better software. Tips are shared for building custom images, and some hope future updates will make things easier.

Now, OpenAI is rolling out new tools for cybersecurity experts, including a special AI model called GPT-5.4-Cyber. This model is trained for defense, to help find and fix software problems faster. Only trusted and verified users can use it, with strong identity checks to keep attackers out. OpenAI has also released tools like Codex Security, which scans code for bugs. The goal is to give real defenders strong tools, not just big companies, and to help keep the internet safe.

Comments are mixed. Many are excited, saying small teams now have better defense tools. Some worry that attackers might still get access, or that strict checks could block good researchers. People debate if OpenAI can really decide who is a “real” defender, and some want more tools to be open source. There’s concern about an arms race between attackers and defenders, but hope that software will get safer in the long run.

Switching gears, we have a story about the fifth normal form (5NF) in database design. The writer says 5NF is often taught in a confusing way, but it’s simple if you start from real business needs. There are two main patterns: the triangle (like friends, ice cream brands, and flavors), and the star (like concerts, musicians, and instruments). If you design tables based on what the business needs, you don’t have to worry too much about high normal forms.

In the comments, most users agree that the usual Wikipedia example is confusing. Many say that in practice, 3NF or 4NF is enough for most databases, and that real examples with music or ice cream are easier to follow. Some talk about when to use composite keys or synthetic IDs, and a few remind us that sometimes it’s fine to break the rules for speed. The main advice: focus on real needs, not just the textbook.

Next, Google has added a new feature to Chrome called “Skills.” This lets you save your favorite AI prompts and use them with one click. For example, you can check recipes, compare products, or scan documents for key points with saved prompts. There’s a Skills library with ready-made tools, and you can change or make your own. Skills are rolling out to Chrome users in English on Mac, Windows, and ChromeOS.

Many users are excited—they say this saves time and makes AI more useful in daily browsing. Some worry about privacy and want more control or support for other languages. Developers are interested in using Skills for teamwork or coding tasks, but some hope the tools don’t get too complex or cause “prompt fatigue.” Overall, people like saving time but want strong privacy and user controls.

Another story is about a person trying to get Flock Safety to delete data about them and their car. Flock runs cameras that record license plates for police and neighborhoods. The person used California’s privacy law to ask Flock to delete their data, but Flock said they can’t—they only process the data for their customers, who own it. The writer thinks Flock should still be responsible and is thinking about legal help.

Many commenters agree, saying Flock is dodging responsibility and that it’s hard to stop this kind of data collection. Some say the law is not clear or strong enough. A few defend Flock, saying they only follow customer orders. Most agree that people should have more control over their own data collected in public.

Now for a lighter but serious topic: toilets in space. The article explains how hard it is to manage waste without gravity. Early toilets were just bags and tubes and could get messy and smelly. Even now, astronauts use bags for solid waste, and the International Space Station recycles urine but not feces. Mars missions will need toilets that work for years and stay clean, which is a big challenge. NASA is even testing ways to roast waste into odorless blocks that can help shield astronauts from radiation.

Hacker News readers laugh about how toilets are still a problem after all these years. Some share stories from NASA, or suggest new ways to handle waste. There are jokes, but also serious talk about the health risks and how much work goes into this basic need. It’s a good reminder that space travel is not all glamour.

Next, we have guide.world, a website that collects good travel writing from around the internet. Instead of regular tips, it shares personal stories and deep looks at places all over the world. The links are clean and easy to browse, by country. There is no search or sorting, just a list. Stories come from famous writers and travelers, and cover both popular and rare destinations.

People like the simple list without ads, and share more travel blogs or stories to add. Some want ways to sort or search, or to add their own links. Many say that first-person stories give a better feel for a place than normal guides. Some wish for more pictures or maps, but others like the plain, text-only style. Most just enjoy “traveling” by reading, and hope the site keeps growing.

Finally, there’s Plain, a new full-stack Python web framework, based on Django, built for both humans and AI agents. It uses Python 3.13+, PostgreSQL, Jinja2, htmx, and Tailwind CSS. The code is more strict and typed than Django, and it comes with over 30 built-in packages for things like auth, jobs, email, and admin. There are tools for both people and code-writing bots, with ways to trigger skills and search docs from the terminal. It’s open source and changing fast.

In the comments, some like the fresh ideas and focus on AI agents and clear code. Others wonder why we need another framework, and worry about dropping MySQL and SQLite support. Some like all the built-in tools, while others fear it could get too complex. There are questions about how stable it is and how well it works with Django code. Many are curious to try it for new apps, especially where agent support or clear code matter.
