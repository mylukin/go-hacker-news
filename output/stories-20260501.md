## Credit cards are vulnerable to brute force attacks

- 原文链接: [Credit cards are vulnerable to brute force attacks](https://metin.nextc.org/posts/Credit_Cards_Are_Vulnerable_To_Brute_Force_Kind_Attacks.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47979839)

This article talks about how credit cards can be stolen, even when companies follow security rules like PCI DSS. The writer shares a story of how their own card was used by attackers, even though only part of the card data was shown.

The PCI DSS rules say that websites and stores can only show the first six and last four digits of a credit card number, along with the cardholder’s name and expiration date. Sensitive things like the full card number, CVC, and PIN should never be shown. This is to make it harder for attackers to steal your card. However, the writer explains that many companies just do the bare minimum to pass these rules. They do not do more to make things safer if they do not have to.

In the writer’s case, their card was saved on a big merchant’s website. One day, they got a message about a purchase attempt they did not make. The attacker first tried to buy something and got to the bank’s 3D Secure page. Even though the order was not finished, the attacker learned that the card was still working, which bank it was from, and the masked credit card number with the full expiration date. Later, the attacker made more attempts and finally managed to take money from the card using a merchant that did not need 3D Secure.

The article explains how attackers can guess the full credit card number. They know the first six and last four digits. This leaves only six digits to guess, making about 100,000 possible combinations. The attackers use special programs and website APIs to try these numbers slowly, so they do not get caught. Some payment systems even tell the attacker if the card number, expiration date, or CVC is wrong, making it easier to guess the right details step by step.

The writer says that some merchants can even take payments without needing all the details or without 3D Secure, which is risky. The problem is not just bad passwords or weak accounts, but also the way companies handle and show card information.

People in the comments had many opinions. Some said that the PCI DSS rules are not enough, and companies should do more than just the minimum. Others pointed out that the way payment systems give helpful error messages (like saying “wrong CVC”) makes brute-force attacks easier. Some commenters were surprised that some merchants can process payments without needing the CVC or expiration date, while others said this is sadly common.

A few people talked about how attackers use many different merchants and slow attack speeds to avoid being caught. Others said that banks and payment processors should have better limits and protection against these types of attacks. Some commenters were upset that merchants do not care about security as long as they follow the minimum rules. Others suggested always using virtual cards or cards with low limits for online shopping.

Some users worried that printed receipts can also leak too much card information, while a few said the real problem is how easy it is to automate these attacks. Many agreed that both users and companies need to be more careful, and that the payment industry should improve its standards to keep everyone safer.

First, you need to have Claude Code (version 2.0 or newer) installed. You add the destiny plugin using simple commands. When you first use it, it asks for your birth date, time, city, and gender, and saves this data. Each day, you can run the `/destiny` command to get a new fortune. The output has two parts: “Today’s Fortune” with star ratings for love, money, career, health, and a lucky number, color, and direction; and a “Life Reading” that talks about your character and life path in plain English. The system uses the Four Pillars (a Chinese birth chart), a perpetual lunar calendar, and the I-Ching (an old book used for divination). All calculations are deterministic, so the fortune is always the same for the same person on the same day. Claude AI writes the text, but the base data is always fixed. No external APIs are used, and it all works locally. There are different commands for different readings, languages, or compatibility checks. The author says this is for fun only, not real advice, and there is no scientific proof that the fortunes mean anything.

In the comments, some people are amused and like the creativity, saying it’s a fun use of AI and classic ideas. A few users are interested in how the plugin uses real calculation methods, not just random text, and they appreciate the technical detail. Some say these kinds of fortune tools remind them of old Unix “fortune” programs, but with a modern AI twist. Another person wonders if people might get too attached to the readings. A few joke about using their fortune to decide if they should fix bugs today. Some users are impressed by the multi-language support. Others simply thank the creator for putting this out as open source and for the clear warning that it’s not real advice. After the issue became public, Flock agreed to stop using Dunwoody’s cameras for demos, especially in sensitive places like children’s gyms and pools. They also say they will only use cameras in less private places, like parking lots, for future demos.

The access logs Jason Hunyar found showed Flock had access to many cameras across the city, not just public ones but also those owned by private businesses. There is a big system in the city called the “Real Time Crime Center,” powered by Flock, that pulls together all these camera feeds. Flock’s public FAQ says customers own their own data and that Flock will not look at footage without permission. But the logs show that employees did view real footage in sensitive locations.

In the comments, some people are shocked and angry. They say it is a huge privacy violation to use cameras in places where children are present for sales demos. ## whohas – Command-line utility for cross-distro, cross-repository package search

- 原文链接: [whohas – Command-line utility for cross-distro, cross-repository package search](https://github.com/whohas/whohas)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47975592)

whohas is a command-line tool that helps you search for software packages across many Linux and BSD distributions at once. It is mainly for package maintainers but also useful for regular users who want to know which distros have a certain package and what version they offer.

The tool supports a wide range of systems, including Arch, Debian, Fedora, Gentoo, Ubuntu, openSUSE, FreeBSD, and even MacPorts and Cygwin. whohas is written in Perl and works by querying the package lists of these distributions, then showing results in a table. The output includes the distro, package name, version, date, repository, and a URL to more details. This makes it easy to compare package versions and availability across distros.

You can filter results using command-line tools like grep. For example, to only show results for the package “gimp,” you can use `whohas gimp | grep "gimp "`. If you want to see results just for Arch Linux, try `whohas gimp | grep Arch`.

The tool can also help you see what version of a package is available in different releases of Debian. The output is formatted so you can use other command-line tools like cut to get just the columns you need, such as the version number.

The author encourages Linux distributions to provide better web interfaces for their package data, including info like package size and maintainer details. whohas only supports x86 architecture for now, and the author advises checking the URLs for full details.

If you find bugs, you can email the maintainer directly. The README also lists other tools for package searching, like Repology and pkgs.org.

In the comments, some people say whohas is very helpful for comparing packages and finding out where software is available. Others note that it is not always up-to-date, since it relies on web scraping or public package lists, and some distros change their site layouts often. A few users wish it supported more modern package systems, like Flatpak or Snap.

Some commenters mention that Repology and pkgs.org provide similar features with web interfaces, making them easier for casual users. Others like that whohas is a command-line tool, which fits well into scripts or automated workflows. There are also comments about the tool being written in Perl, with some users happy about it, while others say it can be hard to maintain old Perl scripts.

A few users share tips on using grep and cut with whohas to get cleaner results or to integrate it with other tools. Some users wish more distributions would offer easy-to-parse package info, so tools like whohas could work better. Others remember using whohas years ago and are happy to see it is still around.

Overall, the community sees whohas as a useful but niche tool, especially for people who work with many Linux distributions or need to compare package versions. Some think web tools have replaced it for most users, but power users still enjoy having a scriptable command-line option.

## Understand Anything

- 原文链接: [Understand Anything](https://github.com/Lum1104/Understand-Anything)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47977470)

Understand Anything is a tool that turns any codebase or knowledge base into an interactive graph you can explore, search, and ask questions about. It helps people see the big picture in complex projects and works with many AI coding tools like Claude Code, Copilot, Codex, Cursor, and Gemini CLI.

The main idea is to make learning a new codebase easier by showing how everything connects. When you run Understand Anything, it scans all your files, functions, and classes, and builds a knowledge graph. You can open a dashboard to see this graph, click on nodes to read plain-English summaries, and follow guided tours that explain the code in order. There is also a business logic view, so you can see how the code matches real-world processes. If you have a wiki or notes, the tool can turn them into a graph too.

You can search the graph by name or by meaning, like asking “which parts handle auth?” and get the answer. The dashboard can change its detail level depending on if you are a junior developer or a power user. You can also see how your changes might affect the system before you commit. Graphs are color-coded by layer, like API, UI, or Data, to make it easy to spot what’s what.

Installing the tool is simple for most platforms—it can be added via plugin marketplaces or auto-discovered by editors. The tool supports sharing the graph with teammates: you can commit the graph file to your repo so everyone sees the same map. For big graphs, you can use git-lfs to track the files.

Under the hood, Understand Anything uses several “agents” that each do a special job, like finding files, analyzing code, building the architecture, and checking the graph for errors. It can update only the parts that changed, so it is fast after the first scan.

From the comments, many people think this tool fills a big need for making codebases more understandable, especially for new team members. Some users say the graphs are much better for learning than just reading files one by one. Others like that it works with many tools and AI platforms, so they can use it no matter what editor they have.

Some comments point out that while graphs are helpful, they can get messy or too big for very large projects, so there needs to be good ways to filter or group nodes. A few users wonder if the tool can handle codebases with mixed languages or strange structures. Others are excited about the business logic view, saying it’s rare to see a tool connect code to real-world processes.

There is also talk about the value of sharing the graph with teammates, so everyone starts with a “mental map” of the project. Some users worry about privacy or sensitive info being included in the graph, asking if there are controls or filters. A few mention performance, hoping it stays quick even for giant codebases.

Overall, the main feeling is that Understand Anything can save time and reduce confusion, especially for teams working on big or complex code. People are interested to see how it grows and if more features, like code reviews or onboarding guides, are added in the future.

## AI uses less water than the public thinks

- 原文链接: [AI uses less water than the public thinks](https://californiawaterblog.com/2026/04/26/ai-water-use-distractions-and-lessons-for-california/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47977383)

This article looks at how much water artificial intelligence (AI) data centers use in California. The main point is that people are worried about AI using too much water, but the real numbers show it uses much less than most believe.

The article explains that AI runs in big buildings called data centers, which need water for cooling computers. People think these centers use a lot of water, but the author did some math and checked with AI models to estimate the true amount. California has about 15 million square feet of data centers. If all these data centers used only water for cooling, they would use between 32,000 to 290,000 acre-feet of water per year. However, the most likely amount is about 20,000 acre-feet per year. This may sound like a lot, but California uses about 40 million acre-feet of water every year for everything. So, AI data centers use less than 0.1% of all water in the state. The article adds that even beer factories in Arizona use more water than data centers there. The author says we should not panic about AI water use, and we should ask for clear numbers before worrying.

One lesson from the article is that public fears often grow because people don't have good facts. AI can actually help by giving quick, rough estimates when needed. The writer warns to watch out for articles and reports that use weak numbers or no numbers at all. He says people should use honest math to guide discussions about water and technology, instead of relying on fears or guesses.

In the Hacker News comments, many people agree that AI data centers do not use as much water as some media reports suggest. Some users point out that farming and other industries use far more water. Others note that water use can still be a problem in places where water is already scarce, and that local impacts matter. A few commenters discuss how some data centers use air cooling instead of water, which changes the numbers. Some are frustrated with how the media covers these topics, saying stories often focus on fears without real data. Others say that even if AI centers only use a little water compared to farms, we should still be careful, especially as AI grows. Some users like that the article uses math and checks facts, while others wish companies were more open about their water use. Finally, a few people joke about how humans themselves use and waste water every day, and suggest that perspective is important.
