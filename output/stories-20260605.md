## pg_durable: Microsoft open sources in-database durable execution

- 原文链接: [pg_durable: Microsoft open sources in-database durable execution](https://github.com/microsoft/pg_durable)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48414367)

Microsoft has open sourced pg_durable, a tool that adds durable workflow execution inside PostgreSQL. This means you can run long background jobs, like data processing, directly in your database and not worry if they stop or crash—the work will continue from where it left off.

With pg_durable, you write your workflow in SQL, and the tool breaks it into steps. Each step is saved (or “checkpointed”) so if something fails, you don’t have to start over. Before, people often needed to use cron jobs, special workers, queues, or extra tables to track job progress. Now, you can handle it all in one place: Postgres. This is useful for things like processing batches of documents, running maintenance, or calling outside APIs in order. You can build workflows that run steps one after another, or even in parallel. Common jobs that need this are data pipelines, AI processing per row or batch, or keeping track of changes in data.

If you’re already using tricks like job tables plus cron, or have complex systems like Airflow or Temporal, pg_durable might replace those. It removes the need for outside schedulers or lots of custom code. Everything is tracked in your database, so you can see progress, retry steps, and keep things simple. But if your job is just a single SQL statement or needs very fast, instant results, this isn’t for you. Also, if you can’t add extensions to your database, you can’t use it.

To use pg_durable, install it as a Postgres extension, define your workflow in SQL, and start jobs with a special command. You can see status and results from normal SQL queries. It’s built using Rust and works on Postgres 17 and 18. Security is handled with strict permissions, and there’s no outside telemetry sent to Microsoft.

In the Hacker News comments, many people liked the idea of moving workflow logic into the database. They said it could make systems simpler by removing extra parts like workers and queues. Some users liked that it uses only SQL and Postgres, with no extra services. Others worried about putting too much logic into the database. They said it might make the database harder to manage or scale, especially if jobs are heavy. A few people mentioned that some teams have strict rules and can't add extensions, so this tool wouldn't work for them. Other commenters compared pg_durable to tools like Temporal, Airflow, or cron jobs, saying it seems easier for some cases but not as flexible for everything. Some liked that it’s open source and doesn’t send data to Microsoft. Others asked about failure modes and how well it handles lots of users or very big workflows. Overall, most agreed it’s a useful tool if you already use Postgres and want simpler, more reliable background jobs.

## Gemma 4 QAT models: Optimizing compression for mobile and laptop efficiency

- 原文链接: [Gemma 4 QAT models: Optimizing compression for mobile and laptop efficiency](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/technology/developers-tools/quantization-aware-training-gemma-4/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48414653)

Google has released new versions of its Gemma 4 AI models, making them smaller and faster for laptops and phones. These new models use a method called Quantization-Aware Training (QAT) to keep their quality while shrinking their size.

Usually, making AI models smaller can make them less smart. QAT solves this by teaching the model to handle being smaller during its training. The article explains that Gemma 4 now comes in two new formats: one for regular computers (Q4_0) and one made just for mobile devices. With the mobile format, the smallest model, Gemma 4 E2B, only needs 1GB of memory.

The article says QAT works better than the usual method, Post-Training Quantization (PTQ), because it keeps higher quality. The team also used special tricks for mobile devices: they fixed the way the model handles numbers to save time, changed how data is organized to match phone chips, and compressed some parts of the model more than others. For example, they use very tiny numbers (2-bit) for the part that writes words, but keep important thinking parts more detailed. They also made the memory use even lower by shrinking the parts of the model that remember past conversation and store words.

Developers can now download these models in different formats, use them with popular tools like llama.cpp, Ollama, or LM Studio, and even run them on the web or with Apple’s MLX. There’s also documentation and ways to fine-tune the models for your own needs.

In the Hacker News comments, some users are excited because now they can run advanced AI on their phones without the cloud. Others like that Google provides open access and many formats, making it easy to use with their favorite tools. A few users talk about how QAT is a big step over PTQ, since it lets the model plan for compression instead of just being shrunk after training. Some people ask about how much quality is lost at these small sizes, and if the 2-bit trick makes the answers worse. Others wonder if the same methods can help other AI models or save power on large servers. There are also questions about how hard it is to use the new formats in custom projects. Some users worry about privacy and if running models locally is really safe. A few developers share tips about getting the models working on different hardware, like NVIDIA or Apple chips. - HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48398925)

This article talks about using AI agents to help write tests with Test-Driven Development (TDD). The author says most AI agents today are bad at writing tests, and this is because they learn from humans—whose tests are also often poor.

The article explains that AI agents make tests that are too vague, complicated, or just bad, and even many teachers show poor testing practices. But with some help, agents can follow a good TDD process. The author points to Kent Beck’s “Canon TDD” as a strong guide and says telling an agent to just follow that can improve results a lot. The author also shares their own TDD process, which uses the “specify-encode-fulfill” (SEF) loop: first, decide what you want to build (specify), write tests for it (encode), then write code to pass those tests (fulfill).

The article gives more detail about TDD steps: make a list of what you want to build, write a test for each item, write just enough code to pass the test, refactor only after passing the test, and repeat until the list is done. The author says this process helps, but doesn’t always make good test design, so they made a “Test Design Review” skill for AI. This skill uses a separate agent to check for design mistakes in tests and give suggestions. If tests still seem wrong, the author runs Test Design Review again.

The author also uses a “Software Design Review” skill to check for general design issues, like bad naming. Sometimes, the agent even pauses to suggest cleaning up code before writing more, which the author finds helpful. The AI is not perfect, but using these skills has improved the author’s workflow. The main idea is that combining AI with classic software principles gives the best results.

In the comments, some people agree that AI agents are bad at writing tests and like the idea of giving them more guidance. Others say most human-written tests are also bad, so it’s not surprising that AI struggles. A few users point out that teaching AI with better examples could help. Some think “specify-encode-fulfill” is a clear and simple way to do TDD and might be easier for both humans and AI to follow.

A few commenters worry that using AI for tests will make people even lazier about learning good testing skills. Some like the idea of a Test Design Review agent but wonder if it can catch deeper logic errors. Others share that they’ve tried similar approaches and also saw better results when combining AI with strong rules and reviews. There are also comments about Kent Beck’s Canon TDD, saying it’s a good standard but sometimes hard to follow in real projects. Finally, some users ask if these skills and review tools could help teach beginners, not just AI, to write better tests.

## New method turns ocean water into drinking water, without waste

- 原文链接: [New method turns ocean water into drinking water, without waste](https://www.rochester.edu/newscenter/what-is-desalination-definition-ocean-water-704732/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48413500)

A new method from the University of Rochester can turn ocean water into drinking water without creating waste. It uses a special black metal panel that works with sunlight to clean the water and collect the leftover salts.

The panel is made with lasers that make it very good at soaking up light and pulling water across its surface. When sunlight hits the panel, it heats up and turns the water into steam, leaving salts and minerals behind. The clever part is how the panel is made: salts are moved to the sides, away from the main working area, so the process does not stop or get clogged up. This idea is similar to what happens when you spill coffee and it dries, leaving a ring of coffee at the edge. The team tested the panel with water from different oceans and found it could clean itself and keep working well. Instead of making dirty, salty water as waste, the panel collects almost all salts in solid form. This is useful because some salts, like lithium, are valuable for making batteries. The team even found a way to separate lithium from other salts using tiny particles on the panel. They showed that it is possible to get about half of the lithium out of salty lake water. The process does not need extra chemicals to treat the water, and it uses only sunlight, making it cheap and clean. The researchers believe this method can help provide clean water to many people and also help with getting useful minerals.

Some commenters liked that the process does not need chemicals, but a few were worried about how often the panels would need cleaning or repair. ## Transformers Are Inherently Succinct

- 原文链接: [Transformers Are Inherently Succinct](https://openreview.net/pdf?id=Yxz92UuPLQ)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48416635)

This article talks about why transformers, a kind of neural network, are very good at describing languages in a short and simple way, even more so than older models like finite automata, recurrent neural networks (RNNs), or logic formulas. The main idea is that transformers can often be much smaller, or more "succinct," when describing the same tasks or languages.

The authors show that fixed-precision transformers (the kind used in real computers) can represent some languages using much less space than other models. For example, there are languages that a small transformer can recognize, but if you tried to use an LTL formula or RNN, you would need something exponentially bigger, and if you tried to use a finite automaton, it would need to be doubly exponentially bigger. This means transformers can be super compact for some problems.

They explain this with technical examples, such as how transformers can use attention mechanisms to count to very large numbers, which is hard for other models to do without getting huge. They also discuss how measuring "succinctness" is important: it’s not just about what a model can do, but also how simply it can describe things.

Because transformers can describe some languages so succinctly, checking simple things about them—like whether they accept any words at all (the "emptiness" problem)—becomes very hard. In fact, it’s EXPSPACE-complete, which means it can take a huge amount of time and memory, much more than for older models. For finite automata, these problems are much easier.

They also compare transformers to other models and show that, while RNNs can recognize more types of languages, transformers can be much more compact for the ones they do handle. The article gives proofs and examples showing that for every language you can describe with a small transformer, if you try to describe it with an automaton, you end up with something gigantic.

In the comment section, some readers are surprised that transformers, despite being less "expressive" than RNNs, can be much more compact. Others point out that in practice, this might help explain why transformers are so popular and effective for real-world language tasks, even if they aren’t the most powerful in theory.

A few commenters worry that this high succinctness comes at a cost: it makes transformers harder to analyze, debug, or formally verify. Some suggest that this is why tools for checking transformers are far behind those for older models. Others are excited by the theoretical results, saying it could lead to better ways to use or understand transformers in software and AI.

There are also discussions about whether these findings mean we should rethink how we compare different machine learning models—maybe "succinctness" is a more useful concept than just raw expressive power. Some mention that this might help in making models easier to train or run on smaller devices.

Finally, a few users ask about future work, like whether these results hold for other types of transformers (like softmax-based ones) or in settings with less strict precision. Some are curious about practical impacts, like model compression or improving explainability in AI. Overall, the discussion is a mix of excitement, curiosity, and some caution about the complexity trade-offs.

## Mouseless – keyboard-driven control of macOS/Linux/Windows

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48383667)

Mouseless is a tool that lets you control your computer’s mouse using only your keyboard. It works on Mac, Linux, and Windows, and aims to speed up tasks and help people work more comfortably.

The main idea is that moving your hand to the mouse, clicking, and then going back to the keyboard takes time. With Mouseless, you can move the cursor and click just by typing, which is much faster. You can do all mouse actions: left click, right click, middle click, back, forward, double-click, triple-click, drag, drop, scroll, and more. There’s a “free mode” that lets you move the cursor smoothly around, instead of jumping from place to place. This is a big update compared to old “mouse keys” tools that come with most operating systems or special keyboards.

Mouseless is also designed to help people avoid pain from using a mouse all day. Too many clicks and long use of a trackpad can hurt your hand or wrist, so using the keyboard for everything can be healthier. It also helps people who can’t use a regular mouse, making computers more accessible. You can use it anywhere—at your desk, on the couch, or even lying in bed, since you don’t need a hard surface for a mouse.

## Gov.uk has replaced Stripe with Dutch provider Adyen

- 原文链接: [Gov.uk has replaced Stripe with Dutch provider Adyen](https://www.theregister.com/public-sector/2026/06/04/govuk-goes-dutch-on-payments-as-it-dumps-stripe/5250763)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48415217)

The UK government has stopped using Stripe for payments on its GOV.UK Pay service and has chosen Adyen, a Dutch company, instead. This change will affect many payments for local councils, police, and other public groups, and brings in a contract worth up to £25.3 million for three years.

The new deal with Adyen covers about 17% of total payments but over 70% of the organizations using GOV.UK Pay. The contract allows new users to start accepting payments in one day, which is faster than before. Around 1,000 services will move to Adyen, but the government says people paying should not notice any difference. The switch also adds a “pay by bank” option, letting people pay straight from their bank accounts without needing to use credit or debit cards. This uses open banking and can make things quicker and safer for some users.

The government also said it will keep using WorldPay for payments from central government and the NHS. GOV.UK Pay was created so public groups did not have to build their own payment systems, making payments easier and cheaper. The system has handled over 137 million payments, worth about £9.2 billion, since 2016. It serves over 1,700 services for more than 600 organizations, from local air cadets to town councils.

Others wonder why the UK picked a non-UK provider and if this could cause problems with data or legal rules. Some users say Stripe’s service was good and are worried if Adyen will be as easy to use, especially for developers. There are also questions about costs—some think Adyen might be cheaper, while others say switching providers often brings hidden fees or problems. A few people discuss if open banking is ready for everyone, pointing out that older users might find it confusing. Finally, some comments praise the government for making payments fast and simple, while others hope this change really will be smooth for users, as promised.
