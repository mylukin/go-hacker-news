Project Glasswing is a new project from Anthropic and partners to use AI to find security problems in important software before attackers can use advanced AI to do harm. In just one month, the team and about 50 partners used the Claude Mythos Preview model to find over 10,000 serious bugs in key software systems. Before, the main problem was finding these bugs, but now the challenge is checking, reporting, and fixing all the issues that the AI finds.

The article explains that, by tradition, companies wait 90 days after finding a bug before telling the public, to give time to fix it. Because of this, Anthropic shares only some results and cannot give full details yet. Still, the early results are big: companies like Cloudflare found thousands of bugs, many of them very serious, at a much faster rate than before. External testers and groups like the UK’s AI Security Institute and Mozilla also found Mythos Preview to be very strong at catching problems. For example, Mozilla found over 10 times more bugs in Firefox 150 than in previous versions.

The article also talks about the impact on open-source software. Mythos Preview scanned over 1,000 open-source projects and found thousands of problems, including serious ones in a popular security library called wolfSSL. Human experts check the AI’s results, but this takes a lot of time and effort, and open-source maintainers are struggling to keep up with all the reports. So, while AI makes it easy to find bugs, fixing them is still slow.

To help, Anthropic has released tools like Claude Security to help companies scan and fix their own code quickly. They are also working with groups like the Open Source Security Foundation to help open-source maintainers manage bug reports. Anthropic stresses that companies and users should patch their software faster and follow good security practices, since attacks could come more quickly with AI.

In the Hacker News comments, some users are impressed by how quickly AI can now find bugs and think this could make software much safer in the long run. Others worry that, since fixing bugs takes much longer than finding them, there could be a wave of attacks before everything is patched. Some point out that open-source projects may not have enough people or money to handle so many bug reports, and that AI-generated reports can sometimes be low-quality or false alarms, adding to the load.

A few commenters debate whether releasing powerful AI tools like Mythos Preview to the public is safe, since attackers could also use them. People also discuss whether companies will really speed up their patching process, or if old habits will be hard to change. Some suggest that more funding and support is needed for the open-source community, while others think companies should take more responsibility for the security of the software they use.

Many agree that the use of AI in cybersecurity is a big shift, with both risks and opportunities. The main worry is that attackers will use these tools just as fast, so defenders need to work even harder. Some users hope that, in the future, software will become much safer thanks to AI, but others warn that the next few years could be very difficult for defenders.

## Why Japanese companies do so many different things

- 原文链接: [Why Japanese companies do so many different things](https://davidoks.blog/p/why-japanese-companies-do-so-many)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48237163)

This article looks at why Japanese companies do many different things, often in very different markets. It starts with Toto, a company known for toilets, but now making most of its profit from making a special part for computer chips. Many other Japanese companies are like Toto—Kyocera makes everything from ceramics to kitchen knives to solar panels, while Yamaha makes pianos, motorcycles, robots, and more. Even paper companies in Japan might run hotels or make diapers.

The article explains that this is not common in rich countries. In the US, companies usually focus on one thing. In Japan, big companies do many things and do them well, even in very technical fields. The reason comes from how Japanese companies are built. They often give workers jobs for life, promote by seniority, and have company-based unions. Most company shares are owned by other Japanese companies, and outside investors have little power. Profits are usually invested back into the company, not paid out as dividends.

The article uses research by economists to explain that companies use “bundles” of practices. In Japan, the “J-firm” bundle is different from the “H-firm” (hierarchy) model seen in America. In Japan, workers get broad training and stay with the company for life, so the company wants to keep them working—even if this means starting new business lines. This bundle also makes companies value survival and stability over making the most money. They diversify into new areas to use extra workers and lower risk.

Japan’s system came from changes during World War II, when the government pushed companies to focus on production and employee stability. This model worked very well for many years, especially for improving existing products. Japanese companies became leaders in cars, electronics, and other fields by making slow, steady improvements. But the article says this system is not good at big, new innovations—like inventing the smartphone—because it values group decisions over bold leadership.

When Japan tried to change to a more American system in the 1990s, it didn’t work well. For example, paying workers based on performance broke teamwork and made things worse. The article says Japanese companies are still very strong in special technical areas, like making important parts for chips, but are less good at creating big new products. The Japanese and American systems each have strengths and weaknesses, and sometimes they need each other.

In the comments, many people are surprised to learn how much Japanese companies diversify and how they manage so many different businesses. Some readers say this model is stable and gives workers security, which is good for society. Others point out that lifetime jobs and strong company loyalty can make it hard to change quickly or reward the best workers. A few commenters think American companies could learn from Japan’s focus on quality and long-term planning.

Some users share stories about working with Japanese firms, saying they value relationships and are slow to fire people. Others note that Japanese companies sometimes hold on to unprofitable businesses just to keep workers busy. A few people wonder if this model can survive as Japan’s population gets older and fewer young people join the workforce.

One commenter points out that many Korean companies are also very diverse, but usually because of government support. Another says the Japanese model works best for making things better, not for inventing totally new things. Some readers ask if this approach is possible in other countries, or if it needs special culture and history. Finally, a few people discuss if the Japanese model will change in the future as global business becomes more competitive.

## Open source Kanban desktop app that runs parallel agents on every card

- 原文链接: [Open source Kanban desktop app that runs parallel agents on every card](https://www.kanbots.dev/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48239413)

This article is about KanBots, an open source desktop Kanban app that lets you run AI agents on every card. The idea is to help developers automate and manage tasks using powerful AI tools, either alone or as a team.

KanBots works by letting you drop a folder to create a Kanban board, then assign AI agents (like Claude Code or Codex) to work on different cards. Each card becomes its own work area, and the board updates live as agents make progress. You can use "autopilot" mode, where personas (like product manager, engineer, tester) split work into subtasks, run in parallel, and even review each other's output. This can keep things moving while you do other things or even sleep.

The app is local-first, meaning nothing leaves your computer—your code and data stay private. All files, settings, and databases are stored in a folder next to your repo, and there's no need for cloud accounts or servers. KanBots supports Mac, Windows, and Linux, and it's free and open source under the MIT license.

KanBots also works well with tools you might already use: you can connect it to GitHub issues and pull requests, Sentry for error tracking, and more. It lets you choose your AI provider, and you can create custom personas—special instructions for the AI that you can save and reuse.

Some other features include tracking the cost of AI runs in real time, setting budget caps to avoid surprise charges, and having the system pause for your input when decisions are needed. You also get a full user interface for reviewing, splitting, and shipping work done by the agents. The "autopilot" feature is especially interesting: you pick personas, set how many can run at once (up to 4), and let them break down and work through your backlog until work is done or your budget runs out.

In the Hacker News comments, many people are excited about the idea of combining Kanban boards with AI agents. Some think it's a unique and useful way to manage software projects, while others are curious about the technical details, like how parallel agents work or how the app keeps everything local. A few users mention concerns about trusting AI agents with code changes, but like that the app pauses for human review. Some people ask if KanBots could be useful for non-coding tasks or for teams with different workflows.

Other commenters talk about how the cost tracking is a smart feature—AI can get expensive, so having built-in limits helps. There are questions about how easy it is to write custom personas, and some suggest more integrations with other tools. A few users mention that the "bring your own CLI" approach is flexible, letting you use your own API keys. Some are excited about the open source model and the pay-what-you-can donation system. Others wonder if KanBots could help indie hackers or solo developers speed up their projects. Overall, the responses show both interest and healthy skepticism, with users sharing ideas on how to use or improve the tool.

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48213529)

A new Minecraft mod called "waylandcraft" lets players run a full Wayland compositor inside Minecraft. This means you can launch Linux desktop apps and open windows right in your Minecraft world.

The mod only works on Linux and is for Minecraft Java Edition. With this mod, you can drag and drop things between windows, play videos in a corner of your screen, and move app windows around—all inside Minecraft. It uses the Fabric mod loader and is client-side only, so you don't need to run a server. The mod is open source and was released just a few days ago.

Wayland is a system that manages windows and displays on Linux. By bringing it into Minecraft, the mod makes Minecraft work a bit like a desktop environment. You can do things like pin a video player to your heads-up display while you play, or open up other apps and move their windows in the Minecraft world. The mod is not for Mac or Windows users.

## Models.dev: open-source database of AI model specs, pricing, and capabilities

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48241172)

Models.dev is an open-source project that keeps a database of AI models, including their specs, prices, and abilities. The goal is to have one place where you can find up-to-date info on any AI model from many providers.

The data is stored in simple TOML files, and you can access it through an API or by browsing the project’s website. Each model entry lists things like which provider made it, if it supports features like file uploads, tool calling, or chain-of-thought reasoning, and how much it costs per million tokens. There are also details about input and output limits, release dates, and what types of data (text, images, etc.) the model can handle. Models.dev also keeps logos for each provider and lets users add new models or providers by following clear steps. If a provider reuses another company’s model, you can just extend the original model’s data instead of copying everything. When you submit changes, GitHub actions check your files for errors.

Anyone can help keep the database up to date by submitting pull requests. The code for the website is open-source, so people can run it locally to test their changes. The project is used internally by another tool, opencode.ai, and the maintainers encourage questions and community help. Models.dev was created by the SST team, who also run a Discord and YouTube channel.

In the Hacker News comments, many people liked the idea of a central place for AI model info. Some said it solves the problem of scattered, out-of-date spreadsheets. Others liked that the data is open and can be checked by anyone. A few users wondered if it will be hard to keep the info current, since AI models and prices change often. Some people asked for features like tracking model benchmarks or adding user reviews. However, some commenters said that using TOML and simple rules should make it easy for the AI community to help. There were also suggestions for adding more models, especially from smaller providers or open-source labs. ## Deno 2.8

- 原文链接: [Deno 2.8](https://deno.com/blog/v2.8)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48234380)

Deno 2.8 is a new update for the Deno JavaScript/TypeScript runtime, and it brings many new features and big improvements. This release focuses on making Deno faster, easier to use, and more compatible with Node.js and web APIs.

Some of the main changes are new commands like `deno audit fix` (to fix security problems in npm packages automatically), `deno bump-version` (to update version numbers), `deno ci` (for clean, repeatable installs in CI), `deno pack` (to package projects for npm), and `deno transpile` (to turn TypeScript into plain JavaScript files). There is also `deno why` to explain why a package is installed.

Deno now treats unprefixed package names as npm packages at the CLI, so `deno add express` works just like `npm install express`. Node.js compatibility jumped from 42% to over 76% of Node’s test suite, which is a big step up, and Deno is now much faster in real-world tasks like installing packages, running HTTP servers, and encoding data.

Many new features are aimed at developers: better debugging (network traffic can be seen in Chrome DevTools), a built-in CPU profiler, and new OpenTelemetry options for tracing and logging. Test runners are improved with per-test timeouts and better coverage reports. Deno also supports more browser APIs like OffscreenCanvas and Geometry types, and better stream and crypto support.

Workspace and package management are better, with the new `catalog:` protocol for sharing versions, cross-platform installs, and a `--prod` flag to skip dev dependencies. Deno can now also use hoisted `node_modules` for legacy Node projects, and `.npmrc` support is improved.

Other nice touches include smaller updates (delta upgrades), easier module loader hooks for custom imports, and a change to `setTimeout` and `setInterval` to match Node’s return values.

Looking at Hacker News comments, many developers are impressed by the fast progress and the new features. Some are happy that Deno is easier to use with Node projects, and that npm compatibility keeps getting better. Others like the speed improvements, especially for installs and HTTP servers. A few people are excited by the new debugging tools and better testing.

Some users still feel that Deno’s biggest challenge is the Node ecosystem’s size and history. A few think that, even with better compatibility, moving big projects from Node to Deno is hard. Some are cautious about the many new features, saying it could make Deno more complex. Others point out that, while Deno is great for new projects, it is still tough to switch over existing Node codebases.

There are also comments about the new `import defer` feature, which delays running code until it is needed—some think this is very useful, while others are not sure how often they would use it. The improved TypeScript support is praised by TypeScript users. Some users are happy about the better support for browser APIs, making Deno a good choice for code that runs on both server and client.

Overall, the community feels positive about Deno 2.8. Many see it as a big step forward, but some are still waiting to see bigger adoption in the real world before moving their own projects.

## Antigravity 2.0 Tops the OpenSCAD Architectural 3D LLM Benchmark

- 原文链接: [Antigravity 2.0 Tops the OpenSCAD Architectural 3D LLM Benchmark](https://modelrift.com/blog/openscad-llm-benchmark/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48234090)

This article is about testing different AI coding tools to see how well they can build a 3D model of the Pantheon in OpenSCAD, a code-based modeling tool. The main goal was to find out which AI could best turn images of the Pantheon into good, parametric CAD code.

The test used two reference images of the Pantheon and asked each AI to write OpenSCAD code to build the model. OpenSCAD is good for this because it works well with shapes like cylinders, domes, and columns, which the Pantheon has. The benchmark compared six tools, including Google Antigravity 2.0 with Gemini 3.5 Flash, Codex 5.5 High, Claude Code 2.1, Cursor Composer 2.5, and a human-assisted run with ModelRift and Gemini Flash 3.0.

The hardest part wasn’t using the tools, but making the model look right. All the AIs could make a simple building, but getting the details and shapes correct was difficult. Antigravity 2.0 did the best job on its own, using real Pantheon measurements and even adding the special ceiling pattern. Codex had good details, like the inscription on the building, but its final 3D file had problems not seen in previews. Claude Sonnet made the most balanced model, but it was slow. Cursor was fastest but made the weakest model. The human-assisted ModelRift run produced a better result because a person could point out mistakes directly on the preview, making it easier for the AI to improve the model.

The results showed that OpenSCAD is a good fit for AI code generation, but making high-quality models still needs careful adjustments. Speed did not mean better results—slower AIs sometimes did better. Also, what looked good in the preview was not always good in the final exported file. None of the models were perfect, but all were at least recognizable as the Pantheon.

In the Hacker News comments, some people praised OpenSCAD as a good tool for AI-driven modeling because its code is simple and easy to change. Others pointed out that building something like the Pantheon is a much bigger challenge than just making cubes or simple shapes, so the test was a fair way to compare AIs. A few users liked that Antigravity 2.0 used real-world measurements, saying this made its model much more accurate.

Some commenters noted that the best results still needed human help, showing that fully automatic 3D modeling by AI isn’t quite ready yet. Others wondered if other CAD tools would work better than OpenSCAD, especially for more complex or organic buildings. There was also discussion about how previews can be misleading—something that looks good in a quick view might have problems in the final file used for 3D printing.

A few users worried about the high price of the newest AI models, saying the cost might keep people from using the best tools. Others thought the human-in-the-loop approach, where you can draw corrections on a preview, was clever and likely the future for design work with AI.

Overall, the community found the benchmark interesting, with many agreeing that AI is getting close, but still needs humans for the best results in detailed 3D modeling.

## Bun support is now limited and deprecated

- 原文链接: [Bun support is now limited and deprecated](https://github.com/yt-dlp/yt-dlp/issues/16766)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48238789)

yt-dlp announced that support for Bun as a JavaScript runtime is now limited and will soon be removed. This change affects people using yt-dlp with Bun, especially with its ejs system. 

The main reason is security: older Bun versions ignore lockfiles, which is risky because of recent npm supply chain attacks. The new minimum supported Bun version is 1.2.11, and the maximum is 1.3.14. Versions outside this range will not be supported. The team is worried about Bun’s recent rewrite in Rust using AI (Claude), and they feel the project is moving in a direction they do not trust. Because of these risks and possible future problems, they plan to stop supporting Bun completely if it becomes too hard to maintain. For now, users must use only the safe versions of Bun listed above. If users want to use newer Bun versions, they do so at their own risk. If yt-dlp drops support for Bun later, they suggest plugins could add back support if the community wants to.

In the comments, some people agree with the decision, saying it makes sense to avoid trouble and not support tools that might break things. Others are unhappy, saying it is inconvenient to have to install another JS runtime just for yt-dlp, especially if they already use Bun for web development. Some users ask for a warning instead of blocking newer Bun versions, so people could still use them if they want. There is some debate about whether most web developers really have only Bun and not Node.js installed—many think most people already have Node. A few people joke that installing another runtime is not a big deal because it’s just a few megabytes. One Deno maintainer explains that Deno uses bots and AI for bug fixes but always reviews the code, trying to reassure the community about AI use in development. Some suggest if people want Bun support, they could write plugins themselves. Overall, opinions are mixed: some support the cautious move, while others think it limits user choice too much.
