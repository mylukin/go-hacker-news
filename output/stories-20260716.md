Kimi K3 is a new AI model with 2.8 trillion parameters and can handle text, images, and videos with a very long context window. The article says Kimi K3 is the biggest open AI model yet, built using special attention methods and a system with many expert “sub-models” to help it scale and learn better.

Kimi K3 is good at coding, especially with long and complex tasks. It can work on big codebases, use screenshots, and even optimize GPU kernels better than many other models. For example, Kimi K3 made GPU code faster, sometimes cutting run times by more than half. It also built a working GPU compiler from scratch, called MiniTriton, which performs as well or better than other known tools. Kimi K3 can design 3D games, create digital assets, and even design computer chips by itself. For science work, Kimi K3 reads research papers, checks formulas, writes large amounts of code, and creates dashboards with results—work that would take a human weeks.

In knowledge work, Kimi K3 can create interactive reports, dashboards, and visualizations. It is especially good at making research presentations, charts, and slides, and can work with financial or scientific data. Kimi K3 can edit videos and explain its own technology using motion graphics. The architecture uses new attention methods and a mixture-of-experts design, making training and running the model more efficient and stable. For big jobs, it needs many GPUs, but it can run on modern hardware and has features to keep costs lower. Technical details are coming in a future report, and the full model will be open-sourced by 2026.

Kimi K3 is available on different platforms: desktop and mobile apps, a code tool for terminals, and an API. Prices for using the API are listed, with discounts for repeated input. There is also an enterprise option for organizations who need privacy and management features. Benchmark tables show Kimi K3 performs well against other models, sometimes winning, sometimes just behind top closed models like Claude Fable 5 or GPT 5.6 Sol.

The article admits some weaknesses: Kimi K3 can be too proactive (taking action without enough user input), and switching models mid-session can reduce quality. It is not quite as smooth as the very top proprietary models, but is competitive overall.

Hacker News comments highlight excitement about Kimi K3’s open nature and large size. Some users are impressed by its technical achievements and ability to handle complex coding tasks and GPU optimization. People appreciate the transparency, open-source promise, and strong benchmarks. Others are more cautious: they point out that real-world usefulness may depend on how easy it is to run the model, actual inference costs, and practical integration. Some users question how “open” Kimi K3 will truly be until the weights are released in 2026, and note that closed models like GPT and Claude are still a bit ahead in quality. A few mention worries about hardware requirements, since running such a big model needs many GPUs and may be out of reach for most users. Some think the benchmarks look good, but want independent tests, not just company claims. In general, the community is hopeful Kimi K3 will push open AI forward, but they want to see it in action before making final judgments.

## Microsoft Comic Chat is now open source

- 原文链接: [Microsoft Comic Chat is now open source](https://opensource.microsoft.com/blog/2026/07/16/microsoft-comic-chat-is-now-open-source/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48936426)

Microsoft has made Comic Chat, its old chat program that showed conversations as comics, open source on GitHub. Comic Chat was first released in 1996 and is famous for introducing Comic Sans, the casual font many people know today.

In the 1990s, most internet chats looked like plain walls of text. Comic Chat changed this by turning messages into comic panels with cartoon characters. Each person in the chat got a comic character. When someone typed something happy, their character would smile; if they were angry, the character would frown or cross its arms. The program tried to make the chat fun and visual, showing emotions and actions based on the words.

Comic Chat was built by a team at Microsoft Research led by DJ Kurlander. The comic art was created by Jim Woodring, a well-known comic artist. The software was written in C++ and released with Internet Explorer 3. The team even wrote a research paper about how Comic Chat could read messages and pick the right comic poses and layouts.

Now, Microsoft has released Comic Chat’s code so anyone can try it out, study how it works, or even improve it. They included some changes to help it run on modern computers, like connecting to today’s IRC servers and working with new versions of Windows. The code is not a finished product, but it is a starting point for hobbyists and fans to play with.

Comic Chat is a good example of the creative ideas from the early web. Back then, people liked to experiment and try new things, like making chat rooms look like comic books. The team behind Comic Chat wanted to make chatting more fun and personal.

In the comments on Hacker News, many people remember Comic Chat fondly. Some say it was ahead of its time and wish more chat apps looked different or more playful. Others are surprised to learn where Comic Sans came from, and a few joke about the font’s reputation. Some commenters are happy that Microsoft is open-sourcing old software, saying it helps preserve computer history. There are also developers curious about how the software worked, especially how it understood emotions and turned them into comic actions. A few people share stories about using Comic Chat when they were young, while others wonder how hard it will be to get it running on today’s machines. Some wish for modern versions or ask if it could be made to work with new chat systems. Overall, people like the idea of saving and sharing creative old software, even if it’s just for fun or nostalgia.

## Decoy Font

- 原文链接: [Decoy Font](https://www.mixfont.com/experiments/decoy-font)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48936584)

This article talks about Decoy Font, a special font that hides what you type from AI programs. The font uses a trick: each letter you type shows a fake letter up close, but from far away, you can see the real, hidden letter.

The way it works is by using two sets of shapes for each letter. The foreground is thin and sharp, while the background is blurred and thick. Up close, your eyes (and AI) see the sharp outline, which is the decoy. But if you squint or move back, the blur blends together and shows the hidden message. This method is based on the “hybrid image” technique, which mixes two images so that different details appear depending on distance—like the famous picture that looks like Einstein up close and Marilyn Monroe from afar.

Most AI models, like ChatGPT and Gemini, can only see the decoy message when given a screenshot of the font. People can see both messages, but computers get confused. The font is free to download and use, and you can try it in your own projects. There’s also a playground on the website to test how the hiding works with different letters. The font is built from DejaVu Sans Mono, and you can use it just like any other TTF font on your computer.

Decoy Font is one of several “anti-AI fonts” made to stop AI from reading private messages or scraping data from images. Another example from the same group is Ghost Font, which hides messages by moving them in an animation. Decoy Font is easier to use, since you can just type with it.

Still, this is not a perfect solution. Smarter AI models, or humans who know the trick, might figure out how to read both the decoy and the hidden message. But for now, it can slow down or stop simple AI tools from reading your text. The creator hopes to expand Decoy Font to support more languages, like Chinese, where the trick might work even better.

In the comments on Hacker News, people share many thoughts. Some are impressed by the clever idea and enjoy the optical illusion. Others point out that it is only a temporary fix, because AI will get better at spotting these tricks. A few people say that making text hard for AI to read also makes it hard for humans, especially for those with poor eyesight. Some developers talk about other ways to hide data from AI, like obfuscating text or using CAPTCHAs. One person suggests this font could be used in puzzles or games, while another is worried about accessibility for people who use screen readers. Some say this will start a “cat and mouse” game, as both font makers and AI researchers keep improving their tools. Lastly, many agree that it’s fun to see creative ways people are trying to keep data private in the age of AI.

They stopped buying new clips when the budget ran out but could still edit what they had. All four runs (two models, two budgets each) finished, making full-length videos with the song added in. Each model chose different video generation tools and editing methods. For example, GPT-5.6 Sol at $25 used images first, then animated them, while others went straight to generating video clips. With more money, both models generated more or longer video clips, but didn’t always spend the full budget. Claude Fable 5 cost more in token usage, even though it finished faster; GPT-5.6 Sol was cheaper to run.

No model checked or improved their own edits; once a video was built, they sent it out, even if some clips were bad. Both models chose only FAL’s video tools, skipping Replicate. The $100 budgets were more than needed; the models didn’t use extra money for better planning or editing.

## Helium escaping from atmosphere of nearby rocky exoplanet in a habitable zone

- 原文链接: [Helium escaping from atmosphere of nearby rocky exoplanet in a habitable zone](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aea9708)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48939742)

A group of scientists found that a rocky planet outside our solar system is losing helium from its atmosphere. This planet is in the “habitable zone,” which means it is at the right distance from its star to possibly have liquid water.

The planet is close to Earth, so scientists can study it more easily. They used a telescope to see a special kind of light (infrared) that helium gives off. This helped them notice that the planet’s atmosphere is leaking helium into space. The planet orbits a small, cool star. Its size is a bit bigger than Earth’s, and it is made of rock, not gas. Finding helium escaping is important because it tells us the planet still has some atmosphere, but it is slowly losing it. If it loses too much, the planet might not be able to support life. Studying how fast helium escapes helps scientists guess how long the atmosphere will last. The team thinks the star’s energy heats the planet’s air, causing helium to float away. This is the first time scientists have seen helium leaving a rocky planet in the habitable zone.

People in the comments had many ideas. Some were excited, saying this helps us learn if other planets can keep their air and maybe have life. Others pointed out that losing atmosphere is bad for life, since it can make the planet too dry or cold. A few said that finding helium is tricky, so this discovery is impressive. Some asked if other gases, like water vapor or oxygen, also escape. There were questions about how the star’s light affects the planet’s air. Some worried that if many rocky planets lose their air, it may be hard to find one with life. Others hoped that new telescopes will let us watch more planets like this. A few said this shows how planets change over time. Most agreed this is a good step for learning about worlds outside our solar system.

## Detecting LLM-Generated Texts with “Classical” Machine Learning

- 原文链接: [Detecting LLM-Generated Texts with “Classical” Machine Learning](https://blog.lyc8503.net/en/post/llm-classifier/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48936880)

This article is about building a tool to detect if a story or article was written by a large language model (LLM) like ChatGPT, using simple, older machine learning methods. The author was annoyed by the rise of AI-generated fiction online and wanted to see if it was easy to catch these with basic tools.

The author first tried the “text perplexity” method, which uses AI to guess how likely each word is, but found it unreliable and expensive to run. Instead, the author switched to using classic machine learning: they gathered thousands of human-written and AI-generated stories, split the text into sentences, and trained a model called Linear SVC (from scikit-learn) using TF-IDF features. This method looks at which words appear and how often. The result was surprisingly good: about 85% accuracy at detecting AI-written sentences, even though the model and data were only roughly prepared.

The author tested different models (like Naive Bayes and even BERT), but the simple SVM worked best for speed and accuracy. To cover more AI types, they trained seven different models (one for each LLM) and used majority voting—if two or more models think a sentence is AI, it gets flagged. They also built a web demo that runs in the browser, using a JavaScript version of the model, which is fast enough for normal articles.

Tests showed the detector works well on both seen and unseen AI models. For real, human-written stories posted before AI tools existed, it almost never flagged them as AI (false positives were below 1%). For current online stories, about a third of popular fanfics on one site were flagged as likely AI-generated, even though none were labeled as such. The author also tried tricks to fool the detector, like translating the text or prompting the AI to “write less like an AI,” but these only dropped the detection rate a little.

In the comments, many readers were impressed the method worked so well with basic tools. Some said this shows modern AI writing is still easy to spot statistically, even if it feels human to read. Others pointed out the limits: as LLMs get better at copying human styles, these models might not work as well, and adversarial users could eventually find ways around them. A few worried about bias, since the detector is trained on a fixed set of AI models and writing genres. Some people liked the open, browser-based demo and the transparency compared to commercial “AI checkers.” Others debated if it’s even important to detect AI text, or if we should just accept it as part of the web now. There were also ethical questions about using AI-generated writing in creative spaces, and if these tools might be used to wrongly accuse writers. Overall, most commenters enjoyed the technical breakdown and practical approach—many said they’d try building something similar themselves.

## Mathematics of Data Science

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48939896)

This article is about the math behind data science and how it helps us understand and work with data. The authors made a book that covers many topics, from basic ideas to deep math used in machine learning.

The book starts with high-dimensional data, which means data with many features. It explains why this is both hard (the "curse of dimensionality") and sometimes helpful. There are chapters about singular value decomposition (SVD) and principal component analysis (PCA), which are tools to make big data smaller and easier to work with. The book also talks about linear regression, which is a way to find patterns in data, and regularization, which helps stop models from making mistakes.

There is a section about graphs and networks, showing how we can study connections between things, like people in a social network. Clustering helps group similar data. The book explains both nonlinear and linear ways to reduce the number of features, such as random projections and diffusion maps. Optimization is another key part; it is about finding the best solution, often needed in machine learning.

Other chapters talk about classification (deciding what group something belongs to), deep learning (using neural networks), and the math behind them. The book also covers special topics, like compressive sensing (finding small signals in big data) and low-rank matrix recovery (rebuilding missing data).

In the comments, some people are happy to see a book that brings together all this math in one place. A few say it is great for students or anyone who wants to really understand how machine learning works, not just use the tools. Others note that some parts might be hard for beginners, but it is a good reference to come back to later.

Some users debate if all this math is needed for most data science jobs. A few say you can do a lot with just high-level tools, but others reply that knowing the math helps you use the tools better and avoid mistakes. There is also talk about which chapters are most useful—some like the parts about SVD and PCA, while others are interested in optimization and deep learning.

One person says the book’s focus on high-dimensional data is important, because modern problems often have lots of features. A couple of commenters wish more books explained these ideas as clearly. ## The privacy problems hidden in your period tracker

- 原文链接: [The privacy problems hidden in your period tracker](https://www.bbc.com/future/article/20260715-how-period-trackers-share-womens-private-details)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48939641)

This article looks at privacy problems in period tracker apps. It explains how some of these apps share users’ private health data with other companies.

The Mozilla Foundation checked six popular period tracking apps: Flo, Clue, Stardust, Spot On, Period Calendar, and Euki. Some apps keep your data safe, but others share details like pregnancy status, moods, or symptoms with companies such as Google, Meta, TikTok, and even lesser-known ones like RudderStack. Stardust, for example, sends health data to RudderStack, even though it doesn’t mention this in its privacy policy. Stardust says the partner only helps process data and cannot see your name or contact info, and that they are not allowed to sell or use the data themselves.

Spot On, made by Planned Parenthood, has its own issues. The app does not share data, but if you use some features, it opens Planned Parenthood’s website, which then shares details about your healthcare search with companies like AB Tasty. Mozilla says this problem could be fixed easily, but it has not been.

Most apps also send basic user info to big ad companies for tracking and ads. This includes device info and IDs. Sometimes, you can’t stop this tracking, and even just using a period app could tell others you might have important health data. This could matter, especially since US law changed around abortion.

Euki is the most private app. It saves your data only on your phone and doesn’t even need an account. It even has a “decoy” mode to hide your real data. Flo and Clue do not share health data with others, but they keep it on their own servers, which could still be risky if there were a data breach or government request. Both say they protect your data well and are based in Europe, where laws are stricter.

Mozilla says you should also think about an app’s history. Flo, for example, had a privacy scandal years ago. After that, it got better, but then added new ad partners. Spot On and Clue have also had past issues. Stardust once said it had strong encryption, but later removed this claim.

In the comments, many people are shocked by how much data these apps share. Some say they never trust apps with health data. Others point out that even if apps are legal, it feels wrong for them to share such private details. A few users say they just use pen and paper to track periods to stay safe. Some developers in the comments argue that it’s possible to build good apps that don’t share data, like Euki does. Others mention that people often don’t read privacy policies and don’t realize what they’re agreeing to. There’s debate about whether storing data on servers is ever safe, with some saying only local storage is truly private. Some commenters also note the risk if the law changes and police want this data. A few suggest everyone should push for better laws to protect health privacy. Others feel that companies only change when they’re caught or get bad press. Finally, some people are glad Mozilla did this research, but wish more people knew about these risks.
