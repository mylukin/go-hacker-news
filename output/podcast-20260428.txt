Hello everyone, this is the 2026-04-28 episode of Hacker News Daily Podcast. Today, we bring you a busy day of stories from the world of software, open source, AI, and hardware. Let’s get started.

First, the open source world saw a major move: Ghostty, a popular open source terminal project, is leaving GitHub after 18 years. The project’s maintainer shared a long, personal post about why he can no longer trust GitHub. He remembers when GitHub was full of joy and hope, and even launched projects like Vagrant there, dreaming of working at GitHub one day. GitHub was his place for work and fun, a big part of his life. Even handling issues and bugs was enjoyable before.

But now, he faces daily frustration—GitHub has become slow and unreliable, with frequent outages and problems with GitHub Actions. Sometimes, he cannot do any work for hours. He started marking an X in his journal almost every day when an outage happened, and says his criticism comes from love and disappointment. Because of this, he decided to move Ghostty to a new platform, with a read-only mirror left behind for users. Other personal projects will stay for now, but Ghostty’s move is the main focus.

In the comments, many people agree that GitHub has become worse over time, especially after Microsoft bought it. Developers complain about slow speed, outages, and too much focus on new AI features instead of basic stability. Some users share stories about moving projects to places like GitLab, SourceHut, or self-hosted servers, but admit these have their own problems. Others say the real trouble is not Git itself, but all the extra tools, like CI/CD and issue tracking. A few defend GitHub, saying running a huge platform is hard, and others think the author is too emotional—but many agree that passion is what keeps open source alive. Some worry about where projects can go if all the big platforms have issues.

This leads well into another story: what open source was like before GitHub. The article looks back to the days when developers ran their own servers using tools like Subversion and Trac. Code hosting was difficult, and trust depended on knowing people personally. Projects could disappear if the server went offline. GitHub changed all that—it made sharing code, tracking issues, and connecting much easier. Projects became easier to find, and the community grew quickly. But now, as big projects leave GitHub and complaints rise, the author worries we may lose a lot of open source history if everyone moves away. The article calls for a true, independent archive for open source, so the community’s memory does not depend on any single company.

Listeners on Hacker News remember the bad old days before GitHub, when contributing was hard and mailing lists were confusing. Some say centralization was good for growth, but is risky if the main company changes or disappears. Others hope for new tools to move projects and issues easily, so open source can be free but not fragile.

Next, a story about the risks of centralization and security. Fedora, a big Linux project, recently switched to using Forgejo to host its code. But a researcher found serious security problems in Forgejo, including SSRF, weak authentication, and even ways to get remote code execution. The researcher used a “carrot disclosure” approach: showing proof of the problems but not giving out all the details, hoping to push the Forgejo team to do a full security review.

In the comments, some people are worried Fedora moved too quickly or did not test enough. Others defend Forgejo, saying all software has bugs, and open source projects often lack enough reviewers. Some like the “carrot disclosure” idea, saying it makes projects take security seriously without putting everyone at risk. Many agree Forgejo needs a public audit, and that all fast-growing open source projects should do regular security checks.

Now, let’s turn to AI. There is news about how ChatGPT now shows ads and tracks users when they click on them. When you chat, ChatGPT can insert ads that match your topic—like food delivery if you ask about Beijing, or NBA tickets if you ask about sports. These ads use encrypted tokens to track your actions, and when you click, the site opens inside ChatGPT’s own window so OpenAI can see everything you do. The advertiser’s site also runs a script to save tracking cookies for 30 days and send data back to OpenAI.

Some people in the comments are surprised and worried about this level of tracking. They say OpenAI is now like other big tech companies, focused on ads and user data. Others say ads are expected for free services, but wish for an ad-free paid option. Some discuss the technical details, like Fernet tokens and how tracking works. There are tips for blocking the tracking, such as filtering certain domains or blocking cookies. Many users want more privacy and control, and some worry about how much data is shared with advertisers.

Another AI story highlights how easy it is to trick LLMs and search engines. A writer created a fake news story and a Wikipedia edit claiming he won a world championship for the card game 6 Nimmt!—even though no such event exists. He spent just $12 on a new website, wrote a fake press release, and linked it to Wikipedia. AI chatbots picked up the false story and started repeating it as fact. The author warns that this attack could be used for much more harm. He advises users to check sources carefully and for LLM makers to show where their answers come from.

Listeners agree this is a big problem. Some remember that Wikipedia and search engines have always faced this risk, but AI makes it faster and easier for false facts to spread. Some hope for more transparency and better tools to spot fake information, while others say users must always check facts for themselves.

Let’s move to AI systems in real work. One team shared how they cut AI costs by using two types of language models: a cheap one for easy tasks and an expensive one for the hard cases. They process CI logs to find build failures. Most failures are repeats, so the cheap agent (Haiku) checks first, using exact and semantic search. Only new or unclear issues go to the expensive model (Opus). The expensive model then plans what to check, and sends small, clear tasks to the cheap model. Data is pulled with SQL as needed, instead of sending huge logs at once. This saves money and keeps context focused.

Readers like this layered approach, saying it is smart and practical. Some are surprised that upgrading to a better model actually lowered costs, since the system is more efficient. Others talk about possible risks if the cheap model misses something, and discuss ways to tune the system. Many hope to use this pattern for other large data tasks, but warn that too many layers can be hard to debug.

Still on AI, Claude, another AI tool, now works with popular creative apps like Blender, Adobe, and Autodesk. With special connectors, Claude can help write scripts, edit 3D models, batch process images, and even search sound libraries. It can also act as a tutor, explain how to use tools, and help students and teachers learn new skills. Anthropic, Claude’s maker, is working with schools to test these features.

On Hacker News, some people are excited about AI helping with boring creative tasks, while others worry about losing skills or jobs. Some like that Blender’s connector is open for any AI, not just Claude. There are concerns about privacy and data control. Teachers want to see real examples before using Claude in class. Overall, many agree the tools could help, but want to watch how it changes creative work.

Another big AI story: OpenAI’s newest models are now available on Amazon’s AWS Bedrock platform. This comes after OpenAI and Microsoft changed their deal, so OpenAI is no longer exclusive to Azure. Now, companies using AWS can use OpenAI’s models directly, with features like security, permissions, and logging built in. The service is designed to be easy and safe to use, and customer data stays inside AWS.

People in the comments have mixed views. Some are excited, saying it will help both startups and big companies use strong AI faster. Others worry it will make AWS and OpenAI too powerful and may lead to vendor lock-in. Some think it is just a business move to catch up with Anthropic and Google. There are questions about costs, hardware, and whether this will hurt Azure. Most agree it is a big step for AI in the cloud, but there are risks to watch for.

Next, a technical bug in Claude Code is causing trouble for users. A reminder about malware is being injected into every file read by Claude Code, starting from version 2.1.111. The reminder tells agents not to improve or change code, but it is not clear that this only applies to malware. As a result, many agents refuse to edit any code, even safe files, breaking workflows and wasting tokens. The bug also makes agents more suspicious and less useful for real coding tasks. Users are frustrated, and many call for a quick fix.

In the comments, people share their own problems and failed workarounds. Some joke about Claude treating every file as “not malware” again and again, but still refusing to help. Most ask for a fast fix and say the bug is hurting productivity and trust in Claude.

Finally, a new hardware review: the Intel Arc Pro B70 GPU, built for professional and AI work. This card has double the Xe cores and VRAM of the B50—now 32 GB—and higher power use. It is more expensive than the B50 and some NVIDIA and AMD cards, but offers more VRAM for the price.

In tests, the B70 did well in some creative apps and was the fastest in AI tasks thanks to the big VRAM. But for many graphics or video jobs, NVIDIA and AMD cards were faster or a better deal. Power use is high, and some users hope for a cheaper version with less memory. Many are glad to see Intel making progress, and hope they keep improving. For now, the B70 is a strong pick for AI tasks that need lots of memory, but for most other work, NVIDIA or AMD may still be better.
