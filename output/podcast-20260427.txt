Hello everyone, this is the 2026-04-27 episode of Hacker News Daily Podcast. Today, we have a full set of stories from the world of tech, software, and science. Let’s get started.

First, there is a major outage on the npm website. Many developers found they could not access the main site, though other npm services like package installation and publishing are still working fine. The outage began on April 27, and the npm team is still investigating the cause. The status page shows that searching for packages, security audits, and replication feeds are all running, but the main website is down. The team is posting updates on the status page and Twitter, and they explain which services are affected. Most users have not seen an outage like this in a long time, as npm has been very reliable. In the comments, some users are frustrated because they use the npm site every day. Others note that installing packages with npm still works, so most projects are not blocked. Some share workarounds, like using the command line or third-party sites to find package info. There’s also discussion about how even big, important sites can go down, and why it is important to have backups or mirrors. A few users joke about checking their own internet before learning it was a global outage. Many thank the npm team for quick updates and hope the site comes back soon.

Next, Microsoft and OpenAI are ending their exclusive deal. This means OpenAI can now offer its AI models to other cloud companies like Amazon. Before, only Microsoft could sell OpenAI’s models, and they shared part of the money with OpenAI. Now, OpenAI is free to make new deals, and Microsoft will not have to pay a share on future sales. This change could help OpenAI grow faster and reach more users. In the comments, people have mixed feelings. Some say this is good for competition, as more cloud companies can use OpenAI’s models. Others think Microsoft will lose its lead in AI, but some believe Microsoft is happy to save money. A few wonder if OpenAI will stay strong without Microsoft’s support, while others expect OpenAI to grow even faster. Some worry about too much power in one company’s hands, so more partners might be safer. Others hope this will make AI tools cheaper and easier for everyone. There is also debate about whether this really helps small companies or just the big cloud providers. Many agree it is a normal business move, and deals change when money is involved.

Our next story asks an interesting question: do people see and name colors in the same way? The article links to a test where you decide if a color is more “blue” or “green.” The idea is that people can look at the same color but call it different names. Some languages use one word for both blue and green, while others use two. The test shows how color categories are learned, not just natural. In the comments, some people say the test made them unsure about color names. Others share stories of arguing with friends or family about whether something is blue or green. Some mention that screens can change how colors look, and that even in English, the line between blue and green is unclear. There are also comments about color blindness and how it affects this question. Many like the test as a fun way to show a real science problem, while others think it does not matter much as long as we can talk about color.

In Toronto, three men have been arrested for using an “SMS blaster,” a device that copies a real cell tower and sends fake text messages to thousands of phones. This is the first time such technology has been found in Canada. The SMS blaster tricks phones into connecting, then sends fake texts that look like they come from trusted companies, hoping people will click on links and give away passwords or bank details. The device was moved around the city in cars, making it hard to catch. Police say tens of thousands of phones were affected, and millions of calls may have been blocked from reaching real cell towers, even emergency calls. The police worked with many groups to arrest the suspects and collect evidence. They remind everyone to avoid clicking on strange links and use only official apps for banking. In the comments, people are surprised and worried that a device can block so many phones. Some want phone companies to do more to protect users and wonder why security is still weak. Others mention similar attacks in other countries and talk about the need for better public education about such scams. Many agree that both police and phone companies must get better at stopping these new tricks.

Next, we have a story about Easyduino, a collection of open source PCB designs for popular microcontroller boards like Arduino, ESP32, Raspberry Pi Pico, and STM32 Bluepill, all made with KiCad. The goal is to let anyone study, change, or build these boards using free tools. The designs match the original boards as closely as possible, but some parts were changed for price or availability, and USB-C has been added for easier use. Each folder has a readme that explains the differences and the parts used. The files are ready for JLCPCB production and are under the CERN Open Hardware License, so anyone can use them, even for business. In the comments, many people like having open KiCad designs for popular boards and say it helps with learning or starting custom projects. Some point out challenges, like hard-to-find parts or board complexity, but they appreciate that the project explains these issues. There are also requests for more boards and tips for editing in KiCad. The community is excited and supportive, with many giving thanks and suggestions.

Spanish archaeologists have found more than 30 shipwrecks in the Bay of Gibraltar, including ships from the fifth century BC to World War II. The team mapped 151 sites, with 134 sunken ships from many times and countries. One interesting find is a Spanish gunboat from the 1700s, with a wooden box that looked like it might hide secret papers, but it only held combs. The team worries about damage from new port buildings, rising sea levels, and new sea plants. To help protect the wrecks, they made 3D models and videos for online and museum viewing. In the comments, people are amazed by the finds and ask about the technology used for underwater archaeology. Some discuss legal issues—who owns the wrecks and if treasure hunters can take things. Others worry that showing locations online could help thieves. There are also comments about climate change and the need for more protection worldwide. Some share stories about shipwrecks in other countries, and a few hope for a “real” treasure ship full of gold.

In another big story, China has stopped Meta from buying Manus, an AI startup from Singapore that started in China, in a $2 billion deal. The Chinese government told both companies to withdraw after checking for legal and security risks. Manus is known for general AI agents that help with research, coding, and data analysis. The company moved to Singapore to grow and avoid China’s rules. Both China and the US watched the deal closely, as the US blocks money going to Chinese AI and China does not want its tech talent to leave. After the block, Meta said the deal followed all laws and hoped for a solution. In the comments, many see this as part of the tech war between the US and China. Some say China is just doing what the US has done to protect its own tech. Others think “Singapore-washing” may not work anymore. Some worry this will slow AI growth and limit startup options. There is also debate about whether it is fair for countries to protect key technology, or if it will hurt global cooperation.

Another article talks about RF engineering, which is becoming important again after years of less interest. The author, who works in aerospace, started as a software engineer but had to learn RF because new projects need both hardware and software skills. After the dot-com bubble, most engineers chose software, and RF jobs moved overseas or stayed in defense companies. Now, more satellites, 5G, and soon 6G, plus car radar and IoT, all need RF engineers. There is a big shortage, and salaries are going up. In the comments, people agree that RF engineers are hard to find. Some say universities don’t teach enough RF, making the shortage worse. Others note that hardware skills will always be needed, even as software grows. Some are excited to learn RF, and others warn it is hard and takes patience. There are also tips on online courses and open-source tools for beginners. Most agree RF engineering is making a real comeback.

Apple is preparing big networking changes in macOS 27. They may remove AFP file sharing, and will require stronger security, like TLS 1.2 or newer for some network connections. AFP is Apple’s old file-sharing protocol, but Apple has used SMB as the main method for years. If you use old devices that don’t support SMB, you might not be able to upgrade. Also, soon only servers with safe encryption and valid certificates will work with Apple devices for things like device management, app installs, and updates. There is no easy way to check if your setup is ready—you must check logs with special tools. The developer beta starts in June, public beta in July, and final release in September. In the comments, some users worry about losing old features and having trouble with older hardware. Others focus on the need for better security. Some miss Apple’s old Server app. Most agree the changes will be hard for some, but will make Macs safer in the long run.

A big data breach happened at Mercor, where hackers stole 4 terabytes of voice samples and ID documents from 40,000 AI contractors. This is serious because it links each person’s voice to their real identity, making it easy for attackers to clone voices and trick banks, companies, or family members. The article gives advice to people who may be affected: remove public voice recordings, set up codewords with banks and family, change voiceprint logins, and use tools to check for fake messages. ORAVYS, the company writing the article, offers free checks for people affected by the breach. In the comments, many are shocked by the amount and danger of the data stolen. Some think companies should not collect both voice and ID. Others wonder why banks still use voice for security. There is debate about whether leaks like this can be stopped once data is collected, and some hope for stronger laws and better detection tools to protect people in the future.
