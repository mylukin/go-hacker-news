Our first story is about the value of writing by hand, especially in a world filled with typing and digital devices. The author, a writer who uses fountain pens, explains that writing by hand activates more parts of the brain than typing and helps you think better. Each word you write by hand is a small problem to solve—spacing, crossing “t”s, dotting “i”s—and your whole arm works together with your mind. The author notes that some schools are returning to handwritten exams because of AI, but many students now have poor handwriting or have forgotten how to write by hand.

He shares his own experience: after writing thousands of book pages by hand, he never gets sore or cramps like he did as a child. He says pain often comes from pressing too hard, more common with pencils or older ballpoint pens. Fountain pens or gel pens need less force, so your hand stays relaxed. Writing on glass with a stylus, he says, has too little friction and can make you even more tired. Good paper is also important; cheap printer paper makes ink spread and look messy, while paper with some cotton is much better. He encourages people to use only one side of the paper and not worry about using too much.

Cursive writing is easier and less tiring than printing each letter. Most people today write only for themselves, so perfect penmanship is not needed. He also gives tips for fountain pen users: keep the nib up on airplanes to avoid leaks, and don’t worry about ink on your fingers—it washes off. For left-handed writers, he says he has not had issues with smudging if you use the right paper.

He suggests starting with simple pens and paper, trying out what works, and not spending too much at first. Cheap gel pens can be almost as good as fountain pens, and expensive pens are more about style than writing quality.

In the comments, many people agree that writing by hand helps memory and feels more personal. Some prefer typing because it’s faster and easier to edit, or because their handwriting is hard to read. Others say their hands get tired, blaming poor pen or paper quality or lack of practice. Some share their favorite pens and notebooks, and teachers mention that students now struggle with handwriting. There’s a wish for schools to teach cursive again. Some say writing by hand is slower, but that’s good because it makes you think. Left-handed people are split—some have no smudging problems, others still do. Many agree that writing by hand has benefits, but it’s not easy for everyone.

Next, we have a heartbreaking story from China. A couple paid over $800,000 for an experimental gene-editing treatment for their daughter, Mei, who had a rare genetic disorder. The new therapy used base editing to fix a small DNA mistake, but sadly, Mei died one week after the treatment because of a severe immune reaction.

Mei’s parents paid for much of the research and the procedure, hoping to help her live a more normal life. The therapy used viruses to deliver gene-editing instructions into her brain. In animal tests, results looked promising, but some experts later said these tests were not fully safe. Doctors warned of possible side effects, but the risk of death was not clearly explained. After the treatment, Mei developed a fever, kidney problems, and blood issues—all known risks for this kind of therapy. She died in the hospital a few days later.

The hospital admitted the treatment caused Mei’s death. The event was not made public, and the hospital received only a small fine. The researchers faced no serious punishment, and the ethics board had not reviewed all safety data before the trial. The parents were upset by the lack of transparency and accountability, especially as the research still got published in a top science journal without mentioning Mei’s death.

On Hacker News, many commenters expressed deep sadness and anger. Some said it’s tragic that parents must risk so much for hope, and that desperate families should not be funding such experiments. Others called for stronger rules and oversight for human trials, especially with new gene-editing methods. Some compared this to past medical tragedies and stressed the need for openness when things go wrong. There was debate about who was more responsible—the researchers or the parents—but most agreed the system failed Mei and her family. Many said that trust in doctors and scientists is vital, and this trust was betrayed. Some called for global standards to make sure tragic outcomes are always made public to protect future patients.

Echo uses modern open models like Mixtral and Llama 3, and the team shares all code and instructions for anyone to try or improve. Moving on, we have a story about the beam engine and how it changed the world during the Industrial Revolution. The article explains how steam can be turned into useful power and how each part of the engine works. Early engines used steam pressure to push pistons, and Thomas Newcomen made the first successful steam engine for pumping water out of mines. His engine used a lot of coal but worked better than using horses.

James Watt improved the design by adding a separate condenser, making the engine much more efficient and smooth. The engine uses parts like a slide valve, crank, flywheel, and governor to control steam flow, keep speed steady, and turn back-and-forth motion into spinning power for factories. With steam engines, factories no longer needed to be near rivers, so cities grew quickly.

Commenters on Hacker News praised the article’s clear writing and interactive diagrams. Some shared stories of seeing old engines in museums. Others explained how early engines were big steps but not very efficient. There were discussions about the dangers of early boilers, the importance of the flywheel and governor, and how moving from water to steam changed where factories could be built. Some readers debated whether steam power or electric power did more to change the world, and thanked the author for making a hard topic easy and fun.

Now let’s talk about TheNumbers.com, a famous movie data website that went offline in March 2026 due to too much bot and AI traffic. The site started in 1997 and tracked over 78,000 movies. In recent years, AI bots and scrapers flooded the site, making up 90% of the traffic. This heavy load crashed the old website and made it too unsafe to restore. A smaller version was put online to keep basic box office numbers available.

The main reason for the attacks was to get early data for betting in prediction markets. The article explains that AI has made it easier for anyone, even with little skill, to hack or scrape websites. Other sites, like Read the Docs and Wikipedia, have also suffered from too much bot traffic. The cost of running these sites has gone up, while the number of human visitors has dropped as people now get info from AI summaries instead.

Companies like Cloudflare are trying to help by blocking unpaid crawlers or making AI companies pay to crawl. But it’s not clear if this will solve the problem.

On Hacker News, many people are sad and worried about losing useful free sites. Developers shared stories of their own sites being overwhelmed. Some said AI companies are not being fair when they take data without giving back. Others discussed blocking bots or charging them, but noted it is hard to enforce. There was debate about whether AI is helping or hurting the web, and some called for better bot detection tools. Many agreed the problem is big and affects everyone who uses or builds websites today.

Next, we look at building apps on ATProto, the protocol behind Bluesky. The author went to a conference and wanted to build review apps on ATProto but found problems with how privacy and data control are handled. ATProto supports public data well, but if you want private or “permissioned” data, it is handled by a separate system, making app development much harder. If users want to change a review from private to public, it is not simple and loses likes and links.

The author also hoped ATProto would let you work offline, like Git, but it stores your data on a server. To support offline use, you must build your own system and handle both public and private data separately. This makes things hard for developers who want to give users full control and offline access.

In the comments, some agree that managing public and private data should be simpler and unified, while others say keeping them separate is safer. Some developers say this will slow down adoption, while others note that identity features already help. There are hopes that the community will improve ATProto, and some miss the old internet with open standards and real user control. Technical commenters discuss possible solutions, but agree it’s a hard problem. The overall mood is hopeful, but right now, ATProto is not easy for privacy-focused developers.

Moving to science, we have news on a new, cleaner way to separate rare earth metals, which are important for things like electric car motors and LED lights. The usual way uses lots of toxic chemicals, but this new method uses water and electricity. Researchers at the University of Chicago, with help from Northwestern and Argonne, used a special layered manganese oxide to separate rare earth elements based on their size and water shells. Adding magnesium ions helped control the process even better, letting them separate elements like neodymium and lanthanum to high purity. The process uses only water and is safer for the environment, though it’s not yet ready for big factories.

In the comments, people were excited about a cleaner way to process rare earth metals. Some pointed out that mining itself is still bad for the environment, but this is a step forward. Others wondered if the method could work for other metals. Some discussed how rare earth processing is mostly done in certain countries, and this could help others set up their own plants. The science behind using ion size and water shells was praised, and some users hoped this would lead to more ideas for cleaner industry.
