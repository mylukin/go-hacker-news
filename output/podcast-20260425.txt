First, let’s talk about beans, gas, and science. An article looked at why beans make people gassy, and if there are ways to stop it. The reason is that beans have special sugars called oligosaccharides, like stachyose, which our bodies cannot digest. When these sugars reach our gut, bacteria eat them and make gas. Many people think soaking beans, cooking with bay leaves, or using canned beans can help. The writer worked with Harvard scientists to test 17 popular remedies. They found that soaking, pressure cooking, using bay leaves or kombu, and even blanching do not really help. In fact, bay leaves sometimes made things worse. Canned beans had a little less gas, but not much. Rinsing canned beans reduced gas by about 20%, but you lose flavor. The big winner was Beano—a pill with an enzyme called alpha-galactosidase. It worked very well in bean puree or dips, but less so with whole beans. The Harvard “Fart Squad” also tested this on real people, and those eating bean dip with Beano had less gas. In the comments, people were surprised soaking does not help. Some care more about flavor and don’t want to rinse away the tasty bean liquid. Others joked that everyone has their own “secret trick,” but science does not support most of them. Some said you get used to beans after eating them often, and a few wondered if removing gas-causing parts also removes health benefits. In the end, most agree beans are a little gassy, but worth it for a healthy, tasty meal.

Next, there’s a story about the Free Universal Construction Kit. This is a set of 3D-printable adapters that let you connect different brands of building toys, like Lego, K’Nex, and Tinkertoy. The kit has about 80 adapters, which means you can mix and match pieces from ten popular toy sets. The creators made these because kids and adults want to join their toys, but companies do not make parts that fit together. Now, with 3D printing, you can print adapters at home. The project is free for personal use and is meant to encourage creativity and fight against company “lock-in.” There are some legal risks because of patents and trademarks, but most adapters are for older toys where patents have ended. In the comments, people loved the idea, saying they wanted something like this as kids. Some worry about how well the adapters fit, as home 3D printers are not as precise as factory molds. Others discussed legal risks, but most just enjoyed the playful hack. Many users shared stories of making their own adapters with glue, and some hope for more adapters in the future as 3D printing improves.

Now, let’s look at a creative art project. Someone made a 1-bit pixel art version of Hokusai’s “The Great Wave” using an old Macintosh computer. The artist plans to recreate all 36 views of Mount Fuji in this style, using the original Mac’s screen size and only black and white pixels. They use Aldus SuperPaint 3.0 on a Quadra 700 or PowerBook 100, both running System 7. The project is just for fun and nostalgia, and the artist shares the pixel art as desktop backgrounds for vintage Macs. In the comments, people praised the attention to detail and the challenge of using only black and white. Some shared their own memories of making pixel art on early Macs, and others talked about how Susan Kare’s early pixel art changed digital design. Many wished the artist good luck with the full set, and liked that the work is shared under a Creative Commons license.

In the comments, some agree that AI is helpful for finally finishing old ideas. Others warn that overusing AI can make you forget how to code, and that AI sometimes makes mistakes. People also talk about the need to set clear rules for AI, and that working on tough projects yourself is still important for learning.

Now, on to hardware. There is news about new 10 Gigabit Ethernet USB adapters that are smaller, cooler, and less expensive than old Thunderbolt models. These new adapters use the Realtek RTL8159 chip and work over USB 3.2. For example, the WisdPi adapter costs $80. But to get full 10 Gbps speed, you need a computer with a USB 3.2 Gen 2x2 port. Most laptops and Macs only get about 6 to 7 Gbps, even if the adapter says 10 Gbps. It’s often hard to know what USB version your port is, and many computers do not label them clearly. If you do not need the highest speed, 2.5 or 5 Gbps adapters are a better value. The new USB adapters use less power and do not get as hot as older models. In the comments, people are happy these adapters are cheaper and smaller, but many warn about USB port confusion. Some say Thunderbolt is still better for real 10 Gbps speeds, but others like the new adapters for being safer and easier to use. There are also tips about checking driver support, and people share that the adapters work well for home servers or quick backups.

Next up, we have a deep look at how people judge the quality of knowledge work, like reports or code. The article says that most people use surface details—like good formatting and no typos—as shortcuts to judge quality, because checking the real work takes too long. With AI tools like ChatGPT, this problem is getting worse. AI can make reports and code that look perfect, but may not be correct underneath. The author calls this a “simulacrum”—an imitation—of real work, where only what looks good matters. In the comments, many agree that this is a problem, but some say it has always been this way, even before AI. Some worry that real quality is getting lost, while others think AI helps save time. A few hope for new tools to check deeper quality, and some joke that soon, even the reviewers will be AI. A few say careful, real work will become more valuable as it becomes rare.

Now, let’s look at a new IDE called Mine, made for Coalton and Common Lisp. Mine works on Windows, macOS, and Linux, and has a full app and a terminal version. You can mix Coalton—a statically typed functional language—with Common Lisp, and use a built-in REPL for quick testing. The IDE shows errors, warnings, and optimization hints as you code. There are built-in lessons for structural editing, but you don’t have to use them. All code is compiled to native binaries for speed. In the comments, people like that Mine brings modern IDE features to Lisp and functional programming. Many are happy it’s cross-platform and works on Linux. Some discuss how easy it is to mix Coalton and Lisp, and others wonder about the “no dependencies” claim. People also like the REPL integration, and hope that better tools like this will help Lisp become more popular.

We also have news from the world of science and writing. Desmond Morris, the British zoologist, author, artist, and TV presenter, has died at 98. He was famous for his 1967 book "The Naked Ape," which argued that humans are still very much like other apes. Morris looked at human behavior as animal behavior, explaining things like fighting, eating, and sex as natural acts. Some of his ideas were controversial, especially about gender and culture, but he helped people see humans as part of nature. In the comments, many remember reading his book and being inspired to think differently about human behavior. Some think his ideas are too simple, but most agree that he helped make science popular and interesting.

Another story is about Martin Galway, a famous composer for Commodore 64 games in the 1980s. Galway has shared his old music source files on GitHub, including code and samples from games like "Wizball." The files are written in assembly language and show special tricks for the C64’s SID sound chip. Galway asks for credit if you use his work, and hopes people will learn from these files. In the comments, people call this a “treasure” and share memories of Galway’s music in their childhood. Some discuss the technical side, and others talk about the importance of saving digital history. There are also questions about how to play or assemble the files today.
