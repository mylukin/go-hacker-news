## For first time, a cell built from scratch grows and divides

- 原文链接: [For first time, a cell built from scratch grows and divides](https://www.quantamagazine.org/for-the-first-time-a-cell-built-from-scratch-grows-and-divides-20260701/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48747304)

Scientists have made a cell from nonliving parts that can grow and divide, something never done before. The cell is not truly alive, but it acts more like life than anything built in a lab so far.

The team made this cell by putting natural biological molecules into a membrane. They gave it a small genome, the tools to copy DNA, and a way to make proteins. The cell cannot make its own food or energy, so researchers fed it extra supplies through special “feeder” bubbles. To help it divide, they used a trick from another study: they put certain proteins on the membrane that attract more proteins, crowding the surface and making the membrane pinch in two.

This process took a lot of trial and error. Getting all the parts to work together was hard. When the cell finally grew, copied its DNA, and divided, it was a huge success. Scientists call this a big step toward building life from nonlife. Still, the cell is simple: it needs constant help, cannot survive on its own, and does not have ways to remove waste or defend itself.

The team named their tiny creations “spudcells” as a joke. Under a microscope, they look like small blobs, but to the researchers, they are very special. The scientists also tried to change the DNA to see if the cells could evolve. Some cells grew bigger and divided faster, but this was not true evolution, since the changes were added by the researchers, not by random mutations. The cell’s DNA-copying enzyme works too well and does not make enough mistakes for real evolution.

Experts in the field say this work is amazing, showing that it is possible to build many parts of a living cell from scratch. However, some warn not to say this is real life yet, as the cell cannot make everything it needs or keep going on its own. The next steps will be to help the cells make their own ribosomes (the part that builds proteins) and maybe add a cytoskeleton (the cell’s inner structure).

In the Hacker News comments, many readers were impressed and excited by this breakthrough. Some saw it as a step toward understanding the origin of life and predicting that it could one day help make new drugs or materials. Others pointed out that the cell is not truly alive and reminded everyone that it cannot live without help from humans. A few commenters worried about the risks if these cells become more advanced, but others said the cells are still very basic and safe.

Some users discussed the challenges of getting evolution to happen in these simple cells, agreeing that copying errors in DNA are important for real evolution. Others wondered if this work might help answer big questions about what life really is and how it might start on other planets. A few debated whether making life from scratch is better than editing existing cells, sharing different views on the best way forward.

Overall, the community agreed this was a big scientific step, even if there is much work left before creating fully living cells. Many were curious to see what comes next and hoped the open sharing of data would speed up more discoveries in synthetic biology.

## What to learn to be a graphics programmer

- 原文链接: [What to learn to be a graphics programmer](https://blog.demofox.org/2026/07/01/what-to-learn-to-be-a-graphics-programmer/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48750710)

This article talks about what you should learn if you want to be a real-time graphics programmer, which is someone who makes computer images and effects in games and other programs.

First, the writer says being a graphics programmer is like having two jobs. One job is learning the CPU side, using APIs like DirectX12, Vulkan, or Metal, and knowing how to load and manage assets. The other job is learning the GPU side, which is about making images look good and run fast with techniques like lighting, shading, and post-processing effects. The writer suggests you can focus on one side first; for example, use simpler tools for the CPU if you want to focus on the GPU side.

For the GPU side, you should learn about path tracing, which is how movies make realistic images. There is a free online book called "Ray Tracing in One Weekend" that is a good start. You should also learn about Physically Based Rendering (PBR), which helps make game assets look good in any lighting. Before PBR, people had to make many versions of assets for different lights, but PBR saves this work.

The article also shares links to learn more about PBR, and even deeper topics like Filament documentation and the PBRT book. The author says it is good to have code samples you can share, like a small engine that loads models and uses PBR and other effects, or a path tracer. Bonus points if you can compare your real-time rendering with your path tracer and explain any differences.

On the math side, you mostly need linear algebra, basic trigonometry, and a little calculus. Knowing basic data structures and algorithms helps too, but often the simplest ones are fastest. In game development, C++ is the main language, though some people use Rust or JavaScript (with WebGPU). For shaders, HLSL is most common, but GLSL is used too.

The author also talks about machine learning (ML). They think ML is not as useful as people say right now, but it can be good for some things like talking about math or finding bugs. They do not think ML can replace most programming yet, but maybe in the future.

In the comments, many people agree that learning both the CPU and GPU sides is hard, and most programmers focus on just one. Some say you should start with simple projects, like drawing a triangle or making a small ray tracer, and build from there. Others talk about the importance of math, saying linear algebra is key and you should not be afraid of it. A few people share their learning paths, like using OpenGL first, then moving to more modern APIs.

Some commenters suggest that understanding how hardware works is also important, so you know what makes code run fast or slow. Others think it is important to read code from open source projects to see how real engines work. Some people mention that having a portfolio with small, finished projects is more useful than trying to make a big engine from scratch.

A few commenters talk about programming languages. Most agree C++ is still the main language, but some think Rust might become more popular in the future. Some people like using WebGPU for learning, since it is easier to get started in a browser. Many agree that showing your work on GitHub or similar is important when looking for jobs.

A few people are less sure about the future of graphics programming with new tools like AI and ML. Some are worried that jobs might change, but most still think learning the basics is the best way to start.

## FFmpeg 9.1's new AAC encoder

- 原文链接: [FFmpeg 9.1's new AAC encoder](https://hydrogenaudio.org/index.php/topic,129691.0.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48747116)

FFmpeg 9.1 now includes a fully rewritten AAC audio encoder. The developer rebuilt every part, focusing on better rate control and smarter use of coding tools like PNS, TNS, I/S, and M/S. Tests show this new encoder beats other popular AAC encoders—like qaac and fdk-aac—using several quality metrics and the developer’s own listening tests. The encoder works best at a constant bitrate (CBR), not in variable bitrate (VBR) mode. It was mainly tuned for 48kHz audio, which the developer says is now the standard, but it still works with 44.1kHz and 96kHz files. Special tricks help avoid wasting bits on sounds you can’t hear, and the encoder turns off extra tools by itself if they’re not needed. There’s also a workaround for a known bug in FFmpeg’s stereo PNS decoding. The developer tested the encoder on 3,000 tracks, mostly music, and made sure it prints extra stats for users who want to report problems.

In the comments, some users point out that most audio in the world is still 44.1kHz, not 48kHz, but the developer says resampling is easy and recommends 48kHz for best results. One person asks what “tuned by ear” means, and the developer explains they adjusted settings based on how the music sounded to them. Several people hope this new encoder will fix the poor quality of the old one, which has been criticized in past listening tests. Some users wish the rewrite had made it into current audio tests, but look forward to comparing new and old results. Testers tried the new encoder with various songs and bitrates—most found clear improvements, especially at 100kbps and above, though some still noticed small issues at low bitrates or with certain sound samples. There are also reports of minor artifacts, like ticking sounds, which might be linked to specific settings, and suggestions for possible fixes. Others share command-line tips for using the new encoder and discuss setting cutoffs to help with audio quality. Overall, most users are excited about the improvements, but some still want more fine-tuning for tricky audio cases.

## Box3D, an open source 3D physics engine

- 原文链接: [Box3D, an open source 3D physics engine](https://box2d.org/posts/2026/06/announcing-box3d/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48745445)

Box3D is a new open source 3D physics engine, created as an upgrade to Box2D for use in modern 3D games. The author built it because his game, The Legend of California, needed better physics than what Unreal’s built-in engine could offer. He had trouble with Unreal’s Chaos engine, especially with spinning objects and falling trees, so he tried other engines and even used a version of Valve’s Rubikon engine before finally making Box3D.

Box3D keeps the same core as Box2D, but adds features like triangle mesh collision, height-field collision, and compound collision for big worlds with many objects. It has a C API, uses C17, supports multi-threading, and aims for cross-platform determinism. The author wanted control and speed, especially for complex things like fast redwood trees falling on voxel terrain and managing thousands of objects at once. He also built a system to load many collision meshes as a single shape to save memory and time.

The second reason for making Box3D is personal: the author wants a physics engine he can keep working on across jobs, to capture all his knowledge and share it openly. Thanks to support from his company, Box3D is open source and anyone can use or help develop it. The author isn’t trying to compete with other engines—he just loves physics programming.

Box3D is already in use in a few games, including s&box, Esoterica, and a multiplayer space game. The code is on GitHub, with samples, documentation, and a community on the Box2D Discord. It’s still early (alpha) and will keep improving, with plans for more features and eventually accepting pull requests.

In the comments, many people are excited to see an open source 3D physics engine from the creator of Box2D. Some praise the author’s openness and long-term support for his projects. Others are happy about the C API, which makes it easy to use in many languages and on different platforms. There are questions about performance, real-world use cases, and how it compares to other engines like Jolt, Bullet, or PhysX. Some users are curious about the challenges in making a physics engine scale to thousands of objects, and a few even share stories of their own struggles with game physics. A few people point out that documentation and good examples are key for adoption. Others discuss the pros and cons of rolling your own engine versus using middleware, with some agreeing that for big, unique projects, custom solutions can be best. There’s also appreciation for the author sharing his journey, technical choices, and opening up his code for the community.

## Internal Combustion Engine (2021)

- 原文链接: [Internal Combustion Engine (2021)](https://ciechanow.ski/internal-combustion-engine/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48746076)

This article explains how an internal combustion engine works, starting from simple ideas and building up to a real car engine. It shows how engines turn small explosions into the turning motion that powers cars, using many moving parts working together.

The article begins with a simple crank, which turns a push or pull into spinning movement. It then explains how a cannon works, with burning gunpowder pushing a cannonball out. To make this power turn a shaft, the cannonball is replaced by a piston. The piston moves up and down inside a cylinder, and is connected to a crankshaft that turns as the piston moves. This setup leads to the basic idea of the engine—using controlled explosions to push the piston down.

To make the engine run smoothly, fuel and air are mixed, set on fire by a spark, and the waste gases are pushed out, in a repeating process called the four-stroke cycle: intake, compression, power, and exhaust. The article shows how more than one cylinder is used in real engines to make them run more evenly and reduce shaking.

Many important engine parts are explained: the engine block (which holds everything), the crankshaft (which turns the up-and-down motion into spinning), pistons (which move up and down), piston rings (which help seal the chamber and keep oil in place), and valves (which let fuel and air in and exhaust out). It describes how oil is pumped through the engine to reduce friction, and how a flywheel helps the engine run more smoothly by storing energy.

Modern engines use computers to control fuel injection and sparks for better efficiency. Timing belts keep everything working together at the right time. The article also explains why engines have several cylinders, how the pressure and speed inside the engine change quickly, and how all these forces are balanced. Other parts, like the cooling and oil systems, manifold, and sometimes a turbocharger, help the engine work better and last longer.

Several extra resources are listed for people who want to learn more, such as videos and courses. The article finishes by saying that, while electric cars are taking over, the classic engine is still an amazing example of careful engineering.

In the Hacker News comments, many people praise the article’s clear animations and simple explanations. Some say they wish they had this kind of resource when first learning about engines. Others point out how helpful visualizations can be for understanding complex systems. A few commenters add their own stories about fixing engines or learning from old cars.

There are discussions about how electric vehicles are replacing internal combustion engines, with some saying that electric motors are much simpler and easier to maintain. Others mention that, even though electric cars are the future, understanding how classic engines work is still valuable for learning mechanical thinking.

Some users talk about how the article could help people who want to repair or build engines themselves. Others note that the author makes a difficult topic feel friendly and fun. A few technical readers add details about engine timing, fuel injection technology, or the differences between engine types.

A handful of people ask questions about things not covered in the article, like two-stroke engines, diesel engines, or rotary engines. Some share links to other favorite resources or YouTube channels. Overall, readers agree that this article is a great place to start learning how engines work, whether for hobby or study.
