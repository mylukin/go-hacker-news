First, Apple just announced “Apple Business,” a new platform for companies to manage their devices, email, calendars, and customer reach. Launching on April 14, Apple Business puts together tools for device setup, security, and user management. Even companies without an IT team can use simple “Blueprints” to prepare Apple devices for new workers. The service works with Google Workspace and Microsoft Entra ID, and it lets companies buy extra iCloud storage and AppleCare+ for support. Businesses can use their own email domains, manage brand profiles, and place ads in Apple Maps to help customers find them. Apple says privacy is strong—ads and location data are not linked to user accounts. Apple Business will be free worldwide and will replace several old Apple business services. In the comments, people like the easy setup and integrated tools, but some worry about lock-in and if Apple can really compete with Google or Microsoft on email and calendar. There are also mixed feelings about ads in Maps and about how advanced the device controls are. Many are waiting to try it before deciding.

Next, there’s a serious warning about the Python package litellm. Versions 1.82.7 and 1.82.8 on PyPI are compromised with malware. In 1.82.8, the package includes a hidden script that runs every time Python starts, even if you don’t use litellm directly. This script steals secrets from your machine, like environment variables, SSH keys, and even crypto wallets, then sends the data to an attacker’s server. The attack is very hard to notice and works on local computers, servers, containers, and CI/CD systems. The advice is simple: remove the bad versions right away, check your system for the malware, and change your passwords and secrets. In the comments, many users share they have been affected and thank others for help finding and removing the malware. There are debates about who is responsible and tips to stay safe—like using containers and only downloading from trusted sources. People are shocked by the scale of the attack and agree that everyone needs to be more careful with dependencies.

Arm has announced its first finished CPU chip, called the Arm AGI CPU, made for AI data centers. Before, Arm only sold chip designs, but now they are selling the chip itself. The AGI CPU is built for “agentic AI” jobs—AI systems that do many things at once. Each server blade can have up to 272 cores, and with liquid cooling, one rack can hold up to 45,000 cores. Arm says the AGI CPU gives double the performance per rack compared to x86 chips, thanks to better memory and efficiency. Companies like Meta, Cloudflare, and OpenAI are planning to use it, and systems can already be ordered from Lenovo and Supermicro. In the comments, many are excited about Arm’s move, the high core count, and better energy use. Some want to see real benchmarks, while others discuss software compatibility and vendor lock-in. Most agree this is a big step for Arm and for AI data centers.

Another new tool is FastMCP, a framework for building applications using the Model Context Protocol, or MCP. MCP lets large language models connect with tools and data, and FastMCP makes it easy to turn any Python function into a tool for LLMs. It handles server connections, security, and protocol details, so you can focus on your own logic. FastMCP is already very popular, with a million downloads each day. It offers servers, clients, and apps, and has easy documentation for both humans and LLMs. Prefect, the company behind FastMCP, offers free hosting. In the comments, people praise the simple API and the way FastMCP lowers the barrier to building LLM tools. Some want to know more about MCP and real-world use cases, and others worry about lock-in or debugging hidden complexity. Many are interested to see more examples and stories from real users.

Now, let’s talk about Hypura, a new open source tool for running large AI models on Apple Silicon Macs, even if the models are bigger than your RAM. Hypura decides which parts of the model should be on the GPU, in RAM, or on the SSD, and only loads the needed data at the right time. For special models like MoE, it only loads the “experts” you need, saving bandwidth and memory. This makes it possible to run very large models, like 31 GB Mixtral or 40 GB Llama, on a Mac Mini with 32 GB of RAM. If the model fits in memory, Hypura runs it at full speed; if not, it streams data from SSD without crashing your Mac. You need to build Hypura from source with Rust and CMake, and it works with tools like OpenClaw or Ollama. In the comments, users are excited about running bigger AI models at home, and many praise how clever the SSD streaming is. There are questions about performance and SSD wear, but most people agree this is a big step for local AI.

Wine 11 is a big new update for running Windows games on Linux. The main change is that Wine now moves some work into the Linux kernel, making games run much faster. With a new system called Hangover, Wine can now run both 32-bit and 64-bit Windows apps together with less overhead. Memory handling is better, game controllers and graphics cards work better, and many games now load faster and run with higher frame rates. Wine 11 is open source, and the update also makes it easier to fix bugs and install updates. In the comments, Linux gamers are happy with the speed boost, and some hope this will make Linux more popular for gaming. There are some worries about security and about full compatibility, but most agree this is a big step forward.

There’s also a story about how London built the world’s first electric grid. In the late 1800s, there were many small, separate power systems, all using different voltages and technologies. This made things messy and inefficient. After World War I showed how bad the patchwork was, the British government pushed for a national grid, finally connecting everything in the 1930s. After World War II, the grid was nationalized, then partly privatized in the 1980s and 1990s. In the comments, people admire the fast progress and smart engineering, but also debate if private or public control works better for the electric grid. Many agree that building big projects is not just about technology—it’s about people, politics, and making hard choices.

He found old, hard-to-use systems and saw that workers and managers often resist change. Even after selling $30,000 of new business in a month, he decided not to sell software to big companies, but to start his own pest control company and build better tools from scratch. Commenters praise his hands-on approach and agree that real-world experience is the best way to find ideas. They also warn that old industries can be slow to change, but the pest control market is interesting because it is steady and not too crowded.

Finally, we have news about Hegel, a new property-based testing library for Rust and other languages, from the team behind Hypothesis. Property-based testing means you write rules about how your code should work, and the library tests it with lots of different data to find bugs you may not think of. Hegel uses Hypothesis as a backend for now, but plans to have a Rust backend in the future. The tool is good for testing tricky logic, edge cases, and even for AI-written code. In the comments, developers are excited to bring these testing features to more languages, though some worry about the Python dependency. Many share stories of finding bugs with property-based testing and are interested in trying Hegel on new projects.
