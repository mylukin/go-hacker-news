## How OpenAI delivers low-latency voice AI at scale

- 原文链接: [How OpenAI delivers low-latency voice AI at scale](https://openai.com/index/delivering-low-latency-voice-ai-at-scale/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48013919)

OpenAI shared how they make their voice AI fast and smooth for users all over the world. The article explains the technical work behind making ChatGPT’s voice feature responsive, even when millions of people use it at the same time.

The main challenge is to keep conversations feeling natural, so there are no awkward silences or delays. OpenAI needs to connect people quickly and keep the system stable, even as users start and stop sessions all the time. Their system must also work everywhere, for almost a billion users each week.

To solve these problems, OpenAI uses WebRTC, a standard way to send audio and video between computers in real time. WebRTC takes care of hard things like connecting through firewalls, encrypting calls, and making sure audio quality stays high. This lets OpenAI focus on building the AI parts instead of reinventing everything from scratch.

Most voice AI sessions are just between one user and one AI, not big group calls. So, instead of using a common server setup (called SFU) made for group calls, OpenAI built a new design. They use a “transceiver,” which is a special service that manages each user’s connection. This service is built to work well with their cloud system, where computers are started and stopped often.

One big problem with WebRTC is that it usually needs lots of open network ports, which is hard to manage and secure at huge scale. OpenAI fixed this by adding a “relay,” a simple server that forwards audio packets to the right place. The relay reads a small piece of info from each packet (called the “ufrag”) to know where to send it, so only one port is needed. This makes it easier to scale up and keep safe.

Another smart idea is to put relays all over the world. When a user talks, their audio goes to the nearest relay, which lowers the delay and makes the conversation faster and more stable. The relay itself doesn’t touch or understand the audio; it just sends it to the right transceiver, which does all the real work.

The relay system is written in Go and uses tricks like sharing network ports and keeping work on the same computer core to be fast and efficient. All of this means OpenAI can run voice AI on the cloud without big security or scaling problems. They keep the complicated session data in one place (the transceiver) and keep the relay simple, so the system is easier to manage.

In the Hacker News comments, many people praised OpenAI’s clear technical explanation and said the design was clever. Some liked the way OpenAI used existing standards (WebRTC) instead of building something totally new. Others pointed out that relays are a popular way to fix network problems at big companies, and liked seeing the details about how OpenAI did it.

A few commenters were surprised at how much engineering is needed just to make voice AI feel natural, and said this shows how hard real-time systems are. Some people asked about the security of relays and were happy to see OpenAI keeps them simple and secure. There was also talk about using Go for the relay service; some developers liked this choice, while others wondered if even faster languages could help more.

One person asked if this system could be used for video, not just audio. Another commenter suggested that the relay idea could help other types of apps, not just AI voice. Some shared stories about similar problems they faced at work and how OpenAI’s solution matched their own experiences.

Overall, people agreed that OpenAI’s system balances speed, security, and scale well. The post also got people thinking about the hidden challenges behind things that seem simple, like talking to a computer in real time.

## I am worried about Bun

- 原文链接: [I am worried about Bun](https://wwj.dev/posts/i-am-worried-about-bun/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48011184)

The article is about Bun, a fast JavaScript runtime, and the author’s worries after it was bought by Anthropic, an AI company. The author likes Bun a lot and thinks it’s the best alternative to Node.js, but now feels nervous about its future.

Bun used to be an independent project. In December 2025, Anthropic bought it. They promised to keep Bun open source and good for developers. Anthropic also uses Bun for their own coding tool called Claude Code, which made the author think Bun would stay great. At first, this seemed like a good thing. But, after a few months, the author noticed problems—not with Bun itself, but with Claude Code.

Claude Code started out as a strong coding tool using AI, good at helping developers work faster and smarter. But lately, the author says it’s become worse. There are more limits, confusing billing, and bugs that make it hard to use. Anthropic did write a postmortem explaining some of these problems, which the author appreciates. But other issues, like charging extra for third-party tools and even blocking requests if certain words are in your project’s history, make the product feel unfriendly to developers.

The author feels these are signs of “enshittification”—when a product gets worse because of company decisions, not because the technology is bad. Since Bun is now part of Anthropic, the author worries the same problems might happen to Bun. The Bun team is still great, but company policies could slowly make Bun worse, even if the team doesn’t want that.

Because of this, the author is switching back to using pnpm, a popular package manager, for new projects. Bun offers more features than pnpm, but the author now prefers to be cautious. Still, the author says this is just a personal choice—other people should decide for themselves.

In the comments, many people agree that Bun is excellent and fast, but they share the author’s concerns about Anthropic’s influence. Some think that big companies buying open source projects is often a bad sign, because the company might care more about business than about developers. Others say that Anthropic still has good AI models, so Bun could stay strong if the company needs it for their own tools.

A few commenters point out that open source means anyone can fork Bun if things go badly. Some believe the worries are too early, and that the Bun team will keep doing good work for now. Others mention that switching tools is normal in software, and it’s smart to be careful if you rely on Bun. There are also voices who trust that competition, like pnpm and other runtimes, will push everyone to keep improving. Finally, some people note that it’s hard to predict the future, but keeping an eye on company actions is always a good idea.

## Securing a DoD contractor: Finding a multi-tenant authorization vulnerability

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48012162)

A security company found a very serious bug in the API of a military training platform called Schemata, which works for the U.S. Department of Defense. The bug let anyone with a normal account see sensitive data from all users and organizations, not just their own.

The article explains that Schemata is a big contractor working in military training with lots of private documents and user records. The security team, Strix, used their own open-source tool to test Schemata’s website. They found that there were no proper checks to stop users from seeing or changing data from other organizations. With a basic account, they could view names, emails, military base assignments, and training manuals, some marked confidential. They could also potentially change or delete important training courses.

Strix tried to tell the company about the problem privately. At first, Schemata replied but seemed to think Strix wanted money. Strix said they just wanted the bug fixed. For five months, Strix emailed several times, but Schemata did not fix the problem until Strix warned them they would publish the issue. After that, Schemata fixed the bug quickly.

The article says this kind of mistake is very dangerous, especially for companies working with the government. Such companies must follow strict security rules. Having no authorization checks means anyone could steal or leak sensitive military data. The article also says all companies should take security reports seriously and answer researchers quickly.

In the comments, some readers were shocked that a defense contractor had such a basic security problem. Many said that missing authorization checks is a common mistake for new developers, but it should never happen in big, important systems. Some worried that other government contractors might have similar bugs. A few people questioned how much security testing these companies really do, and if they just trust their developers too much.

Others praised Strix for being patient and responsible in their disclosure. Some said it is hard to get companies to listen, even when the risk is high. ## Formatting a 25M-line codebase overnight

- 原文链接: [Formatting a 25M-line codebase overnight](https://stripe.dev/blog/formatting-an-entire-25-million-line-codebase-overnight-the-rubyfmt-story)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48014325)

Stripe wanted to format all 25 million lines of their Ruby code in one night, using a tool called rubyfmt. Before this, there was no good autoformatter for Ruby, and other tools would crash or were too slow for such a big job.

The rubyfmt project started after engineers realized that arguing about code style was wasting time. Tools like rubocop could check style, but they needed lots of settings and still led to debates. The goal for rubyfmt was to be fast, need no configuration, and just make decisions for everyone—like gofmt does for Go code.

Making rubyfmt fast was hard. Starting up Ruby was slow, so they wrote parts in C and later in Rust. Ruby’s syntax is flexible and complex, so the formatter had to handle tricky cases. At first, rubyfmt used Ruby’s own parser (ripper) and turned its output into JSON so Rust could read it. But this was slow, so they later made Rust read Ruby’s memory structures directly. This part was unusual but made things faster.

When Stripe wanted to use rubyfmt, they needed it to work on everything and avoid breaking code. They let teams opt-in slowly, tested a lot, and compared the results to make sure nothing was wrong. On a weekend, they flipped the switch so almost all files were formatted at once, avoiding merge problems. Now, rubyfmt runs on all code at Stripe, and formatting is no longer something engineers argue about.

Later, a new Ruby parser called Prism made things even faster and simpler. Rubyfmt moved to use Prism, making the tool smaller and removing old, complex code. Now, Stripe’s engineers, even those new to Ruby, don’t have to worry about code style or spend time fixing it in reviews.

People in the comments like how Stripe tackled such a big challenge and made their tool open source. Some are amazed by the technical work, especially the mix of C, Rust, and Ruby. Many agree that autoformatters save time and reduce stress, pointing to similar tools in other languages. Some wish there were more tools like this for other languages, or that Ruby had something like this earlier.

A few commenters wonder about edge cases or bugs that might be missed, and ask how Stripe handles those. Others mention that changing code style across a giant codebase can make looking at code history harder, since so many lines change at once. Some suggest that formatting tools should be language features, not separate tools.

Several people praise Stripe for investing in developer productivity and keeping the tool open source, hoping more companies do the same. Some mention that using autoformatters helps new hires get up to speed faster. Others debate whether strict formatting is always good, but most agree it’s better than endless style arguments.

A few share stories about trying to format large codebases themselves, and note it’s never easy. Some are curious if rubyfmt can become the standard for the Ruby world, or if it works well outside Stripe’s codebase. There’s a tone of admiration for making something so invisible but important—good infrastructure is often like that.

## Microsoft Edge stores all passwords in memory in clear text, even when unused

- 原文链接: [Microsoft Edge stores all passwords in memory in clear text, even when unused](https://twitter.com/L1v1ng0ffTh3L4N/status/2051308329880719730)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48012735)

Microsoft Edge, the web browser from Microsoft, stores all your saved passwords in computer memory as clear text. This happens even when you are not using those passwords at the moment.

The original post says that anyone with access to your computer’s memory can see your passwords easily. The passwords are not encrypted or hidden in any way in the memory. This makes it possible for malware or hackers to read all your passwords if they get access to your computer while Edge is running. The problem is not just when you enter a password but also when Edge is only open. All saved passwords are loaded into memory in plain text automatically. This is risky because it only takes a simple tool to see what is in the memory. The post shows that many users are at risk, not just a few. It is also a problem for people who use shared or public computers. Some people use Edge because it is the default browser on Windows, so they may not know about this risk. The post suggests that Edge does not handle password security well. It is different from how some other browsers work, which may protect passwords better.

People in the comments have different opinions. Some are very worried and say this is a big security mistake by Microsoft. They point out that passwords should always be protected in memory, not just on disk. Others mention that other browsers may also have problems, but Edge seems worse in this case. A few users ask if this is really a new issue or if it has always been like this. Some say that it is hard to make browsers totally safe, but this is still a basic mistake. There are people who suggest using a separate password manager instead of saving passwords in the browser. Others say that if someone has memory access to your computer, you may already have bigger problems. Some users are frustrated because they expect better security from Microsoft. A few comment that this is a good reminder to keep your computer safe from malware. Some people joke about switching to another browser, while others are now thinking about not saving passwords in Edge at all. Overall, most users agree this is a bad look for Microsoft Edge and hope for a fix.

## Does Employment Slow Cognitive Decline? Evidence from Labor Market Shocks

- 原文链接: [Does Employment Slow Cognitive Decline? Evidence from Labor Market Shocks](https://www.nber.org/papers/w35117)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48009983)

This study looks at whether having a job can help slow down problems with thinking as people get older. The authors use data from the United States and focus on people who stop working before they turn 65. 

They find that when there are fewer jobs in an area (labor market shocks), people’s thinking skills (cognitive scores) go down more over time. This effect is strongest for men between ages 51 and 64. The study uses special tools to make sure the results are not just a coincidence, but actually show that losing jobs can cause faster mental decline. The authors say their findings are important because more people are living longer now, and more people are having trouble with memory and thinking. They also say that working longer might help people keep their minds sharp as they age, not just until the usual retirement age, but even before that. The results are less clear for women and men who are already older, maybe because their work choices are less affected by local job changes.

In the comments, some people agree and share stories about older relatives who stayed sharp by working or staying busy. Others point out that not all jobs are good for your mind, and stressful or boring work might have the opposite effect. A few mention that people who keep working might already be healthier, so it’s hard to know if work helps, or if healthy people just work longer. Some say the study is good because it uses “causal” methods, not just simple links. There’s also talk about other ways to keep your mind healthy, like learning new things, socializing, or doing puzzles. A few worry that this kind of research could be used to push people to work longer, even if they don’t want to. Others say that having a choice is important—being forced to work is not the same as wanting to work. Some suggest that retirement can be good if you stay active in other ways. In general, people think the topic is interesting, but there are many factors to consider.

## Let's talk about LLMs

- 原文链接: [Let's talk about LLMs](https://www.b-list.org/weblog/2026/apr/09/llms/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48011904)

This article talks about Large Language Models (LLMs) and their real impact on programming. The author explains that people are debating if LLMs are a big revolution, just hype, or something in between. He says he will focus on LLMs in software development, not general AI or other fields.

He starts by saying that calling LLMs “AI” is confusing, so he uses the more precise term. He also says that most of the excitement about LLMs is about how they can write code quickly. The author brings up the famous “No Silver Bullet” essay, which says there’s no single tool or technique that can make software development ten times faster or easier. The hard part is not just writing code, but understanding and building the right thing. Many problems, like talking to users, planning, testing, and reviewing, are still hard, even if code is written faster.

The article reviews studies and reports, like DORA and CircleCI, which say LLMs can speed up code writing but often cause more mistakes, bugs, and “delivery instability.” Teams using LLMs may write code faster, but they also break things more often and take longer to fix them. These reports also show that better results come from strong teamwork and good practices, not just from using LLMs. Some people say newer LLMs or better workflows will fix these problems, but the author is not convinced because the same issues keep appearing.

The author shares examples where LLMs were used to rebuild software, but the results had big bugs and security issues. He argues that fast code generation does not solve the real problems in software projects. Writing code is only a small part of the whole process.

He also discusses the idea that not using LLMs now will leave you behind. He says there is little risk in waiting, because if LLMs truly become revolutionary, everyone will need to learn new skills anyway, and early adoption won’t give an unbeatable advantage.

Another claim is that LLMs will let anyone build software, even people who aren’t programmers. The author doesn’t believe this, because LLMs still require users to think like programmers, plan clearly, and check results. He worries that non-programmers may make mistakes they don’t notice, causing bigger problems, especially in areas needing privacy or accuracy.

In the comments, some people agree with the author and say LLMs are just another tool, not magic. They share stories about LLMs making mistakes or giving wrong answers. Others think LLMs are useful for simple tasks or saving time on boring code, but not for hard problems. A few people argue that LLMs are getting better and will soon solve these issues, so it’s smart to learn them now.

Some commenters point out that most software problems come from unclear requirements or people not knowing what they really want, not from slow coding. They agree that strong teamwork, communication, and testing matter more than code speed. Other readers say LLMs help beginners learn faster, but experts still need to check everything carefully.

A few comments worry that companies will push people to use LLMs too much, hoping for big savings, but might end up with more broken projects. Some suggest that using LLMs without good practices is like building on sand. Others say that, for now, it’s safe to take time and focus on basics like testing, version control, and clear design.

In summary, most commenters think LLMs can help in some ways, but they are not a miracle. The main work in software is still about understanding problems, talking to people, and making good decisions. LLMs might get better, but right now, strong fundamentals are more important than rushing to adopt new tools.
