## Does that use a lot of energy?

- 原文链接: [Does that use a lot of energy?](https://hannahritchie.github.io/energy-use-comparisons/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47253364)

This article explains how much energy different things in daily life use. It compares the energy used by household items, digital devices, kitchen appliances, and vehicles.

The author shows energy use in watt-hours (Wh), which is power times time. For example, a 60-watt lightbulb used for one hour uses 60 Wh. LED bulbs use much less energy for the same light. Charging a phone takes about 20 Wh. Watching TV takes 60–90 Wh per hour, depending on the size. Using a laptop for normal tasks uses around 20 Wh per hour. Desktop computers, game consoles, and WiFi routers all use different amounts, but desktops and game consoles use more than laptops. Streaming Netflix or YouTube only adds a very small amount of extra energy, but the device you watch on uses much more. Kitchen devices like kettles, ovens, and microwaves use a lot of energy in a short time. Washing machines, dryers, and dishwashers also use a lot, especially dryers. Heating and cooling, like using a hairdryer, electric shower, or heater, can use hundreds or even thousands of Wh per hour. Heat pumps are more efficient than normal electric or gas heaters. Electric vehicles (cars, bikes, scooters) use much less energy per mile than petrol ones. Garden tools like electric mowers use less than petrol mowers. The article gives sources and shows these numbers are general—real energy use depends on how you use things.

In the comments, many people liked how clear the comparisons were. Some said they were surprised that streaming a video uses so little energy from the internet itself, and that most energy is used by the device, not the servers. Others pointed out that using heat pumps or LED bulbs can save a lot of energy. Some said the numbers for washing machines and dryers were higher than they expected, and asked about ways to use less, like using eco modes or drying clothes outside. A few worried that small daily things like charging phones don’t matter much, but big things like heating, driving, and drying do. Some users checked the math and said the numbers matched their own electricity bills, while others wanted more data for things like AI or video calls. People also talked about energy use in different countries and how prices change. Some said the tool helped them decide what to upgrade in their homes. Overall, readers found the tool useful and easy to understand, and liked that it helps them think about saving energy.

## Nobody Gets Promoted for Simplicity

- 原文链接: [Nobody Gets Promoted for Simplicity](https://terriblesoftware.org/2026/03/03/nobody-gets-promoted-for-simplicity/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47246110)

The article talks about how software engineers often get more praise and promotions for making things complicated instead of keeping things simple. It explains that companies and teams often reward people who build big, fancy systems, even when a small, simple solution would have worked better.

The main story compares two engineers. Engineer A solves her task with a short, easy-to-read piece of code and finishes quickly. Engineer B builds a big system with extra features and spends much more time. When it comes time for promotions, Engineer B’s work sounds impressive on paper, while Engineer A’s simple solution is hard to describe and easy to overlook.

The article says this is not because managers want complexity, but because the way they judge work makes big projects look better. In interviews, people are often pushed to show more complex designs, even if a simple one is enough. In meetings, people are told to "future-proof" their code, so they add extra layers that might never be needed.

The writer says sometimes complexity is needed, for example, if a system has millions of users or lots of teams working on it. But often, people add extra steps before they are truly needed, which makes things harder for the next person to work on.

The article gives advice to engineers: make your simple choices visible. When you pick a simple design, explain why you did it and what other options you considered. Write about the problems you avoided by not making things more complicated. In meetings, if someone asks for extra features "just in case," explain the cost and why it's better to wait.

For managers and team leaders, the advice is to change what they reward. They should ask people to justify complexity, not just accept it. They should help their team members describe the value of simple solutions and give praise for clean, easy-to-maintain work.

In the comments, many people agree with the article. Some say they have seen the same thing at their work: simple solutions are ignored, while complex ones get more attention. Others argue that sometimes complexity is needed if you want to build products that last a long time or can grow with more users.

A few commenters say that company culture is the real problem. Some workplaces really do value simplicity, but most do not. One person says that many engineers add complexity because they want to learn new tools or look smart, not because the project needs it.

Others share stories about managers who only notice “big” work, so people learn to make things look harder than they are. Some point out that simple code is harder to sell to non-technical people, who are more impressed by technical buzzwords.

Some commenters say that writing simple code is actually harder and takes more skill. A few give tips: keep things small, explain your choices, and help others see the value of easy-to-read code. Many agree that if you want to be recognized for simple solutions, you have to tell the story clearly—because good work does not always speak for itself.

## The Rust calling convention we deserve (2024)

- 原文链接: [The Rust calling convention we deserve (2024)](https://mcyoung.xyz/2024/04/17/calling-convention/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47208688)

This article talks about how Rust programs call functions, and how the way Rust does this today is not very good. The author says that Rust is too careful and uses the same way as C, even when better ways are possible. Rust often passes simple things by pointer, but it could use registers for faster code. The author shows that even C can be better at this if you use special calling conventions. He explains a new idea: let Rust use a “fast” calling convention when you want speed, and keep the old one for compatibility. He says this can be controlled with a flag like `-Zcallconv=fast`.

The article explains how to choose which function arguments go in registers and which go on the stack. He uses examples with assembly and LLVM IR code to show how this works. The author says we can flatten structs, pack booleans into bits, and sort arguments by size to use registers better. He compares Rust to Go, which already uses a smarter way to pass arguments. The post also explains some problems, like how to handle function pointers and debugging, but says these can be solved.

He notes that changing this is hard because LLVM, the tool Rust uses, does not make it easy to pick registers for arguments. He gives ideas to work around this, and shows that we can make Rust much faster by using these tricks. He also suggests we can make the calling convention depend on how the function is used, or even profile the code to pick the best way. The main point is that Rust could do much better, and it does not have to be stuck with C’s old ways.

In the comments, some people agree and say they want Rust to have a faster, better way to call functions. They think Rust is held back by having to be compatible with C. Others point out that changing the calling convention can make debugging harder, or break some tools. A few commenters worry about mixing code from different Rust versions, because the “ABI” (how data is passed) can change.

Some people are excited about the idea of using more registers and packing structs tightly. Others think it will be hard to make this work on all CPUs. A few mention that Go’s way is not perfect, but still better than C’s. Some developers like the idea of making the function signature depend on how the function is used, but they worry it might make Rust builds slower or more complex.

One commenter says that most programmers do not need to think about calling conventions, but for people writing libraries or performance-critical code, it matters a lot. Another person wonders if the extra speed is worth the extra complexity. A few ask if this idea could break FFI, where Rust calls C code or the other way around. Some people say that, even if this is hard, it is a good direction and hope the Rust team tries it. Overall, people see the current system as slow and old, but they know making it better is not easy.

## NanoGPT Slowrun: Language Modeling with Limited Data, Infinite Compute

- 原文链接: [NanoGPT Slowrun: Language Modeling with Limited Data, Infinite Compute](https://qlabs.sh/slowrun)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47251259)

This article talks about NanoGPT Slowrun, a project to make language models learn better with small data but lots of computer power. Today, computers get much faster, but the amount of good data does not grow as fast, so soon, data will be the limit for AI, not compute.

The main idea is that most learning algorithms need both more data and more compute to get smarter. In fields like robotics and biology, people cannot just use more compute to get better results—they are stuck because there is not enough data. The team at Q Labs wants to fix this by making new ways to train models that work well even when data is limited but compute is almost unlimited. 

NanoGPT Slowrun is an open challenge: you train a model on 100 million tokens from FineWeb, you can use as much compute as you want, and you try to get the lowest validation loss. Only improvements that help with data efficiency are merged. Unlike speed-focused challenges, Slowrun is for testing ideas that need a lot of compute, like strong regularization, new optimizers, or even different ways of learning.

So far, the team found that Muon, a new optimizer, works better than AdamW and others. Training for many epochs (going over the data more times) also helps. Using bigger models with strong weight decay and dropout gives better results. At first, they got 2.4 times more data efficiency than normal NanoGPT.

With help from the community, they improved to 5.5 times better data efficiency. The biggest changes were shuffling data at each epoch, using learned projections for embeddings, changing the activation function, and combining several models together. They think 10x or even 100x data efficiency could be possible soon, but it will need lots of new ideas.

The team is interested in more research on second-order optimizers, diffusion models, curriculum learning, new gradient methods, and making models smaller and more compressed. They invite others to join in and share new ideas.

Hacker News comments had many different opinions. Some people were excited about focusing on data efficiency, saying this is a real bottleneck for future AI. Others liked the open repo approach and thought it encouraged more creative research. A few said they were surprised how much shuffling and multi-epoch training helped, showing that old ideas sometimes still matter.

Some commenters wondered if these tricks would also help with bigger datasets, or if they only work when data is small. Others worried that using lots of compute for tiny data might not make sense for real-world problems, since compute also costs money. One pointed out that regularization can sometimes hurt model quality, so it is important to test results carefully.

There were questions about whether these methods would work for tasks other than language, like images or robotics. A few users asked for more details about the new optimizer and activation functions. Some people liked that the project was not about speed, but about quality, and hoped it would bring more ideas from theory into practice.

Overall, the discussion was positive, with many saying this type of research is important as AI moves forward. Some wished more labs would share their experiments in public like this. Others wondered what the next big breakthrough for data efficiency might look like.

## Data Has Weight but Only on SSDs

- 原文链接: [Data Has Weight but Only on SSDs](https://cubiclenate.com/2026/03/04/data-has-weight-but-only-on-ssds-blathering/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47251987)

This article asks if data has weight and looks at why SSDs (solid-state drives) might get a tiny bit heavier when full. The writer explains that this topic is just for fun and not something we need to worry about in real life.

The article says SSDs store data by moving electrons into special parts called "floating gates." Electrons have a very small mass, so when you write data to an SSD, you add more electrons into it. This means, in theory, that a full SSD weighs a little more than an empty one. For a 1TB SSD, the extra weight is only a few femtograms (a femtogram is a billionth of a billionth of a gram). This is so small that no normal scale could ever measure it.

Hard drives (HDDs) work differently. They store data by changing the direction of magnets on a spinning disk. They do not add or remove anything, so their weight does not change, except for an unbelievably tiny amount due to energy changes (E=mc²), which is even less than with SSDs.

The article explains the science behind SSDs: To store a bit, the SSD adds or removes electrons using electricity. More complex SSDs (like TLC) can put several bits in each cell by using different numbers of electrons. When you erase data, the SSD removes electrons. The insulation inside the SSD keeps the electrons in place for many years.

The writer also points out that some people think drives get lighter, but that is not correct with modern SSDs, because adding data means adding electrons. In the end, the difference in weight is so tiny that things like dust or plastic parts moving will make a much bigger difference. It is just a fun fact and not important for real life.

Commenters on Hacker News had a lot of fun with this idea. Some enjoyed the playful science and liked learning that electrons really have mass, even if it is tiny. Others joked about weighing their SSDs before and after copying files, even though it is impossible to measure. A few commenters said this is a good example of how physics works in the real world, but the effect is so small it does not matter for most people.

Some users pointed out that the energy in a battery or even a powered-on computer also makes it weigh a bit more, thanks to Einstein’s equation. Others explained more about how SSD memory works and corrected small mistakes about how data is stored. A few people asked if this extra weight matters for things like space travel or high-precision science, but the answer was always no—the change is much too small.

There were also people who said they had never thought about this before and enjoyed the new perspective. Some remembered old debates about floppy disks and CDs getting heavier, and laughed at how this question keeps coming back with new technology. Overall, most commenters agreed that while the science is real, the effect is just good for a smile, not something to worry about.
