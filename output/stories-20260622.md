## British Columbia, Time Zones, and Postgres

- 原文链接: [British Columbia, Time Zones, and Postgres](https://www.crunchydata.com/blog/british-columbia-and-time-zone-changes)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48634787)

British Columbia has decided to stay on Pacific Daylight Time all year, starting in 2026, so their clocks will not “fall back” in November anymore. The article explains how this new rule can cause problems with storing dates and times in Postgres databases, especially if you use the default way of saving timestamps with time zones (called `timestamptz`).

Normally, databases store timestamps in UTC, and then convert to local time using time zone rules. If a user in British Columbia books an appointment for 10 AM in November 2026, but the time zone rules change after you save it, the database might show the wrong time when you check later. For example, someone might show up at 10 AM, but the database now thinks their appointment is at 11 AM because the time zone offset changed.

This happens because `timestamptz` stores the UTC value and uses the current time zone rules for conversion, not the rules from when you first saved the appointment. If your system’s time zone data (called `tzdata`) isn’t updated, you might see the old offset. If you update `tzdata`, the conversion uses the new offset, which can shift appointments by an hour.

The article suggests a safer way: use a dual-column pattern. This means you store three things—what time the user wanted (local time), the name of the time zone, and the calculated UTC time. The UTC time is calculated from the local time and time zone. If the time zone rules change, you can update all future UTC times by recalculating them from the stored local time and time zone. This method makes sure you always know what the user meant.

There’s also mention of RFC 9557, a new format that includes the time zone name in the timestamp, but it doesn’t solve the problem of time zone rule changes for future events. So, the dual-column pattern is still best for storing future appointments or events.

If you have already updated your `tzdata` and have future appointments that might be wrong, the article gives steps to fix your data. These include finding when the change happened, identifying affected records, telling users about the shift, and updating your database.

In the comments, some people say time zones are always a pain and they wish everyone just used UTC. Others share stories about how political decisions often mess up software that deals with time. A few suggest that local time is important for real-world events like meetings or deliveries, so you can’t avoid the problem by just storing UTC. Some developers like the dual-column solution, but worry it adds complexity and room for mistakes. Others think database systems should make this easier for everyone. A few comments discuss how their companies handle time zones, with some using libraries to help, and others writing their own fixes. There are also debates about how often `tzdata` updates and whether system admins keep up with changes. Some readers mention that it’s important to warn users when their appointments might be affected by time zone shifts. Finally, a few people joke that even after all these years, time zones still cause more bugs than anything else in programming.

## Japanese symbols that speak without words

- 原文链接: [Japanese symbols that speak without words](https://arun.is/blog/japan-symbols/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48634803)

This article talks about how Japan uses special symbols to share information without using any words. These symbols help people understand important details quickly, like who is driving a car or who might need help, without needing to read anything.

In Japan, symbols like the family crests (kamon) have been used for hundreds of years. These crests are simple shapes, often based on plants or animals, and you can still see many of them today, especially in formal settings. Police cars have a golden badge with sun rays, and fire trucks have a snowflake symbol that stands for water, unity, and purity. Even trains used to have a gold winged triangle to show they were the fastest type.

For drivers, there are four main marks: the green and yellow “new driver” mark (wakaba), the “elderly driver” clover mark, a yellow and green butterfly for hearing-impaired drivers, and a four-leaf clover for drivers with physical disabilities. Some marks are required by law, and others are recommended. On public transit, you might see the “Help Mark”—a white cross and heart—showing someone might need help even if they look fine. There’s also a “maternity mark” for pregnant women, especially important in early pregnancy.

The author explains that these symbols are important because sometimes you can’t see who is driving or what someone’s condition is. In Japan, people are expected to “read the air,” which means understanding what others need without being told. But when you can’t see what’s happening, symbols help fill in the missing information, making it easier for everyone to be considerate.

In the comments, many people said they liked the idea of using symbols instead of words. Some said these marks make life easier for everyone, especially people who need extra help. Others thought it was interesting how well these symbols work in Japan, but they wondered if they would work as well in other countries where people are not used to “reading the air.” A few commenters shared stories about seeing these symbols in Japan and how helpful they were. Some people wanted their home countries to use more symbols like this, but a few worried that too many symbols might get confusing. Others pointed out that in Japan, people learn the meanings of these symbols as part of daily life, which helps them work better. Finally, some said that using symbols feels more polite and universal, since you don’t need to know the language to understand them.

## Nearly Half of LG Smart TV Apps Contain Residential Proxy SDKs

- 原文链接: [Nearly Half of LG Smart TV Apps Contain Residential Proxy SDKs](https://spur.us/blog/smart-tv-apps-residential-proxy-sdks)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48635954)

This article is about how many LG smart TV apps have hidden software that turns your TV into a “residential proxy” for other people’s internet traffic. The authors scanned over 6,000 TV apps and found that about 2,000 were selling or sharing your internet connection without most users knowing.

A residential proxy works by letting other people use your home’s IP address to access the internet, making it look like the traffic comes from your house. Many apps, like screensavers or games, include these proxy tools because they make money in the background. TVs are good targets because people don’t check them like computers, and the apps can keep working even after you close them. Companies often ask for your consent only once, and users may not understand what they are agreeing to.

Some apps are made by the proxy companies themselves, not just by random developers. These companies release many simple apps just to get their proxy software into homes. Other TV platforms, like Amazon and Roku, have rules banning these proxy apps, but LG and Samsung do not, so the problem is worse on their devices.

The risk is bigger than just sharing your IP address. If the proxy software is not careful, it could let outsiders reach other devices on your home network, like your router, printer, or computers. This has happened before, where hackers used these proxies to attack devices inside people’s homes. Some companies have blocklists to stop this, but not all do, and users can’t check what is really happening.

To find all this, the researchers downloaded and checked the real app files, not just what the app store says. They found clear proof of proxy software inside many apps. They also asked the proxy companies for comments. These companies say they have rules, vetting, and security checks to reduce abuse, but the main problem remains: users often do not know or control what is happening.

In the comments, many people were surprised and worried to learn their TV apps could be used this way. Some said this is a big privacy risk because people trust their TVs more than computers and don’t expect hidden software. Others pointed out that even if companies claim to have good rules, it is hard for users to trust or verify them. A few felt that banning these proxy apps, like Amazon and Roku do, is the best solution. Some argued that the real problem is a lack of clear rules and warnings from TV makers like LG and Samsung. Others noted that even if there’s a consent popup, most users don’t understand what it means or may just click “yes” to use the app. There were also worries about how these practices could be abused by criminals if the proxy controls fail. Some commenters suggested that people should treat smart TVs more like computers and be careful about what they install. Finally, a few were skeptical that the situation would improve without stronger action from regulators or the TV companies themselves.

## Moebius: 0.2B image inpainting model with 10B-level performance

- 原文链接: [Moebius: 0.2B image inpainting model with 10B-level performance](https://hustvl.github.io/Moebius/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48630171)

Moebius is a new AI model for image inpainting that uses only 0.22 billion parameters, but performs as well as much larger models with over 10 billion parameters. The main idea is to make image editing, like filling in missing parts or removing objects, much faster and possible on normal computers or even phones.

The team rebuilt the model using a special block called LλMI, which helps the model understand both local details and the big picture, but with much less computing. They also use a smart training method called distillation, where a small model learns from a big, powerful model, but does this learning in a way that saves even more time and memory. Moebius only needs a small amount of hardware to run, and it works at least 15 times faster than the big models, taking just 26 milliseconds for each step on a single GPU.

Tests show Moebius works just as well as, or even better than, the state-of-the-art giant models (like FLUX.1-Fill-Dev) on both natural and portrait photos. In some cases, Moebius is better at handling tricky details like faces or textures. The key tricks are its new architecture, which shrinks the size without losing important information, and its training method, which copies knowledge from a big model without wasting effort on unnecessary details. The model is designed to be a “specialist” for inpainting, rather than a general-purpose tool, making it much more efficient.

Many users are impressed by the speed and small size of Moebius, saying it could make good image editing possible on phones or laptops. Some are surprised that such a small model can match or beat much larger ones and ask for more open-source releases or demos. ## Show HN: Oak – Git alternative designed for agents

- 原文链接: [Show HN: Oak – Git alternative designed for agents](https://oak.space/oak/oak)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48631726)

Oak is a new open-source version control system made for use by AI agents, not just humans. It is positioned as a faster, more flexible alternative to Git, designed for tools like Claude, Codex, or other coding agents to work with code in a way that fits how these agents operate.

Oak focuses on "branch-per-session" workflows. This means that every time an agent starts work, it creates a new branch, and uses branch descriptions instead of detailed messages for every commit. Oak uses content-addressed storage and "lazy mounts," so agents can start editing code in a repo almost right away, without waiting for a full download. This is faster than Git for these kinds of tasks, but speed is the result of the design, not the main reason for Oak’s existence.

Oak is split into two main parts: a core library ('oakvcs-core') that handles the low-level version control logic (like hashing, diff, merge, and storage), and a command-line tool ('oakvcs-cli' or just 'oak') that agents or users can call. The library and CLI are both open source and can be used by other projects. Oak supports macOS (Apple Silicon), Linux (x86_64), and Windows (with some extra setup for mounting repos as virtual file systems).

You can install Oak by downloading a pre-built binary or building from source with Cargo. There’s also a focus on being easy to script, with machine-readable JSON output and exit codes, so agents or other programs can use it safely.

In the Hacker News comments, some users like the idea of a version control system made for AI agents and see that traditional tools like Git are not always a good fit for non-human users. They note Oak’s focus on agent workflows, and how features like fast, on-demand access and simple branch management match the way agents work. Some are interested in how Oak uses BLAKE3 hashes (instead of Git’s SHA-1) for better performance and security, and how its design might help with very large repos or projects with lots of automated changes.

Others are cautious. They wonder if Oak can really replace Git, since Git is so common and has a huge ecosystem. Some want to know how Oak handles tricky cases, like merge conflicts or history rewriting, and if it will work well with existing Git-based tools. A few ask about the risks of using a new system, or if Oak will be maintained in the long term. Some like that Oak is open source and easy to try, while others point out that switching version control systems is a big step, so Oak needs to prove itself over time. Overall, people are curious and see potential, but they want to see real-world use and more details before making a switch.

Canada wants to build up to 10 new nuclear reactors in the next 15 years, spending over $100 billion. The plan is to double Canada’s electricity grid by 2050 and make the country a leader in clean energy and uranium exports.

The government says nuclear power is important to reach low-carbon goals, since it gives steady, clean electricity. Right now, Canada has four nuclear power plants, making about 15% of its electricity. The new plan includes building big reactors, small modular reactors, and even sending a small reactor to a remote community. Funding for all this is not clear. The government may use the Canadian Infrastructure Bank and Canada Growth Fund. The government says reactor exports could help Canada build better foreign relationships and become an “energy superpower.”
