Hello everyone, this is the 2026-07-12 episode of Hacker News Daily Podcast. Today, we bring you a mix of stories about software, AI, math, and technology’s effect on the world. Let’s get started.

First, a small math function in Chrome is making big waves in browser fingerprinting. Since Chrome version 148, the Math.tanh function now gives slightly different results depending on your operating system—Linux, macOS, or Windows. Even if you try to hide your OS, websites can guess it just by looking at the last digit of Math.tanh(0.8). This is because Chrome’s JavaScript engine now uses the system’s own math library for this function, while others like Math.sin or Math.exp are still the same everywhere.

A table in the article shows that, for JavaScript, only Math.tanh leaks OS details, but for CSS and Web Audio, even more functions do this. It’s very hard to fake or spoof this—copying every tiny rounding error or math quirk is almost impossible. Sometimes, even different CPUs or Mac versions give slightly different results. Some companies, like Scrapfly, work hard to make their browser match a real one down to every math bit, loading Windows math libraries inside Linux to avoid detection.

Hacker News users are both surprised and concerned. Many never expected such a tiny difference could be used for fingerprinting. Some call it clever, others worry about privacy—how even simple math can break your cover. A few wish browsers would use the same bundled math for all systems to avoid leaking such details, while others see it as part of the endless cat-and-mouse game between privacy tools and tracking.

Next, we look at Tiny Emulators—a website where you can use your browser to play with dozens of classic 8-bit computers and games, from the Commodore 64 to the ZX Spectrum, and even East German machines like the Z1013. You can play games like Boulderdash, Bomb Jack, Bruce Lee, Pacman, and Prince of Persia, or try out old programming languages like BASIC and FORTH. Everything runs in your browser using JavaScript and WebAssembly, with no downloads needed.

The Hacker News comments are full of nostalgia. Readers share memories of their first programming steps and favorite childhood games. Many are surprised how smooth and accurate these emulators are, even on modern browsers. Some wish for more platforms, while others talk about how these tools help young people learn about computer history and programming. There are some complaints about missing sound or keyboard issues, but most people are impressed and grateful for the free, easy-to-use collection.

Moving on to AI coding tools, we have a deep dive comparing Claude Code and OpenCode. The article tests both tools on the same tasks and measures how many tokens each uses. Claude Code sends much bigger prompts—about 33,000 tokens for a simple reply, compared to OpenCode’s 7,000. With more complex tasks or subagents, Claude Code’s token use goes up even faster. In one test, running subagents with Claude Code used over 500,000 tokens—four times more than doing the same task directly.

OpenCode keeps its requests smaller and more stable, saving money on caching. Only in long, multi-step tasks did Claude Code use fewer total tokens because it finished more steps in one go. Both tools gave the same quality of answers, so the extra tokens did not mean better results.

In the comments, people are surprised by the huge difference in token use. Some are worried about costs, while others point out that Claude Code might be worth it for its features. Many like OpenCode’s simple, stable prompts for business use. Several users stress the importance of watching your logs and knowing what your AI tools are really sending and receiving—otherwise, you might get a big bill. Many are happy to see such a clear, data-driven comparison.

Now to a story about Terence Tao, a famous mathematician, bringing his old and new math applets back to life using AI agents. In the 1990s, Tao built Java applets to help teach math, but as web standards changed, these stopped working. With modern AI, he converted the old Java code to Javascript and put the applets on a new website. The process was quick and smooth, and the AI even found bugs in his old code.

Tao also built new tools, like a special relativity demo he’d always wanted to make, with the help of AI. He says the AI made the work easy and fast, though he still needed his own math and coding skills to guide the process and fix issues. Commenters praise the beauty and usefulness of the new tools, ask for more features, and discuss how AI makes it easier to keep educational tools up to date. Tao believes that while AI can do the coding, human skills are still needed for design and understanding.

Next, we look at how researchers are trying to understand large language models like GPT and Llama. Thomas Icard from Stanford uses “causal abstraction” to find simple explanations for how these complex models work, a bit like describing a gas by pressure instead of every molecule. His team found that BERT learned a kind of logic for words like “every” and “not,” and that Llama models can solve calendar problems by turning months or days into numbers, doing math, then converting back.

Hacker News users are interested but divided. Some think this is key for AI safety and reliability. Others say these models might only look like they are reasoning, when they are just very good at matching patterns. A few think that even if we can never fully explain LLMs, every step toward more understanding makes AI safer and more useful.

In another AI update, a team moved their website-building agent from Claude Opus to the new GPT-5.6 Sol model. After careful tests and fixing their tools, they found GPT-5.6 was faster, cheaper, and gave as good or better results. There were some problems—like the new model filling in too many tool options and issues with how prompt caching worked—but these were solved by changing code and settings.

Comments praise the detailed write-up, with some surprised by how much test settings can affect results. Others discuss the business risks of being tied to one AI provider, and the challenges of keeping up with changing tools. Overall, most agree that the speed and price improvements are worth the work.

Now to Ireland, where datacenters now use 23% of all electricity—up from 5% in 2015. That’s more than all city homes combined. The government has set new rules: if a datacenter wants a big connection, it must have batteries or generators and be able to give power back to the grid. Some big companies already do this. The high datacenter power use has sparked protests and worries about stress on the grid and the environment, but others say the jobs and money are worth it. Some suggest datacenters should be built where there is extra green power, not where the grid is tight. There are jokes about “bit barns” and questions about whether this will push up prices for regular people.

Next, we hear a strong opinion about why people should still write code in 2026, even as AI agents get better and faster at it. The author argues that writing code gives humans a deeper understanding of the system—something you can’t get just by reading or telling an AI what to do. Humans notice problems, fix details, and make better design choices. AI often does just enough to make things work, but does not always improve the code or fix deeper issues. The comments are split: many agree that writing code helps you learn and control your work, while others think most code will soon be written by AI, with humans guiding and reviewing.

Let’s turn to a story about math in the age of AI. AI is now able to solve hard math problems, like the old Erdős “unit distance problem.” But at the same time, fewer people in the US really understand math, and funding for math education is getting cut. The author warns that math skill is a kind of national infrastructure. If we lose it, we can’t get it back quickly, and we won’t be able to check AI’s answers or find mistakes. Some comments agree that losing math skills is risky, while others see this as just another step in the history of new tools changing what people do. Many think AI should show its reasoning in a way computers can check, not just give answers we can’t follow. There are worries about national security and the risk of losing too much human skill.

Finally, Rich Sutton’s article warns about the “one-step trap” in AI. This is when people think that making good one-step predictions in a model will let you predict the future by repeating those steps. In reality, small errors add up and predictions get worse and worse. Also, the future branches in many directions, so it’s hard for one-step models to handle real life. Sutton suggests using bigger, more abstract model steps—like “options” or “GVFs”—to help AI plan better. Hacker News users agree that one-step models have limits, but some say they are still useful for simple tasks. Others want more examples and better tools to fix this problem.
