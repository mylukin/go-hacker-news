## Norway's 2 petabytes of Huawei flash storage and LLM training

- 原文链接: [Norway's 2 petabytes of Huawei flash storage and LLM training](https://www.blocksandfiles.com/flash/2026/05/22/norways-2-petabytes-of-huawei-flash-storage-and-llm-training/5244910)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48270770)

Norway’s National Library is building a large language model (LLM) for Norwegian, using 2 petabytes of Huawei flash storage. The project started because no big company is making an LLM in Norwegian, and Norway wants an AI that knows its own language, culture, and history.

The library has the biggest collection of Norwegian books, newspapers, and web pages, because it must keep every published work in the country. Since 2005, they have digitized 20 petabytes of unique data, stored in three copies and on different media, making about 60 petabytes in total. They use this data—including text, pictures, and audio—for their AI.

A special agreement lets them use copyrighted content from Norwegian newspapers, which private companies can’t easily do. The data is stored mostly on disk and tape, which is good for long-term safety but slow for AI work. For the AI pipeline, they use fast Huawei flash storage (2 PB), along with Nvidia and CPU clusters, to process and clean the data before training.

The cleaned data goes to Norway’s supercomputer, Sigma2 Olivia, which has many GPUs and its own fast storage. Moving so much data from the slow archive to the fast AI system was a big problem, and the team had to create their own solutions.

There are also tough questions about how to judge the AI’s quality since Norwegian has two written forms and many dialects. The team has to make their own evaluation tools. Deciding who controls and uses the AI is also still unclear. Getting all the systems—archive, AI environment, and supercomputer—to work together is still a challenge.

In the comments, many people praised Norway for thinking about data sovereignty and building something for their own language. Some worried about using Huawei hardware because of political risks and security, while others said Norway is just being practical by choosing the best available hardware. Several users talked about the value of having a local AI that knows the country’s culture, and how this could be important for other small countries too.

A few pointed out the technical challenges—especially moving so much data, and making sure the AI stays up to date with new content. Some wondered if the project could inspire other nations to do the same. Others asked about the cost and if open-source tools might help.

Some commenters said that building local AIs is the future, as English-only models can’t truly understand every country. Others argued that it could be hard for small countries to afford the needed hardware and people. A few people said it was smart to use the National Library, which already has the data and the legal right to use it. Finally, some thought it was cool that Norway is leading by example, showing the world how to build AI that respects language and culture.

## California moves to exempt Linux from its age-verification law after backlash

- 原文链接: [California moves to exempt Linux from its age-verification law after backlash](https://www.tomshardware.com/software/linux/california-moves-to-exempt-linux-from-its-upcoming-age-verification-law-after-backlash-over-forcing-operating-systems-to-collect-users-ages-amendment-proposed-by-the-same-lawmaker-who-wrote-the-original-law)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48269961)

California is changing a new law so Linux and other open-source operating systems do not have to check a user’s age. This comes after many people spoke out against rules that would force all operating systems, even free and open ones, to collect users’ ages for the state’s Digital Age Assurance Act. 

The first version of the law wanted every operating system to ask for your age or birth date when you set up your device. Then, the system would tell apps and app stores how old you are, using age groups like “under 13” or “16–17.” Many open-source systems, like Linux, are built by volunteers and don’t have a company in charge. They also don’t have accounts, tracking, or a way to check ages. Critics said the law was too broad and would be almost impossible to follow in the open-source world. Privacy groups said it was dangerous because it could be used to track people. Some worried how California could control software anyone can change or share. After a lot of protest, lawmakers wrote a change that says the rule does not count for software that lets people copy, share, and change it—that is, open-source. Most popular Linux systems, like Ubuntu and Fedora, will not have to follow the new law. But platforms like SteamOS, which is based on Linux but tied to a company and a store, might still have to follow the rules. The new bill does not remove the old law, it just says which systems have to follow it. The change is not final yet, but it’s moving forward in California’s government.

Another asks if running Linux inside Windows (in a virtual machine) would be a way to get around the law; another user explains that the law would still apply to Windows, not the Linux part. Some say age verification is pointless because users can just lie about their age. A commenter wonders if Microsoft or Apple might make open-source versions to avoid the law, but others doubt that. Some people worry this change is unfair to Windows and MacOS, since only open-source systems get a pass, and think companies might challenge the law in court. Others point out that it’s very easy to install Linux and use it to avoid age checks, so the law does not really work. There are complaints that lawmakers do not understand technology and that these rules are not practical. A few users are frustrated that the media does not cover these important tech stories enough, and think journalists miss the real news. Overall, many see the law as a bad idea, and the change for Linux as a win for open-source and privacy, but they still have concerns about the law’s fairness and logic.

## C extensions, portability, and alternative compilers

- 原文链接: [C extensions, portability, and alternative compilers](https://lemon.rip/w/6-c-extensions-compilers/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48267126)

This article talks about why it’s hard to write C code that works everywhere, especially when using different compilers. The author shares problems faced while building a new C compiler, showing how many projects use special features or tricks specific to certain compilers, not just pure standard C.

First, the author explains how system libraries like glibc can be difficult for new compilers. For example, some struct layouts in Linux use special attributes like `__attribute__((packed))` that only work in GCC or similar compilers. If your compiler does not support this, things can break. There are also strange checks in header files that only help popular compilers, leaving others out.

Next, the article mentions that some headers, like limits.h, expect compiler-specific features. For instance, glibc often uses GCC’s way of including files and expects certain macros, making it tough for other compilers to work smoothly. Clang, another compiler, also has to work around these issues.

The author gives more examples, like SDL’s approach to choosing the best way to swap bytes (for endianness). SDL checks for compiler features in a way that can be confusing if you are not GCC or clang. Sometimes, it might try to use special assembly code when it shouldn’t.

OpenBSD’s code is also tricky. They use inline functions in a way that relies on old GCC behavior. If your compiler is not GCC, you might get broken code or conflicting definitions. Some projects, like Gnulib, have huge checks to try and fix these messy differences, but it’s still a pain for compiler creators.

Android’s bionic libc is different again. It expects clang, using its own set of special features. The author ran into these when compiling code on an Android phone using Termux.

The article ends by listing ways a new compiler could handle these problems: try to fix issues in other projects (hard), become popular so people care about your compiler (rare), patch things as you go (easiest), or copy GCC’s features to trick code into working (most practical).

From the comment section, many people agree that C portability is very hard in real life. Some say it’s nearly impossible to avoid compiler-specific code because so many libraries and projects use these tricks. Others point out that even big projects like the Linux kernel or glibc are full of non-standard code.

A few commenters share their own struggles making code portable across multiple compilers. They say the only way to win is to act like GCC or clang, even if your compiler is different. Some wish that projects would use standard “feature test” macros instead of checking for GCC or clang, but admit this is rare.

Others admire the work of small or independent compiler developers, saying it’s impressive they keep up with all these complicated differences. Some point out that the C language standard is not enough—real code often relies on behavior that isn’t covered by the standard.

A couple of people mention that the situation is better in other languages, where things are more standardized, but C is just too old and too widely used for this to change soon. Some even joke about how ugly and confusing the code in system headers can get. 

In the end, most agree that dealing with C extensions and portability is a constant challenge, and it’s unlikely to get easier any time soon. Many thank the author for sharing detailed, real-world examples of these struggles.

## Toshifumi Suzuki, founder of Seven-Eleven Japan, has died

Toshifumi Suzuki was the founder of Seven-Eleven Japan and helped change retail in Japan. He brought the idea of franchising and convenience stores to Japan in the 1970s, turning small shops into a network of over 10,000 stores by 2003.

Before Suzuki, Japanese retail was slow and old-fashioned. He saw that people in Japan liked shopping often for fresh food, and their homes were small, so they could not store much. Suzuki thought that a chain of small, local convenience stores could work well in Japan. Many experts and his own company bosses did not agree, but he convinced them and also got the American company 7-Eleven to help. Suzuki made sure the new stores used modern technology, like computer systems that tracked sales and what customers wanted. This made the stores better at restocking and serving customers quickly.

Suzuki also changed how products were delivered and sold, making the system more efficient and focused on what shoppers needed. He kept the company management flat, meaning there were not many layers between store workers and top bosses. He held regular meetings with managers and franchise owners to share ideas and solve problems. Suzuki’s company even rescued the original 7-Eleven business in America when it was in trouble and brought Japanese-style improvements to the U.S. stores.

He was strict with staff and always pushed for change and new ideas. Suzuki also started other businesses, like a bank inside convenience stores and tried internet shopping before it was common. He believed that business must always adapt because the world keeps changing.

Some users talked about how Seven-Eleven Japan is famous for being clean, helpful, and super organized, much more than convenience stores in other countries. A few said Suzuki’s use of data and technology was ahead of its time, helping the company know what customers wanted before they even asked. Some users pointed out that Suzuki’s story proves that copying ideas from abroad and making them fit local needs can really work.

Others discussed how Suzuki’s focus on small, local shops helped keep communities strong, instead of just building giant supermarkets. Some said his tough style could be hard for workers, but it led to great results. There were comments about how Seven-Eleven’s system is now studied in business schools around the world. A few people wondered what will happen to the company now, but most agreed Suzuki’s ideas will last. Many were surprised to learn that the Japanese Seven-Eleven ended up saving the original American company. ## Show HN: Write your BPF programs in Go, not C

- 原文链接: [Show HN: Write your BPF programs in Go, not C](https://github.com/boratanrikulu/gobee)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48225338)

This article is about “gobee,” a tool that lets you write BPF programs in a subset of Go, instead of C. BPF (Berkeley Packet Filter) programs usually run inside the Linux kernel and are often written in C, but gobee changes that by translating Go code into BPF-compatible C code.

The main idea is that Go has great tools for userspace BPF work, but until now, the kernel side always needed C. Gobee fills this gap. It takes your Go code, checks it for mistakes, and then turns it into readable C code that works with clang’s BPF backend. It also creates Go bindings, so your userspace code can talk to the BPF program easily and safely.

For example, you can write a Go function that listens to every “execve” system call (when a program starts) and sends info to userspace. Gobee turns this Go function into the matching C code for the kernel, plus the Go code you need outside the kernel. If there’s a problem, gobee makes it easy to know where in your Go source the error happened.

Gobee supports many BPF program types (like XDP, tracepoints, kprobes, etc.) and map types (like arrays, hashes, ringbufs, etc.). It knows about ~200 kernel helper functions and can auto-detect kernel versions, failing early if your kernel is too old. The C code it creates is meant to be readable, so you can check it yourself. Gobee does not try to replace major tools like clang or cilium/ebpf; it works with them and keeps things open and clear.

The toolchain still needs clang to compile the C into BPF bytecode, but gobee does not hide this. It’s a thin layer to help Go users, not a magic box. There’s no plan to make a direct BPF backend for Go, because that’s a huge project. Instead, reusing clang gives stability and access to modern features.

You can get started by installing gobee, writing your Go code, and running make to build everything. The project layout keeps BPF code and build artifacts organized, and examples are provided to help you learn.

In the Hacker News comments, some people are excited because Go is easier for them than C, and this tool helps them work with BPF without learning all the tricky parts of kernel C programming. They like that gobee keeps the generated C readable, so errors are easier to track down. Others point out that only a subset of Go is supported, so you can’t use all Go features. There are questions about how well this fits with existing Go workflows and toolchains.

Some users worry that since you still need to know how BPF works, gobee is not a full beginner tool. They note that you still need to use clang, and kernel programming stays complex. A few wonder about performance and whether the extra translation step adds bugs or problems. Others compare gobee to Rust’s Aya, which gives a more “native” experience but needs LLVM support in the Rust compiler.

Many agree that gobee is great for teams already using Go and cilium/ebpf, making the whole process more seamless. Some wish Go had its own BPF backend, but agree that this is unlikely soon. Others think gobee sets a good example for other languages and could inspire similar tools. Overall, the community sees gobee as a useful bridge for Go lovers working with BPF, but not a replacement for learning the basics of BPF or kernel programming.

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48270812)

Japan is testing a new hypersonic engine that could make flights from Tokyo to Los Angeles take only two hours. Engineers from JAXA and top universities in Japan did a successful ground test of a ramjet engine that could power planes at five times the speed of sound.

The ramjet engine they tested does not have moving parts. It works by using the plane’s fast movement to force air inside, then mixes that air with fuel to create thrust. This means the engine is lighter and can go much faster than normal jet engines. However, ramjets cannot start from zero speed—they need to be pushed to high speed first before they work.

The test took place at JAXA’s space center, using a wind tunnel to simulate flying high above the Earth, at about 25 kilometers up. At this altitude and speed, the air becomes very hot, reaching over 1,000°C. To keep the plane safe, engineers built a special heat shield to protect the electronics and control systems inside the aircraft. They used sensors to check the temperature on the plane’s surface and make sure it matched their computer models.

This test was only on the ground and with a small model. The next step is to put the engine on a small rocket and try an actual flight at Mach 5. If this works and they solve other problems, Japan hopes to have passenger planes flying at these speeds by the 2040s. Such a plane could fly twice as high as normal planes and make long trips across the Pacific in just two hours.

## Jensen–Shannon Divergence

- 原文链接: [Jensen–Shannon Divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jensen%E2%80%93Shannon_divergence)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48240356)

The article talks about Jensen–Shannon divergence, a way to measure how close or different two probability distributions are. This method is popular in statistics and information theory.

Jensen–Shannon divergence (JSD) is based on another measure called Kullback–Leibler divergence, but it fixes some problems. Unlike Kullback–Leibler, JSD is symmetric (the order of the distributions doesn’t matter), and it always gives a finite result. You can use JSD to compare two or more probability distributions. When the JSD is near zero, the distributions are very similar. The square root of JSD is called Jensen–Shannon distance, and it is a true metric, meaning it follows all the “distance” rules. JSD is also bounded: for two distributions, it is always between 0 and 1 (if you use base 2 logarithms). JSD is related to mutual information, which tells you how much knowing one variable tells you about another. There is also a version of JSD for quantum systems, used to compare quantum states. In practice, JSD is used in fields like bioinformatics, machine learning, and social sciences to compare data sets. There are software tools in Python, Ruby, and R for calculating JSD.

In the Hacker News comments, some readers say they use JSD often in machine learning, especially for comparing models or clustering data. Others like that JSD fixes the “infinite” problem with Kullback–Leibler divergence, which can give strange results if one distribution has a zero where the other does not. A few people point out that JSD is easier to interpret because of its clear upper and lower bounds. Some mention using JSD in natural language processing to compare word distributions. There is discussion about how JSD is used in generative adversarial networks (GANs) and why sometimes another distance, like Wasserstein, is chosen instead. A few comments ask for code examples or practical tips, and others share links to libraries. Some users warn that JSD still needs careful handling with small samples or in high dimensions. Overall, the comments show that JSD is popular, useful, and known by many in the tech and data science community.
