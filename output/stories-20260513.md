This article explains how people in the US can get a free domain name like somename.city.state.us. These domains are special because they show your connection to a city or state, and they have been around since 1992.

First, to get a locality domain, you must be a US citizen, live in the US, or have a company in the US. You start by checking if your city or state has a delegated domain registrar. The article links to an old list, but you may need to do extra searching because some registrars have changed names. If your city is not on the list, you probably cannot get a domain unless you work for the local government.

Next, you need nameservers before you can apply. The article suggests using Amazon Lightsail, which lets you set up DNS for free even if you do not buy hosting. You must create a DNS zone for your planned domain and write down the name servers.

Then, you fill out a special form called the Interim .US Domain Template. The article helps you with each part of the form—like using your own address if you are not a company, describing how you will use the domain, and adding the nameserver details. You must also show you are a US citizen and say the domain is for personal use.

After you send the form to the right registrar, you wait for them to process your request. This might take days or weeks. If they approve, you set up your DNS records in Lightsail to point to your website or any other service. The article suggests free web hosting like GitHub Pages.

There are also answers to common questions. For example, you do not need to live in the city to keep your domain, and your personal address will not show up on WHOIS searches.

A few commenters wondered why this system is not better known, and some worried these domains could be used for scams or fake government sites.

## MacBook Neo Deep Dive: Benchmarks, Wafer Economics, and the 8GB Gamble

- 原文链接: [MacBook Neo Deep Dive: Benchmarks, Wafer Economics, and the 8GB Gamble](https://www.jdhodges.com/blog/macbook-neo-benchmarks-analysis/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48125617)

The article looks at the new MacBook Neo—a $599 Apple laptop using the A18 Pro chip from the iPhone 16 Pro instead of Apple’s usual M-series chips. The writer explains how Apple is able to offer strong single-core performance at a low price, but the laptop comes with only 8GB of memory, which cannot be upgraded.

The A18 Pro has 6 CPU cores (2 high-power, 4 efficient), a 5-core GPU, and a 16-core Neural Engine. Benchmarks show that for short, single-core jobs, the Neo is as fast as M3 or M4 Macs, and much faster than cheap Windows laptops. But if you run heavy tasks for more than a minute, the fanless design causes the chip to get hot and slow down a lot—up to 87% lower performance after several minutes of hard work. For most users—web, email, simple coding, light photo editing—this isn’t a problem, since those tasks are “bursty” and finish before heat matters.

The Neo’s price is possible because the A18 Pro chip is smaller and cheaper to make than M-series chips, and Apple already uses it in millions of iPhones, spreading out development costs. Apple also saves money by cutting features: the Neo has a good screen and battery, but only 8GB RAM, no backlit keyboard, no MagSafe charger, only one fast USB-C port, and a basic webcam.

A big part of the story is the global RAM shortage in 2026, as memory makers focus on chips for AI servers instead of regular laptops. This makes memory more expensive for everyone, so by using just 8GB, Apple keeps the Neo’s price lower while competitors are forced to charge more for the same specs.

The article says the Neo is good for students or anyone needing a basic, reliable laptop with long battery life and a strong build. But it’s not for people who need to do heavy work like video editing, running many apps at once, or gaming. If you need more RAM or ports, the MacBook Air is better, but it costs much more.

In the comments, people have different opinions. Some like the energy efficiency and quiet operation, saying most cheap laptops are loud and slow even on easy tasks. Others worry that the Neo is too limited for students, arguing that kids need flexible machines to explore things like IT or 3D modeling, and the Neo’s weak sustained performance and low RAM could block that. The article’s author replies that for basic schoolwork, the Neo is a good deal, but agrees it isn’t right for young people who want to dig deep into tech or creative work. Another reader says they would rather buy a Windows laptop with more RAM and storage for the same price, but the author points out that many of those laptops have poor screens, weak batteries, or are hard to actually find at that price.

Overall, some people think the Neo is a smart, affordable choice for simple tasks, while others believe its limits make it a bad deal for anyone who might want to do more with their computer. The discussion shows that the right choice depends on what you need from your laptop and how much you can spend.

## A History of IDEs at Google

- 原文链接: [A History of IDEs at Google](https://laurent.le-brun.eu/blog/a-history-of-ides-at-google)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48073979)

This article is about how Google’s IDE (Integrated Development Environment) tools changed over the years, especially for their huge codebase. At first, Google let engineers use any editor or IDE they liked, which meant everyone used different tools.

The company had strict rules for code style and tools, but not for IDEs. Some leaders, like Jeff Dean, said forcing everyone to use the same IDE would make people unhappy. So, for years, everyone used what they wanted: Vim, Emacs, Eclipse, IntelliJ, or others. But this caused problems, because every tool needed its own plugins for things like Bazel (the build system), Starlark, code formatting, and code search. Sometimes, engineers built their own tools and shared them across teams. Google encouraged these side projects by letting people use part of their work time for them.

In 2015, Google made a team to focus on IntelliJ integration. This happened because Google has special tools and a huge codebase, so normal IDEs didn’t always work well. Around 2013, some people built a web-based editor called Cider (short for “Cloud IDE”). At first, Cider was simple and used mostly by writers for quick changes. Over time, it got more features useful for developers, like code completion using the language-server protocol.

Cider’s backend could handle Google’s massive codebase better than normal local IDEs. It made searching and jumping between files fast, even with millions of files. Later, Google made a new version called Cider V, which used VSCode as the frontend. This way, they got all of VSCode’s features and extensions, and it was easier to support more languages and workflows.

It took years to finish Cider V and get people to switch, because even small changes to the workflow or color schemes could upset users. By 2023, most Google engineers were using Cider V, and teams started building many internal extensions. Google began adding AI features, like smart code completion and machine learning for code reviews.

Having one main IDE meant Google could invest more in making it better, and more teams could share tools and features. The author believes this move to a standard IDE was very helpful for Google, even though it was expensive.

Hacker News comments shared many views. Some said that making everyone use the same IDE is hard because developers are very loyal to their favorite tools. Others said that Google’s huge codebase really needs special tools, so a custom IDE makes sense. A few people wondered why Google did not just use off-the-shelf solutions, but others pointed out that Google’s size and special needs mean normal tools often do not scale well.

Some commenters liked the idea of a cloud-based IDE, especially for people using Chromebooks or working remotely. Others worried about privacy and performance with cloud tools. There were also jokes about how color schemes and keyboard shortcuts can start big arguments among developers.

A few former Googlers joined the discussion, saying they liked how open Google was about building tools and letting employees help improve them. Some people said it is nice that Google gives time for “20% projects,” but others thought it could be hard to keep all the tools working together. Many agreed that no single IDE makes everyone happy, but good integrations and AI features can win people over. A few wondered if smaller companies could ever build something like Cider or if this is only possible at Google’s scale. Overall, readers found the article honest and interesting, and many shared their own experiences with big company IDEs.

The challenge is to put four black queens and one black bishop on a chessboard. The goal is to place them so that every square on the board is attacked by at least one of these pieces. That means there is no safe spot left for a white king; anywhere the king goes, it will be in check. The article lets readers try this puzzle by moving the pieces on a digital board.

The puzzle is interesting because both queens and bishops can control many squares. Queens can attack in straight lines, both across and up and down, as well as diagonally. A bishop can only attack diagonally. The trick is to find positions for the pieces so that together, they cover every square. You have to think carefully—putting too many pieces close together wastes their power, but spreading them out too much might leave some squares open.

## Composer leaks contents of tokens configured as GitHub OAuth tokens

- 原文链接: [Composer leaks contents of tokens configured as GitHub OAuth tokens](https://github.com/composer/composer/security/advisories/GHSA-f9f8-rm49-7jv2)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48120514)

Composer, a popular PHP package manager, leaked full GitHub OAuth tokens in logs when those tokens did not match an expected format. This happened because Composer checked GitHub tokens with old rules and did not allow the hyphen character, but GitHub started using new token formats that include hyphens. When Composer saw a token with a hyphen, it threw an error and printed the whole token in the error message, which went into logs. Many GitHub Actions, like the common `setup-php` action, add the GitHub token to Composer's config by default, so this problem could happen even if users did not change anything. These tokens usually only work for one repository and expire quickly, usually after a job ends or after six hours, but on self-hosted runners, the token could last up to 24 hours. The problem was fixed in Composer versions 2.9.8, 2.2.28, and 1.10.28. The issue is serious because logs with leaked tokens could be copied, read, or sent somewhere unsafe. The leak happens because Composer puts the whole invalid token in the error message, and GitHub’s secret masking tool sometimes misses it due to how the error is printed. The security score is high, with no user action needed to trigger it and no special permissions required. The leak could happen with any future credentials that fail Composer’s checks, not just the new GitHub tokens.

In the comments, some people said this shows why you should never print secrets, even in error messages. Others felt Composer’s strict token checking was too old and should have allowed the new formats from GitHub. Some pointed out that secret masking in CI systems is not perfect, especially if error messages wrap or change the token text. A few users said this is not so bad because the tokens expire fast and only work for one repo, but others replied that even a short-lived token can be dangerous if someone grabs it quickly. There was talk about how hard it is to keep up with token format changes from big platforms like GitHub. Some commenters gave advice to always review logs for secrets and to use extra logging filters. Others joked that it’s a classic example of strict input checks causing more harm, and a few said they would now check their own projects for this kind of problem.

## S-100 Virtual Workbench

- 原文链接: [S-100 Virtual Workbench](https://grantmestrength.github.io/S100/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48123546)

This article is about the S-100 Virtual Workbench, a web tool that lets you build and run old computers like the Altair 8800 using virtual hardware cards. The tool is made for people who want to learn about or play with vintage computers, especially those that used the S-100 bus.

The workbench lets you drag and drop different cards into a virtual chassis. You can pick CPUs like the Intel 8080 or the Zilog Z80. Each CPU has its own features—like the Z80 supports more instructions than the 8080. You can add RAM and ROM cards, and set their address ranges. The ROM card can use built-in images or custom binaries, and can be paged out when needed.

There are several I/O cards you can add. The Serial SIO card provides a system console, letting you talk to the virtual computer. Floppy disk controllers (FDC) are available for disk support, including ones that match real historical hardware like the MITS 88-DCDD and the WD1793. These controllers work with disk images, so you can run CP/M operating system like on the original machines.

There are also special cards for graphics and video, like the Cromemco Dazzler for color output and the VDM-1 for text display. The SOL-IO card emulates the Sol-20's built-in I/O, including keyboard and serial ports. You can assemble a full system, boot it up, and see how all the cards work together. The tool includes a bus analyzer and a terminal to see and control what’s happening inside the machine.

In the comments, many people liked how easy it is to use the virtual workbench. Some said it’s great for learning about old computers without needing real hardware. Others remembered using real S-100 systems and liked seeing them recreated online. A few users discussed the technical accuracy of the emulation; some pointed out small differences from original hardware, while others said the emulation is close enough for most purposes.

One commenter wished for more detailed documentation or tutorials for beginners. Another person asked about adding support for more hardware, like different CPUs or video cards. Someone else mentioned how nice it is to upload custom ROMs and disk images, making the tool useful for software testing. A few users wondered if this could help teach computer architecture in schools. Some shared ideas for improving the user interface, like adding tooltips or easier ways to save system setups.

Overall, people thought the S-100 Virtual Workbench is a fun and educational project. They liked that it brings vintage computing to more people, even those who can't get real machines.
