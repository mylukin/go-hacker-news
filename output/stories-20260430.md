## LinkedIn scans for 6,278 extensions and encrypts the results into every request

- 原文链接: [LinkedIn scans for 6,278 extensions and encrypts the results into every request](https://404privacy.com/blog/linkedin-is-scanning-your-browser-extensions-this-is-how-they-use-the-data/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47967262)

LinkedIn checks your browser for over 6,000 different Chrome extensions every time you visit the site. The article explains that LinkedIn uses this data to build a very detailed profile about you, linking your browser software to your real identity—like your name, job, and company.

This scanning started years ago and the list of extensions has grown fast, now covering thousands of extensions, including ones about job searching, politics, religion, and disability. LinkedIn does not tell users about this scanning in their privacy policy or ask for consent. The scan works by sending requests to see if certain files from extensions are present in your browser; if they are, LinkedIn knows which extensions you have. The scanning is hidden inside LinkedIn’s JavaScript code, which is hard to read on purpose. Results are encrypted and sent to LinkedIn’s servers, then added to every request you make while using the site.

The article warns that this is not just about tracking you on LinkedIn. Because your LinkedIn profile is tied to your real-world identity, this extension data can be connected to other data about you from outside LinkedIn, creating a much bigger privacy problem. This can affect not only you but also your employer, as LinkedIn can see which tools and software are used in your company. The scanning is part of a larger device fingerprinting system that collects many details about your computer and browser. There is now a criminal investigation about this in Germany, as LinkedIn may be breaking European law by doing this without consent and by acting against users who use certain extensions.

Hacker News users had strong reactions. Many were shocked at how detailed and hidden this extension scanning is. Some said it was a new level of privacy violation, especially by a company trusted with professional data. Others pointed out that browser extension scanning is not new, but LinkedIn’s scale and linking to real identities makes it much worse. A few users explained that browser makers like Chrome allow this kind of probing, and suggested switching to browsers that block or limit it, like Firefox. Some commenters shared tips for detecting or blocking these scans, using privacy tools or browser settings. Others worried that even these defenses might not be enough, and that the practice could spread to other sites. There were also voices noting that large tech companies often ignore privacy until forced by law, so criminal investigations may be the only way to stop such behavior. Some felt frustrated that privacy policies are often too vague to warn users about these risks. A few developers said LinkedIn’s actions might hurt trust in browser extensions and harm the ecosystem for everyone. Overall, the comments showed a mix of anger, worry, technical advice, and calls for stricter rules.

## How Mark Klein told the EFF about Room 641A [book excerpt]

- 原文链接: [How Mark Klein told the EFF about Room 641A [book excerpt]](https://thereader.mitpress.mit.edu/the-whistleblower-who-uncovered-the-nsas-big-brother-machine/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47965060)

This article tells the story of how Mark Klein, a retired AT&T technician, gave evidence to the Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) about secret government spying. He showed that the NSA was copying internet traffic by tapping into AT&T’s fiber cables in San Francisco after 9/11.

After the Patriot Act passed, the line between spying on foreigners and Americans got weaker. The NSA started collecting Americans’ phone and internet data, even though it was likely against the law. Mark Klein worked at the Folsom Street AT&T building and noticed a special room, 641A, that only certain people with NSA clearance could enter. He saw that the building’s main internet cables were split: one copy went to the internet, and the other went into this secret room. This setup let the NSA make copies of everyone’s online activity without slowing anything down or leaving clear signs. Mark called this the “Big Brother machine.”

Mark brought real documents and network diagrams to the EFF. Before him, nobody had given proof strong enough to take to court. The EFF checked with experts, who said this method could copy almost all internet traffic. With this evidence, the EFF started a lawsuit against the government’s mass surveillance. The team made sure Mark had his own lawyers, since he risked legal trouble for exposing this. They worried the documents might be classified, but also believed the public had a right to know about illegal actions. The article also describes the tension and fear the EFF team felt as they prepared to challenge the government.

In the Hacker News comments, many people praised Mark Klein for his courage and called him a hero. Some said the article reminded them of how hard it is to protect privacy today, and that most people do not realize how much data is collected. Others pointed out that mass surveillance is not just a U.S. problem; many countries do this. A few commenters discussed the technical side, saying the method was both simple and powerful—splitting a fiber line is easy, but the effect is huge. Some worried that not enough has changed since this story became public, and that laws have grown even weaker. Others argued that some surveillance is necessary for national security, but many replied that spying on everyone is not the answer. There was also talk about whistleblowers, with some sharing that people like Mark Klein often face big risks and little reward. Finally, a few users wondered how many similar secret rooms exist today, and if we will ever know the full truth.

## Shai-Hulud Themed Malware Found in the PyTorch Lightning AI Training Library

- 原文链接: [Shai-Hulud Themed Malware Found in the PyTorch Lightning AI Training Library](https://semgrep.dev/blog/2026/malicious-dependency-in-pytorch-lightning-used-for-ai-training/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47964617)

A popular Python package called ‘lightning’ was attacked by hackers, and two versions (2.6.2 and 2.6.3) had hidden malware. Many people use this package for AI training, so the risk is big.

The malware was hidden in a special folder and started running as soon as the package was installed. It could steal secrets like passwords, tokens, and cloud keys from any computer using it. It also tried to spread itself by infecting GitHub repositories—creating strange new public repos with Dune-themed names and leaving hidden files in projects. The attack used clever tricks to avoid being seen, like hiding its real commands and sending stolen data in different ways, including using GitHub commits and public repos.

If the malware found credentials for npm (a JavaScript package manager), it would infect npm packages, spreading the attack even further. The stolen secrets could come from files, environment settings, cloud provider accounts, and even memory from GitHub Actions runners. The malware worked on AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud, so it could steal from almost anywhere. It also set up special hooks so that opening a project in developer tools like Claude Code or VS Code would run the malware again and keep it active. The malware sometimes added fake workflow files in GitHub repos to steal more secrets.

If you used the bad versions of lightning, you should check for strange files and rotate all your secrets right away. Tools like Semgrep can help find if you are affected. Signs of an attack include weird commit messages like “EveryBoiWeBuildIsAWormyBoi” or new repos with “A Mini Shai-Hulud has Appeared” descriptions. The attackers used similar methods as in a previous campaign called Mini Shai-Hulud, using Dune-themed names.

In the Hacker News comments, some people were surprised at how complex and wide-reaching the attack was. They talked about how scary it is that just installing a library can let hackers into your computer or cloud. Some said open source is getting harder to trust because there are so many dependencies in projects now. Others pointed out that supply chain attacks like this are increasing and that package managers need better checks. A few mentioned that even careful developers can be hit, since the malware spread into trusted libraries used by many big projects. Some users debated whether developer tools like VS Code are too powerful and risky if they can be abused this way. Others shared advice on rotating secrets and using tools to scan for problems. Many agreed that fixing the supply chain problem is very hard, and more people are calling for stronger protections in open source software.

## I built a Game Boy emulator in F#

- 原文链接: [I built a Game Boy emulator in F#](https://nickkossolapov.github.io/fame-boy/building-a-game-boy-emulator-in-fsharp/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47965503)

This article is about someone building a Game Boy emulator in the F# programming language. The author wanted to better understand how computers work, so they chose to recreate a simple but meaningful device from their childhood—the Game Boy.

To prepare, the author first completed the "From NAND to Tetris" course to learn computer basics, then made a basic CHIP-8 emulator. Later, they spent months building "Fame Boy," a full Game Boy emulator with sound, working on both desktop and web browsers.

The emulator’s design is split between a core (the actual emulation) and a frontend (display and controls). The interface is simple: just arrays for the screen and sound, and a few functions to step the emulator and get button states. The CPU code is modeled after the Game Boy’s real chip and uses F#’s type system to make sure only valid instructions can be written. The author explains how F# helps avoid bugs by making some mistakes impossible to compile. For example, separating where data comes from and goes to means you can’t accidentally write an illegal instruction.

They discuss making trade-offs between pure functional code (which copies data to avoid side effects) and using mutable variables for speed. While the CHIP-8 emulator was pure, the Game Boy’s higher speed needs forced more mutability. The author also describes the process of testing: at first, they implemented instructions as errors popped up, but later wrote unit tests with help from AI to cover more cases and catch mistakes.

The article covers tricky parts of emulation, like the PPU (graphics), the joypad (controller), and especially sound, which was hard to get right across platforms. The author learned that syncing audio and video is tough, and sometimes you have to pick between smooth pictures or smooth sound. They made audio the main driver, since bad sound is more annoying than dropped video frames.

For the web version, they used Fable to compile F# to JavaScript, running into problems with number sizes but fixing them by hand. Performance tuning came next: at first, they used nice, readable code, but profiling showed it was too slow. Removing extra object creation and branching doubled the speed. Turning off debug mode made it even faster.

The author used benchmarking tools to compare performance on different machines and browsers, finding that sound processing slowed things down more than graphics. They also explain how AI tools helped, both in finding bugs and suggesting performance improvements. In some cases, AI even fixed problems the author had struggled with for weeks.

From the comment section, many readers praised the author’s clear explanations and the choice of F#. Some found the technical details about F#’s type safety and domain modeling very interesting. Others noted that building an emulator is a great way to learn how computers work. A few commenters shared their own emulator projects or asked questions about F# vs. other languages, like Rust or C++. People discussed the trade-offs between code purity and speed, with some saying it’s okay to mix functional and imperative styles for real-world tasks. Some asked technical questions about handling sound or browser issues, and the author replied with more details.

There were also comments about AI: some people said using AI to help with tests or bug fixing is smart, while others wondered if it takes away from the learning process. A few readers liked that the author was honest about where AI helped and where it fell short. Many said the article inspired them to try similar projects, or to pick up F# and functional programming. Some praised the open-source code and the web demo, saying it’s a fun way to explore how emulators work. Overall, readers appreciated the honesty, the technical depth, and the lessons learned through the project.

## CopyFail was not disclosed to Gentoo developer

- 原文链接: [CopyFail was not disclosed to Gentoo developer](https://www.openwall.com/lists/oss-security/2026/04/30/10)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47965108)

A serious Linux bug called “CopyFail” lets local users become root, which means they can take control of the system. This bug started in Linux version 4.14 back in 2017 and was fixed only recently in newer versions like 6.18.22, 6.19.12, and 7.0.

The problem did not get fixed in older but still widely used “longterm” kernels such as 6.12, 6.6, 6.1, 5.15, and 5.10. It is hard to fix those versions because the new code does not work well with the old code (it does not “apply cleanly”). Gentoo, a Linux distribution, made its own workaround to keep users safe, but it is not a perfect solution. The developer said they tried to fix the bug for older versions but could not do it in a safe way.

Another issue is how the Linux community shares information. Usually, if someone finds a bug, they can warn Linux distributions early using a special mailing list. But this did not happen for CopyFail. Some developers and distributions did not get any warning before the bug was made public. This made it harder for them to protect their users in time.

In the comments, some people are worried that many servers are still running old kernels and are now open to attack. Others say the Linux security process is broken because not all bugs are shared with everyone who needs to know. Some users defend the developers, saying these bugs are hard to fix and people are working very hard under a lot of stress. There is also talk about how backporting (moving fixes from new to old code) is very tricky and can easily break things. A few people mention that the process feels rushed or that mistakes happen more often now, maybe because of more AI-generated code. Some think the community needs better rules for sharing security news, while others argue that too many warnings might also help attackers. Overall, people agree that Linux security is a tough job and that clear, early communication is important.

## How an oil refinery works

- 原文链接: [How an oil refinery works](https://www.construction-physics.com/p/how-an-oil-refinery-works)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47962548)

This article explains how oil refineries turn crude oil into useful products like gasoline, diesel, and plastics. Even with more wind and solar energy, the world still uses a lot of oil—over 100 million barrels a day.

Oil comes from ancient plants and animals that changed over millions of years. When pumped from the ground, crude oil is a mix of many chemicals, mostly hydrocarbons (carbon and hydrogen). Different oils have different mixes of heavy and light molecules, and different amounts of sulfur (“sweet” or “sour” oil). The first big job in a refinery is to separate these chemicals. They do this by heating the oil until it becomes vapor, then letting it cool in a tall column with trays. As the vapor rises, heavier molecules cool and turn back to liquid first, while lighter ones rise higher before condensing. This separates the oil into groups called fractions.

Some refineries only do this basic separation, but most go further, using more steps. For example, cracking breaks heavy molecules into lighter, more valuable ones like gasoline. This can be done with heat or special materials called catalysts. There are other steps too, like using hydrogen to clean up the oil, or rearranging molecules to make better fuels. Refineries use big tank farms to store liquids, and special tanks or underground spaces for gases.

The article looks at a real refinery in Richmond, California, which handles about 250,000 barrels per day. It has many steps: atmospheric and vacuum distillation, cracking, reforming, and more. The US has over 130 refineries, mostly along the Gulf Coast. Some are simple, others are very complex and huge, like the world’s largest in India.

Complexity is measured by the Nelson Complexity Index. A refinery with more advanced processing steps has a higher index. The average US refinery is quite complex, and the biggest ones process huge amounts of oil, keeping the modern world supplied with fuels and chemicals. The sheer size and scale of refineries is what stands out—they process massive amounts every day, quietly keeping society running.

Commenters on Hacker News found the article well-written and clear, with several praising its simple explanations and good diagrams. Some said they learned a lot about how refineries work and were surprised by the scale. Others pointed out safety and environmental concerns, saying refineries are risky and have caused accidents in the past. A few people discussed the future, wondering how refineries will adapt as we use more electric vehicles and renewable energy. One user mentioned how hard it is to replace all the products made from oil, not just fuels but also plastics and chemicals. Another person shared a story about living near a refinery and noticing the constant smell and noise. Some users debated if more refineries should be built or if we should focus on cleaner energy instead. Others asked technical questions about specific processes, like how catalysts are cleaned and reused. Overall, the discussion showed respect for the technical challenge of refining and curiosity about how things might change in the next decades.
