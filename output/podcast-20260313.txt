First, let’s talk about Channel Surfer, a new web app that lets you watch YouTube videos like you are flipping TV channels. The idea is to bring back that old feeling of surprise, where you never know what will play next. You just press a button, and Channel Surfer picks a random YouTube video for you. If you don’t like it, you press again and get a new one. There are no accounts, no comments, and no recommendations—just video after video, with a simple design that makes it feel like classic cable TV.

Many people on Hacker News say they miss this kind of channel surfing and are happy to see it return in a new way. Some like that you don’t have to pick what to watch, and they hope it helps them discover new things. A few worry about getting strange or unsafe videos, and some want filters or categories. Others think the simple design is best and hope it will not get too many features. There is also talk about how the YouTube algorithm might still affect what you see, and some users are curious about mobile support. Overall, most people enjoy the idea, sharing memories of TV surfing and hoping Channel Surfer helps them find new, surprising videos.

Next, we look at CanIRun.ai, a website that helps you check if your computer can run different AI models locally. It lists many open-source models and gives each one a score from S (best) to F (worst), based on memory and power needs. Small models like Qwen, Llama, Gemma, and TinyLlama get high scores and work well on phones, laptops, or even Raspberry Pi. Medium models use more memory, and big models need a high-end desktop or server to run at all.

The site uses public data and shows you memory use, speed, best tasks, and model features. In the comments, people say this is a helpful tool, making it easier to try AI at home. Some warn that the numbers are just estimates, and real results may differ. A few people share tips for running big models with tricks like quantization or swap memory, though it can still be slow. Some want more info about GPU benchmarks or install steps. Many like that running AI locally means better privacy. There’s hope that open models will get better, so more can run on normal devices soon. The community sees CanIRun.ai as a good project that helps bring AI to more people.

Now, let’s talk about Mouser, a free and open-source app for changing button functions on the Logitech MX Master 3S mouse. It works on Windows and macOS, with no accounts, no cloud, and no tracking. You can remap all six buttons, set profiles for different apps, pick from 22 actions, adjust mouse speed, and save settings locally. It’s easy to use: just download, run, and click the button you want to change.

People in the comments are happy to see an open alternative to Logitech’s own software, which many dislike for being slow and needing an account. Some want support for other Logitech mice and for Linux. There are also requests for more features, like mouse gestures or more key options. Some are curious how Mouser works compared to Logitech’s app, and if it supports all the mouse functions. A few warn that Logitech might block third-party apps in the future. Others share tips for running Mouser with other software or ask about safety and privacy. Many thank the developer for keeping things local and not using telemetry. Some offer to help add new features or Linux support. Most people are excited and hope Mouser will grow to support more devices.

Moving on, Hammerspoon is a tool for automating tasks on macOS using Lua scripts. It acts as a bridge between your Mac and Lua, letting you control things like window movement, settings, and reactions to events. You write code in an `init.lua` file, and Hammerspoon runs your scripts. It’s open source and has many extensions for different tasks.

In the comments, users say Hammerspoon is powerful and flexible. Many share how they use it to move windows, control music, or handle daily tasks. Some say it is hard to learn if you don’t know Lua, but the docs are helpful. People compare it with Keyboard Maestro and BetterTouchTool; Hammerspoon gives more control, but the other tools are easier for beginners. Some worry that macOS updates may break automations, but most say Hammerspoon is well maintained. A few wish for more ready-made scripts. Many are surprised it’s not more popular, given how much it can do. Most agree it’s a great tool for people who like to tinker.

Let’s switch to global news. Qatar has shut down its helium production, and this is causing big problems for the world’s chip supply chain. Helium is key for making computer chips, keeping machines clean and cool. Most helium comes from Qatar, and if the shutdown lasts more than two weeks, chip factories could stop. This could delay computers, phones, and cars, and raise prices. Making new helium plants is slow and costly.

In the comments, people are surprised so much helium comes from one place. Some ask why chip makers do not keep more in storage. Others point out helium is also used in hospitals, like in MRI machines. Some people remember past shortages and say not much changed. There are worries about higher prices for electronics. Some think governments should help make the supply chain stronger. Others say this shows how small things can have big effects on tech.

Now to a science story: Stanford researchers have recorded a blue whale’s heart rate in the wild for the first time. They used a special sensor tag with suction cups to collect data near the whale’s left flipper. The heart slowed to just two beats per minute during deep dives and sped up to over 25 beats per minute when the whale came up to breathe. This was surprising to scientists, showing bigger differences than expected.

The team thinks the whale’s heart is specially built to handle these extremes, and this may be why blue whales can’t get any bigger. The researchers want to improve the sensor and study other big whales. In the comments, people are amazed at how slow the whale’s heart is, comparing it to tiny animals like hummingbirds. Some wonder how the team put the tag on such a huge animal. There are jokes about how big the whale’s heart must be, and praise for the technology and teamwork. Some ask if this could help human medicine, or be used with other wild animals. Most are excited to learn more from the world’s largest animal.

Turning to Apple news, Parallels says you can run Windows on the new MacBook Neo using a virtual machine. The Neo has Apple’s A18 Pro chip and just 8GB of RAM, with no upgrade option. Running a Windows VM needs at least 4GB of RAM, so only 4GB is left for macOS. Parallels says it works for light tasks, like old business software, but is not good for heavy use. If you need better performance, a MacBook Air with 16GB RAM is a better choice.

In the comments, people say it is possible, but you should not expect good speed. Some suggest trying VMware Fusion, which is free. Others say even 16GB can feel low for VMs. There are questions about why people want to run Windows on a Mac at all, but some say it’s useful for special tools. Most agree it’s nice you can do it, but warn about the limits.

Now, a story about new technology: researchers in Australia have made a way to send secret data by hiding it in heat radiation, using “negative light.” Special LEDs and diodes blend data into normal heat, so only special receivers can see it. This makes the data invisible to outsiders, even with a thermal camera. In lab tests, they could send 100 kilobytes per second, and hope to go faster with better materials like graphene. The system could help the military, banks, or anyone needing secret communication.

People in the comments are excited about this idea, though some wonder if it will work outside the lab or if it can be blocked. Some ask if it could be used for spying or if it can be detected with new tools. There are worries about dangerous materials, but the team is looking for safer options. Many joke about “invisible flashlights” or “Morse code in the dark.” Most agree it’s clever and want to see more real-world tests.

Next, TUI Studio is a new tool for building terminal user interfaces with a visual editor, like Figma but for terminal apps. You can drag and drop over 20 components, use layout systems like Flexbox and Grid, and choose from 8 color themes. There’s a live preview in ANSI, and you can save designs as JSON files. The big feature will be exporting designs to code for frameworks like Ink, BubbleTea, Blessed, Textual, OpenTUI, and Tview, but this is not ready yet. The app works on Mac, Windows, and Linux, and is free for now.

In the comments, people are excited about making terminal apps without writing all the code by hand. Some are waiting for the export feature before trying it. There are a few worries about code-signing warnings and how well complex layouts will work. Some want more frameworks in the future. Most people are positive and curious to see how TUI Studio grows.

Finally, Context Gateway is a tool that compresses chat history for AI agents before sending it to an LLM, helping avoid size limits and delays. It works as a proxy between your agent and the LLM API, always summarizing in the background. When the chat history gets too big, the summary is ready, so the agent can keep working without waiting. Features include a dashboard, prompt history, Docker support, and open source code.

In the comments, many think this is useful, especially for saving LLM costs or running your own agent. Some ask if important info gets lost in the summaries. Others wonder how it compares to just using bigger LLM context windows. Some want to plug in their own summarizer model. There are privacy questions about storing chat logs, but people like that it’s open source. A few worry that a bad summary could confuse the agent. Most agree it makes AI agents faster and more reliable, but there are tradeoffs between speed, accuracy, and context.
