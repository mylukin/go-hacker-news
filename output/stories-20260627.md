## Show HN: Adrafinil – keep a lid-closed Mac awake only while agents work

- 原文链接: [Show HN: Adrafinil – keep a lid-closed Mac awake only while agents work](https://github.com/kageroumado/adrafinil)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48701512)

Adrafinil is a new macOS app that keeps your Mac awake with the lid closed, but only when AI coding agents are actually working. Unlike tools like Caffeinate or Amphetamine, which keep your Mac awake all the time, Adrafinil only steps in when an AI agent is busy, and lets your Mac sleep as soon as the agent stops.

The app runs in your menu bar and integrates with popular AI coding tools like Claude Code, Codex, and Cursor. When these agents start a task, they send a signal to Adrafinil, which then prevents your Mac from sleeping—even with the lid closed. As soon as the job is done, Adrafinil lets your Mac go back to normal sleep mode. This is helpful if you want to run long AI jobs overnight without keeping your Mac awake for no reason.

Adrafinil uses a special helper running as root to override clamshell (lid-closed) sleep. It only uses this power when needed and keeps the helper small and easy to audit for security. The app also checks for overheating: if your Mac gets too hot while the lid is closed, it will stop blocking sleep to avoid damage. If an AI session dies or goes idle, Adrafinil will also release its hold and let your Mac sleep.

The app works with a simple command-line interface, and you can set it up to automatically work with many different agents. There are extra features, like a sound when you close the lid to tell you it’s still running, a summary when you open the lid again, and easy uninstall that cleans up everything. You can download a pre-built version or build it yourself with Xcode if you want to check the code first.

In the Hacker News comments, some users are excited about this tool, saying it fixes a real problem for anyone running long AI tasks or builds on a MacBook. They like that it’s less wasteful than always-on tools and appreciate the security-focused, open-source approach. Others mention they often use Caffeinate or Amphetamine, but don't like how those keep the Mac awake all the time—so Adrafinil’s agent-aware approach feels smarter.

A few people discuss the technical challenges, especially how hard it is to control clamshell sleep on macOS and the need for root privileges. There’s some concern about the risks of running helpers as root, but users like that Adrafinil keeps the privileged part small and easy to check. Some wonder about compatibility with older Macs or different macOS versions, since the tool targets the latest release.

A number of commenters ask if it could work with other background tasks, not just AI agents. Others share tips for running jobs on Macs with the lid closed, and mention tricks they’ve used in the past. Overall, most see the value in a tool that keeps the Mac awake only when it’s actually needed, and like the careful design that avoids unnecessary power use and risk.

## AI learns the “dark art” of RFIC design

- 原文链接: [AI learns the “dark art” of RFIC design](https://spectrum.ieee.org/ai-radio-chip-design)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48660021)

This article talks about how AI is now being used to design radio-frequency integrated circuits (RFICs), which are the chips in our wireless devices. RFIC design is very hard and has been called a “dark art” because it relies so much on human skill and experience.

The article explains that RFICs are key to wireless technology, like 5G, autonomous cars, and satellite communications. Traditionally, designing these chips took years and cost millions, because engineers had to balance many complex rules from physics and electronics. The design process involved choosing the right “blueprint” for the circuit, figuring out how to connect all the parts, and making sure signals move smoothly—like plumbing in a house. Even a small mistake could ruin the chip. Human designers often used old templates, but these were not always the best.

The Princeton research team wanted AI to help. Instead of improving on old templates, they used reinforcement learning and inverse design. This means the AI starts from nothing and tries out many new circuit designs, learning what works best as it goes. The system uses a neural network trained on lots of chip layouts, so it can quickly guess how a new design will perform, without doing heavy math each time.

The AI can now generate completely new and strange-looking circuits that work better than human-made ones. One example was a 5G amplifier chip designed by AI, which looked like a QR code but had record efficiency and bandwidth. The team also used diffusion models (like those used for making AI art) to control how “human-readable” the final chip layout looks, making it easier for engineers to debug.

The article says that for AI to get even better at chip design, the field needs big, shared datasets—just like how image-recognition AIs improved after huge photo libraries were shared online. But today, most chip design data is private, locked away by companies. Some government programs tried to open this up, but were closed down; still, the community is moving forward.

In the comments, many readers are amazed that AI can now handle such a complex and specialized task. Some say this could make wireless tech much cheaper and faster to develop. Others warn that AI-designed circuits might be hard to understand or fix if something goes wrong, so engineers will still need to check the work. A few are excited about the “art-like” designs, thinking AI could help discover new physics or better ways to build chips. Some point out that open data sharing is a big problem—companies will not want to give away their secret designs. Others wonder if this approach could work for analog chips and other hard engineering problems. A few are worried about AI mistakes, saying “hallucinations” (bad designs) could sneak through, so human oversight is important. There’s debate about whether this will replace human engineers or just make their jobs easier. Overall, most see this as a big step forward, but think there’s still a long way to go.

## DSpark: Speculative decoding accelerates LLM inference [pdf]

- 原文链接: [DSpark: Speculative decoding accelerates LLM inference [pdf]](https://github.com/deepseek-ai/DeepSpec/blob/main/DSpark_paper.pdf)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48696585)

DSpark is a new method to make large language models, like ChatGPT, work faster. The main idea is called “speculative decoding,” which lets the model guess several words at once instead of just one at a time. Normally, these models predict the next word step by step, which is slow. DSpark uses a smaller, faster model to first guess a group of possible next words. Then, the big model checks these guesses. If the guesses are correct, the model can skip ahead, saving time. If not, it just goes back and fixes the mistake. This process repeats for every new word, making the whole system much faster. The paper shows that DSpark can speed up response times by two to four times, depending on the task. It also uses less computer power, which can lower costs. DSpark is open-source, so anyone can use or improve it. The authors tested DSpark on popular models and shared good results. They also explain some technical details about how to balance speed and accuracy.

In the comments, some people are excited about DSpark making large models faster and cheaper to use. Others ask if this might lower the quality of answers, but the authors say accuracy stays high. A few readers say the idea is not new and compare it to similar methods, like “draft-and-verify” and “speculative sampling.” Some users want to know how easy DSpark is to add to their own projects. One person worries that using a smaller model to guess could be risky if the small model is bad. Another suggests that future models may use tricks like this as standard. Several people thank the authors for making DSpark open-source. A few debate if the speedup numbers are as big as promised, asking for more real-world tests. Overall, most readers see DSpark as a useful step to make LLMs better and faster for everyone.

## Fintech Engineering Handbook

- 原文链接: [Fintech Engineering Handbook](https://w.pitula.me/fintech-engineering-handbook/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48696982)

This article is about the Fintech Engineering Handbook, a guide for building software systems that handle money. It explains key rules, patterns, and problems that developers face when working with payments, currencies, and financial data.

The main idea is that money systems need to be extra careful. The handbook shares three main rules: never make up data (don’t create or lose money by mistake), never lose any data (track every change), and never trust anyone or anything fully (always check and verify). It explains how to represent money safely in code, warning not to use floating-point numbers because they round off values in a way that can break the math. Instead, use integers or special data types, and be careful about rounding and currency conversions. It tells you to always keep track of both the amount and the currency, and to avoid mixing different currencies without clear rules.

The handbook says that recording all money movements is very important. It describes double-entry bookkeeping, where every money move is tracked from one account to another, and balances are always based on these records. It explains that every transaction should have clear timestamps for when it happened, when it was recorded, and when the money actually moved. Audit trails should record what changed, when, who did it, and why, helping with future checks and reports.

Event sourcing is mentioned as a way to store only the changes (events) and calculate the current state from them, which makes it easier to audit. Changes must be unchangeable (immutable), and mistakes are fixed by posting corrections, not by deleting records. The system should always be able to recover from crashes, and steps in processing money should be idempotent—running them twice should not double the effect.

The guide also warns about outside systems, like payment providers and APIs. It says never to trust their data blindly: always double-check, save all requests and responses, and expect failures, duplicates, or missing messages. Webhooks should be treated as hints, not facts, and all input should be validated. Systems should reconcile their records with outside data regularly to catch mistakes or missing information.

Controls over who can move money or change the system are also discussed. Sensitive actions should need approval from more than one person, and all changes to permissions and code should be tracked. Testing is important, with focus on checking that core rules always hold, and that the system survives retries, crashes, and unexpected inputs.

In the comment section, people liked how thorough and practical the handbook is. Some said it covers real-world issues that are often missed in other guides, like how to handle rounding and the problems with floats. Others appreciated the focus on audit trails, immutability, and reconciling with external systems. A few commenters shared stories of bugs caused by using floating-point numbers, or by not handling idempotency correctly, leading to money being lost or duplicated.

Some readers debated the use of event sourcing, noting it adds complexity, but others said it’s worth it for auditability. There were comments about the challenges of handling webhooks, especially with delayed or out-of-order messages. One person pointed out that even with careful design, outside systems can still surprise you, so defensive coding is a must.

A few developers wished for more code examples, or practical tips for scaling ledgers and handling billions of transactions. Some noted the advice about permissions and audits is useful beyond fintech, and could apply to any critical system. There was also praise for the appendix that explains common finance terms, which helps newcomers. Overall, the community thought this handbook was a valuable resource for both beginners and experienced fintech engineers.

## Suspicious Discontinuities (2020)

- 原文链接: [Suspicious Discontinuities (2020)](https://danluu.com/discontinuities/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48698151)

This article talks about “suspicious discontinuities,” which means places where rules or systems have sharp cut-off points, causing strange or unfair results. The author starts by showing how, in the US, people sometimes try to lose money on purpose at the end of the year, just to get under an income limit for health insurance subsidies—because earning even one dollar over the line can cost you thousands more in insurance.

The article gives many examples of these cut-offs, or “discontinuities,” in real life. In taxes, welfare, and health programs, earning just over a limit can mean losing big benefits. In college admissions, students just above or below grant thresholds can have very different chances, so families try to adjust their income to get grants. In law, drug charges often show bunching right at the cut-off for big prison sentences, hinting that prosecutors may adjust charges to fit the rules.

The author also mentions examples in technology and science. For example, a computer queue may suddenly drop new items when it is full, instead of dropping them smoothly as it fills up. In science papers, many results have p-values just below 0.05, because this is the magic number for “statistical significance,” so people might fudge data or only publish certain results. In sports, kids born just before the cut-off date for teams may get less chance to play, even if they’re just a few days younger than others who make the cut.

Other examples include used car prices dropping at round numbers, restaurant grades just making it into “A” or “B,” and marathon runners speeding up at the end to finish at a round time. The main theme is that sharp rules or thresholds can cause people to change their behavior in odd or unfair ways, or even try to “game the system.”

In the comments, many people agree that these discontinuities are real problems, especially in taxes and social programs. Some share personal stories where they or friends had to make strange choices because of cut-offs. Others say these rules are often made by lawmakers who don’t think about the real-world effects. Some suggest that “phase-outs”—making benefits decrease slowly instead of dropping all at once—would make things fairer.

A few commenters point out that sometimes, hard rules are needed to keep systems simple or to prevent cheating, but agree the negative effects should be studied more. Some mention that similar discontinuities appear in software, like network queues or voting systems, and that smoothing out rules can help computers and people. Others are surprised by the examples in science and sports, and discuss ways to fix or avoid these problems.

There are also debates about whether people should blame the rules or the people who try to game them. Some say everyone is just acting logically; others think changing the rules is the real answer. Many like the article’s use of data and graphs to make these patterns clear, and a few add their own examples, showing that sharp cut-offs cause odd behaviors in many fields.
