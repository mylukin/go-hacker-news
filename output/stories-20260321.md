## Do Not Turn Child Protection into Internet Access Control

- 原文链接: [Do Not Turn Child Protection into Internet Access Control](https://news.dyne.org/child-protection-is-not-access-control/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47470991)

This article talks about laws that want to protect children online by making everyone prove their age before using websites or apps. It explains that age checks are now spreading to many parts of the internet, not just adult sites, but also social media, games, and even operating systems.

The article says the main idea behind these laws is to keep children safe from bad content and online dangers. It admits these dangers are real, like harmful videos, online predators, and websites designed to keep people hooked. But the writer argues that age checks are not just about child safety. They change the internet from a place where everyone can join freely, to a place where you must always show proof or ID.

Some new laws want age checks to be part of the computer’s operating system, so every app can see your age. This means your device could share your personal info with many companies. Even open-source systems like Linux are now thinking about adding age fields to user data to follow these laws. The article warns that this is not just about blocking bad content, but about changing how the whole internet works.

The writer says there is a big mistake here: confusing content moderation (blocking or filtering bad stuff) with guardianship (parents or teachers helping children learn what is safe and appropriate). Content moderation is a technical job, but guardianship is personal and local. The article says laws that mix these up make everyone less private, give more power to big companies, and take control away from families.

The article also points out that these age checks do not really work well. Kids can use VPNs, fake accounts, or borrowed devices to get around them. This makes the system hard to use, expensive, and not very safe. The writer thinks these laws mostly help companies collect more data, rather than truly protect children.

They worry that once a system is built to check age, it will be used for other things, like checking where you live or your legal status. The article suggests a better idea: keep content filters close to where children use the internet (like on their device, or at school), and let parents and teachers decide what is best for each child. The operating system can help families, but should not share age details with every website or app.

Looking at the Hacker News comments, some people agree with the article. They worry about privacy and think making the internet a “checkpoint” is a bad idea. Many say kids will always find ways around these controls, so the laws mostly just bother regular users and collect more personal info. Some developers point out that adding new data fields (like birthdates) to systems like Linux could create more security risks.

Other commenters are more supportive of the laws. They say that since parents cannot always watch their kids online, some kind of protection is needed. A few believe that big platforms must share responsibility because the internet is too large for parents alone to manage. Some also remind others that not all families are safe or involved, so laws might help children who do not have good guardians.

A few users suggest technical solutions, like better content filters and local controls, instead of big, central age checks. Others think that these laws are just “security theater”—they look good, but do not really solve the problem. There is also debate about whether these laws will hurt open-source projects, or make it harder for people without ID to use the internet at all.

One commenter says the real problem is with business models that make money by keeping people online too long, or by promoting shocking content. They agree with the article that fixing these business models is more useful than adding ID checks. Overall, the comments show a mix of worry about privacy, doubts about the rules working well, and some support for protecting kids in any way possible.

## Tinybox- offline AI device 120B parameters

- 原文链接: [Tinybox- offline AI device 120B parameters](https://tinygrad.org/#tinybox)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47470773)

This article is about tinygrad, a simple neural network framework, and tinybox, a powerful computer for AI that runs offline and supports huge AI models. Tinygrad is growing fast and aims to make neural networks easier to understand and use.

The framework breaks all neural network operations into three simple types: ElementwiseOps (like adding or multiplying tensors), ReduceOps (like summing or finding the max), and MovementOps (like reshaping or moving data). Instead of complex operations for things like convolution or matrix multiplication, tinygrad uses these basic building blocks. The code is open and simple, so people can read and learn how even the hardest neural networks work.

Tinygrad is funded and is hiring engineers, especially those who have contributed to the project. They pay bounties for trying out tasks, making it easier for new people to join. The team is small, focused, and driven to make deep learning easier and cheaper.

Tinybox is their hardware product. It's a computer made for deep learning, and it claims to be the best for its price. It comes in different versions, with the top one (exabox) supporting massive power and memory, enough for huge AI models (up to 120 billion parameters). The boxes use strong GPUs, lots of RAM, and fast storage. They are shipped worldwide, but only come in fixed versions to keep prices low. Payment is by wire transfer only.

Tinybox was tested against much more expensive computers and did very well on standard AI training benchmarks. The company wants to make high-power AI hardware and software cheaper and open to everyone.

In the comments, many people are excited about the simplicity of tinygrad and how easy it is to read and learn from the code. Some say it is a great way to learn how neural networks work, better than big frameworks like PyTorch or TensorFlow. A few developers shared that they used tinygrad to study AI or to try their own ideas.

Other users are impressed by the tinybox hardware specs, saying it offers a lot of power for the price, especially compared to big cloud providers. Some people ask about how well it performs in real use, and if it’s as fast as claimed. A few are worried about things like noise, power usage, and the lack of customization options.

Some commenters are unsure if tinygrad can really replace major frameworks for big projects, since it is still in alpha, and not as stable or fast as PyTorch yet. Others like the open hiring style, where contributions count more than resumes. There are also jokes about paying only by wire transfer and the focus on keeping things simple, both in code and in business.

Overall, the community likes the open, simple approach but is watching to see if tinygrad and tinybox can grow to handle even bigger AI projects in the future.

Some commenters say that fast tools like AI help with simple tasks but cannot build real quality or trust. A few people warn that too much focus on speed leads to mistakes and short-lived products.

Other users ask if it’s always bad to go fast, noting that sometimes quick experiments are useful for learning. Some people mention that not everyone can afford to wait, especially when money or jobs are at risk. There are also jokes about trees and time, with one person saying, “The best time to plant a tree was 20 years ago. ## Revert "userdb: add birthDate field to JSON user records

- 原文链接: [Revert "userdb: add birthDate field to JSON user records](https://github.com/systemd/systemd/pull/41179)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47471213)

The article is about systemd removing a new “birthDate” field from user records in its JSON database, after first adding it. The team decided not to keep this field because of worries about privacy, freedom, and legal problems.

The main reason for removing the birth date field is that storing user ages or birth dates creates new, sensitive data on the system. This could be used for age checks or even more controls in the future, which many users do not want. The team believes that open-source Linux systems should not act like authorities checking your age. They want Linux to just work, without needing accounts or extra steps. There is also no good way to check if someone enters a real birth date, unless the system uses strong and invasive checks, which they do not want. Even if the birth date is only stored on your own computer, websites could start blocking systems that do not share age data, making it almost required.

Legal rules in some places (like California and Colorado) now ask for age checking, but these laws do not fit well with free and open-source projects. Some Linux groups already choose not to follow these laws and just block users from those places. Also, there was no agreement among big Linux groups to add this feature. Major Linux distributions and related projects did not agree to the change, and the idea did not move forward.

The team says they might reconsider if a new, private way to prove age is created, or if laws change, or if the community really wants it. For now, they do not want to store age data.

In the comments, some people agree with the decision, saying it protects user privacy and keeps Linux simple. They worry that adding age data is a step towards more control and less freedom. Others point out that even an optional birth date field could be misused or forced on users over time. A few say that legal rules are pushing open-source projects in a bad direction. Some discuss that websites and apps might start blocking users if the operating system does not provide age data, making things worse for Linux users. There are also voices who think it is fine to have the field as optional; one developer says that systemd is not enforcing any policy and just making it possible for those who want it. But most comments show that people do not trust the idea, and they want to keep their systems free of personal data. Some are also frustrated by how heated the debate became, and the discussion was eventually locked to cool things down.

## Grafeo – A fast, lean, embeddable graph database built in Rust

- 原文链接: [Grafeo – A fast, lean, embeddable graph database built in Rust](https://grafeo.dev/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47467567)

Grafeo is a new graph database written in Rust that focuses on speed and small memory use. It works both as a library you can add to your app and as a server you can run on its own.

The main ideas behind Grafeo are high performance, support for many query languages, and flexibility. It uses special tricks like vectorized execution, SIMD, and adaptive chunking to run fast. You can use popular graph query languages like GQL, Cypher, Gremlin, GraphQL, SPARQL, or even SQL, so you don’t have to learn something new if you already know one of them. Grafeo supports two main ways to store graph data: labeled property graphs (LPG), which are good for things like social networks, and RDF triples, which are common for linked data and the semantic web.

There’s built-in support for vector search using HNSW, so you can find similar things based on meaning, not just structure. You can use Grafeo directly in lots of programming languages, including Python, Node.js, Go, C, C#, Dart, and WebAssembly. It has strong ACID transaction support, uses modern Rust safety features, and can be used without any external dependencies if you like. The project comes with tools for AI integration, visual web dashboards, and notebooks. Installing is simple, with commands for different language package managers. The license is Apache 2.0, so it’s open source.

People in the comments like that Grafeo is written in Rust, saying it should be safer and faster than older C++ databases. Some are excited about the many query languages supported, as this makes it easier to switch from other systems. Others ask about real-world performance and how Grafeo compares to big names like Neo4j or TigerGraph. A few worry about how hard it is to support both LPG and RDF models well, and if this might make things more complex or slower. Some users are happy to see vector search and AI features, saying this is missing in other graph databases. There are questions about how stable Grafeo is, and if it’s ready for production. People also mention that having simple bindings for many languages is a big plus, especially for teams with mixed tech stacks. Some ask about backup, clustering, and how easy it is to migrate data from other databases. Overall, the reaction is positive, but people want to see more benchmarks and real-world use cases before fully trusting it.

## How Invisalign became the biggest user of 3D printers

- 原文链接: [How Invisalign became the biggest user of 3D printers](https://www.wired.com/story/how-invisalign-became-the-worlds-biggest-3d-printing-company/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47435028)

Invisalign is a company that sells clear plastic aligners to straighten teeth, and it has become the biggest user of 3D printers in the world. The article explains how this happened and what it means for the future of dental care.

Invisalign started out nearly 30 years ago by making molds and vacuum-forming plastic over them, but now it wants to directly 3D print the aligners themselves. This change could make the process faster, cheaper, and less wasteful. The company already handles millions of cases each year and controls almost every part of the process, from scanning teeth with their own machines to using AI software to help doctors plan treatments. Invisalign’s CEO, Joe Hogan, says the company had to invent new ways to use 3D printers for mass production, which was hard because 3D printers were mostly for prototypes at first. Now, Invisalign prints more parts in-house than any other company in the world.

The company had to develop special materials for 3D printing aligners, buying a company in Austria to help make a new kind of resin. This resin is strong and flexible, but making millions of aligners every day is a big engineering challenge. Even though the process sounds simple, it actually needs thousands of workers and many steps, like cleaning, bagging, and shipping the aligners all over the world.

The cost of Invisalign is high, partly because of shipping and the many steps needed to create each set. Doctors set the final price for patients, and prices can range from $3,000 to $8,000. There are also concerns about eating with aligners (which can stain or damage them), and the company says not to eat while wearing them. Invisalign has also worked to make its products fit different people in different countries, and has expanded research to improve results for everyone. Some patients need extra “touch-up” aligners if their teeth move again, often because they didn’t wear their retainers.

In the Hacker News comments, some users are impressed by the scale and technology behind Invisalign’s operation, saying it’s a true example of “mass customization.” Others point out that the high cost is still a barrier for many people, and wish the company would pass cost savings to customers. A few commenters share their own experiences with Invisalign, mentioning problems like needing extra aligners or discomfort during treatment. Some users discuss the environmental impact of using so much plastic, expressing worry about waste and microplastics. Others argue that 3D printing is a good use of technology here, showing how it can change industries beyond just making prototypes. A few people debate whether clear aligners really work as well as metal braces, saying studies are not always clear or include all types of patients. Some are hopeful that as 3D printing technology improves, costs will come down and more people will have access to better dental care. Others remain skeptical about whether traditional braces will ever fully go away, suggesting some doctors and patients will always prefer them. Overall, the discussion highlights both the excitement about new technology and the challenges that come with scaling it up for millions of people.

## The seven hour explosion nobody could explain

- 原文链接: [The seven hour explosion nobody could explain](https://phys.org/news/2026-03-hour-explosion.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47411110)

A strange and very long gamma-ray explosion called GRB 250702B was seen in July 2025. This blast lasted for seven hours, had three separate bursts in one day, and left an afterglow for months—much longer than normal gamma-ray bursts, which usually last just seconds or minutes. 

Most gamma-ray bursts happen when two neutron stars crash or a big star collapses, and they don’t repeat. But this one was different: it lasted much longer and fired three times. Scientists think something special happened—a normal star got too close to a rare, medium-sized black hole (called an intermediate mass black hole) and was pulled apart. As pieces of the star fell into the black hole, it shot out jets of energy nearly as fast as light, making the gamma rays we saw.

Because the burst happened away from the center of its galaxy, it fits the idea of a wandering intermediate black hole. The repeated bursts could mean the star was not destroyed all at once, but lost parts of itself in several passes. This would be the first time we’ve ever seen a jet like this from a medium-sized black hole eating a star, which makes it a big deal for astronomers.

But the mystery is not solved yet. Some scientists say other things could have caused this long burst, and they want more proof. In the Hacker News comments, people were excited by how rare and strange this event was. Some users loved the idea that new types of black holes might be out there, hiding. Others pointed out that it’s hard to tell what really happened from just one event, and they want more data. A few commenters explained how hard it is to study something that happens so far away and so rarely. Some thought the repeating bursts could mean we need new theories for gamma-ray explosions. Others were just amazed at how much energy these bursts can release, much more than our sun will ever make. A few were hopeful that new telescopes might catch more of these events soon. Some users reminded others that science is often slow, and it may take years to understand what really happened. Others joked about how the universe still surprises us, even after decades of study. Overall, most people agreed this was a very exciting discovery, and they look forward to what astronomers will learn next.

## No evidence cannabis helps anxiety, depression, or PTSD

- 原文链接: [No evidence cannabis helps anxiety, depression, or PTSD](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/03/260319044656.htm)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47470750)

A new study says cannabis does not help with anxiety, depression, or PTSD, even though many people use it for these problems. The study is the largest review so far, looking at 54 clinical trials over 45 years. The report comes from the University of Sydney and was published in The Lancet. The researchers found no real proof that cannabis is useful for these mental health issues. In fact, they warn it could make things worse, such as raising the risk of psychosis or addiction, and delaying better treatments. Some small benefits were seen for other conditions like insomnia, autism, and Tourette’s, but the evidence is weak. There is stronger proof that cannabis helps with some forms of epilepsy, multiple sclerosis, and certain types of pain, but not for mental health. For substance use, cannabis might help people stop using cannabis itself, but it can make cocaine cravings worse. The study says doctors and patients should be careful, and calls for better rules and more research, since cannabis use is growing fast.

Hacker News comments show many mixed views. Some users say this result is not surprising and matches past studies. Others feel the study might miss personal stories where people say cannabis helps their anxiety or depression. A few point out that medical studies look at averages, not individual cases, so some people might still benefit. Some users worry that these findings could be used to block all access to cannabis, even for cases where it helps. Others argue that cannabis is safer than alcohol or some prescription drugs and want it to stay legal. A few users question if the study considers different cannabis types, doses, or ways to use it. Some mention that the placebo effect is strong with mental health, so results are hard to measure. Others are glad for more careful science and think strict checks are good before making health claims. There are also comments about the need for more open, unbiased research, especially as cannabis laws are changing fast.
