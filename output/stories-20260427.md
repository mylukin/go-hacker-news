## Three men are facing 44 charges in Toronto SMS Blaster arrests

- 原文链接: [Three men are facing 44 charges in Toronto SMS Blaster arrests](https://www.tps.ca/media-centre/stories/unprecedented-sms-blaster-arrests/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47927070)

Three men in Toronto were arrested and charged with 44 crimes after police found them using an “SMS blaster,” a device that sends fake text messages to thousands of phones. This is the first time this kind of technology has been found in Canada, and it is a new threat to both public safety and people’s bank accounts.

The SMS blaster copies a real cell tower and tricks phones nearby into connecting to it. When people’s phones connect, they get fake texts that look like they come from trusted companies. These texts often have links to websites that steal passwords and bank details—a trick called “smishing.” The device was hidden in cars and moved around the city, which made it harder to catch. Police say that in just a few months, tens of thousands of phones were affected, and over 13 million times, people could not connect to real cell towers. This could mean even emergency services like 911 might not work for some people during those moments.

Police worked with many other groups, like the RCMP and phone companies, to find the people behind the SMS blaster. They searched homes, arrested two men, and collected a lot of evidence, including more devices. A third man turned himself in later. Police reminded everyone to never click on links in strange texts, to use only official apps for banking, and to report fraud if it happens.

The main message is that, while the technology is new, the goal is the same: to steal personal information. Being careful and informed is still the best way to stay safe.

In the comment section, some people were surprised and worried that it is possible to block so many phones from normal service using such a device. Others asked how hard it is to build or buy an SMS blaster, and if more of these devices could appear in other places. Many said that phone networks should do more to protect users, and some wondered why phone security is still so weak. A few pointed out that the real danger is not just the scam texts, but also the fact that emergency services might be blocked.

Some readers mentioned that similar attacks have happened in other countries, and they are not shocked that it is now in Canada. Others believe that police should teach people more about these scams so fewer people fall for them. There were questions about why telecom companies do not warn users when their phones connect to a fake tower. A few commenters said it is important for everyone to be careful with messages, and to think twice before clicking links or entering passwords. Some people also felt that this shows how fast cybercrime is changing, and that both police and phone companies need to get better at stopping these new tricks.

## Easyduino: Open Source PCB Devboards for KiCad

- 原文链接: [Easyduino: Open Source PCB Devboards for KiCad](https://github.com/Hanqaqa/Easyduino)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47924813)

Easyduino is a collection of open source PCB designs for popular microcontroller boards, such as Arduino, ESP32, Raspberry Pi Pico, and STM32 Bluepill, all made using KiCad. The project wants to make it simple for anyone to look at, change, or build these devboards using free and open tools.

The designs try to match the original boards as much as possible, including pinouts and layouts, but some changes were made when parts were hard to get or too expensive. For example, cheaper or more common parts are used in some boards, and USB-C is added for easier use. Each board’s folder has a readme file that explains differences from the original, what parts are used, and anything special to know.

All boards use a 4-layer PCB design to keep wiring simple, and files are made to work with the JLCPCB factory. Each project folder includes KiCad files, production files (like BOMs, Gerbers, and PDFs), and extra data like datasheets and pictures. To use Easyduino, you just need to install KiCad, download the files, and open them. You can also just look at the finished schematics or Gerbers if you don’t want to use KiCad.

The project is under the CERN Open Hardware License, so you can use the designs freely—even for business—if you include the license. People are welcome to help fix mistakes or add new boards, as long as they follow the same style. The project has a to-do list for future boards and improvements, and thanks community members for their help.

In the comments, many people like the idea of having open KiCad designs for these popular boards. Some say it’s helpful for learning how real devboards are made or for starting custom projects. Others point out challenges, like the need for hard-to-find parts or the complexity of some boards, but they appreciate that the project explains these issues. A few users wish more devboards were included or ask about support for other chips, like NXP. Some comment that using open tools like KiCad is great for the hardware community, while others discuss possible problems with manufacturing or using the designs with different PCB services. One person shares tips for editing or making changes in KiCad, while another asks about making their own custom boards based on these files. Overall, the community seems excited and supportive, with some giving suggestions and others just saying thanks for sharing.

## Spanish archaeologists discover trove of ancient shipwrecks in Bay of Gibraltar

- 原文链接: [Spanish archaeologists discover trove of ancient shipwrecks in Bay of Gibraltar](https://www.theguardian.com/science/2026/apr/15/hidden-treasures-spanish-archaeologists-discover-trove-of-ancient-shipwrecks-in-bay-of-gibraltar)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47907175)

Spanish archaeologists have found more than 30 old shipwrecks in the Bay of Gibraltar, between Spain and Africa. These ships are from many different times, starting as early as the fifth century BC up to World War II. The team from the University of Cádiz has mapped 151 sites in the bay, including 134 sunken ships. Some are Phoenician, Roman, medieval, and even British, Dutch, and Venetian ships. They also found a plane engine from the 1930s. The area is special because it is a busy sea passage, so many ships from many countries passed through and sometimes sank there.

One interesting find is a small Spanish gunboat from the late 1700s that was used to surprise British ships. The archaeologists found a wooden box that looked like a book on this ship. At first, they thought it was for hiding secret papers, but it only held wooden combs. The team is excited about these discoveries because they teach us about trade, war, and life in this busy part of the world. They are worried, though, because new port buildings, rising sea levels, and new sea plants are damaging these sites. To help protect the wrecks, the team has made 3D models and videos for people to view online and in museums. They hope showing these treasures will make people want to save them.

In the comments, some people say this is an amazing find and want to learn more about the technology used for underwater archaeology. Others ask about the legal side—who owns the wrecks and can treasure hunters take things? Some worry that showing the locations online might make it easier for thieves to steal from the wrecks. A few comment on how climate change and new sea plants might destroy these sites before they can be studied. There is also debate over whether more should be done by governments to protect shipwrecks around the world, not just in Spain. Some users share stories about similar discoveries in their own countries. Others are just excited and say they want to see these virtual models themselves. A few wonder if we will ever find a "real" treasure ship full of gold.

## The quiet resurgence of RF engineering

- 原文链接: [The quiet resurgence of RF engineering](https://atempleton.bearblog.dev/quiet-resurgence-of-rf-engineering/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47903439)

This article talks about how radio frequency (RF) engineering is becoming important again, after years of being less popular. The author works in aerospace and started out as a software engineer, but soon had to learn about RF because modern projects need both software and hardware skills.

The article explains that RF engineering seemed to be shrinking after the dot-com bubble. Many jobs moved overseas, and most new engineers chose software, not hardware. For a while, only big defense companies kept hiring RF engineers. But now, things are changing. More satellites are getting launched every year, each needing RF parts like antennas and amplifiers. The space industry is growing fast, and this means more RF work.

5G technology is another reason. Each 5G base station needs many more RF parts than 4G—sometimes up to 16 times more. This makes the RF component market very large. Work has started on 6G too, which will push RF technology even further. Other areas, like car radar, Wi-Fi 7, and the Internet of Things (IoT), also need RF engineers. 

There is now a big shortage of people with RF skills. Companies have trouble filling jobs, and salaries are rising. Some companies are working with universities to train more RF engineers. Even so, it takes years to become really good at designing RF hardware, because it requires knowledge of physics and lots of hands-on practice.

In the comments, many people say they have seen the same trend: RF engineers are in high demand but hard to find. Some say universities don’t teach enough about RF anymore, making the problem worse. Others point out that even though software is popular, hardware skills will always be needed—especially for things like satellites and wireless networks. Some readers are excited about RF’s comeback and want to learn more themselves. Others warn that RF is hard to learn and takes patience.

A few people say the pay in RF is finally catching up to software jobs, which might attract more students. Some share stories about learning RF on the job, and agree it can be done, but takes effort. Others note that, while RF is growing, it may never be as big as software engineering. A couple of comments mention resources to help beginners, like online courses and open-source tools. There is debate about whether companies or schools should do more to train new RF engineers. Some think the field is finally getting the respect it deserves, while others say the shortage is a sign that people forgot how important hardware really is. Overall, most readers agree: RF engineering is making a quiet, but real, comeback.

## Networking changes coming in macOS 27

- 原文链接: [Networking changes coming in macOS 27](https://eclecticlight.co/2026/04/23/networking-changes-coming-in-macos-27/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47923010)

Apple is getting ready to make big networking changes in macOS 27, and these updates may affect people who use older Macs or manage Macs at work. The two main changes are: Apple might finally remove AFP file sharing, and it will soon require stronger security (TLS 1.2 or newer) for some network connections.

First, AFP (Apple Filing Protocol) has been around for a long time, but Apple switched to SMB as its main file-sharing method years ago. Now, they’re warning again that AFP could be dropped in macOS 27. If you use old devices like Time Capsules or NAS systems that don’t support SMB, you might not be able to upgrade your Mac without updating your storage. However, if you keep using older macOS versions, AFP will still work.

Second, Apple is raising the bar for secure connections. Soon, Macs and Apple devices will only accept connections to certain servers if they use at least TLS 1.2, with TLS 1.3 preferred, and must use safe encryption and valid certificates. This mostly affects things like device management, app installs, and Apple software updates. Regular file sharing and most other things should not be affected. Checking if your setup is ready isn’t easy—admins will need to check log files using special tools, because there’s no simple way in the Mac’s settings to see if your servers meet the new rules.

These changes are expected soon: the developer beta is coming in early June 2026, public beta in July, and the final release in September.

In the comments, some users worry about old security methods like SHA1—one says their old server is having trouble because some software now refuses SHA1, even though it still uses TLS 1.2. The article’s author says Apple now needs SHA-2, especially with TLS 1.3, and thinks old algorithms are not likely to be allowed. One commenter is sad about the end of Apple’s old Server app, saying it was easy to use and misses it. Another person asks if using AFP is still OK for older Macs, and the answer is yes, as long as you don't upgrade to the new system. There’s also a question about what happens to managed devices if updates aren’t done, but the answer is unclear—likely, they’ll act like any device that can’t connect to the network.

Some people are worried about losing old features, while others focus on security and the need to keep up with new tech. A few say these changes make life harder for people running older hardware or special setups. Others think it’s time for Apple to move on and drop weak security and old protocols. Most agree that these changes will be tough for some users, but will help keep Macs safer in the long run.

## 4TB of voice samples just stolen from 40k AI contractors at Mercor

- 原文链接: [4TB of voice samples just stolen from 40k AI contractors at Mercor](https://app.oravys.com/blog/mercor-breach-2026)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47919630)

A big data breach happened at Mercor, where hackers stole 4 terabytes of voice samples and ID documents from 40,000 AI contractors. These people gave their voices and IDs to help train AI systems, but now both are leaked together.

The breach is serious because it links each person’s voice to their real identity. In the past, leaks were either just voice or just ID, but not both together. Mercor’s process asked contractors to record their voices in a quiet room and upload ID scans. This makes it very easy for attackers to clone someone’s voice and pretend to be them.

With this data, attackers can do things like trick banks that use voice as a login, pretend to be workers and steal money from companies, create deepfake video calls, commit insurance fraud, or scam family members by sounding like someone in trouble. The technology to clone a voice only needs a few seconds of clean audio, and these samples are much longer and clearer.

The article gives advice to people who may be affected. It says to search for and remove public recordings of your voice, set up secret codewords with family and banks, change your voiceprint logins if you can, ask your bank to turn off voice login, and use tools to check if any messages you get are fake. There is also a list of technical signs that experts look for when checking if a voice recording is fake, like strange breath patterns or unnatural speech.

ORAVYS, the company writing the article, offers free checks for three samples to people affected by this breach. They use many technical tools to detect if a voice is fake, and promise to protect your data.

In the comments, many people are shocked at how much data was collected and how dangerous the leak is. Some say companies should not be allowed to collect both voice and ID together. Others wonder why banks still use voice for security, since it is easy to fake now. A few users share tips, like always using codewords with family or not trusting voice calls asking for money.

Some commenters are worried about older people being targeted, since they may not understand deepfakes. Others think the lawsuits will change how companies handle personal data. Not everyone agrees—some think it is impossible to stop this kind of leak once data is given to a company. There is also debate on whether AI companies should pay more to contractors if their data is so valuable. A few users are hopeful that stronger laws and better detection tools will help protect people in the future.
