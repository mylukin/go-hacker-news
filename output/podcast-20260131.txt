First, let’s talk about an article comparing Rust and Swift. The writer says Swift feels like a more convenient version of Rust. Both languages have strong type systems, enums, pattern matching, and can compile to native code or WASM. Rust is designed for low-level system work but lets you work higher-level if you want. Swift starts high-level but allows you to go lower when needed. In Rust, you have to manage memory with ownership and borrowing, and use wrappers like Cow or Box for special cases. Swift uses value types and copy-on-write by default, making things simpler but sometimes less fast. Swift hides some complexity with friendly syntax, like a C-style switch that works much like Rust’s match. Both languages avoid nulls—Rust uses Option, Swift uses T? optionals. Their error handling is also similar. For recursive data, Rust makes you use Box, while Swift just needs an indirect keyword.

Swift has more features—classes, async/await, actors, property wrappers, and result builders. This makes it larger and less “pure” than Rust, but easier for new users. Rust is faster by default; Swift is easier but needs more care for speed. The article also highlights that Swift is now cross-platform, running on Windows, Linux, and even small devices. The Swift community is working to improve tools and package support, but it still lags behind Rust in some areas, like compile times and ecosystem size.

In the Hacker News comments, some agree that Swift is more convenient, especially for UI and server work. Others like Rust’s strictness for catching bugs. Some think Swift is still too tied to Apple. Swift’s optionals and error handling get praise for being less clunky, but others prefer Rust’s explicitness. There’s also talk about compile times, and how both languages can be slow to build. Many users say the choice depends on the project—Rust for system-level and performance-critical code, Swift for apps and fast prototyping. Both communities are seen as friendly, and people are excited to see where each language goes next.

Next, privacy and mobile phones. A recent article explains that mobile carriers can get your exact GPS location from your phone, not just a rough estimate from cell towers. Apple’s new privacy feature in iOS 26.3 tries to limit this, but only on newer iPhones with Apple’s own modem. While cell towers can guess your location within tens or hundreds of meters, hidden protocols in mobile networks let carriers ask your phone for its real GPS coordinates, accurate to a few meters. Your phone does this automatically, and it’s built into the network standards. This ability has been used by law enforcement and governments for years, including during COVID-19 contact tracing. The author warns that these protocols are not widely known and could be abused. Apple’s update helps, but the author thinks users should be able to turn off these location requests completely and get alerts when their location is accessed.

Many people in the comments were surprised phones can share GPS data directly with carriers. Some see this as a privacy risk and want stronger controls. Others point out these features are needed for emergency services. There are stories about police and governments using this tech for both good and bad reasons. Some argue that the real problem is how easy it is for governments or companies to access this data without strong legal checks. Experts say almost all phones follow these rules, so switching devices or software won’t help much. Some praise Apple’s efforts, but others want more user control. There are also worries about hackers or foreign actors getting this information, but most agree the main risk is from local carriers and governments.

Now, let’s look at AI and Wikipedia. Wiki Education studied how AI tools like ChatGPT affected Wikipedia editing in 2025. They found that editors should never copy and paste AI-generated text into Wikipedia. While few articles had fake sources, most AI-written articles failed a key test—when you check the source, the information isn’t actually there. They used a tool called Pangram to detect AI-generated text, and after ChatGPT’s launch, the amount of AI-written content grew. Many flagged articles had to be cleaned up. Wiki Education now trains new editors to use AI for finding sources or gaps, but not for writing article text. Most new editors follow these rules, and AI-written text is quickly removed when found. AI can help with research, but can’t be trusted to write verifiable Wikipedia content yet.

In the comments, people agree Wikipedia must be careful, because errors can spread widely. Some want better AI tools in the future, while others worry about over-relying on detection tools like Pangram. Many say teaching AI literacy is important, and Wikipedia’s policies must keep changing as AI gets better. The real problem, some say, is not AI itself, but people misusing it or not checking facts.

Staying with AI, another article warns about the risks of letting language models do our thinking and writing. The writer says using AI for simple tasks can make us weaker thinkers. Writing and talking to others is about showing who we are; if we let AI change our words, we lose our real voice and trust. While AI can help people who struggle with writing or use another language, too much use stops us from learning. Even boring tasks, the writer argues, help us grow and connect. The article shares a story about learning jazz piano—real skill comes from practice, not shortcuts. The writer does not believe that technology can “extend” our minds in the way some people claim.

Many commenters agree that overusing AI can make us lazy or less skilled. Some share stories of students using chatbots for homework and not learning. Others recognize AI’s value for people who struggle with writing. Some see this as just another step in the long history of technology changing how we work. Many agree that being honest about AI use is important, especially in personal or public writing. There are worries that if everyone uses chatbots, all writing will sound the same. Most agree that we need to use AI carefully and remember what makes human thinking special.

Now, let’s turn to hardware. There’s a new open-source library called MpGEMM that makes matrix math much faster on ARM’s new SME hardware. Matrix math is key for deep learning and science, but most software doesn’t use all the power of new hardware. The MpGEMM library uses tricks like cache-aware partitioning and on-the-fly transposition to get the best performance. It was tested on an Apple M4 Pro chip and beat Apple’s own Accelerate library by about 23%. It’s also much faster than other open-source options.

In the comments, people are glad to see open-source libraries that help use new hardware better. Some ask if these tricks work on older chips or other architectures. There are worries about code being hard to maintain if it’s too specialized. Some point out that big companies already have their own fast math libraries, so this will help smaller teams more. Others are excited to see real code from academic papers, and note that faster math saves both time and power—important for laptops and phones.

On to security: Minimal is a new set of open-source, hardened container images with very few known security problems. These images only include what’s needed to run your app, cutting down on possible security holes. They’re rebuilt every day, so problems are fixed quickly, usually in less than two days. Most images are “shell-less”—you can’t open a shell inside them, which helps protect against attacks. They run as non-root users by default, are cryptographically signed, and come with a full list of included software.

In the comments, developers like the security benefits and fast patching. Some worry that removing the shell makes debugging harder. Others point out that very small images may miss tools you sometimes need. Non-root users are good for security, but can break apps expecting root. People compare Minimal to other minimal image projects, and some worry about depending on newer tools. Overall, Minimal is seen as a strong choice for teams who care about security, but you need to balance minimalism with real-world needs.

Now, a quick look at Wikipedia’s sandbox. The sandbox is a special page where anyone can try editing or adding text without risk. You can use it to learn wiki markup or test changes before editing real articles. Your work won’t stay forever—the sandbox is cleared often. There are tools to preview and compare edits, and if you have an account, you get a private sandbox. The page warns not to post copyrighted or illegal content, and there’s help for new editors.

Commenters say the sandbox is a great way to learn, and many remember trying out wiki markup there first. People like that you don’t need an account to use it, making Wikipedia open to everyone. Some wish more websites had a sandbox for safe practice. A few worry about spam or misuse, but agree it’s mostly cleaned up fast. The sandbox is seen as a simple but important part of what makes Wikipedia work for everyone.

Next, some sad news. William Foege, a key leader in the fight to end smallpox, has died at age 89. He led the CDC’s Smallpox Eradication Program in the 1970s and helped make smallpox the first disease wiped out worldwide. His work saved hundreds of millions of lives. Foege later led the CDC, advised the Gates Foundation, and received the Presidential Medal of Freedom. He was a strong supporter of vaccines and spoke out about public health policy.

On Hacker News, people shared respect for Foege’s work and the global effort to stop smallpox. Some talked about the “ring vaccination” strategy he used, and how it could be a model for other diseases. There were personal stories from people who got the smallpox vaccine, or who work in public health. Some discussed why diseases like polio are still around, and what we can learn from smallpox. There was agreement that public health leadership and vaccines are crucial, and that we must stay vigilant to avoid old diseases coming back.

In the comments, many enjoyed the humor and say Mickens is a favorite writer. Some point out that Byzantine fault tolerance is important for things like blockchains, but agree the papers are often too complex. There are stories about real systems failing due to simple human mistakes. Some wish for better ways to explain these topics, and there’s a mix of laughter and respect for the hard work of making computers reliable.

Finally, an article connects Ferrari imports with the stock market and Bitcoin. From 2020 to 2026, about 8,800 Ferraris were imported to the U.S.—about 121 cars each month. The number of Ferraris imported matches the ups and downs of Bitcoin, the S&P 500, and the Nasdaq. The article uses customs records to show this trend, and also discusses fraud in shipping and customs. It shares real cases, like companies lowering invoice prices to dodge taxes, smuggling goods, selling Chinese products as “Made in USA,” and mislabeling chemicals. Each case shows how better use of customs data can help stop fraud.

Some readers were surprised that Ferrari imports match the stock market so closely, calling Ferraris a “rich people’s index.” Others want more data or a chart. There’s also talk about how easy it is for companies to cheat on customs, and how only big cases seem to get caught. Some think technology can help catch fraud, while others say people will always find ways to cheat. There are concerns about national security risks from “Made in USA” scams, and stories about the complexity of customs paperwork. Many agree that trade data tells interesting stories about the economy, fraud, and even luxury trends.
