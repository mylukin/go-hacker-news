## Building a Procedural Hex Map with Wave Function Collapse

- 原文链接: [Building a Procedural Hex Map with Wave Function Collapse](https://felixturner.github.io/hex-map-wfc/article/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47311815)

This article talks about making a medieval island map using hex tiles and a special algorithm called Wave Function Collapse (WFC). The map has roads, rivers, forests, and villages, and it is made by computer, not by hand.

The main idea is to use WFC, which works like a puzzle. Each tile has edges, and the rule is that edges must match with their neighbors (for example, road to road, grass to grass). Hex tiles are harder than square tiles because they have 6 edges instead of 4, so there are more ways the tiles can fit together. The map uses 30 different tile types, each with 6 rotations and 5 levels of height, making many possible options for each place on the map.

The map is quite big—about 4,100 tiles—so it is split into 19 smaller grids. Each grid is solved separately, but the edges where grids meet must match. Sometimes, the algorithm gets stuck because of these border rules. To fix this, the author uses backtracking (going back and trying a new tile), and special tricks like “local WFC” to patch small problem areas. If nothing works, the map adds a mountain to hide the mistake, because mountains can connect to any tile.

The map is not flat; it has 5 levels of height, so roads and rivers must be at the right level, or they look strange. For extra features like trees and buildings, the author uses Perlin noise (a kind of random but natural-looking pattern), because WFC is not good for placing things in groups. Water is hard to make look good, so the author uses special tricks for waves and sparkles using shaders and textures.

Hex grids are also tricky in math. The best way to handle them is with “cube coordinates,” which make it easy to find neighbors. The 3D tiles are made in Blender, and everything is drawn fast by grouping (batching) tiles and using only one material, so it works smoothly even on a phone. Special effects like shadows, blur, and grain make the map look nice. The whole thing builds in about 20 seconds and runs at 60 frames per second.

People in the comments are very impressed by the project. Many think the graphics and technical details are great. Some users talk about their own tries with WFC and agree that hex tiles and big maps are much harder to solve than squares. A few suggest other tricks for handling stuck situations, like different tile sets, more backtracking, or smarter border solving. Some discuss using WFC for other types of maps, or for games like Dwarf Fortress. Others are curious about using WFC for placing towns or forests, but the author and commenters agree noise or different rules are better for that. A few people point out that the code and live demo are helpful for learning. Some mention the visual style, saying it reminds them of games like Carcassonne or Bad North. Others like the attention to making the map feel real, with rivers, roads, and forests in the right places. One or two users mention possible improvements, like better water effects or more natural terrain, but most are just happy to explore the map and see how it works.

## Is legal the same as legitimate: AI reimplementation and the erosion of copyleft

- 原文链接: [Is legal the same as legitimate: AI reimplementation and the erosion of copyleft](https://writings.hongminhee.org/2026/03/legal-vs-legitimate/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47310160)

The article talks about a big debate in open source software: is something okay just because it’s legal, or should we also ask if it’s fair? It looks at a real example where a popular Python library called chardet was remade using AI and then changed from a copyleft license (LGPL) to a permissive one (MIT).

The new version of chardet was built by giving only the old library’s API and tests to an AI, not the source code itself. The result was much faster and very different from the old code. The maintainer says this means it’s a new work, so he’s not required to keep the old license. But the original author disagrees, saying the LGPL means changes must be shared the same way, and this AI rework breaks that rule.

Some well-known open source developers, like the creators of Flask and Redis, supported the relicensing. They argued that if you don’t copy code directly, it’s legal. They also compared it to how Linux and GNU were made by reimplementing UNIX. But the article says this is different: those projects made more software open, but this change makes it easier to close things off.

The article also explains that copyleft (like GPL) doesn’t stop people from sharing; it just says if you share, you must share with the same rules. With a permissive license (like MIT), people can take code and make it closed, which helps big companies more than small developers. The article points out that when companies see their own code reused by others, they may get upset—even if they support open licensing in other cases.

The main point is that what is legal and what is right are not always the same. Law sets the lowest bar, but communities and users also care about fairness and trust. In open source, many people contributed under a promise that changes would stay open. If AI makes it easy to get around these promises, is that okay? The article suggests we need new rules to protect not just code, but also things like APIs and tests.

In the comments, some people agree with the article. They feel that using AI to bypass copyleft feels wrong, even if it’s legal, and say it betrays the spirit of open source. Others think the law is clear and developers should adapt, since reimplementing software has always been allowed. Some point out that stronger licenses or new rules, like “specification copyleft,” could help. A few think the problem is not new and that open source has always had to deal with big companies using their work without giving back.

Many commenters discuss the risk that fewer people will want to contribute to open source if their work can be taken so easily. Some wonder if AI will make most software licenses useless. Others say that open source should be about sharing and that making it easier to use code is a good thing for everyone. There’s also debate about whether copyleft really helps or just adds barriers. Overall, people have strong and mixed feelings, and many agree that the line between “legal” and “legitimate” is more important than ever.

## Show HN: The Mog Programming Language

- 原文链接: [Show HN: The Mog Programming Language](https://moglang.org)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47312728)

Mog is a new programming language made for AI agents to write and run code safely. It is like Lua, but with static types and made to be fast and secure, especially for tasks where code is generated or changed by an AI.

Mog is compiled, not interpreted, so agent-written plugins run with low delay. The language is simple: it has clear types, no implicit conversions, and no operator precedence—parentheses are always needed when mixing operators. All I/O and system actions are provided by the host program, which decides what a Mog script can do using a capability system. For example, a script can only write files or make HTTP requests if the host gives those permissions. Mog has modern features like async/await, pattern matching, and native support for tensors (multi-dimensional arrays).

You write Mog code in `.mog` files, compile them with a Rust-based compiler, and can run them as standalone programs or as plugins loaded by a host. The syntax looks like a mix of TypeScript, Rust, and Go, and is designed to be easy for LLMs (AI models) to read and write. There are many examples in the guide, from simple “Hello, world!” to more advanced scripts like HTTP fetch with retries or a Fast Fourier Transform on tensors. Mog has strict error handling—no exceptions, just Results you match on. Variables are declared with `:=`, reassigned with `=`, and there are no uninitialized values.

The main idea: agents can extend themselves, write hooks, or even rewrite parts of their logic, but only with the permissions the host allows. This is safer than letting them use Bash or Python scripts, which can easily escape sandboxes or cause harm if permissions are not controlled. Mog aims to fill the gap between too-trusted scripting and heavy sandboxing (like Docker), by making permissions fine-grained and easy to control.

In the Hacker News comments, people liked the idea of a statically typed, AI-friendly language for plugins and scripting. Some liked the strictness—no operator precedence and no implicit conversions—saying it prevents bugs, especially when code is written by AIs. Others were unsure if the language is needed, since Lua and WASM already exist for embedding and sandboxing. Some pointed out that Lua is hard to sandbox, and WASM is slower or more complex than needed for plugins. There were positive remarks about the compiler being in Rust, which helps with security and easier audits.

A few users worried about writing and maintaining yet another language. Some said the language might be hard for humans to pick up, but that’s fine if it’s mostly for AI to write. There were questions about the real-world security of the capability system and if it’s audited yet. Others asked about examples for more advanced AI agent use, or how Mog compares to existing solutions like JavaScript containers or WASM. Some wanted more built-in library features for things like JSON or SQLite, but understood that keeping the core language small is a feature.

Overall, the community saw Mog as an interesting, practical experiment for the future of agent self-extension, with a strong focus on safety and speed. Some think it may find a niche for agents or systems needing secure, fast, and controlled scripting. Others are waiting to see if it gets more adoption or proves itself in real-world AI agent frameworks.

## Thomas Selfridge: The First Airplane Fatality

- 原文链接: [Thomas Selfridge: The First Airplane Fatality](https://www.amusingplanet.com/2026/03/thomas-selfridge-first-airplane-fatality.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47315079)

This article tells the story of Thomas Selfridge, the first person to die in a powered airplane crash, and explains the events around his fatal flight in 1908. It gives details about Selfridge’s life, the accident, and how it changed aviation history.

Thomas Selfridge was a young officer and an early aviation enthusiast. He worked with the U.S. Army and was involved in early flight experiments, even designing aircraft and flying them. On September 17, 1908, he flew as a passenger with Orville Wright in a Wright Flyer during a demonstration for the Army. The plane was very simple—made from wood and cloth, with no cockpit or seatbelts, and both men lay side by side on the lower wing.

At first, the flight was going well. They completed several laps around the field. Suddenly, a wooden propeller broke, which made the plane shake badly and caused even more damage. The Flyer lost control, crashed nose-first, and both men were thrown forward. Orville was badly injured but survived, while Selfridge suffered a serious head injury.

At that time, pilots did not wear helmets, so Selfridge’s head injury was fatal. He died that evening, becoming the first fatality in powered flight. The Army later required pilots to wear protective headgear because of this accident. After the crash, Orville recovered and kept flying, and the Wright brothers improved their designs to make planes safer. The Army finally bought a Wright airplane in 1909.

Selfridge’s story is not only about the accident. He was a skilled engineer, a pilot, and a supporter of new ideas in flying. Today, he is remembered at Arlington National Cemetery and honored in aviation history. Pieces from the crash, like the broken propeller, can be seen in museums.

People in the Hacker News comments shared different thoughts. Some admired how quickly early flight technology advanced, saying it was a time of great risk and bold ideas. Others pointed out how safety rules often come after tragic events, using this accident as an example. A few commenters discussed how the lack of safety equipment, like helmets, was common in many new technologies, not just airplanes. Some users highlighted Selfridge’s important role as an engineer and innovator, not just as a victim. There were also comments about how the Wright brothers learned from this crash and improved airplane design. Some people wondered how many early aviation pioneers faced similar dangers and how their sacrifices helped make flight safe today. A few thought about how history often remembers people for how they died, but it’s important to remember their achievements too. Others were surprised at how quickly aviation moved from risky experiments to reliable transportation. Finally, some commenters reflected on how every new technology, from cars to planes to space travel, has its first accidents and victims, but these hard lessons lead to safer futures for everyone.

DARPA has announced the X-76, a new experimental aircraft that aims to combine the speed of a jet with the vertical take-off and landing skills of a helicopter. The X-76 is part of DARPA’s SPRINT program, which wants to solve the problem of needing a long runway for fast planes, while helicopters can land anywhere but are much slower.

The X-76 is now being built by Bell Textron, after passing a key design review. This plane is meant to be both fast—able to cruise at over 400 knots—and flexible, taking off and landing on rough ground, not just runways. The goal is to create a military aircraft that can quickly reach places where there are no airports or prepared landing strips. The SPRINT program is working with U.S. Special Operations Command to develop the technology. They hope the X-76 will let military teams move fast and surprise enemies, or help in emergencies, without being tied to airports.

Phase 2 of the project just started, which means they’re now making and testing the first X-76. Flight tests are planned for 2028. DARPA hopes this will “break aviation’s oldest trade-off” between speed and flexibility. The project also honors America’s history by naming the plane X-76, a nod to 1776.

Others ask about the technical challenges—can a plane really be as fast as a jet but take off like a helicopter?
