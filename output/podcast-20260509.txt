Hello everyone, this is the 2026-05-09 episode of Hacker News Daily Podcast. Today, we bring you a collection of top stories and discussions from the tech world, from new software projects and developer tools to security issues and personal growth in engineering. Let’s get started.

First, Bun, the JavaScript runtime similar to Node.js, is making big news. Its creator, Jarred Sumner, shared online that Bun is being rewritten in Rust, and now 99.8% of its tests pass on Linux x64 with glibc. This Rust rewrite matters because Rust is famous for speed and safety. Moving Bun to Rust should make it faster, more stable, and more secure. Passing almost all tests means the new Bun works almost like the old one, so developers can trust it. Bun is a complex tool, running JavaScript, handling files, and managing servers, so reaching this level of test coverage is a true achievement. Only a small 0.2% of tests are left to fix.

The Hacker News community is excited and impressed at how fast the Rust rewrite has reached high compatibility. Many praise the move to Rust, saying it helps avoid bugs common in languages like C++. Some are curious about which tests are not passing yet and if those will be hard to fix. Others discuss how fast or different the Rust version will be, and many are eager to try it. There are also warnings that big rewrites can bring new bugs or miss old features, so careful testing is needed. Some see Bun as a strong alternative to Node.js and Deno, and agree that good tests are important, but real-world use will be the real test. Overall, the mood is hopeful and positive for Bun’s Rust future.

Next, there is a serious security story from FreeBSD. A new bug, tracked as CVE-2026-7270, lets someone on the system get superuser rights by using the execve() system call. This problem affects all supported versions of FreeBSD and comes from a mistake in how execve() handles its arguments, leading to buffer overflow. This means a non-admin user could get root access. There is no simple workaround—systems must be updated and rebooted to be safe. The FreeBSD team acted fast, releasing updates for all main versions, and gave clear steps for fixing the bug.

Commenters were surprised to see such a basic bug in core system code. Many thanked the FreeBSD team for their quick fix and clear guidance. Some pointed out that buffer overflows are a common issue in all operating systems, not just FreeBSD. People discussed whether OpenBSD or Linux have the same problem, but it seems FreeBSD-specific. The story also sparked talk about the need for automatic updates, how hard it is for small teams to keep up with patches, and how important it is to check for CVEs on servers. Many agree that open security advisories and fast patches show a healthy, active project.

Now, let’s look at the digital preservation world. The Internet Archive has started a new branch called Internet Archive Switzerland, based in St. Gallen. This non-profit will focus on saving important digital content, especially archives that are at risk, and will also start collecting AI models for future research. The Swiss group will work with the University of St. Gallen, and the city was chosen for its long history in record-keeping and research support. This branch joins others, like Internet Archive Canada and Europe, to help build a strong, distributed digital library.

On Hacker News, most people are happy about the expansion. Some say Switzerland is a good choice because of its privacy laws and neutral status. Others say making the archive more global helps protect it from being shut down or attacked. There are some worries about legal risks, funding, and how the new branch will work with others, but overall, most see this as a positive step for saving digital history.

Switching topics, Chris Morgan, a software engineer, wrote about why he blocks all unknown query strings—like ?utm_source=example—in URLs on his website. He dislikes when tracking data or marketing tags are added to his links without permission. He says if you want to know where a visitor comes from, you should look at the “Referer” header, not add extra tags. Chris also says he is okay breaking old links that used query strings for things like cache-busting, as they are not needed now. He blocks all requests with unknown query strings using his Caddy server.

The comment section shows mixed views. Some agree, saying tracking codes are messy or annoying. Others say query strings are useful for things like searches or filtering, and blocking them could break real features. People warn that this might confuse users or break links, especially for bigger sites. Many respect Chris’s right to make his own rules, though they might do things differently.

Now to developer tools. Zed has released a Theme Builder for its code editor, letting users make and customize their own themes by changing colors for text, backgrounds, borders, and more. The Theme Builder is available from the Zed website, but only works on desktop computers for now. Users can also see and download themes made by others.

Hacker News users are mostly happy to see a tool for easy theme building. Many say editing theme files by hand is boring, so this builder helps. Some want to use it on the web or on mobile, but that’s not possible yet. There are questions about sharing themes with friends or importing from other editors. Some people want more support for accessibility, like for color blindness. Overall, users like that Zed is moving fast and making theme building easier.

Security issues are never far away, and cPanel had a rough week. Hackers used a bug to attack 44,000 servers, installing ransomware. Soon after, cPanel found and patched three more high-severity bugs. One let logged-in users read any file, another allowed them to run code with Perl and possibly take over the server, and a third problem let users change file permissions by making special links. All these needed an account, but on shared hosting, that is a big risk.

Many in the comments were shocked that the first bug was in use for so long before being fixed. Some blamed cPanel for slow code review, while others blamed web hosts for not patching quickly. People shared tips for checking if servers are infected and discussed whether automatic updates should always be on, though some worry about updates breaking things. The story shows that all software has bugs, but what matters is how fast they are fixed. There were also calls for better security education and more openness about attacks.

Next, let’s talk about “let-go,” a new programming language that looks and feels like Clojure but is written in Go. The main idea is to have a fast, small, and easy language for making standalone apps, web apps with WASM, or as a scripting engine inside Go programs. Let-go starts in just seven milliseconds, uses less memory than tools like Babashka or Java, and has lots of built-in libraries. You can use Go code from let-go and vice versa. It is not a full drop-in for Clojure, but it tries to be as close as possible.

Hacker News commenters liked let-go’s fast startup and small size. Many praised its clean Go integration and Clojure compatibility. Some discussed missing features, like Clojure’s STM, and hoped they might be added later. Others compared let-go to Babashka or Joker, weighing startup time against features. There were a few questions about long-term support, but most people were excited to try let-go for small tools or as a scripting language in Go projects.

Now for a story on career growth. One software engineer shared how they became a Senior Software Engineer just two and a half years after starting their first job. They set a goal to get promoted quickly, inspired by a mentor, but later realized this goal was not very meaningful. They were lucky to get a big project when other seniors were busy, and their manager liked to promote people. When first trying for promotion, they were denied, but after finishing more projects, they got the title. The author felt proud, but the excitement faded soon, and the pay bump was smaller than expected. They learned that true satisfaction comes from solving problems and helping others, not just from job titles.

Comments were mixed. Some agreed that titles are overrated and real happiness comes from the work itself. Others said promotions are still important for getting better jobs or pay. People pointed out that luck is always part of career progress, and good managers and mentors help a lot. Some warned that not all companies reward hard work, so it’s okay to move on if you feel stuck. The advice is to focus on building skills and relationships, which matter most in the long run.

In another story, Mark Doss from Jane Street gave a talk about running trading software that moves billions of dollars every day. He explained that even small mistakes can cost a lot of money, so their systems must work perfectly, with many layers of safety and fast ways to catch problems. The team works closely together, and when something goes wrong, they act fast to fix it and make lasting changes. Traditional ways to measure system health do not always fit, so they use direct alerts and checks.

Hacker News users were impressed by how much care and teamwork goes into keeping trading systems safe. They noted that trading software is different from normal web apps because the stakes are so high. Some asked about stress and burnout, while others wondered if these methods could help in other high-stakes industries. Many agreed that the real value comes from people and teamwork, not just the technology.

Finally, there is a story about how hard it is to distribute Mac software. The writer wants to release a small developer tool, but Apple’s rules make it very stressful. On Windows or Linux, you can just build and share your app. On macOS, you have to pay for a developer account and sign your app, which is costly for hobby projects. The process to verify your identity is also slow and confusing. Some people agree and share their own struggles, while others say the security checks protect users. There are calls for cheaper or easier ways for indie developers, and some think things will only get better if there is more competition or public pressure.
