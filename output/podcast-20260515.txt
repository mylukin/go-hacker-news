First, we look at Project Gutenberg, one of the oldest and biggest online libraries for free eBooks. Project Gutenberg offers over 75,000 books, mostly classics with expired copyrights, all for free. You can download them in formats like epub or Kindle, or just read them online without signing up. The website is run by volunteers and has been active since 1971. Books are easy to find by popularity, category, author, or language. You can even help by joining their proofreading team, fixing errors, or recording audiobooks for LibriVox. The site provides help guides, FAQs, and ways to donate. In the comments, many people thank the volunteers and share stories about finding favorite books or using the site to learn new languages. Some wish the website design and search could be better, and there are talks about legal limits for some books in different countries. Users also discuss donating, making new tools, and the history of the project, calling it a true gift to the internet. Many mention using LibriVox for free audiobooks based on Gutenberg texts.

Next, someone managed to run Windows CE 2.11 on a real Nintendo 64 game console. This hobby project needed a lot of custom code, since the N64 uses a MIPS processor and the Windows CE kernel had to be adapted. The system uses EverDrive-64 X7 hardware to load files from an SD card, and the N64 controller acts like a mouse. You can see a desktop, open windows, and run some Windows CE apps. Custom drivers were written for display, storage, input, and sound, and there is even a 3D demo using the N64 graphics chip. You need special tools and real N64 hardware for this project. There is no ready-made ROM due to licensing rules, and much of the system under the Windows API is original code. Hacker News users praised the project for its technical skill and fun, even if it is not practical. Some hope Microsoft will open-source old software like Windows CE, while others discuss the challenges of writing drivers and using real hardware. There is also praise for the open documentation and the MIT license.

Mitchell Hashimoto shared thoughts about how companies are handling AI risks. He sees companies rushing into AI, sometimes without thinking clearly about long-term problems. He remembers old arguments about building strong systems—whether to avoid all failures or focus on fixing them fast. Now, some teams believe AI can fix software bugs faster than humans, but Mitchell warns this can hide deeper risks. He says just having good test coverage or fewer bug reports does not mean the system is really safe. If changes happen too fast, the system could break before anyone notices. In the comments, some people agree that companies are moving too quickly and not thinking about real risks. Others argue that all new tech brings excitement and fear, and people will learn over time. Some say AI tools help find bugs, but teams should still be careful. Many agree that relying only on numbers like test coverage is risky, and it is important to keep talking openly about these issues.

Zulip, an open-source team chat tool, is now run by the nonprofit Zulip Foundation instead of its original company. The founder and some team members are leaving to join Anthropic, an AI company, but regular services will continue. The foundation owns Zulip and will guide it with leaders from the open-source world. They will keep Zulip Cloud, self-hosted support, and open-source sponsorships running. This move is meant to protect Zulip’s values and user privacy, and opens new ways to raise money through grants and donations. Many commenters praised the move to a nonprofit, saying it keeps Zulip open and trustworthy, and compared it to Mozilla and Signal. Some worry about losing key team members, but others say the team is experienced. There are questions about long-term funding and advice on running a nonprofit. Many are hopeful for Zulip’s future and thankful for its focus on community and open development.

Researchers recently found a new way to hack the Pixel 10 phone without any user action, similar to what was done with the Pixel 9 before. By updating an old exploit for a Dolby audio bug, they found the Pixel 10 had a new driver called VPU, which had a serious bug. This bug allowed any app to map large areas of the phone’s memory, including the kernel, leading to full control of the device with just a few lines of code. The bug was patched in 71 days, much faster than in the past, showing Android is improving its security response. Still, many are shocked at how such a simple bug got through. Some blame rushed hardware driver development, while others defend the Android team, saying all complex software has bugs. There are technical tips on spotting such bugs, and many agree that while patching is faster now, more needs to be done to prevent these problems in the first place.

Palantir, the big data company, has hired over 30 high-level UK government workers, including people from health, military, and other departments. Palantir has worked with the UK government on health data projects, especially during COVID-19. Some worry this hiring gives Palantir unfair advantage and inside knowledge, making it easier to win contracts. Rules exist to limit such “revolving door” moves, but they are not very strong. Palantir says they follow the law and hire for skills. Commenters are mixed—some worry about conflicts of interest and loss of public trust, while others say this is normal in the tech industry. Some want stronger rules or longer wait times, while others see benefits in hiring people who understand government needs.

California is moving forward with a new law that would make game publishers offer refunds or ways to keep playing if they shut down online games. The Protect Our Games Act just passed a key vote. If it becomes law, publishers must offer patches for offline play or refunds, and warn players 60 days before servers close. This would apply only to paid games sold in California after 2027. The bill was inspired by Ubisoft shutting down “The Crew” in 2024, which angered many players. Supporters say players deserve to keep what they paid for, while game companies argue that buyers only get a license, not full ownership, and that keeping games online forever can be impossible. In the comments, some support the law to protect consumers, while others worry it could hurt small studios or push up costs. There are questions about how it would work if a company goes out of business, and whether the law could apply outside California. Many hope this bill will lead to more respect for digital ownership.

There was also a story about a small but powerful science demo showing the thermite reaction using two rusty iron balls, one wrapped in aluminum foil. When hit together, they create bright sparks and loud noise. The chemistry is simple: aluminum reacts with iron oxide, making aluminum oxide, iron, and a lot of heat—the reaction is hot enough to melt iron. The demo is much safer than full thermite, but safety goggles and care are still needed. The balls can be reused, and rust can be replaced by soaking in salty water. In the comments, people are amazed by the simple setup and the dramatic reaction, but there are reminders to be careful. Some share tips for similar reactions and explain the chemistry. Most agree it is a great way to show real science, as long as safety comes first.

The U.S. Department of Justice is asking Apple and Google for information about over 100,000 people who downloaded a car-tuning app called EZ Lynk Auto Agent. The DOJ says the app helps people break emissions laws, so they want download data and buyer information from Amazon and Walmart as well. EZ Lynk says their app is for car checks and updates, not for illegal use, and that the DOJ is asking for too much information. Privacy groups agree, saying innocent users could be caught up in the sweep. Apple and Google are preparing to fight the demand in court. In the comments, many worry about privacy and call the DOJ’s request “authoritarian.” Some blame the government and warn that this could set a new rule for how much data tech companies must give to the government. Most agree this is about privacy and the right to fix your own car, not just about one app.

Finally, Waymo had to recall about 3,800 robotaxis because some cars were driving into flooded roads and getting stuck. The problem was with the software, which did not always see flooded roads as dangerous. Waymo has updated the software and paused service in some cities until it is safer. In the comments, some people are glad that software updates can fix such problems quickly, but others worry about robotaxis making serious mistakes. Some say even human drivers make errors, but self-driving cars should be extra careful. There are calls for more testing in bad weather, and some worry that too many mistakes could slow down the growth of robotaxis. Still, many believe each fix makes the system better and safer for the future.
