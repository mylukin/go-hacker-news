Hello everyone, this is the 2026-03-12 episode of Hacker News Daily Podcast. Today, we have a mix of stories about technology, art, science, and a bit of humor from the software world. Let’s get started.

Our first story is a very short and fun post from GitHub Gist. The whole post is just one question and one answer: “Shall I implement it?” The answer is: “No.” That’s all. There’s also an image, but it does not add much. The idea is simple—sometimes, when you get a new idea for a project, maybe the best thing is not to build it at all. Many developers joked about how often they start new side projects but never finish them. Some said this is the shortest and best advice. Others agreed that not building can save time and energy, and that saying “no” is sometimes the smartest move. But a few people pointed out that building things, even small or silly ideas, can help you learn. Some remembered times they did not build something, and someone else later became successful with that idea. Many enjoyed the joke, and some wanted more context, but most agreed that it’s a nice reminder to think before you start yet another project.

The next story is about a company called Malus, which offers something called “Clean Room as a Service.” Their service claims to help companies avoid open source license rules by using robots to write new code that works just like the original, but without ever looking at the original code. The robots read documentation and API specs to make new code, so companies do not have to share changes or give credit, even for strict licenses like GPL or AGPL. Malus says their process is legal and even offers legal protection, joking that if anything goes wrong, they will refund you and move their company. The website has a sarcastic tone, with fake testimonials from “happy” companies and jokes about “guilt” not showing up on company reports. Many people on Hacker News think the whole thing must be a parody, making fun of companies that try to dodge open source rules. Others worry that, if real, it could hurt open source software by taking code without giving back. Some noted that copying APIs or functionality is still a legal gray area, and that “clean room” methods in the past were always careful and expensive. There is debate about whether the service would work in court, and if it is even practical, since rebuilding code from docs is still a lot of work. In the end, the comments are split—some see humor, some see a real danger, and some just think it’s not worth it.

Next up, something more creative—a project called “Bubble Sorted Amen Break.” This project takes the famous Amen Break drum sample and slices it into pieces, then uses the bubble sort algorithm to rearrange the sounds by their “value,” like loudness or pitch. As the bubble sort runs, you can listen to how the beat changes, sometimes becoming more ordered, sometimes just strange. The creator, Vee, built this using the Godot engine and released it for both Windows and HTML5. People loved the idea—mixing code and music in a playful way. Some asked for more features, like making new tracks from the sorted samples or sharing the source code. Others wondered about using different sorting algorithms or samples. Most people simply enjoyed hearing how code can make music sound new and unexpected.

Now for some science. A new study with mice found that old mice can get their memories back by fixing how their gut and brain talk to each other. As mice get older, their gut bacteria change, which leads to memory loss because the signals through the vagus nerve to the brain get weaker. When young mice lived with old mice, the young also got old bacteria and started to lose memory. Giving antibiotics helped their memory again. One kind of bacteria, Parabacteroides goldsteinii, was especially bad for memory. By boosting the vagus nerve, old mice’s memory went back to normal. The researchers think this shows gut health is very important for brain health, and that we might be able to help people keep their memory by changing gut bacteria or using nerve treatments. The comments are mixed—some are excited and hope for new treatments, like probiotics or nerve stimulation. Others remind us that what works in mice may not work in humans, and that gut bacteria are very complex. Some shared stories about memory improving after diet changes, and others joked about “trusting your gut” having a new meaning. Many agree it’s an interesting area for more research.

Our next article looks at why ATMs did not kill bank teller jobs, but the iPhone did. When ATMs first came, banks needed fewer tellers per branch, but could open more branches, so the total number of tellers did not fall much. Tellers also started doing more complex jobs, helping customers with advice and loans. But when the iPhone and smartphones arrived, people could do almost everything from their phones. This meant banks closed many branches, and teller jobs dropped a lot. The article says that real job loss happens when new technology changes the whole way we do things, not just when it makes one task faster. In the comments, many agree and say the biggest changes come from new “paradigms.” Others point out that online banking before smartphones also played a role, and that teller jobs still exist in some places. Some say banks were already looking for ways to cut costs, and technology made it easier. There is also worry about what AI will do to jobs in the future, and discussion about “job polarization,” where mid-level jobs disappear. Most commenters like how the article links old stories to new worries.

Now, a serious story about AI and law enforcement. In Detroit, police used AI facial recognition to match a woman’s face to a shoplifting video. The AI said her face was a match, so police arrested her. She lost her job and spent time in jail before it was found she was innocent. The article explains that facial recognition often makes mistakes, especially for people with darker skin. Experts warn that AI should not be the only evidence for arrests. Many in the comments say this is a warning about using AI in policing. Some think police should always check other evidence, not just trust computers. There is debate about whether AI companies should be responsible for mistakes. Some people are now afraid of more AI in daily life, while others think facial recognition could still help if used carefully. A few suggest stopping its use until the technology gets better.

Next, some good news from the art world. The Metropolitan Museum of Art has put high-definition 3D scans of 140 famous objects online. Now, anyone can view detailed 3D models of things like Van Gogh paintings, ancient sarcophagi, and Japanese screens from home. You can zoom in, turn the objects around, and even use AR or VR to see the art as if it’s in your own room. The project was made with help from a Japanese TV network, and covers art from many times and countries. People on Hacker News are excited, saying it’s great for students, teachers, and people who can’t travel. Some wonder about using the files for 3D printing, or saving art in case of damage. A few worry that 3D scans can’t replace seeing the real thing, and some ask about copyright. Most agree it’s a big step for art and education, and hope more museums will do the same.

Now, a story that mixes exercise and food. Some runners are making butter while running by carrying heavy cream in bags. Libby Cope and her boyfriend tried this by putting cream and salt into double bags in their running vests, then running a trail. At the end, they had fresh butter for their bread. The science is simple—moving cream shakes fat together to make butter. They learned that warmer cream churns faster, but not too warm, or it just melts. Their videos went viral, and now others are trying churn-running, even making things like honey butter or ice cream while running. Comments are mostly positive, with people enjoying the creativity and science. Some remember making butter by shaking jars at home. A few worry about food safety, cleaning up, or just think it’s a social media trend. Others like how it makes running more fun and less serious.

Next, we have a technical but important article about AI security. It explains how attackers can trick AI systems that use Retrieval-Augmented Generation, or RAG, by adding fake documents to the knowledge base. The fake documents use official-sounding language and keywords so the AI thinks they are more important than real documents. This can make the AI give wrong answers, like reporting a financial loss instead of a profit. The attack is easy and lasts until someone finds and removes the bad files. Usual defenses, like checking who can add documents, do not work well. A better defense is “embedding anomaly detection,” which checks if new documents are too similar to each other or to what is already there. This stops most attacks, but not all. In the comments, developers worry that most teams do not use strong defenses, and that many ways exist to add documents—by bots, scripts, or syncing. Some think AI should check source trust, not just keywords. Others share worries about using RAG in business or health, where wrong answers can be serious. Many like that the article gives simple code and advice, especially on anomaly detection.

Lastly, some news from Google. Chrome is now available for ARM64 Linux devices, like Raspberry Pi or ARM-based laptops and desktops. Before, only Chromium or other browsers were available for ARM, and they sometimes missed features or had slower updates. Now, users can get official Chrome, with all the features and security updates. This is good news for web developers, schools, and makers, since many use ARM devices. Chrome for ARM64 is now out as a Debian package and will come to more Linux systems soon. In the comments, people are happy and say “finally.” Some worry about Chrome’s memory use on small devices. Others prefer Firefox but agree more choice is good. Some are glad they can now test websites on real Chrome. There are also questions about privacy and if Chrome will come to even more ARM devices in the future.
