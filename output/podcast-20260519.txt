Next, we look at a project called the Virtual OS Museum, a huge collection of old and new operating systems that you can try on your computer. The museum is a Linux virtual machine that works with QEMU, VirtualBox, or UTM, so it runs on Windows, macOS, and Linux. It has its own launcher and a snapshot feature to undo changes. The museum has over 250 platforms and more than 570 operating systems, from the first computers in 1948 to today. Rare systems like CTSS, early Unix, and Xerox Star are included. There are two versions: full (runs offline) and lite (downloads as needed). Updates are easy, and the creator spent 20 years collecting and fixing these systems. Each OS comes with extra software to show how it was used.

On Hacker News, people call it an amazing resource for learning computer history and for nostalgia. Many thank the creator for the hard work and share their own memories. Some point out technical challenges, like the large download size or legal issues. There are questions about adding more systems and ideas to improve the launcher. Some wonder about copyright, especially for old commercial systems. Many say the museum is great for teaching and digital preservation, and most are excited to try it.

Turning to AI-generated images, OpenAI now uses Google’s SynthID watermark to mark images made by its AI and has a tool to check if an image is from OpenAI. This is part of a push for “content provenance”—helping people trust what they see online. OpenAI joined C2PA, a group making standards so information about an image’s origin stays with the file. But metadata can be lost if files are changed or screenshotted, so OpenAI is adding a hidden watermark using SynthID, which is hard to remove and survives small changes. Both metadata and watermark are used, so if one is lost, the other might stay. The public tool can check if an image has OpenAI’s watermark or metadata, but it only works for OpenAI images for now.

Hacker News users see this as a good step for transparency. Some are glad there’s a public tool but worry people will find ways to remove watermarks. There are questions about open source use and if it will slow down creative projects. Some worry about privacy and tracking, and others discuss if this can help fight fake news or deepfakes. Most agree using several marking methods is better, but say this is only the beginning.

Now, let’s talk about Forge, a new Python framework for running local language models (LLMs) on multi-step tasks with much higher reliability. Forge uses “guardrails” and context management to help 8B models go from 53% to as high as 99% accuracy on agent-like workflows. Small local models often make mistakes, but Forge adds checks, fixes, and retries to make sure the model does what you want. It offers three main modes: WorkflowRunner for full workflows, Guardrails middleware for adding checks, and a proxy server for connecting any OpenAI-compatible client. Forge supports llama-server, Ollama, Llamafile, and Anthropic backends.

You need Python 3.12+ and a running model backend. Forge is easy to install with pip, and the docs have clear examples. The proxy server keeps models in tool-calling mode using a “respond” tool, avoiding confusion between text and tool outputs. There’s a test suite and guides for setup and choosing models. Forge is open source under MIT license and has a research paper.

Hacker News users are very interested in reliable agentic workflows with local models. Many are impressed by the jump in reliability. Some worry about hardware needs or Python version, but others say local LLMs are now easier to set up. There’s discussion about performance on small GPUs and pros and cons of each backend. Developers are excited to use Forge for chatbots, voice assistants, and long services. Some ask about delays from the guardrails, Windows support, and want more simple examples. Overall, Forge is seen as a strong step for reliable, self-hosted AI agents.

There’s also an article about why open source projects die, even when many people still use them. Sometimes, the main maintainer leaves, loses interest, or changes jobs. Projects can stop when money runs out, or no one has access to the code. Sometimes, a project looks alive, but only bots are working or only small fixes are made. Maintainers might be rude, driving helpers away, or the person who knows the system best might leave. Projects can also stop if two maintainers fight or if someone with bad intentions takes over and adds harmful code.

Other reasons include publishing tools breaking, big changes between versions, or the world changing—like losing a platform or a better tool coming out. Projects can split, confusing users, or change license, causing forks. Even “dead” projects may be used for years in old systems.

Hacker News users agree with these points and share their own stories. Some say this is normal for fast-moving software. Others feel sad that small problems can kill important projects. Some want maintainers to find new helpers before leaving, but others note it’s hard to work on free projects without pay. There’s talk about company-owned open source and the need for easier ways to hand over projects. Many agree users rarely check if a project is alive if it still works. Some say having backups or forks is important.

In Minnesota, a new law will ban prediction market websites like Kalshi and Polymarket. These sites let people bet on future events—sports, elections, or even what someone will say in a speech. From August, it will be illegal to run or advertise these sites in Minnesota, and using VPNs to get around the ban is also illegal. Companies can be charged with a felony if they break the law. Farmers can still use weather contracts, and trading stocks or insurance is not banned.

This law has started a fight between Minnesota and the Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC), which says only the federal government should make rules for prediction markets. The CFTC is taking Minnesota to court, saying the law hurts farmers and regular people. Some think prediction markets are too big to stop, and other states are considering bans, but the markets keep growing.

Hacker News users are split. Some think bans are useless and will just move activity overseas, making it less safe. Others worry prediction markets are dangerous, as people could try to change real events for money. Some say these markets help farmers and businesses, so a full ban could hurt the economy. There’s debate over who should regulate—states or the federal government. Some think bans protect kids and stop gambling problems, but others say people will always find a way to bet. There are worries about insider trading and if prediction markets are just gambling with a new name. Most think the law won’t stop prediction markets but will make the rules less clear.

Finally, Tesla’s new lithium refinery in Texas, called “clean” and “acid-free,” was found releasing black wastewater into a local ditch. The plant puts out up to 231,000 gallons of treated water daily, but the local drainage district was not told. The Texas environmental agency gave Tesla a permit but did not require checking for heavy metals or lithium. When drainage workers saw the dark water, they tested it and found small amounts of dangerous chemicals like hexavalent chromium, arsenic, and high levels of lithium and other metals. The salty water is killing grass along the ditch, which could cause flooding if the soil washes away. The state did not check for these chemicals, because the permit didn’t require it. Tesla says it follows all rules and the water is within legal limits, and claims the pollution might come from elsewhere. The drainage district, however, says the chemicals match what comes from a battery plant.

The problem is not that Tesla broke the law, but that the rules may not be strict enough. This is worrying because the nearby city is low on drinking water. Hacker News users are shocked that a “clean” plant is releasing toxic chemicals. Some say this is common for factories, and the bigger issue is weak government rules. A few defend Tesla for following the permit, saying the state should set tougher rules. Some argue that “green” technology still has hidden costs, and regular independent testing is needed. Many say this shows people should be careful about believing marketing claims.
