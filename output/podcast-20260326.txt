Hello everyone, this is the 2026-03-26 episode of Hacker News Daily Podcast. Today, we have a wide range of stories, from color theory in control rooms to open research, a fresh supply chain attack, a game played over DNS, and more. Let’s get started.

Our first story takes us into the world of old control rooms, like those found in nuclear plants, where seafoam green paint covered the walls. The article’s author visited the Oak Ridge site from the Manhattan Project and was curious why this green was everywhere. After some research, she found that a man named Faber Birren, an expert in color, helped set color standards for factories during World War II. Birren showed that colors could affect how people feel and work. For example, using green or blue walls in meat factories made products look fresher, and workers felt less tired. He worked with DuPont to create a color code: red for emergencies, yellow for caution, orange for machine dangers, blue for information, green for safety, and especially light green for walls, which was easy on the eyes and calming. These standards spread widely, even used at Hanford nuclear site and in German bridges.

In the Hacker News comments, many people shared memories of seeing the same green in old hospitals, schools, and submarines. Some explained that green is gentle under harsh lights and helps workers make fewer mistakes. Others wondered if the color also hid dirt and wear. There was praise for learning about Birren, and debate about whether offices today should use more color theory instead of plain white walls. Some joked that seafoam green reminds them of old movies and games. Most agreed that color matters in work spaces, and enjoyed the story behind the famous green control rooms.

Our next story is about CERN hosting the next phase of Open Research Europe, or ORE, Europe’s main open access science platform. Supported by the European Commission and many funders, ORE lets researchers from supported countries publish for free. Its “publish–review–curate” model means articles are checked, published openly, and then reviewed by peers, with all reviews public. Since starting in 2021, ORE now has over 1,200 articles and thousands of authors. CERN will provide the technical base using Open Journal Systems, but decisions stay with the ORE group, not CERN alone.

CERN has a big history with open science, running projects like Zenodo. Leaders hope this step will build more trust in science and make sharing easier. Groups like OPERAS are helping to spread the word and get more authors involved. In the comments, people are happy about more open access, because it means research is free for all. Some discuss the technology behind ORE, while others warn about quality and whether the model is sustainable. There are worries that open peer review might mean less honest feedback. Many see this as Europe taking the lead and hope it will push other publishers to open up. Some worry commercial publishers may push back. The overall mood is hopeful but realistic about the challenges.

Our third story is a real-time account of a malware attack through the Python package litellm on PyPI. An engineer noticed his laptop was running thousands of Python processes, with strange commands using exec and base64. After investigation, he found a hidden file in litellm that ran on every Python start, stealing SSH keys, cloud credentials, and API keys, and sending them to a fake server. It even tried to spread to Kubernetes clusters. The malware came from a fake version of litellm uploaded to PyPI, not from the official GitHub release. The attack caused a “fork bomb,” crashing computers.

The author quickly reported the issue, wrote a blog post, and shared cleanup steps. In the comments, many thanked him for acting fast. Some were shocked at how easy supply chain attacks can happen through package updates. Developers discussed how .pth files can be abused, and called for better package manager checks. There was debate if AI tools helped or slowed down the investigation. Some shared tips for secret rotation and recovery, and many agreed that better security education is needed for all developers.

Next, we have a fun hack—a project that lets you play DOOM by downloading all game data from DNS TXT records, using just PowerShell and public DNS servers. The game files are split into nearly 2,000 pieces, stored in DNS records on Cloudflare. PowerShell fetches each piece, rebuilds the game in memory, and runs it—no need to save files to disk. Uploading your own copy needs splitting the files and using Cloudflare’s API. Free Cloudflare accounts can hold about 185 chunks, so you need more domains or a paid plan to upload the full game.

In the comments, people loved the creativity, saying it’s a clever way to use basic internet tools. Some pointed out that DNS is not meant for big data, and if this becomes popular, DNS providers might block or limit TXT records. Others joked about playing DOOM everywhere, while some worried about speed and stability. Overall, the community was impressed by the technical skill and playful spirit.

Our fifth story is about Deploytarot.com, a website that uses tarot cards to describe different types of software deployments and tech roles. The site mixes tarot themes with real deployment tasks like A/B testing, database migrations, and hotfixes, using humor to describe each one. Company roles from CEO to Intern get their own jokes too. The site turns the stress of deploying into something fun, using tarot as a metaphor for the unknown risks of shipping code.

Hacker News users loved the humor, sharing their own deployment stories and saying the jokes felt true. Many liked how each role got a joke, especially for interns and consultants. Some said the site could help teams talk about deployment risks in a lighter way, or be used as a team icebreaker. Others wished for real “tarot readings” for their own deploys. The community enjoyed the mix of tech culture, humor, and truth.

Next, we look at the history of fermented foods and how they shaped human evolution. The article says eating foods like sauerkraut changes our immune system and can lower inflammation, because special molecules talk to our cells. Researchers found that D-PLA from sauerkraut can trigger a unique human immune receptor, HCA3, which only humans and great apes have. This suggests our bodies evolved with fermented foods, which helped make food safer and more nutritious.

In the comments, some people were excited and wanted to eat more traditional foods, while others wanted more proof. Some pointed out not all fermented foods are the same, and a few shared stories about making their own at home. There was debate about whether modern diets are too focused on shelf life and profit. Others warned that focusing only on “good” microbes ignores how complex our guts are. Many want more big studies before changing their diets. Overall, the topic brought lots of interest and some healthy skepticism.

Our next story is about a free online book, “The Little Book of C,” which teaches C programming from basics to more advanced topics. The book covers what C is, why it matters, how to install compilers, write and run code, use command-line tools, and organize projects. It explains data types, operators, control flow, functions, memory, pointers, arrays, and dynamic memory functions.

In the comments, people praised the book for being simple and clear, and for including command-line and project structure basics, often missing in other books. Some compared it to classics like “K&R C,” saying this one is easier for beginners. There was debate about whether C is a good first language—some say yes, some worry it’s too hard. A few advanced users noted it doesn’t cover every tricky detail, but most agreed it’s a solid place to start. Readers liked the practice exercises and hope the book will keep getting updates.

Our next news comes from New York City’s public hospitals, which will stop using Palantir software after their contract ends in October. The main reason is pressure from activists and worries about how Palantir handles sensitive health data. The contract helped hospitals with insurance claims but allowed Palantir to use “de-identified” data for other uses with city agreement. Now, hospitals will use their own software.

Palantir is also working with the UK’s NHS, but there are concerns about privacy and re-identifying data with new AI tools. Some experts warn that de-identified data is not always safe. In New York, activists see ending the contract as a win. Others think in-house solutions are better for privacy, but may cost more. Some say the problem is weak government rules, not just Palantir. There was debate about whether AI fears are overblown, but most agreed health data needs special care.

Next, we have Turbolite, a new tool that lets SQLite databases live in S3 or similar storage, with fast query times even if the data is not cached. It’s written in Rust and acts as a custom virtual file system for SQLite. Turbolite fetches groups of database pages from S3, caching and compressing them for speed. It works well for many small databases in the cloud, but only allows one writer at a time.

Hacker News users were impressed by the speed and clever design for S3’s slow round-trips. Some think it’s perfect for apps with many customers, but others worry about reliability and the single-writer limit. There were questions about costs and comparisons to other projects like Litestream. Some wondered if it could work offline too. The community sees it as a good step for SQLite in the cloud, but wants more real-world testing.

Finally, we remember John Bradley, creator of the xv image viewer, who has died at age 61. A friend wrote a tribute, sharing memories and a song in his honor. Bradley was also a guitarist in a band called Booster Patrol. Many in the community expressed sadness, remembering xv as a simple, fast tool that just worked. Some shared stories of Bradley answering emails, showing he was kind and helpful. There was talk about licensing issues, and how tools like xv fade as technology changes, but the people behind them leave a strong impact.
