Next, we have a story about the Rodecaster Duo audio interface. A writer tried to update the firmware and found that SSH was turned on by default. They discovered the firmware was just a simple gzipped tarball, with default SSH keys included and no signature checks. This made it very easy to flash any firmware, including their own custom version. The author added their own SSH keys and allowed password authentication, making it easy to log into the device. They liked being able to “own” the hardware, but wondered why SSH was enabled by default and why default keys were used. They tried to contact Rode about these security issues but got no reply.

In the comments, many people worry about security risks from having SSH enabled with default keys. Some say this is common for hardware, and companies sometimes leave these “back doors” for support. Others like the freedom to install custom firmware and learn from open devices. There are debates about the risks of not having signature checks and whether most users would even notice. Some suggest Rode should offer a better way to report security problems, and others share tips for making devices safer at home, like using firewalls. The discussion shows both concern for security and excitement for hacking and learning.

Now, for something different: a team of archaeologists found a piece of Homer’s Iliad inside the wrappings of a Roman-era mummy in Egypt. The discovery was in Oxyrhynchus, a place known for many ancient finds. The text comes from the “Index of Ships” in Book 2 of the Iliad, listing Greek warriors at Troy. Some of the mummies had gold or copper tongues placed in their mouths, and gold leaf on their bodies. The tomb had wooden coffins, but many were damaged by ancient grave robbers. In another part of the cemetery, they found stone burial chambers with adults, a baby, and even cats wrapped in cloth. There were also small statues of gods like Harpocrates and Cupid, showing a mix of Greek and Egyptian culture.

A blog commenter named Frank linked the Iliad fragment to real places in Greece and Crete, and shared a modern story about American hippies and soldiers in Crete in the 1960s. This mix of old and new shows how ancient stories still connect to people today. On Hacker News, readers are likely amazed by how old texts survive in strange places and may discuss the importance of Oxyrhynchus, the use of gold tongues in burials, and the mix of cultures. Some might share their own stories about ancient sites or learning about the Iliad. The story brings out both the mystery and excitement of discovering the past.

A new operating system called Lightwhale is getting attention. It is made for home servers and runs Docker containers easily. Lightwhale boots from an ISO straight into Docker, with an immutable core system that cannot be changed by accident or malware. Data and settings are stored separately, making backups easy and the system more secure. Lightwhale is lightweight, fast, and designed to be plug-and-play. By default, the system is read-only, but you can enable persistence for data. The idea is to avoid the pain of updates or package managers—if something goes wrong, just reboot.

Hacker News users like the idea and compare it to RancherOS, Flatcar, or NixOS, but appreciate the simple home focus. Some want support for ARM or Raspberry Pi, but it’s x86-64 only for now. Others ask about installing more software, but the creator explains this would break the system’s purpose. There are tips about changing the default password and questions about data safety if the USB fails. Some discuss using Lightwhale for education or teaching Docker basics. The community finds the project useful and is glad to see a new simple server OS.

The idea of “learning mechanics” excites some, while others prefer old views like statistics. Most agree more theory is good for AI safety and reliability.
