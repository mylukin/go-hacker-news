Hello everyone, this is the 2026-06-24 episode of Hacker News Daily Podcast. Today, we have a lot of interesting stories for developers and tech fans. Let’s get started.

First, OpenAI has made its first custom chip, called Jalapeño, with help from Broadcom. This chip is designed for AI “inference” tasks, which means running AI models after they are trained. OpenAI says Jalapeño uses less power and works better for each watt than other top chips. OpenAI even used its own AI models to help design the chip. The goal is to depend less on Nvidia and lower the cost of running AI for millions of users. Jalapeño is not for training big models yet—OpenAI will still use Nvidia for that for now.

On Hacker News, people have mixed opinions. Some think it’s a smart move to avoid relying only on Nvidia, which can be risky or too expensive. Others remind us that making good chips is hard and many companies have failed before. Some are excited that OpenAI’s own AI helped design the chip, showing how strong these tools are. But some are not sure if Jalapeño can really beat Nvidia, or if it’s only for special jobs. There’s also discussion about Broadcom’s role—some believe Broadcom did most of the hard work, while OpenAI gave the ideas. One person worries about vendor lock-in, where custom chips make it harder for outsiders to join in. But some are hopeful that more custom chips will lower prices and help everyone using AI. Most agree this is a big step, but they want to see how Jalapeño works in practice.

Next, Qualcomm is buying Modular, a company making software tools to run AI models faster and easier. Modular made Mojo, a new programming language for AI. Mojo aims to be as fast as C++ and as easy as Python. Modular’s AI Engine also lets AI run on many types of hardware, like phones, laptops, and servers, without changing code.

Qualcomm wants to make it easier to run AI on devices, not just in big data centers, so AI can be faster and use less power. With Modular, they hope to attract more developers and sell more chips.

Comments are mixed. Some think it’s a smart move, as AI needs better tools. Others worry big companies buying startups will slow innovation. Some developers are excited about Mojo, but others say it’s still too new. One hopes Modular will make Qualcomm chips easier to use, as they are hard now. Some worry the tools will only work on Qualcomm chips, but others think more choices for AI programming is good. People agree AI on devices is important, but want to see what Qualcomm does next.

Now, let’s talk about RubyLLM, a new Ruby framework that lets you use many different AI providers with the same code. Instead of learning a new API for every AI service, RubyLLM gives you one simple interface. It only needs three dependencies and works with over 800 models from providers like OpenAI, Anthropic, Gemini, and Ollama. You can use it for chatbots, AI agents, making images, reading files, and more. It even supports Rails and can add a chat UI to your web app.

In the comments, people are happy to see a modern AI tool for Ruby. Some compare it to Python’s LangChain and wonder if RubyLLM is just as powerful. Others worry about vendor lock-in or API changes. Some ask if Ruby is fast enough for big AI jobs, while others say speed is not a problem for most use-cases. There are also questions about open-source licensing and how RubyLLM will keep up with new models. Many like the easy code samples and say it could help them add AI to their apps quickly. Some worry about privacy, but others note that RubyLLM can use private setups. Overall, the mood is positive, with people excited to try it out.

Next, Bunny.net has made its DNS service, Bunny DNS, free for up to 500 domains per account. DNS is important for making the internet fast, and Bunny DNS helps route web traffic quickly and smartly. Bunny DNS started as an internal tool, but now anyone can use it. It supports smart routing, IPv4 and IPv6, and modern security features like DNSSEC with privacy. You can move domains easily from other providers, and there are no usage fees—just a $1/month minimum spend if you use other paid services.

Many users are happy about the free offer, saying it’s great for small projects and startups. Some compare Bunny DNS to Cloudflare and wonder if Bunny can handle big traffic. Others like the privacy focus and support for new features. A few ask about the minimum spend, and Bunny staff say most users will pay nothing extra unless they use other services. Some users worry about how long “free” will last, but others think it’s a good way to attract users to Bunny’s platform. People praise the easy migration, the modern features, and quick support. Most see this as a strong move and a big win for users who want fast, simple, and reliable DNS for free.

Moving on, there’s an article about a rise in spammy pull requests (PRs) in the OpenClaw open source project after it got popular. Most new PRs now come from AI coding agents and are low quality, with merge rates dropping from 48% to less than 10%. The author says PR spam is like email spam in the 2000s. Some projects are now using reputation tools like Vouch to check if contributors are trusted. But, just having more contributors is not helpful if they all use the same AI tools and send the same ideas. The real value comes from different ways of thinking.

In the comments, people agree AI-generated PR spam is a problem and makes open source harder to manage. Some say blocking all AI contributions is not fair—AI can help with boring tasks. Others like reputation systems, but worry it will be hard for new people to join. Some think the real problem is about scale, not just AI. A few think tools like Vouch could act as gatekeepers and make open source less open. Some remember when email spam first became a problem, and hope open source will find new solutions. Many agree that the best PRs need real understanding, and that will not change even as AI gets better.

Next, Google has added a built-in “computer use” tool to Gemini 3.5 Flash. Now, developers can build AI agents that work across browsers, mobile devices, and desktops. Before, this was only in Gemini 2.5, but now it’s in the main Gemini Flash model. Agents can see, understand, and take actions in different apps. Google is also focusing on safety, adding new security options like user confirmation and stopping tasks if someone tries to trick the AI.

Developers are excited about easier automation, but some worry about security, like prompt injection and AI having access to real systems. Some want even stronger protections and more user control. Privacy is also a concern—people want to know who can see or control what the agent does. There are questions about how Gemini 3.5 Flash compares to OpenAI’s tools and if API pricing is fair. Overall, people see this as a big step, but want more details on safety and real-world use.

Now, let’s talk about the Xteink X4, a small and cheap e-ink reader costing just £40. It’s about the size of a phone, very light, and easy to carry. The screen is clear, battery lasts long, and it supports ePub books. The built-in software is simple, but you can add custom firmware to get more features, like more languages and better formatting. Firmware options include CrossPoint, Papyrix, and Inx, each adding different tools and making the device more powerful. You can even use it with a Bluetooth keyboard or run classic Palm apps.

Hacker News commenters have mixed views. Some love the small size for quick reading, while others think it’s too small for long sessions. Many are impressed by the custom firmware and how easy it is to install. Some find the microSD slot hard to use, and others want support for more book formats. There are concerns about the phone mount not working well, but many praise the active community and fun extras like custom wallpapers. Some say having a simple, distraction-free reader is nice in today’s world. Overall, at £40, most people think the X4 is fun to try and good for reading and tinkering.

Next, we have an article about BitTorrent’s Distributed Hash Tables, or DHTs. The authors built tools that can quickly copy all torrents from the network to make new search engines, but also to watch what users are sharing. BitTorrent used to use central servers, but now uses DHTs, which are like shared phone books. The authors’ tool, ClearView, acts like many fake users to collect lots of info. They made a search engine that found over a million torrents in two hours using just one computer. They could also see which IP addresses were sharing which files.

Most popular torrents are for movies, TV, and music, and almost all are copyrighted. The authors warn that DHTs do not protect privacy—anyone can see what files are shared and by whom. In the comments, people are surprised how easy it is to crawl the DHT and rebuild a search site. Some worry about privacy, while others say VPNs or proxies can help, but not everyone uses them. Some see this as both good and bad—it helps keep the network alive, but also makes spying and lawsuits easier. There are technical discussions and stories from people running their own crawlers. People are both excited and worried about the risks for users.

Now, John Carmack, famous for making Quake, talked about his regrets from the early days. He said Quake was too ambitious, and they tried to do too much at once. He thinks they could have built multiplayer and modding inside the old Doom engine, which was more stable, and saved big technical jumps for later games. Carmack also admitted he pushed the team too hard, leading to burnout, and made mistakes with company stock and team roles.

In the comments, some agree Quake was risky and too complex, but others say the ambition made it special. Many admire Carmack’s honesty and say more leaders should talk openly about mistakes. Some point out burnout is still a big problem in games. There are also talks about mixing design and art, which is still hard today. Some thank Carmack for sharing and say it helps new developers. Others wonder if Quake would be as famous if the team had played it safe. Many agree that balance and rest are important for long-term success.

Last, LookAway is a Mac app that reminds you to take breaks, blink, and fix your posture, but tries not to interrupt you at the wrong time. It waits for a good moment to remind you—like when you’re not in a meeting or playing games. You can snooze or delay breaks, and the app syncs with your iPhone or iPad. You can change timers, sounds, and messages, and the app works with Apple Shortcuts. There are new features like planned breaks, website stats, and a “Screen Score” to track your habits.

In the comments, many people say they like the app because it helps avoid eye strain. Some like that it doesn’t interrupt during meetings or games. Others say other reminder apps are too annoying, but LookAway feels smart and gentle. Some want Windows or Linux versions, and others ask about privacy—the creator says everything is private. Some find any reminders annoying, but most agree it’s healthy in the long run. People praise the friendly design and clear notifications.
