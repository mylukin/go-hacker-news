## A Look into NaviDial, Japan's Legacy Phone Service

- 原文链接: [A Look into NaviDial, Japan's Legacy Phone Service](https://www.tokyodev.com/articles/a-look-into-navidial-japan-s-legacy-phone-service)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47788633)

This article talks about NaviDial, a phone service in Japan that uses 0570 numbers. Many companies and even public services use these numbers, but they are not free and can be expensive, especially from a mobile phone.

NaviDial started in the 1990s when businesses wanted to save money. Before NaviDial, there was FreeDial (0120 numbers), which let people call companies for free. But FreeDial was costly for businesses, so NaviDial was created to make callers pay instead. NaviDial keeps the same smart routing features as FreeDial, like sending calls to the right office and managing waiting lines, but puts the cost on the person calling. 

If you call a 0570 number from a mobile phone, you pay about 33 yen per minute, and this price will go up soon. Even if you have an unlimited calling plan, it does not cover NaviDial calls. This can be a big problem, especially for people with low income or those who need help from public services, like hotlines for human rights or emergencies. Sometimes, people are left waiting on hold for a long time, making the call even more expensive.

NaviDial is still common in Japan because changing to a new system is hard. Many companies and government offices are used to 0570 numbers, and switching would take money, time, and technical skills. Also, businesses trust NTT, the company behind NaviDial, because it is big and offers support. Other options, like VoIP numbers (050 numbers), are seen as less trustworthy in Japan. Some new companies use them, but old companies stick with 0570.

Other countries, like the US and UK, took a different path. In the US, toll-free numbers are common for important services, and paid numbers are used only for special cases. The UK used to be like Japan, but later banned paid customer service numbers after people complained. Japan has not had such a big push yet, so NaviDial remains.

Many commenters on Hacker News were surprised at how expensive NaviDial calls are, especially for public services. Some people shared stories about being stuck on hold and paying a lot. Others pointed out that this system makes life harder for poor or vulnerable people. Some users said their countries also had paid numbers in the past, but moved to cheaper or free systems after public complaints. Others talked about how hard it is to replace old systems in big organizations, especially when they trust old companies like NTT. A few people wondered why Japan doesn’t use more internet-based solutions, like chat or email, but others replied that old habits and regulations make change slow. Some commenters suggested that real change will only happen if enough people complain or if the government makes new rules. Others gave tips, like looking for alternate numbers or using online support to avoid NaviDial fees. Overall, most people agreed NaviDial is outdated and unfair for many users, but it will likely stay until there is pressure to change.

## Too much discussion of the XOR swap trick

- 原文链接: [Too much discussion of the XOR swap trick](https://heather.cafe/posts/too_much_xor_swap_trick/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47750486)

This article talks about the XOR swap trick, a programming method to swap two variables without using a temporary variable. The author explains what XOR means, how the XOR swap works, and if it is actually useful in real code.

First, XOR stands for “exclusive or.” It is a bitwise operation that returns true when only one input is true, not both. In code, XOR is often written as `^`. For example, `a ^ b` flips the bits where `a` and `b` are different. The XOR swap trick uses XOR’s properties: if you XOR a value with itself, you get zero; and if you XOR with zero, you get the original value.

The swap trick works like this:  
```
a ^= b;
b ^= a;
a ^= b;
```
After these three lines, the values of `a` and `b` are swapped. The article shows with examples and tables why this works.

The author tests if the trick is useful in modern programming. When swapping local variables, compilers like Clang or GCC can optimize both the XOR swap and the regular swap (using a temp variable) into the same assembly code. So, there is no real benefit to using XOR swap here.

When swapping values through pointers, the XOR swap uses more instructions and is slower than the temp variable method. Also, if both pointers point to the same memory, the XOR swap will erase the data, while the temp method is safe. Compilers cannot optimize this away unless you use the `restrict` keyword, which promises the pointers are different.

The article also talks about the addition swap trick (`a = a + b; b = a - b; a = a - b;`). This method can fail with integer overflow or with floating point numbers, so it is unsafe.

The author explains why people still talk about the XOR swap trick. It looks clever and is a fun puzzle, so it often appears in interviews or programming discussions. In real assembly code, the trick is only useful on some old CPUs when you do not have enough registers. On most modern CPUs, a simple swap or even a special swap instruction (`XCHG` on x86) works better.

Finally, the article mentions other neat uses of XOR, such as finding a unique value in a list where all others appear twice. This is genuinely useful and efficient.

In the comments, many readers agree that the XOR swap trick is mostly just a curiosity today. Some point out that using it in real code can cause bugs, especially if the same variable is passed twice. Others share that modern compilers are smart enough to optimize regular swaps, making the trick pointless.

A few commenters remember using XOR swap on very old computers, but say those days are gone. Some people mention the trick is still fun to explain, especially to students or in job interviews, but not for real software.

Others note that the main lesson is to trust the compiler and write clear code. Swapping with a temp variable is easy to read and just as fast. One commenter adds that knowing about XOR’s properties is still useful for other algorithms, like checksums and cryptography.

A few readers discuss other XOR tricks, such as XOR linked lists, but most agree these are rare in practice. Some say the real value of learning about XOR swap is understanding how bits and operators work, not using the trick itself.

Overall, the discussion shows that while XOR swap is clever, it is not helpful in most real code today. Writing simple, clear code is better, and modern compilers will do the right thing for you.

## Moving a large-scale metrics pipeline from StatsD to OpenTelemetry / Prometheus

- 原文链接: [Moving a large-scale metrics pipeline from StatsD to OpenTelemetry / Prometheus](https://medium.com/airbnb-engineering/building-a-high-volume-metrics-pipeline-with-opentelemetry-and-vmagent-c714d6910b45)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47788818)

Airbnb needed to move from an old StatsD metrics system to a modern pipeline using OpenTelemetry and Prometheus. They wanted to collect all metrics first, so they could see problems early and make sure dashboards and alerts would work with the new tools.

The old system used StatsD with a helper called Veneur. For the new system, they chose OpenTelemetry Protocol (OTLP) for most services and kept StatsD only for old apps that were hard to update. They set up a method where their code could send metrics to both StatsD and OTLP at the same time—this is called dual-write. This helped them move to the new system slowly and safely. After switching, they saw big wins: CPU use for metrics dropped a lot, reliability improved, and they could use better features from Prometheus like new kinds of histograms. Also, OTLP is an open standard, so it will work well with other tools in the future.

Some very busy services had problems after the switch. These services made lots of metrics every second and started using too much memory. The team fixed this by changing how they count metrics—using a “delta” mode for busy apps, which uses less memory but can create small gaps in data if something goes wrong.

To save money and keep things fast, they needed to “aggregate” metrics (combine some data) before storing it. They tried a few tools, but most were too complex or didn’t fit their needs. They picked vmagent because it was simple, easy to set up, and could be changed if needed. They used two types of vmagent: one to route data, and one to aggregate it. This setup helped the system scale up to handle over 100 million metric samples per second.

After moving to Prometheus, they found a bug: some counters were undercounted. This happened because Prometheus handles counters differently than StatsD, especially when a metric starts at zero and only updates sometimes. The team fixed this with a trick called “zero injection,” where the system sends a fake zero value to help Prometheus count correctly. This fix was easy to add and didn’t make extra work for users.

Commenters liked the technical depth of the article. Some said the dual-write migration was smart, letting teams switch slowly and safely. Others noted that running two systems at once is hard, but sometimes needed for big changes. A few people warned that high-cardinality metrics (lots of different labels) can be expensive in Prometheus, so teams should watch out for that. Some mentioned they also had issues with Prometheus counters missing data, and thought the zero injection idea was clever. One reader asked if Airbnb considered other tools like TimescaleDB or M3, but others replied that Prometheus is more standard now. Some said they liked vmagent because it’s easy to scale and change, while others wondered if Airbnb would open source their custom changes. Finally, several readers noted that migrations like this are never simple, and praised Airbnb for sharing both successes and problems.

## Fast and Easy Levenshtein distance using a Trie (2011)

- 原文链接: [Fast and Easy Levenshtein distance using a Trie (2011)](https://stevehanov.ca/blog/fast-and-easy-levenshtein-distance-using-a-trie)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47738673)

This article shows how to speed up finding similar words using Levenshtein distance and a trie data structure. The main idea is to help search functions deal with typos or find rhymes by matching words that are close to what users type.

First, the author explains the simple but slow way: compare the target word with every word in a dictionary using the Levenshtein function. This function checks how many changes (insert, delete, replace) are needed to turn one word into another. For each pair, it fills a table that shows the minimum number of edits. This basic method works, but it’s very slow for big dictionaries, taking several seconds even on a fast computer.

To make things faster, the author suggests using a trie. A trie is a tree where each node is a letter, and words with the same beginnings share the same path. With a trie, you can check common parts of many words at once. The improved algorithm fills in only the needed rows of the table as it moves through the trie, avoiding repeated work. This makes the search much faster—over 300 times in the author’s tests.

The article also talks about memory use. Tries can be big, so sometimes more compact structures like MA-FSA or DAWG are used. The author explains how this method was used for RhymeBrain, a site that finds rhymes even in a huge dictionary of 2.6 million words. With the trie method, RhymeBrain could search quickly, even on a slow server.

Other approaches, like Peter Norvig’s method, create many possible misspellings and check if they’re in a hash table. This can be efficient for small errors but doesn’t scale well for bigger differences. Some people use memoization, only checking parts of the table, but this can be complex and sometimes slower. Another advanced idea is to use a Levenshtein automaton, which acts like a big regular expression to match all possible misspellings at once, but the code is complex.

In the comments, many readers like the trie approach and share their own uses for similar algorithms. Some say they used tries for spell checkers, autocompletion, or even DNA matching. Others point out that for small dictionaries or low error limits, Norvig’s hash-table method can be simpler and just as fast. A few discuss memory problems with tries, especially for huge word lists, and suggest DAWG or MA-FSA for saving space.

Some readers mention that Levenshtein distance is not always the best choice, especially for languages with different rules or for fuzzy matching in other alphabets. Others debate if Python is too slow for this task, and share code in C++ or Rust for even faster searches. Several people link to other articles and tools, showing there is a lot of ongoing research in this area.

A few comments warn about building your own spell checker without enough test data, saying it is easy to miss real-world mistakes. Some like that the author explains things in a simple way and gives working code. Others wish for more benchmarks or comparisons with advanced libraries.

In general, people agree that using tries for Levenshtein search is clever and practical, especially for big dictionaries or when you need to find all similar words fast. But they also note that the best method depends on the size of your data, your language, and how close the matches need to be.
