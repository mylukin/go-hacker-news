## Every new car sold in the European Union must include a driver monitoring camera

- 原文链接: [Every new car sold in the European Union must include a driver monitoring camera](https://allaboutcookies.org/eu-mandatory-distracted-driver-system)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48823557)

Every new car sold in the European Union must now have a camera inside to watch the driver. This is a new rule starting from July 2024, and it is meant to improve road safety and reduce accidents.

The camera’s main job is to check if the driver is sleepy, distracted, or not paying attention to the road. If the system thinks the driver is in danger of losing focus, the car can warn them with a sound or a message. The law says the camera cannot record or save video or photos, so it is not supposed to invade privacy. Instead, it only looks for signs like the driver’s eyes closing or looking away for too long. Car makers can choose where to put the camera, but it must work even if the driver is wearing glasses or a hat. The rule comes from the EU’s General Safety Regulation, which also adds other safety systems, like automatic emergency braking and lane keeping.

Some people are worried about privacy. They ask if these cameras could be hacked or if companies might change the rules later to keep more data. Others worry about the cost of adding these cameras to every new car. Car companies say they will follow the rules, and some already have similar systems in place.

On Hacker News, some commenters think the rule makes sense, since most accidents happen because drivers are not focused. They like that the system does not save video. Others are not sure if the technology works well enough, especially in different lighting or with people who fidget a lot. Some fear this is just the start, and the cameras might later be used for other things, like tracking drivers or showing ads. A few people point out that some modern cars already have cameras for safety, so this is not a big change. Others say that good drivers might find the alarms annoying or distracting. There are also concerns about who gets to decide what counts as “distracted”—a machine or a human. Some comment that rules like this show how fast governments can use tech to watch ordinary life. Finally, a few suggest that, overall, making roads safer is more important than these worries.

## Chat Control 1.0 and 2.0 Explained

- 原文链接: [Chat Control 1.0 and 2.0 Explained](https://fightchatcontrol.eu/chat-control-overview)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48818311)

This article explains how the EU tried to make tech companies scan private messages for child abuse material. First, a temporary law called Chat Control 1.0 allowed companies to scan messages if they wanted, but this was set to end in 2024. Since a permanent law (Chat Control 2.0) could not be agreed on, the temporary rule was extended a few times. Each time, there were big debates in the EU Parliament and Council. Some wanted to keep scanning all messages, while others wanted strong limits—like only scanning if a judge suspects someone, and never forcing companies to break end-to-end encryption. In 2026, Parliament finally rejected another extension. The law expired, but big companies said they would keep scanning messages anyway. The EU Council then tried an unusual move to bring back the expired law as a new, almost identical regulation. They wanted to rush this through Parliament with an urgent vote, making it very hard to stop.

For the permanent law (Chat Control 2.0), the EU Commission wanted to force all platforms to scan for abuse, even bypassing encryption. Parliament did not agree and wanted to protect privacy, only allowing targeted searches with a judge’s permission. Germany and other countries pushed back against general scanning. Even the Council’s own lawyers warned that scanning everyone’s messages, without cause, breaks EU privacy laws. Talks kept failing because no side would give in on the key issues, especially encryption and suspicionless scanning.

In the Hacker News comments, some people strongly oppose the idea of scanning private messages, saying it is a huge privacy risk and could lead to government overreach. Others point out that stopping child abuse is very important, but worry that such laws set a dangerous example for other countries and could be misused. A few commenters argue that big tech companies already scan messages, so these laws only make it official. Some are surprised that the EU Council is trying to bring back a law after Parliament rejected it. Others say that once scanning is allowed for one reason, it is easy for governments to expand it to other crimes. There are also worries about the technical risks of breaking encryption, and that real criminals will just use other tools. Some people hope that public debate and legal challenges will slow these laws. Finally, a few users think that the general public does not understand how much privacy is at risk.

## Show HN: Davit, a Apple Containers UI

- 原文链接: [Show HN: Davit, a Apple Containers UI](https://davit.app)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48821848)

Davit is a new macOS app that helps you run Linux containers on Apple silicon Macs without needing Docker Desktop. It’s free, open source, and made to work well with Apple’s own container system.

This app talks directly to Apple’s container daemon, using the same method as the command line, so it doesn’t use extra background tools or web technologies like Electron. You can see all your services, disk use, and live CPU stats in one dashboard. Each container row shows live CPU, memory, IP info, and gives you options to start, stop, restart, or delete. You can follow logs live, see charts, and check detailed container settings.

Davit lets you open a shell in any running container, straight into Terminal or iTerm, without needing to type commands in the CLI. If you need to change a container, Davit helps you create a new one with most settings copied over, so you can quickly adjust things like ports or environment variables. You can pull images with live progress, make or remove volumes, manage networks, and see what’s being used before deleting.

You can also edit platform settings, like CPU or memory defaults, and Davit uses Apple’s own config checker to help you avoid mistakes. If you don’t already have Apple’s container platform, Davit can install and set it up for you, without needing admin rights. The app is small (about 17 MB), built in SwiftUI, and offers menu bar actions and live charts right in the app.

Unlike Docker Desktop, Davit uses Apple’s container engine. Each container runs in its own lightweight virtual machine, starts quickly, and has its own IP. It supports images from Docker Hub and other registries, with no extra accounts or license agreements needed. The app is also much lighter and simpler.

Hacker News users had mixed reactions. Some were excited to have a native, lightweight alternative to Docker Desktop, especially for Apple silicon Macs. They liked the open source license and small app size. A few users pointed out that Apple’s container engine is new, so it may not support every feature or use case yet.

Others shared worries about compatibility, especially for people using advanced features from Docker. Some mentioned that the requirement for macOS 15 and Apple silicon limits who can use Davit right now. People also wondered how stable Apple’s container system is, and whether it will get more updates.

One commenter said they liked that Davit doesn’t need admin rights or background daemons, which makes it safer and easier to try. Another user appreciated that the app avoids Electron and web views, making it feel fast and more “Mac-like.” Some users were unsure if Davit is ready for serious work yet, but many were hopeful and said they would watch its progress. A few asked about plans for supporting older Macs or more features. Overall, the community liked the idea and the clean approach, but some want to see how it grows before switching from Docker.

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48756578)

The article explains that Herdr is not a traditional app, but a terminal multiplexer—like tmux or Zellij—but it understands and manages coding agents. You can split your terminal into panes, create tabs, and keep agents running even when you disconnect. Herdr works on Linux, macOS, and Windows (in beta), and you don’t need to make an account or worry about telemetry. It stays fast and simple, runs in your normal terminal, and doesn’t use Electron or web dashboards.

With Herdr, you can connect to coding sessions from anywhere, even your phone, by using SSH. For example, you can start Herdr on a server, run several coding jobs, and later rejoin from another device. It also has a mobile-friendly layout for small screens. The tool keeps track of your agents’ states—like blocked, working, or done—so you can see what’s happening at a glance. You can use the command line or a JSON API to control sessions, split panes, run commands, and check on agents.

Herdr supports many kinds of coding agents, such as Claude Code, Copilot, or your own scripts. Any terminal-based agent should work, and there are plugins for deeper integration. The marketplace lets users share plugins that add features, like notifications or custom layouts. Herdr is popular with engineers at big tech companies, but it’s meant for individuals, not company-wide deployments.

Compared to other tools, Herdr combines the persistence of tmux with agent management and works everywhere your terminal does. It’s not a replacement for your terminal emulator or a browser dashboard; it’s a layer that helps you manage coding agents from anywhere.

## l: A new runtime for k and q

- 原文链接: [l: A new runtime for k and q](https://lv1.sh/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48821378)

The article talks about “l,” a new runtime for the k and q programming languages, which are popular for high-speed data work, especially in finance. “l” lets you run old k4, q, and qSQL code without changes, but makes it faster by using compression and automatic parallel processing.

The k and q languages are known for array programming, meaning you work with whole arrays at once for speed and simplicity. “l” keeps the same language but changes how things run under the hood. The big idea is that data stays compressed all the time, even when in memory or moving between machines. This means less data has to move around, making everything run faster. You don’t have to rewrite your code; “l” is fully compatible with all the usual k and q features, like tables and partitions.

“l” uses modern hardware features automatically. It can pick between using single-core, multiple cores, or even the GPU (graphics chip) for different jobs, depending on what’s fastest. For example, commands like “sum x” will run as fast as possible, using SIMD (doing many things in one go) or threads (using many cores), without you needing to think about it.

Columnar tables are optimized, so database queries stay fast, even when working with huge data sets (like 86 million rows). Compression always happens, so there’s no waiting for data to “unpack.” The runtime is smart—it chooses when to use different hardware tricks, like NEON on Apple or AVX-512 on Intel chips.

There’s also a new mailing list for people interested in using or building on “l,” set up like an old-school plain-text email group.

People in the Hacker News comments are excited about a fresh take on the k/q ecosystem. Some users love that “l” runs old code without changes and uses hardware better, making it easier to get more speed. Others point out that keeping everything compressed is a big win for both speed and memory use, especially for big finance data.

A few commenters ask how “l” compares to kdb+ (the main commercial k/q database), especially for very large or live-data systems. Some are curious about real-world benchmarks and how “l” will handle edge cases, like updates or mixed workloads. There are questions about open-source plans, pricing, and how much support there will be for new hardware types, like GPUs and NPUs.

Some developers are happy about the mailing list for technical discussion, saying it feels like the early days of programming communities. Others share concerns about learning curve, since k and q are already niche languages with steep syntax. Finally, several people wonder if “l” can help bring array programming ideas to more developers, or if it will stay a tool mostly for finance pros.

## AI Meets Cryptography 1: What AI Found in Cloudflare's Circl

- 原文链接: [AI Meets Cryptography 1: What AI Found in Cloudflare's Circl](https://blog.zksecurity.xyz/posts/circl-bugs/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48821749)

This article talks about using AI to find bugs in Cloudflare's open-source cryptography library, CIRCL. The authors used their AI tool, called zkao, to scan the code and found seven real bugs, all of which are now fixed.

The team at zkSecurity is building zkao to act as an AI security auditor. Their goal is for zkao to keep looking at code until no more bugs can be found by AI. They tested zkao and other large language models (LLMs) on CIRCL, which is a cryptography library that includes advanced and post-quantum cryptography features. The AI found several bugs, but humans on the team checked the findings to make sure they were real and serious.

Here are some of the most interesting bugs:
- One bug happened because the code used floating-point math (float64) for secret sharing, which can lose precision and make key shares incorrect. The fix was to use big integers instead.
- Another bug let attackers control a security parameter in a proof, making it much easier to fake proofs. The fix was to make the verifier set the parameter, not the attacker.
- There was a problem with BLS signature aggregation: the code did not check if messages were unique, which made it possible for attackers to use a well-known rogue key attack.
- A tricky bug involved the way negative numbers were handled in a proof: two different numbers could lead to the same hash, making it possible to trick the system. The fix was to check that numbers are always positive.
- In one case, a switch statement in Go was written in a way that skipped important checks, letting empty secrets pass. Changing the code to use a comma instead of a bitwise OR fixed the bug.
- Another bug was about integer overflow and truncation when combining secret shares; using big integers and changing the calculation order fixed it.
- The most serious bug broke access control in a policy-based encryption feature; a mistake in sharing the secret let anyone with one attribute read messages, instead of needing all required attributes.

The article points out that AI is not very good at judging how serious a bug is. Sometimes the AI thinks a bug is very dangerous when it’s not, and sometimes it misses critical bugs. Humans are still needed to decide how important each problem is. The severity of bugs also depends on how the code is used, which the AI cannot always know.

In the Hacker News comments, some people were impressed that AI found deep and subtle bugs in cryptography code. They liked that all findings were checked by humans before reporting. Others worried that AI might generate too many false alarms, making it hard for experts to focus on real problems. Some developers said that using AI to audit code could help catch mistakes that regular reviews might miss, especially in complex math and cryptography. A few commenters pointed out that these bugs show why cryptography code should be reviewed by experts, not just tested with tools.

There was also discussion about how AI models are getting better, but they still need guidance and final checks from humans. Some people asked if this kind of AI auditing could be used on other security-critical projects, not just cryptography. Others shared stories of similar bugs found in other projects and wondered if AI would eventually be able to explain the impact of bugs better. Overall, the community saw this as a positive example of AI helping to improve open source security, as long as humans stay involved in the process.

This website lists 27 important machine learning papers, said to be from a list Ilya Sutskever gave to John Carmack. The goal is to help beginners understand key ideas in ML by linking to papers and clear explanations.

The list starts with basics, like CS231n, which teaches how convolutional neural networks (CNNs) work for image tasks. Speech recognition is covered by Deep Speech 2, which worked in both English and Mandarin. 

The collection introduces important ideas like attention, with the “Attention Is All You Need” paper that launched the Transformer architecture, used in most modern language models. For learning, there are resources showing how to build models step by step, like the “Annotated Transformer.” There are also ideas on how to process sets, create Pointer Networks, and use neural networks with external memory (Neural Turing Machines). 

The list covers special topics too, like graph neural networks for chemistry, scaling laws for language models, and how to train very large models using pipeline parallelism. It goes into theory, discussing the Minimum Description Length principle, Kolmogorov Complexity, and why some models generalize better than others. There are papers and essays on how complexity changes in closed systems, using simple models like mixing coffee and cream to explain these ideas. Finally, there is a thesis about measuring machine intelligence.
