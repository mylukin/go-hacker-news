First, let’s talk about a new rule in the EU called “Chat Control.” This rule would let governments scan all private messages and photos, even those that are encrypted. The goal is to stop child abuse, but it means everyone’s digital chats and images could be checked, even if there is no reason to suspect them. The article says this is a big problem for privacy. Every message, photo, and file might be scanned, and there is no way to opt out. Computer scanners can also make mistakes, sometimes flagging harmless vacation photos or jokes as illegal, which could lead to innocent people being wrongly accused.

The article also questions if the rule will actually help protect children. Experts, including the United Nations, say mass scanning often misses real abuse and could even make things worse by making messages less secure. Another worry is that if the EU starts scanning, other countries with less freedom might copy this idea, leading to more spying around the world. Most EU countries support the rule, but some, like the Czech Republic, Italy, Netherlands, and Poland, are against it. The article shows which countries and politicians support or oppose the rule, and asks people to contact their representatives.

Next, we look at ARC-AGI-3, a new benchmark for testing if AI can think and learn like humans. It gives AI agents tasks inside games where they must explore, set goals, learn from mistakes, and change plans—just like people do. The tasks are not simple puzzles; the AI must figure out what to do from its own experience. A perfect score means the AI can solve every task as fast as a human. The benchmark looks for quick learning, good planning, and the ability to adapt to new information. Humans can play the games easily, and AIs cannot cheat by using hidden knowledge. You can watch replays to see how each agent works. The platform is open, with tools for developers and clear guides.

In the comments, some people are excited about this new way to test AI, saying it better measures if AI can really learn like humans. Others point out that most AI today is good at set problems, but not at adapting, so this test is harder. Some worry the tasks may still be too easy or miss some types of intelligence. Others ask about scoring and fairness, since humans and AIs learn at different speeds. Some suggest adding harder tasks or using the system for teaching. Many like the idea of measuring real learning, but agree there is still a long way to go before AI can match humans.

Now let’s talk about farming science. There’s a new study from the UK on how overplowing, or tilling, changes how soil holds water. Scientists used earthquake sensors and fiber optic cables to measure how soil moved and how sound traveled through it. They looked at different farm rows: some never tilled, some tilled a little, and some a lot. They also tested how tractors pressing down on the soil made a difference. The main finding is that tilling and heavy tractor use break the small channels in the soil that help it soak up water. This makes the ground less like a sponge and causes water to sit on top, creating mud and crusty soil. This can lead to more erosion and flooding. The method, called distributed acoustic sensing, is cheap and gives lots of data quickly. It helps track soil moisture and could help farmers know when fields are at risk of flooding.

In the comments, many readers agree that overplowing is bad for soil health. People liked using earthquake tools for farm science. Some farmers shared success with no-till farming, saying they see less erosion and better water use. Some were surprised that tilling, which is supposed to help plants, can actually hurt the soil over time. Others asked how easy this technology is for normal farmers and if it can be used on big farms or in other countries. Some noted that changing old farming habits is slow, but new tools and lower costs might help. There was also talk about soil health and its link to climate change and food security. Overall, commenters liked the research and hoped it will help push for better farming.

Next, a story from the Supreme Court. The court decided that Cox Communications, an internet provider, is not responsible for users who pirate music on its network. The case started when music labels sued Cox for not cutting off repeat offenders. The main question was if Cox should pay big fines just because it knew some customers were breaking copyright laws, but did not stop their internet. The court said no: simply giving internet access is not enough to make Cox guilty. They said a provider is only responsible if it wanted people to break the law or pushed them to do so.

In the comments, many people support the decision, saying it is not fair to blame internet companies for what users do—just like you do not blame the phone company for bad calls. Some say if Cox lost, it would create problems for all internet providers and other services. Others think copyright owners should find better ways to stop piracy. Some worry the ruling might make it too easy for piracy to continue, while others say enforcement should be the job of courts, not private companies. A few say this decision protects internet freedom. Some wonder if music labels will find new ways to protect their work. There is also some joking about how copyright cases keep coming back.

Now, let’s talk about quantization, a way to make large language models smaller and faster. The article explains that even huge models with billions of parameters can be compressed to a quarter of their size with only a small drop in quality. The trick is to use smaller numbers—like 16-bit or 8-bit floats, or even integers—instead of 32-bit floats. There are different ways to quantize, like symmetric and asymmetric methods. Quantization is usually done in small blocks to avoid problems. Tests show that 8-bit quantization gives almost the same results as the original, and 4-bit is still good for many tasks. If you go down to 2-bit, the model becomes much worse. Quantized models also run faster. The article explains how to measure the quality of quantized models.

In the comments, some are excited about running strong models on normal computers. Others warn that quantization can sometimes cause errors, especially for tasks needing high accuracy. Some point out that there are other ways to make models smaller, like pruning and distillation. Developers share tips on which settings work best for different hardware. There are questions about why 2-bit models are sometimes slower than 4-bit, and some suggest reasons like hardware or software limits. Some say quantization-aware training gives better results, but is more complex. Most agree quantization is a great tool, as long as you check that the quality is good enough.

The team believes LLMs are too important to be controlled by big companies, and want to give everyone privacy and control. Local LLMs have not been as strong as cloud models, but the gap is getting smaller. Ensu is a chat app that works like ChatGPT, but keeps everything on your device. It is free, open source, and runs on many platforms. It is not as powerful as ChatGPT yet, but can already help with personal thoughts, book talks, and offline chatting.
