First, we talk about fast16, a cyberweapon hidden for more than 20 years. Fast16 was made in 2005, five years before the famous Stuxnet worm. Unlike Stuxnet, which broke machines, fast16 quietly changed math results in scientific and engineering software. It made outputs look normal, but they were actually wrong. Researchers found fast16 in a Windows file called svcmgmt.exe. Inside was a Lua scripting engine, rare for malware back then, and a driver called fast16.sys. Fast16 only worked on old single-core computers and had three parts: a carrier to spread it, a worm for moving through weak networks, and a driver that changed floating-point math in certain programs. It targeted software for nuclear simulations, building safety, and water modeling, including LS-DYNA, PKPM, and MOHID.

Fast16 avoided computers with antivirus and spread secretly. The driver waited for a login before corrupting math, and only changed results in programs built with the Intel C++ compiler. It never touched original files, so mistakes were hard to spot. The article links fast16 to the NSA’s Equation Group, based on leaked files and old Unix version marks in the code. For 21 years, almost no antivirus detected fast16, and it sat in public malware databases until researchers found its real use.

The article warns anyone using old engineering software to check results on a clean computer. Hacker News commenters were shocked at how long fast16 stayed hidden, and many worried there could be more malware like it. Some discussed how targeting math is so sneaky, because wrong results are hard to catch in complex simulations. Many pointed at state actors like the NSA, but some said the evidence is not 100% clear. Commenters praised the technical skill behind fast16, especially its use of Lua and its “cluster munition” design, with different payloads. Some wondered if fast16 ever caused real damage, or if it was mostly for spying. Others warned that old tricks like this could be used again, and questioned why antivirus software missed it for so long. There was also debate about the ethics of government cyberweapons. The main lesson: always double-check important calculations, and never fully trust one system for sensitive work.

He beat the old record by over a minute. Before this, Eliud Kipchoge ran under two hours in 2019, but that race did not count for records. Yomif Kejelcha and Jacob Kiplimo also finished under two hours, with Kiplimo coming third. In the women’s race, Tigst Assefa broke her own world record in 2:15:41. Sawe had tried to break the record before, but was stopped by hot weather. This time, the conditions were perfect, and he finished strong. He thanked the London crowd for their support. Many experts called this a huge moment for running, saying it changes the standard for what is possible.

Hacker News commenters were amazed and called it a historic day for sports. Some talked about the special shoes used and the role of technology, while others discussed new training and nutrition. There were worries about doping, but many noted Sawe is often tested. Some were sad that previous record holder Kelvin Kiptum was not there to race Sawe, but most people enjoyed the special moment. Turning to nature, butterflies in North America are disappearing, with Western Monarchs in real trouble. In the 1980s, millions of Monarchs gathered in California, but now there are only thousands. Loss of habitat, less milkweed, pesticides, and climate change are the main reasons. Monarchs migrate long distances, but many do not survive the trip anymore. Some groups plant milkweed and make gardens to help, and scientists use tags and community projects to track their journeys.

In the comments, people were sad and worried about Monarchs. Many shared stories about seeing fewer butterflies and blamed pesticides, big farms, and climate change. Some suggested planting native plants and avoiding chemicals. One warned that using the wrong kind of milkweed can hurt Monarchs. While some wonder if it’s too late, most still want to try to save them.

Now, a story about learning Japanese by playing Magic: The Gathering. The writer had passed the N2 exam, but wanted to speak more naturally. By playing Magic only with Japanese cards, he learned real words and phrases from native speakers. He chose simple decks, practiced card names and rules, and studied translations before games. At events, he listened to locals and copied their phrases. Over time, speaking Japanese became automatic, even under pressure.

Magic helped not just with game words, but real conversation—from chatting to handling disagreements. The skills for explaining game moves were useful at work as a project manager too. His advice: pick a hobby you love and use it to practice, because real-life use makes learning fun.

Commenters liked this approach and shared their own stories about learning languages through hobbies. Some said games make practice feel natural, not forced. Others said Magic’s special words are hard, but learning them helps memory and confidence. Some warned not everyone has a deep hobby or a big local group, so pick what is fun and easy to join. Many agreed that real progress comes from using a language in real life, not just in school.

Next, OpenAI says their coding benchmark, SWE-bench Verified, is no longer good for measuring code-writing AI. The test is “contaminated”—AI models have seen the test problems and answers during training, so their scores are not reliable. Many test cases are flawed, and most come from public open-source projects, so models may just “remember” the answers. OpenAI found that models can give exact answers just from a problem’s ID or a hint. This makes it hard to know if models are really smarter, or just memorizing.

OpenAI has stopped reporting scores for SWE-bench Verified and now recommends SWE-bench Pro, which is less contaminated. They suggest future benchmarks use private, original problems and be reviewed by humans. Commenters had many views: some agreed contamination is a big problem, others said OpenAI should have seen this coming. Some think code-writing AI is already close to the test’s limits, so new tests are needed. Others said that automated tests are always tricky, and real projects have many correct answers. Some worry that private benchmarks make it harder for outsiders to check progress. Others think “remembering” is not so bad, since real developers often Google for answers. Many hope for open collaboration on better benchmarks.

Now, a creative hardware story: making printed circuit boards from clay instead of plastic and metal. The team wanted a more ethical way to build hardware, using local and natural materials. They reused chips from broken Arduino boards, shaped clay into hexagons, and used a 3D-printed stamp for the circuit pattern. They used porcelain for strength and insulation, fired the boards in a wood fire, and used special silver paint for the circuits. After firing, they cooled the boards and tested them.

Some commenters liked the creative and ethical approach, though others pointed out that silver and gold are expensive and not always ethical. Some doubted the reliability of clay PCBs for real electronics, but liked the open-source and artistic aspects. There was debate about practicality for large projects, but many agreed it is a fun and inspiring idea for small, ethical hardware experiments.

Next, we look at the code for Zork 1, a classic interactive fiction game from the early 1980s. The “VERBS” file shows how the game understands player commands, from “look” and “take” to “attack” and “eat.” The code handles light and darkness, inventory, puzzles, and funny or impossible actions, giving playful or sarcastic replies. Zork tracks your score, moves, and has routines for saving, restoring, or quitting.

Commenters were impressed by the personality and cleverness of the code. Some learned programming from Zork or were inspired to make their own games. Many agreed that Zork’s code is still interesting and can teach us about making interactive and memorable games.
