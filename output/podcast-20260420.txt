He also grew Apple’s services business to over $100 billion per year and made Apple one of the world’s largest companies, growing its value from $350 billion to $4 trillion. Cook pushed Apple to be stronger in new markets, focused on privacy, accessibility, and a lower carbon footprint, and led the company to build more of its own chips and hardware.

John Ternus joined Apple in 2001. He has been important in designing key products, like iPad, AirPods, and various Mac computers. He helped launch devices such as the MacBook Neo and the iPhone 17 series, and focused on making products more durable, easier to repair, and more eco-friendly by using recycled materials and new production methods. Ternus is seen as someone who knows Apple well and has strong engineering skills.

Arthur Levinson, Apple’s board leader for 15 years, will now be the lead independent director. Both Ternus and Cook thanked each other and said they will work closely during this transition.

Next, Kimi has released a tool called Kimi Vendor Verifier, or KVV, to help check if AI models run correctly on different systems. This matters because open-source models can act differently depending on where and how they are used.

After making their model open-source, Kimi saw users get strange results, often from wrong settings or technical mistakes. They tried to fix some problems by making their API stricter, but many issues still happened when others used third-party systems or ran the model themselves.

To fix this, Kimi made KVV—a set of benchmarks to check if a provider’s system runs the model correctly. There are six main tests, checking things like images, long answers, and coding tasks, and looking for common errors like wrong settings or memory bugs. KVV helps providers test their systems before letting users try the model. Kimi plans to make the results public, so everyone can see which vendors run the models well, and works with open-source communities to solve deeper problems. KVV tests can take time to run, but Kimi made scripts to help speed up the process. They also invite more vendors to join and help improve the tool.

On Hacker News, some people are happy that Kimi is focusing on trust and quality in open-source AI, saying it is easy to blame a model for mistakes when the real problem is often with the setup. Others like that Kimi is making the tests public. Some users share stories about getting weird results from AI models and not knowing if it was a model or setup problem. A few worry that running these checks could be costly for small companies, or that public results could lead vendors to “game” the tests. Others suggest more benchmarks and easier tests for smaller vendors. Some say this tool could help with many AI models, not just Kimi’s. There are also questions about whether real users will care about these scores, or just pick the cheapest provider. Most agree that having a way to check if AI models are running as they should is a good step for the community.

Another interesting project shows a real transformer AI model, like a tiny version of ChatGPT, running on a 1 MHz Commodore 64. The model has about 25,000 parameters and uses real transformer parts: multi-head attention, softmax, and RMS normalization, all in 6502 assembly code.

The model is small—2 layers, 4 attention heads, 32-dimensional embeddings, a 128-token vocabulary, and a 20-token context window. All parameters are stored as 8-bit integers, and the code fits on a floppy disk. It takes about 60 seconds to generate one word, so a full answer takes minutes. Training is done on your own text data with Python and PyTorch, then exported to the C64. The project shares all code, training scripts, and test suites. The C64 version is slow and not very smart, but proves that transformer models can run on old hardware with tight memory and no math coprocessor. The repo explains how quantization-aware tricks and integer softmax make it work with low-precision math.

You can use the project in an emulator or on real hardware, with full build instructions. Most code is in Python, but the core model is hand-written assembly.

On Hacker News, people are amazed this is possible on a Commodore 64. Some call it a fun demo, even if not useful for real tasks. Others say running a transformer on a 64KB, 8-bit machine is a great technical challenge. Some ask about practical uses, but most feel it’s more for learning and showing what’s possible. Some remember simpler C64 programs and are impressed by how much ML code fits in such a small space. There are jokes about the long wait for each token. A few discuss ways to make it faster, and some want to try it on other old computers. Many agree it’s a good way to understand AI by seeing it in its simplest form, and a great mix of old tech with new ideas.

Now to a topic about fake GitHub stars—how people buy stars to make their projects look popular and trick investors. Researchers studied 20 repositories and found many stars are not real, with some projects having up to 76% fake stargazers.

A study from Carnegie Mellon found 6 million fake stars across thousands of projects, mostly on AI or blockchain projects. People can buy stars for as little as three cents each on many websites, or order them on Fiverr and Telegram. Some sellers offer “aged” GitHub accounts or “non-drop” stars that try to avoid detection.

You can spot fake stars because real projects have active stargazers, while fake ones have empty profiles, no followers, and few repos. The fork-to-star ratio also helps: real projects have many forks compared to stars, while fake ones have few forks. Investors often use star counts to find new startups, so some startups buy stars to get noticed, creating a loop where fake stars lead to more investment.

Fake metrics are also a problem on npm, VS Code extensions, and social media. The law is catching up: the FTC now bans buying fake influence metrics, with big fines. GitHub says it bans fake stars and accounts, but enforcement is slow. The article suggests looking at monthly contributors or fork-to-star ratios for a better measure of popularity.

On Hacker News, many agree that GitHub stars are not a good way to measure real value. Some have seen suspicious star growth on projects that do not seem useful. Others worry about investors being misled. Some developers say they have felt pressure to boost star counts instead of doing real work. The fork-to-star ratio is seen as a helpful tool, but not perfect—some projects get starred a lot but rarely forked. People want GitHub to do more to stop fake stars, maybe by making detection public or using better algorithms. There are concerns about legal risks, and some worry that the “fake it till you make it” idea is still common. Many say to look for active issues, discussions, and contributors before trusting star counts.

Next, a new tool called Lucebox helps large language models run faster and more efficiently on regular GPUs, like the RTX 3090. The team rewrote the LLM inference code by hand, making it work very well on specific hardware instead of using general-purpose tools.

The first project is a “megakernel” for the Qwen3.5-0.8B model, running all 24 layers in a single CUDA operation, with much higher speed and better energy use than tools like llama.cpp or PyTorch. The megakernel gets 413 tokens per second and nearly 2 tokens per joule, while others are much slower. The second project is DFlash for the larger Qwen3.5-27B model, using a new way called speculative decoding, plus a tree-checking system, to reach up to 207 tokens per second—3.5 times faster than normal methods. It can fit a huge 128,000-token context into one RTX 3090.

Lucebox is open source and helps people run strong AI models at home or in small offices, without cloud services or privacy worries. The team explains that making special code for each chip used to be too hard, but now, with AI and better tools, deep optimizations are possible and can be shared.

In the comments, users are impressed by the high speed, saying 207 tokens per second for a 27B model is amazing. Others ask about installation, support for other GPUs, and if it works on the 4090 or older cards. Many agree that hand-tuned code unlocks more power but needs careful work. Some worry about compatibility with other models or future updates, but are happy to see open source code and clear benchmarks. There are questions about memory needs and adapting the code to smaller cards. The community is excited and hopes for more projects like this for different chips and model types.

Another new tool is ggsql, which lets you make data plots using SQL commands. It brings the “grammar of graphics” to SQL, so SQL users can create structured charts without R or Python.

You write plots with commands like VISUALIZE and DRAW, mapping data columns to axes or colors, and picking the plot type. The syntax is similar to SQL, so it feels natural for SQL users. You can add layers—like regression lines or labels—step by step. ggsql lets you write both the data query and the plot in the same place, and works with big datasets by only fetching needed data. It is designed to be easy for LLMs to generate code for, and simple to add to other tools.

The team behind ggsql says they created it to help SQL users make good plots without moving data to other tools. They think SQL and the grammar of graphics match well, and promise more features in the future, like interactivity and maps, while keeping support for ggplot2.

On Hacker News, many people like the idea and think it will help analysts who work mostly in SQL. Some say the syntax is wordy but clear. Others wonder if it will be as powerful as R or Python, and are excited about using ggsql with big databases. There is discussion about using ggsql with AI tools, making it easier to generate plots with natural language. Some ask about limits—like how customizable the plots will be, and if it will support all chart types. People are happy ggsql does not need R or Python and hope it will connect with their favorite SQL databases and tools. Some ask if ggsql will inspire updates in ggplot2, and are glad both will keep getting support.

Switching to defense news, the F-35 fighter jet is a great, high-tech plane, but the article says it was built for the wrong kind of war. The F-35 has cost over two trillion dollars, but modern wars use cheap, simple drones and missiles in big numbers, like in Ukraine and the Red Sea. Drones are easy to replace if lost, but the F-35 is expensive, slow to build, and needs lots of support and maintenance. In a long war, the U.S. could lose F-35s faster than it can build new ones.

There are two main problems: U.S. air bases are within range of enemy missiles, so planes could be destroyed on the ground, and the F-35 needs a lot of parts and support to keep flying. Spreading out planes to many bases is hard, since it makes support more difficult. The article suggests using more drones, which are cheaper, can be built quickly, and need less support. Drones can use smaller bases and are easier to replace, but carry less and have fewer sensors than the F-35. Still, a mix of fewer F-35s and more drones could be better for future wars.

On Hacker News, some agree the F-35 is too expensive for modern wars and point to Ukraine as proof. Others say the F-35 is still needed for special missions. Some argue the military is slow to change because of politics and big companies. There is talk about how hard it is to fix and build new F-35s during a war. Some say the F-35 should play a smaller role, but not be dropped. Most agree that wars are changing, and the U.S. must think about cost, speed, and being able to replace lost gear quickly.
