Now let’s look at Adrafinil, a new macOS app that keeps your Mac awake with the lid closed, but only when AI coding agents are working. Unlike always-on tools like Caffeinate or Amphetamine, Adrafinil only acts when an AI agent is busy and lets your Mac sleep as soon as the job finishes. The app sits in your menu bar and works with tools like Claude Code, Codex, and Cursor. When these agents start a task, Adrafinil stops your Mac from sleeping—even with the lid closed—and lets it sleep again when the work ends. There are features for safety, like checking for overheating, and a small helper that runs as root but only when needed. The app can be used from the command line, is easy to uninstall, and can be downloaded or built from source.

Hacker News users are excited. Many say it solves a real problem for anyone running long AI jobs or builds on a MacBook, and like that it is less wasteful than always-on tools. The open-source, security-focused approach is also praised. Some mention using Caffeinate or Amphetamine, but dislike how those keep the Mac awake all the time. There’s discussion about the technical challenges of controlling clamshell sleep and the need for root privileges, but users like that Adrafinil’s privileged part is small and easy to check. Some wonder about compatibility with older Macs or different macOS versions. Others ask if it could work with background tasks beyond AI, and share their own tricks for running jobs with the lid closed. Most see the value in a tool that is smart about when to keep the Mac awake, and like the careful design.

Next, we have an article about AI being used to design radio-frequency integrated circuits, or RFICs. These chips are key to wireless tech like 5G and satellites, and designing them is very hard—often called a “dark art.” Normally, it takes years and costs millions, as engineers balance complex rules from physics and electronics. The Princeton team used AI with reinforcement learning and inverse design, letting the system try new layouts and learn what works best. The AI can now create new designs that work better than old, human-made ones. One 5G amplifier chip looked like a QR code, but had record efficiency and bandwidth. The team also used diffusion models, like those for AI art, to control how “human” the designs look, helping engineers debug.

The article says that for AI to get even better, big, shared data sets are needed, but most chip design data is private. Some government programs tried to open this up, but were closed. In the comments, many are amazed that AI can now handle such specialized work. Some say this could make wireless tech faster and cheaper, while others worry about understanding or fixing AI-designed circuits. There’s debate about data sharing, and if this approach could work for analog chips or other hard problems. Some warn about AI mistakes slipping through, so human oversight is still important. Most see this as a big step, but think there’s still a long way to go.

Next, DSpark is a new method to make large language models like ChatGPT run faster. The idea is called “speculative decoding.” Instead of predicting one word at a time, a smaller model first guesses a group of possible words, and the big model checks those guesses. If the guesses are right, the model can skip ahead, saving time and computer power. If not, it goes back and fixes the mistakes. The paper says DSpark can make models two to four times faster, with similar accuracy, and uses less power. DSpark is open-source and has good results on popular models.

In the comments, people are excited about making large models faster and cheaper. Some ask if it lowers answer quality, but the authors say accuracy stays high. A few say the idea is not new, comparing it to “draft-and-verify” or “speculative sampling.” Some want to know how easy DSpark is to use, and one person worries about using a small model for guessing. Others think future models will use tricks like this as standard. Many thank the authors for open-sourcing the work, and some ask for more real-world tests. Overall, most see DSpark as a useful step forward.

Now, let’s talk about the Fintech Engineering Handbook, a guide for building money systems. It gives three key rules: never make up data, never lose any data, and never trust anyone or anything fully. The book explains how to safely represent money in code, warning against floating-point numbers due to rounding errors, and says to use integers or special types. It covers double-entry bookkeeping, where every money move is tracked, and says all transactions should have clear timestamps and audit trails. Event sourcing is recommended for auditability, with changes fixed by corrections, not by deleting records. The system must survive crashes and be idempotent—steps can run twice without bad effects.

The guide warns about trusting outside systems, like payment providers. Always double-check their data, save all requests and responses, and expect failures or missing messages. Webhooks should be treated as hints, not facts, and reconciliation with outside data should be regular. Permissions and approval controls should be strong, and all changes must be tracked. Testing should focus on core rules and survival of retries and crashes.

In the comments, people liked how practical the handbook is, covering real-world issues like rounding and audit trails. Some shared stories of bugs from using floats or missing idempotency. There’s debate over the complexity of event sourcing, but others say it’s worth it. Some talk about the problems with webhooks and outside surprises. A few wish for more code examples or tips for scaling. Many say the advice on permissions and audits is useful even outside fintech. The handbook’s appendix on finance terms is praised, and most agree it’s a valuable resource for both new and experienced engineers.

Finally, we have an article about “suspicious discontinuities”—sharp cut-off points in rules or systems that cause strange or unfair results. For example, in the US, going one dollar over an income limit can make you lose thousands in health insurance subsidies. The article gives many other examples: tax and welfare cut-offs, college grant thresholds, drug charge limits, computer queues dropping items suddenly, p-values just below the magic 0.05, sports team age limits, and more. These sharp rules can make people change their behavior or try to game the system.

In the comments, many agree these discontinuities are real problems, especially in taxes and social programs. Some share stories of making strange choices because of cut-offs. Others say lawmakers don’t always think about the real effects. Some suggest “phase-outs”—making benefits decrease slowly instead of dropping all at once—would be fairer. A few say hard rules are sometimes needed for simplicity or to prevent cheating, but agree the negative effects should be studied. Others discuss similar problems in software and science. There are debates on whether to blame the rules or the people gaming them, but most agree that smoothing out rules can help in many fields.
