## Google releases Gemma 4 open models

- 原文链接: [Google releases Gemma 4 open models](https://deepmind.google/models/gemma/gemma-4/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47616361)

Google has released Gemma 4, a new family of open AI models built from Gemini 3 research. These models are designed to be very smart while also being efficient, so they can run well on different kinds of devices, from small mobile phones to powerful computers.

Gemma 4 comes in several sizes: smaller E2B and E4B models for mobile and IoT devices, and larger 26B and 31B models for personal computers and servers. The small models can run offline on devices like phones, Raspberry Pi, and Jetson Nano, supporting real-time audio and visual processing. The big models are made for coding assistants, agent-based workflows, and advanced reasoning, and they are optimized for consumer GPUs so developers can run them locally.

These models are strong in many areas. They can handle tasks in 140 languages, understand both text and images (multimodal), and support building autonomous agents that can plan and use apps. Developers can fine-tune Gemma 4 for their own needs using popular frameworks. Google says Gemma 4 offers top efficiency and high scores on benchmarks for language, coding, math, and scientific knowledge. The 31B model, for example, beats older models on tasks like multilingual Q&A, competitive coding, and math tests.

Safety is important for Gemma 4. Google says these models go through strong security checks and are safe for enterprise use. The model weights are available for download on sites like Hugging Face, Ollama, Kaggle, and Docker. There are many ways to run, train, and deploy the models, including on Google Cloud, Keras, and Google Kubernetes Engine.

In the Hacker News comments, many users were happy to see Google releasing open models, saying it helps the AI community and makes research better. Some people liked that the small models can run offline and are good for edge devices. Others pointed out that, while these models are open, the license is not fully "open source" in the strictest sense, so there are limits to commercial use. Some developers said they wanted to see even smaller models for weaker devices. A few users compared Gemma 4 to other models like Llama and Mistral, debating which is faster or smarter. Some people were worried about possible "phone home" features or tracking in the downloads, but others thought Google's reputation would help keep things safe. Overall, the community is interested and plans to test Gemma 4 for tasks like coding, research, and running on their own hardware.

## Tailscale's new macOS home

- 原文链接: [Tailscale's new macOS home](https://tailscale.com/blog/macos-notch-escape)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47618189)

Tailscale had a problem: on some newer MacBooks with a screen notch, the Tailscale menu bar icon would disappear behind the notch, making it hard for users to find or use the app. The article explains how this happened because Apple does not let developers control where their icons show in the menu bar, and there is no warning or overflow area when icons get hidden.

Tailscale first tried a quick fix: if the app detected its icon was hidden, it would show a pop-up message warning the user. This helped a bit, but it wasn’t perfect. The warning could appear at the wrong times, like when switching monitors or closing the laptop lid. The real solution was to add a new windowed interface for macOS. Now, starting from version 1.96.2, users can open Tailscale from the Dock or with Spotlight, not just the menu bar. This windowed app has many features: you can search your devices, check their status, copy IPs, send files, pick the best exit node, and see errors with a red dot. There’s even a “mini player” mode and a product tour for new users. The menu bar app still exists, but the windowed app makes Tailscale much easier to use, especially if your menu bar is crowded.

In the comments, some users were surprised that Apple hasn’t fixed the menu bar problem, saying it’s a poor experience for users and developers. Others shared their own struggles with lost menu bar icons, not just with Tailscale. Some developers said they use third-party apps like Bartender to manage crowded menus, but wish Apple would offer a built-in solution. A few people praised Tailscale’s quick warning pop-up, even if it wasn’t perfect, and liked that the team shared code for it. Many users were happy to see the new windowed interface, saying it makes Tailscale easier to use and find. Some wondered if other menu bar apps would do the same. A few commenters wished the menu bar could be customized more, or that Apple would add an overflow menu. Some mentioned they don’t like notches in general, while others said they’ve just gotten used to them. Overall, people liked that Tailscale listened to users and kept improving the app.

## George Goble died recently – known for first dual-CPU-Unix and fast BBQ lighting

- 原文链接: [George Goble died recently – known for first dual-CPU-Unix and fast BBQ lighting](https://www.legacy.com/us/obituaries/wlfi/name/george-goble-obituary?id=61144779)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47618176)

George Goble, known as "GHG," has died at age 73 in West Lafayette, Indiana. He was an early computer expert and helped shape modern computing, especially at Purdue University.

George worked on the first dual-CPU Unix system and created early networking for computers. He helped connect DEC VAX systems and made PNET, an early network. He even shared one of the first live web videos of a solar eclipse. George once checked his email on a beach using a laptop and cell phone, long before smartphones were common. He worked with famous people like Dennis Ritchie, Ken Thompson, and Bill Joy. He also received an award from Purdue for his work.

Besides computers, George worked on better refrigeration and helped invent a way to make hydrogen from water using aluminum. He became a legend after lighting a barbecue grill in three seconds using liquid oxygen, which won him the Ig Nobel Prize and was written about by Dave Barry. George liked simple things, such as eating at Pizza Hut and driving his bright yellow Hummer around town.

He was known for his humor, kindness, and for helping friends. In his last years, he had a close friend and caregiver, Jennifer Isbell, who made sure he was comfortable and happy.

People on Hacker News remember George as a real legend in both computers and engineering. Many shared stories of his clever ideas and fun experiments, especially the fast BBQ lighting. Some talked about how much he helped others in the tech community and how he always answered questions with patience. Others were amazed by how far ahead he was—reading email on a beach years before it was normal. Some comments also thanked him for his work on refrigeration, which helped the environment. A few said they never met him but loved reading about his adventures. One comment pointed out that George’s sense of humor made science fun for everyone. Another user said that George showed what was possible with curiosity and a little bit of risk. Some said the world needs more people like him, while others simply said, “Rest in peace, GHG.” Overall, people felt lucky to have learned from his life and stories.

## Artemis II's toilet is a moon mission milestone

- 原文链接: [Artemis II's toilet is a moon mission milestone](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/artemis-iis-toilet-is-a-moon-mission-milestone/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47609356)

NASA’s Artemis II mission is sending astronauts around the moon, and for the first time, they have a real toilet onboard. Earlier moon missions used plastic bags and tubes, which were messy, hard to use, and made astronauts very unhappy.

The new toilet is called the Universal Waste Management System (UWMS). It took over ten years to design and was made to fix old problems. The UWMS lets astronauts pee and poop at the same time. It works for both men and women. The bathroom even has a door, so the crew gets privacy, which is a big change from before. The toilet is made from 3D-printed titanium, so it is strong but light. It uses handles to help astronauts stay steady in microgravity. Earlier toilets, like those on the International Space Station and the space shuttle, used vacuum hoses to suck away waste. These older systems did not recycle water as well and did not work for all body types. Some even let waste float around the cabin if something went wrong, which was very unpleasant.

The UWMS was first tested on the ISS in 2020 and installed in 2021. Now, a special version is on the Orion capsule for Artemis II. NASA hopes this toilet will work for future moon and Mars trips, too. Project scientists are excited to see how astronauts like the new design after the mission.

In the comments, many people joke about space toilets being a big deal. Some say it is funny, but also important, because a bad toilet can ruin a mission. Others remember the old Apollo stories about floating poop and how hard it was to go to the bathroom in space. Some users point out that normal things, like toilets, become very hard in microgravity. People are happy NASA now includes women’s needs in the design. A few mention that space toilets are more complex than most people think. Others wonder if this tech will help make better toilets on Earth. Some users question the cost, but most agree that comfort and safety for astronauts are worth it. There is also respect for NASA engineers who solved such a tricky problem. Overall, the mood is light, but many agree this is real progress for space travel.

## Good ideas do not need lots of lies in order to gain public acceptance (2008)

- 原文链接: [Good ideas do not need lots of lies in order to gain public acceptance (2008)](https://blog.danieldavies.com/2004/05/d-squared-digest-one-minute-mba.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47617415)

This article is about how good ideas should not need many lies to be accepted by people. The writer uses examples from business and politics, especially the Iraq War, to show this point. He says that when people push an idea with many lies, it is a sign the idea is not good. He learned this lesson in business school during a debate about how companies should handle stock options. Tech companies did not want to call stock options an expense, using many strong reasons. But the teacher said, if stock options were really so great, companies would want to show them off. The fact that companies tried to hide them showed they were not so good. 

He then applies this lesson to the Iraq War. He says that if the war was really a good idea, leaders would not have needed to lie so much about weapons of mass destruction. He states that when someone lies about one thing, you should not trust any of their predictions. He explains that in business, if you do not check if forecasts match reality (an "audit"), bad forecasts become common, and companies make bad decisions. He says the same is true in politics: if you trust people who lied before, you will make bad choices. He ends by saying that people who lie often get a reputation, and then nobody believes them, even when they tell the truth.

In the comments, some readers strongly agree with the main point. They say that if an idea needs tricks and lies to sell it, it is probably a bad idea. Some people share stories from their own work or government where this rule also works. Other readers point out that sometimes, even good ideas need to be "sold" with some exaggeration because people do not like change. A few say that sometimes the public does not listen to honest arguments, so leaders feel forced to use simple or even false messages. Some commenters discuss how hard it is to know when someone is lying or just mistaken. Others focus on the importance of checking facts and doing audits, both in business and government. There are also people who say that sometimes a mix of good and bad reasons are used to push ideas, so it is not always black and white. Finally, a few readers joke about business school lessons, saying they are often just common sense but expensive.

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47616242)

A former Microsoft engineer wrote about big mistakes inside the Azure cloud team. He says these mistakes almost made Microsoft lose OpenAI as a customer and hurt trust with the US government.

The author joined the Azure Core team in 2023. He already had a long history at Microsoft and knew Azure well. On his first day, he joined a team meeting where engineers were talking about moving many Windows features to a small, energy-efficient chip called Overlake. This chip was much less powerful than a normal server chip. The team wanted to move a lot of Windows code to run on this small chip, even though it was made for Linux and had very little memory and power.

The author was shocked. He knew the chip couldn’t handle so many Windows programs. He learned the current system already used too many resources and didn’t scale well. The team had a plan to move forward, but the author believed it would never work. He also found out there were 173 different agents running on Azure nodes. No one at Microsoft could explain what all of them did or why so many were needed.

He worried that all these agents could make Azure fragile. If something went wrong, it could affect important customers like OpenAI or even government systems. The author tried to warn leaders at Microsoft, but he says he was ignored. He hints this was just the start—there were more problems, including wasted efforts, communication failures, and features promised before they were ready.

In the Hacker News comments, many people agreed with the author’s worries. Some said big tech companies often have too much legacy code and too many teams doing the same thing. A few commenters shared their own stories about failed projects or how hard it is to change direction in a large company. Others thought the article made sense, especially about how adding too many agents can make systems fragile.

Some people wondered if the author was being too negative or missing reasons why things were done this way. A few defended Microsoft, saying it’s not easy to run a giant cloud and sometimes strange decisions are needed. Others were surprised by the number of agents and thought it showed a lack of control. There were also jokes about how tech companies love to make things more complex than needed.

Overall, the comments showed both agreement with the author’s concerns and some doubt about whether things are as bad as he says. Many people said they would be more careful if they depended on Azure for important work.

## Lemonade by AMD: a fast and open source local LLM server using GPU and NPU

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47612724)

Lemonade is a new open source server from AMD that lets you run AI models for text, images, and speech on your own computer using your GPU or NPU. It wants to make local AI fast, private, and easy for everyone.

The server is small and lightweight, only 2MB in size, and it works on Windows, Linux, and macOS (beta). You can install it in about a minute with a simple installer. Lemonade can use different AI engines like llama.cpp, Ryzen AI, and FastFlowLM. It auto-detects your hardware and sets things up for you, so you don’t need to worry about compatibility. You can run more than one model at the same time, and the built-in app makes it easy to download and switch models. It works with many other apps because it follows the OpenAI API standard, so developers can use Lemonade as a drop-in replacement. You can chat with AI, generate images, and do speech tasks, all using the same API. The latest release brings easier package management, better integration with Hugging Face for finding models, and faster support for new models and NPUs.

In the comments, many people are excited about running big AI models locally, without sending data to the cloud. Some like that Lemonade is open source and private, saying it’s great for personal projects and sensitive data. Others talk about the technical side, sharing tips about tuning settings like “--no-mmap” for faster loading. Some users wonder if their hardware is good enough, especially for the largest models, while others are happy that Lemonade works on different platforms. A few developers mention how easy it is to switch from cloud APIs to Lemonade, since the API is compatible. There are questions about how well it runs on older GPUs and laptops. Some people suggest that the lightweight design and easy install are major advantages. Others share concerns about keeping up with fast changes in AI model formats. Some hope Lemonade will add more features for advanced users. Overall, most comments are positive and show excitement about making local AI more accessible.

## LinkedIn is searching your browser extensions

- 原文链接: [LinkedIn is searching your browser extensions](https://browsergate.eu/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47613981)

LinkedIn is using hidden code to check which browser extensions you have installed when you visit their website. This scan happens without telling or asking users, and the data is sent to LinkedIn and outside companies. The article says this is illegal under EU law, because LinkedIn collects very personal information, like religious or political views, disabilities, and even if you are looking for a new job—just by seeing which extensions you use.

LinkedIn also scans for extensions that are its business competitors, letting it find out which companies use those tools. This could help LinkedIn target its rivals or even punish users of those tools. The article explains that LinkedIn gives very limited access to its platform for others, while secretly using a private API for itself. It says LinkedIn told the EU they are open, but in practice, they block competitors and watch for people using them.

The article also claims LinkedIn shares your data with third-parties, including a cybersecurity firm, using hidden trackers and fingerprinting scripts. None of this is mentioned in LinkedIn’s privacy policy. The group behind the article, Fairlinked, represents LinkedIn users and wants people to help stop this by supporting legal action.

People in the comments have strong reactions. Some are shocked and angry, calling this a big privacy violation. Others say this is not new—many big sites use similar tricks to track users, and browser extension scanning has happened before. Some worry this makes it unsafe to use browser extensions at all. A few point out that browser makers, like Chrome and Firefox, could do more to stop this kind of scanning.

Other commenters think legal action is the only way big companies will stop. Some ask if this is really illegal everywhere, or just in the EU. There are also people who say it’s not surprising—if you use free services, you pay with your data. But some are frustrated that LinkedIn does not warn users or let them opt out. Finally, a few suggest technical ways to block this scanning, like using privacy tools or disabling JavaScript, but agree this is not easy for most users.
