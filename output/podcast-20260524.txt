First, let’s talk about Reasonix, a new open-source coding agent that works right in your terminal and connects directly to DeepSeek, an AI coding API. The standout feature of Reasonix is its special caching system—DeepSeek’s “prefix cache.” This means most of your chat history does not need to be sent again, saving you about 80% on token costs during long coding sessions. You can install Reasonix easily with Node.js, and you do not need to install anything globally. Just paste in your DeepSeek API key and use your terminal as usual, with tools like git diff and ls. The agent is designed to work smoothly with DeepSeek’s cache by never changing the message order, so the cache stays effective and costs stay low.

Other features include skill extensions using Markdown files, connecting external tools with a simple protocol, and saving every session for replay or auditing. All settings are plain text, easy to track in git, and there are no hidden tricks. The roadmap includes a project scaffolder, cross-device sync, plugins, and more UI themes. Reasonix only supports DeepSeek, since other AI backends cannot keep the same kind of cache, which would make things more expensive and less stable. Reasonix is free under the MIT license, but you still pay for DeepSeek API usage.

On Hacker News, many users like that Reasonix is terminal-focused and not another heavy IDE plugin. People are excited about the high cache hit rate and cost savings. Some wish it supported more AI backends, but understand the technical reasons for focusing on DeepSeek. A few ask for a way to try it without signing up for DeepSeek, but others say the API is cheap and easy to get started with. Security is a highlight—nothing is written until you approve a plan, and all edits are sandboxed. The Markdown skills system is seen as clever and simple. Some wonder about team support or multi-user sessions, and others are happy that self-hosted DeepSeek servers are possible. Most agree that transparent, plain-text config files are a good choice. People are curious about the real savings and ask for more usage stats and documentation, but overall, the community is positive about Reasonix’s approach to making AI coding cheaper and more stable for terminal users.

Next, let’s look at work trends. An Australian study followed 15 companies that tried a four-day work week with full pay but fewer hours. Most companies said productivity did not drop—six even saw it go up. Almost all kept the new schedule after the test. Each company could choose how to measure success: profits, on-time projects, worker or customer happiness. The study was not just about working less, but about rethinking work—cutting out wasted time, fewer meetings, and focusing on what matters. People were not just squeezing five days into four, but actually doing better work in less time.

Many companies wanted to fight burnout, not just boost output. Burnout was a big worry, especially for young people and parents. For client teams, days off were staggered to make sure someone was always on duty. The study notes that different jobs need different ways to make a four-day week work. Other countries like Germany and the UK found similar results with shorter weeks. Technology like AI could help even more by letting people work less without losing results.

Comments are mixed. Some people hope more companies will try this model, while others are careful, saying the test group is small and maybe the results come from the newness of the change. Some say it is hard in jobs needing someone always on duty, like healthcare or customer service. There are worries that managers might just push the same amount of work into less time, raising stress. Some point out that if each company picks their own way to measure productivity, it is hard to compare results. Still, many agree the five-day week is old-fashioned and support trying something new. Some share stories of better morale and lower turnover after switching to a shorter week. Most want to see bigger, longer studies before calling it a big success.

Now, a story from computer history. The article tells how the old DOS game LAN-LOK, made at Palmer Station in Antarctica in 1991, was found and brought back. LAN-LOK was made by researchers as their first computer network was set up. The game is about “breaking” as many computers as you can in five minutes, while an AI called Evil Al tries to fix them. You choose computers by their real names, then choose how to break them—overloading printers, deleting files, or formatting drives. You score more by doing more damage, but Evil Al can take points away by fixing things. If you format your own computer, you lose. The humor comes from real-life network problems the team faced.

LAN-LOK was only shared in Antarctica and was almost lost. The author found a copy in 2025, talked to someone from the original team, and now you can play it in your browser or with DOSBox. The article also mentions other “break and fix” games, but says LAN-LOK was one of the first with this idea.

On Hacker News, people enjoyed the story and called it a great piece of computer history. Some shared memories of old LAN games and the fun of crashing computers in a safe way. Others discussed how hard it is to keep old software alive and asked if the source code could be found or recreated. Some joked about the stress of being a sysadmin and how the game turns real pressure into a funny challenge. There was talk about finding and saving other lost games from remote places. Technical discussions covered why DOSBox works so well and how modern tools help decompile old software. Many thanked the author and shared links to similar projects. Overall, the community loved the mix of history and tech.

Moving on, another article looks at the struggle of making “good” commits with Git, especially for big features. The writer says commits should be small and focused for easy review, but in reality, they often get messy. Enter Jujutsu, or jj, a new version control tool. Jujutsu makes it easier to jump around commits and clean up history, but it is still hard to keep things tidy. Tools like jj absorb and jj squash help, but are not perfect—absorb can put changes in the wrong commit, and squash can make messy merge conflicts.

The author suggests a new method: first, make your ideal commit history, with each commit for one type of change. Then, use Jujutsu’s squash tool to move changes into the right commits, repeating until all changes are sorted and old messy commits are empty. This is like sorting laundry—make a mess first, then clean it up at the end. This saves effort and avoids “git fatigue,” though it means that not every commit will compile, which may be a problem for some teams.

In the comments, many feel the same about Git fatigue and are curious about Jujutsu. Some like the “mess first, sort later” approach, while others prefer to make careful commits as they work. There are debates about whether it is better to clean up at the end or keep things tidy all the way. Some worry about learning new tools or having commits that do not compile. Others share tips for cleaning up history and ask for more examples. Overall, people are positive and open to new ways of working with code.

Next, we look at AI hardware costs. The article explains that memory, especially high-bandwidth memory (HBM), now makes up about 63% of the total cost to make AI chips, up from 52% last year. The study looked at Nvidia, AMD, Google, and Amazon chips, using public data to estimate costs. The cost of memory is rising much faster than other parts. In one year, spending on HBM jumped from $12 billion to $32 billion. The logic part of the chip stayed about the same, while packaging and extra parts dropped. The total cost for making these chips more than doubled, from $22 billion to $52 billion.

Memory is likely to get even more expensive, since there are not enough HBM chips, and prices keep rising. Big cloud companies plan to spend billions more on memory next year. The article says the cost numbers are not exact, but the trend is clear—memory is taking a bigger part of the total.

In the comments, some are surprised that memory now costs more than the logic part. Others say it makes sense, since AI models need more memory. HBM is hard to make and only a few companies supply it, which pushes prices up. Some wonder if chip designers will try to use less memory or invent new solutions. There is also talk about how this could slow AI progress, since memory is now the bottleneck. Some hope new memory tech or more factories will bring prices down. Others worry that rising costs will make AI more expensive to run, and suggest focusing on simpler models or more efficient software. A few readers joke that soon, “it’s all just memory,” showing how much things have changed.

Shifting gears, another article explains how to set up a Go server to use HTTP/2 without encryption—called “h2c”—for Google Cloud Run. The author needed this because their app uses server-sent events (SSE), and Cloud Run does not notice client disconnects with HTTP/1.1. Before Go 1.24, using h2c was complex and needed extra packages. Now, with Go 1.24 or newer, it’s much simpler: just set new protocol options directly on your server, no extra packages or wrapping needed.

To check if it works, you can use curl to connect with HTTP/2 cleartext. For Cloud Run, you also set up the service to use h2c, possibly with Terraform. The config sets the right port, longer timeouts for SSE, and higher concurrency. No load balancer changes are needed, since Cloud Run handles upgrades as needed.

Many in the comments welcome the new, simpler setup in Go 1.24. Some say the old way was too hard. Others warn that h2c is only safe inside trusted networks, since it lacks encryption. Cloud Run keeps users safe by only allowing h2c behind their secure frontend. There is discussion about when you need h2c, mostly for internal service-to-service use, and tips for longer SSE timeouts. Some ask about doing this with AWS or Azure, and others share that h2c can improve performance for SSE or gRPC. There are warnings to never expose unencrypted services to the public. People thank the author for showing both the old and new ways, and for including Terraform examples.

Next up, we have a story about moving backend projects from Go to Rust. Both are fast, typed languages used for backend work. Go is easy to learn, with a simple toolchain and fast compile times. Rust has a stricter compiler, more powerful types, and checks for more mistakes at compile time. Go lets you use tools and conventions to avoid bugs, while Rust builds these checks into the language.

Teams move to Rust to avoid production bugs from nil pointers and data races. Rust forces you to handle missing values with Option types, and its type system prevents most data races at compile time. Error handling in Go is manual, while Rust uses Result types and the ? operator for clearer code. Rust’s generics are central and make code more flexible, while Go’s generics are new and less used. Rust also has more predictable performance, with no garbage collector pauses.

The migration is not easy. Rust’s borrow checker is new for Go developers, and compile times are slower. Async code in Rust is more complex, and its library ecosystem is smaller in some backend areas, so you may have to build more yourself. The best way to migrate is step by step, rewriting one service at a time, keeping APIs the same, and learning the Rust way.

Go is still a good choice for many jobs—Kubernetes tools, CLI apps, and simple APIs, where you need to move fast and do not need all of Rust’s safety. Many use both: Go for simple services, Rust for critical or high-performance parts.

In the comments, some say Go is good enough for most backend work and like its speed and simplicity. Others value Rust’s strictness and safety, especially for big systems. Some warn that learning Rust is hard at first, but after a few months, the strict compiler feels like a teammate. There is debate about error handling styles, and agreement that both languages have their place, depending on team and project needs.

Another article explores how well large language models (LLMs) can write backend code when they have to follow strict rules. The study gave LLMs 80 new code projects and 20 feature additions, all with strict “API contracts.” Eight web frameworks were tested, from simple Flask to complex Django and FastAPI. The code was checked with tests and static tools.

The main finding is that LLMs do well with few rules, but much worse as more rules are added—a problem called “constraint decay.” For simple tasks, LLMs pass most tests, but for strict tasks, scores drop by about 30 points. Weaker LLMs almost fail with many rules. LLMs do better with simple frameworks with clear instructions, but struggle with hidden rules, especially around databases and ORMs.

On Hacker News, some say these results are expected—LLMs are good at “guessing” code, but not at following all the small rules in big projects. Others say this shows why humans are still needed for hard backend work. Some suggest better prompts, more examples, or feedback could help LLMs perform better. There is discussion about how real-world projects have hidden rules that are not in the code, making it hard for AI. Some hope models will get better, but most agree we should not trust LLMs for big backend jobs yet. The study is seen as useful for showing both the power and limits of AI coding tools.

Finally, an article explains a U.S. Customs and Border Protection rule for searching electronic devices at the border. The rule allows agents to search devices like phones, laptops, and USB drives, without a warrant or suspicion, when travelers enter or leave the U.S. There are two types of searches: basic (just looking through the device) and advanced (using tools to copy or examine data). The rule explains when and how data can be kept or shared and gives steps to protect private or sensitive data, like lawyer-client records. Agents should not access data stored only in the cloud, unless it is already on the device. There are rules for keeping records and making sure searches follow privacy and civil rights laws. The directive is public, so people know what agents can do.

In the comments, many people are worried about privacy and think these rules give too much power to border agents. Some share tips for protecting data, like using clean devices or encryption. Others say refusing to unlock your device could lead to delays or your device being taken. Some think the rule is better than nothing, since it gives steps for agents, but others feel it is not strong enough to prevent abuse. There is debate about whether these searches help security or just create trouble for regular people.
