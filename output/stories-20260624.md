OpenAI has made its first custom chip, called Jalapeño, with help from Broadcom. This chip is designed to run AI tasks faster and use less power, especially for "inference," which is when AI models answer questions or do jobs after they are trained.

The Jalapeño chip is still being tested, but OpenAI says it already works better per watt than other top chips. OpenAI’s own AI models helped design Jalapeño. The company wants to use custom chips to depend less on Nvidia, which makes most of the AI chips used today. Other big companies, like Google and Amazon, also make their own special AI chips for similar reasons. These chips are called “AI accelerators” and help AI run faster and cheaper.

OpenAI says Jalapeño is made for inference tasks, not for training big models, which will still use Nvidia’s chips for now. By making their own chip, OpenAI hopes to lower the cost of running their AI products, like Codex, in real time. Lowering these costs is important, because running AI models for millions of users is expensive. OpenAI is also building other parts of its system, like data centers and software, to make everything work better together. The company believes that controlling every part of the stack—from hardware to software—will let them make their AI faster, cheaper, and more reliable for users.

Now, let’s look at what people on Hacker News are saying. Some commenters think this is a smart move, because relying only on Nvidia could become risky or too expensive. Others point out that making a good chip is hard; it takes a lot of money and time, and many companies have failed before. A few users are excited that OpenAI used its own AI to help design the chip, saying this shows how powerful modern AI tools are. Some are skeptical and wonder if Jalapeño will really beat Nvidia or if it’s just for special tasks. There is also talk about Broadcom’s role—some feel Broadcom did most of the hard work, while OpenAI just gave ideas about what they needed. One commenter worries about vendor lock-in, where companies that make their own chips become less open to outsiders. Others are hopeful that more custom chips will mean lower prices and faster progress for everyone using AI. Overall, people agree that this is a big step for OpenAI, but they will wait to see how well Jalapeño works in the real world.

## We’re making Bunny DNS free

- 原文链接: [We’re making Bunny DNS free](https://bunny.net/blog/were-making-bunny-dns-free/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48657030)

Bunny.net announced that their DNS service, Bunny DNS, is now free for up to 500 domains per account. They say DNS is key to making the internet faster, and Bunny DNS helps route web traffic quickly and smartly.

Bunny DNS started as an internal tool to make their content delivery network (CDN) fast. Now, it’s a product for everyone, and it can handle smart routing using things like latency, health checks, and even custom JavaScript. Instead of charging per DNS query, Bunny.net has removed all usage fees for DNS. The only cost left is a $1/month minimum spend, but DNS itself won’t create more charges. They have also made it easy to move domains from other providers by scanning and copying your DNS records or letting you upload a BIND file.

Bunny DNS now supports dual-stack IPv4 and IPv6 automatically, so users don’t need to do extra work for modern internet addresses. For security, they added DNSSEC with a special privacy feature called NSEC Black Lies, which helps keep your domain structure private while protecting from tampering. They have also added modern record types like HTTPS, SVCB, TLSA, CDS, and CDNSKEY, making it easier to use new web and security technologies.

With Bunny DNS, users can enable CDN and security features with just one click, helping protect against attacks and speeding up websites. Bunny.net says their goal is to keep improving DNS and make it a smooth part of their whole platform, so developers can build fast and safe applications without worrying about hidden costs.

Some compare Bunny DNS to Cloudflare and wonder if Bunny can handle very large numbers of requests. Others like the focus on privacy and support for new features like DNSSEC and IPv6. A few users ask about the $1/month minimum spend, and Bunny.net staff reply that most users will pay nothing extra unless they use other paid services. Some users worry about what “free” means long-term and if Bunny.net can keep this model, but others point out that free DNS is a good way for Bunny.net to attract customers for their paid CDN and security tools.

There are also comments about how easy it is to move from other DNS providers, with users saying the automatic migration tools are helpful. A few people discuss the importance of DNS security and are glad to see modern features included at no extra cost. Some developers share positive experiences using Bunny DNS in production and praise the quick support and fast response times. Others are excited to try Bunny DNS because they were waiting for features like DNSSEC and IPv6. ## PR spam today looks like email spam in the early 2000s

- 原文链接: [PR spam today looks like email spam in the early 2000s](https://www.greptile.com/blog/prs-on-openclaw)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48660579)

This article looks at a big rise in spammy pull requests (PRs) on the OpenClaw open source project after it got popular. The author, Rahul from Greptile, shows how most new PRs come from AI coding agents and are low quality, with merge rates dropping from 48% to less than 10%.

He says PR spam now is like email spam in the 2000s. Like with email, projects now need to check who is sending PRs and if they have a good history (reputation). Some projects, like Ghostty, have limited AI-generated PRs and started using tools like "Vouch" to manage trust. People who have sent more good PRs before are more likely to get merged.

Rahul points out that just having more contributors does not help if they all use the same AI tools and send the same ideas. For example, many people made PRs for the same feature or bug fix because their AI gave them the same advice. The real value in open source comes from different ways of thinking, not just more people or more AI.

The stats show that PRs for features only get merged 9% of the time, but refactor PRs (which need deeper knowledge of the code) get merged 35% of the time. Good contributions need deep understanding, not just fast typing or simple AI prompts. The author compares this to how building design split into separate jobs—now, surviving PRs are more like "architecture," needing real insight.

He ends by saying open source is moving faster than ever, but new problems (like spam) need new tools for trust, identity, and validation.

In the Hacker News comments, some people agree that AI-generated PR spam is a real problem and say it makes open source harder to manage. Others point out that blocking all AI contributions is not fair—AI can help with boring or simple tasks if used well. Some users like the idea of reputation systems, but worry it might make it hard for new or unknown people to start helping. A few think that the problem is really about scale: open source projects always get more spam and low-effort PRs as they get popular, AI just makes it faster.

One user says that tools like Vouch are good but could become gatekeepers, making open source less open. Another says we need better ways for humans and AIs to work together, not just more rules. Some users note that the best PRs always need real understanding, and that will not change, no matter how good AI gets. Others are worried that focusing on reputation could create cliques, where only old contributors matter.

A few commenters remember when email spam first became a problem, and say open source will have to invent similar solutions. Some are hopeful, saying open source has solved bigger problems before. Others warn that if open source becomes too hard to join, it could lose the energy and new ideas that made it great.

## Crawling BitTorrent DHTs for Fun and Profit [pdf]

- 原文链接: [Crawling BitTorrent DHTs for Fun and Profit [pdf]](https://www.usenix.org/legacy/event/woot10/tech/full_papers/Wolchok.pdf)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48619159)

This article is about how people can use BitTorrent’s Distributed Hash Tables (DHTs) to quickly create new torrent search engines and also to watch what users are sharing. The authors built tools to show both how easy it is to copy all the torrents from the network (good for making new search websites) and how easy it is to track what files people are downloading (bad for privacy).

The article explains that BitTorrent once used central servers (trackers) to help people find each other, but these servers were easy for copyright owners to attack with lawsuits. To fix this, BitTorrent switched to a more decentralized system using DHTs, which are like large, shared phone books spread across many computers. Now, users find other downloaders by looking up codes called “infohashes” inside the DHT.

The authors built a program called ClearView. It acts like many fake users (called Sybils) to collect a lot of information from the DHT. With this, they made a search engine called SuperSeed. It could find and index over one million torrents in under two hours, using just one computer. They also showed that someone could use this method to track what files are popular and which IP addresses (computers) are sharing them. In their tests, over 16 days, they saw 1.5 million torrents being downloaded by about 8 million different IP addresses.

They found that most of the popular torrents were for movies, TV shows, and music, and that almost all of the top downloads were copyrighted material. They could also see when new episodes of TV shows became popular right after airing. The system could also spot the difference between single heavy downloaders and proxy services (which hide many users behind one IP address).

The authors say that even if big torrent search sites get shut down by the law, it is easy to build new ones by crawling the DHT. They also warn that DHTs do not protect users’ privacy—anyone can watch and record what files are being shared and by whom. They suggest that copyright owners may try to use these tools to sue individual users, or use other tricks like spreading fake files.

In the comment section, many people were surprised by how easy it is to crawl the DHT and rebuild a search engine. Some said this shows that shutting down sites like The Pirate Bay does not really stop file sharing. Others worried about privacy, saying that users are much more exposed than they thought. A few commenters pointed out that using VPNs or proxies can help hide your real IP, but not everyone uses these tools. Some thought the technique could be used by both good and bad actors—helping to keep the network alive, or for spying and lawsuits. A number of users wondered if there were ways to make DHTs more private, but others replied that the open nature of DHTs makes this very hard. There were also technical comments about how the crawler works and if it would still be effective today, since the paper is a few years old. Some mentioned similar research, and a few shared stories about running their own crawlers or search sites as a hobby. Overall, the discussion was lively, with both excitement about the technology and concern about the risks for users.

## There are a few things that I look back on as my mistakes in the early days

- 原文链接: [There are a few things that I look back on as my mistakes in the early days](https://twitter.com/ID_AA_Carmack/status/2069799283369345247)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48661825)

John Carmack talked about his regrets from the early days of making Quake, a famous video game. He said Quake was too ambitious and they tried to do too much with new technology all at once.

Carmack thought they could have built the multiplayer and modding features inside an improved Doom engine, which was more stable. Using a well-known engine would have helped designers work better, without big changes happening all the time. He wished they saved the big technical jumps—like full 3D movement and characters—for a later game. Carmack also admitted he pushed the team too hard, running them at “startup speed” for too long, which led to burnout. He found out that even working as hard as he could, he still missed his deadlines. He talked about mistakes with how the company gave out stock; their custom approach led to bad incentives, and the standard way (vesting over time) would have worked better. Another problem was asking level designers to also be great at art, making things look as good as they played, which got harder as technology improved. Team fights happened because some designers could handle visuals and others could not, causing tension. Carmack apologized to one designer, Sandy, for this tough situation.

In the comments, some people agreed that Quake was risky and maybe too complex for its time, but others said the ambition is what made it special. Several users admired Carmack’s honesty about past mistakes, saying it’s rare for tech leaders to share this much. Some said burnout is a big problem in game development, and more leaders should notice it. Others felt Quake’s technical leap was important for gaming history, even if it was hard on the team. A few pointed out that mixing design and art is still a challenge in games today. There were also comments about company stock—some agreed that standard vesting is best, while others thought custom plans can work if done carefully. One user thanked Carmack for being open about team struggles, saying it helps younger developers learn. Another person wondered if Quake would have been as famous if the team had played it safe. Some people defended the “push hard” approach, but many said balance and rest are important for long-term success.
