First, big news for the media technology world: SMPTE, the group that has set video and audio standards for over a hundred years, is now making all its standards and guidelines free online. Before, you had to pay or become a member to read these documents. Now, anyone can access them, which will help small companies, students, and engineers everywhere. SMPTE wants to push new ideas and help more people build tools that work well together. They are also updating how they work, using GitHub and HTML for faster and easier updates. Big tech companies like Amazon, Apple, Google, and Sony support this move. The hope is to stop mistakes from using unofficial versions and make the media industry more open and strong. In the comments, readers are very happy. Many share stories about how hard or expensive it was to get these documents in the past. Some call for other groups, like IEEE, to open their standards too. There is debate about the cost of making standards, but most agree that sharing documents online is cheap and helps everyone. Some talk about how standards are made and the problems with closed processes. Overall, most think this is a great step forward.

Next, let’s talk about UHF X11, a new project that brings the classic X11 window system to Apple Vision Pro and visionOS. With this, you can run old desktop X11 apps, like xterm or xclock, as floating windows in your real space. The display even has effects to look like old CRT monitors. You can connect apps from other machines over the network, and there is some support for 3D apps using OpenGL. Security uses X11 cookies, and you can add your own fonts. The idea is to bring a retro feel to modern AR. In the comments, people are amused and excited. Some joke about "3D in 2D in 3D" and wonder if xeyes could follow your gaze, though privacy rules block this. Some wish the app worked in more countries or on other devices. There are tips for running Linux desktops in VR, and some discuss the tech behind UHF X11. Overall, people think it’s a fun and creative way to mix old tech with new hardware.

Moving on, there’s a new tool called PostgresBench, made by the ClickHouse team, for benchmarking managed Postgres database services. It lets you test services like AWS RDS, Aurora, Crunchy Bridge, Neon, and ClickHouse’s own Postgres using fair and open methods. The tool uses `pgbench` to simulate real workloads, like lots of small transactions, and tests with different hardware setups. Results show that ClickHouse’s Postgres is fastest, mostly because it uses fast NVMe storage close to the compute. Other services using network storage are slower, especially for write-heavy work. All test data and scripts are open, so anyone can try it or share their results. In the comments, people want to see more providers tested, like PlanetScale, and some want to compare with plain Postgres on a VPS. Others say client-side code also affects performance and wish Postgres had protocol compression. Some ask if they can test their own setups, and the answer is yes—it’s easy and quick. A few joke about using AI to tune settings if they had lots of cloud budget. Most agree that open benchmarks like this are very useful.

Now, let’s look at a hobby project where someone is reverse engineering the old DOS game "F-15 Strike Eagle II" from 1989. They have moved all the original assembly code to C, and now need testers to find bugs in the new version. People are asked to try playing with the old game files and report any crashes or problems. The project wants to keep the game just like the original, even keeping the old bugs for now. In the comments, many share memories of playing the game, with some being Air Force veterans. People discuss why reverse engineering is important, saying it helps fix bugs and port games, while emulators like DOSBox are helpful but limited. Some talk about problems with old copy protection and the need for special tools to save data from floppies. There are questions about the legal side, and some worries about using AI to help with reverse engineering. Others share stories about missing game manuals, and there’s nostalgia for old flight sims. People are mostly supportive, with some technical and legal concerns.

Next, a story about copyright and AI. A design agency copied the book "The Dictionary of Obscure Sorrows," put its content on a new website, made fake art with AI, and even used affiliate links to make money. The agency did not ask the author, John Koenig, for permission. The fake site now comes up first in Google results, and even AI tools like ChatGPT and Gemini point to it, confusing people. Koenig’s publisher tried to remove the site with DMCA, but Google did nothing. The article says this is a big problem: AI is making it easier to copy and remix work without consent. In the comments, many share stories of their own work being stolen, with or without AI. Some say DMCA only works for big companies, while small creators get ignored. There is debate about copyright, open source, and if giving work away for free means you should expect copying. Many are angry at the agency, saying this hurts real creativity. Some worry that better AI will make things worse, while others joke about making up new words for the sadness of being copied. There’s also talk about how AI tools are often wrong, and that copying is too easy today. Overall, there’s a lot of frustration about how hard it is to protect original work online.

Let’s move on to cssQuake, a project that lets you play the classic game Quake in your browser using only HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. The graphics are rendered by CSS, which is a technical achievement. You can play single or multiplayer on classic maps. Controls are like the original, and there are many options for gameplay and debugging. The engine is built with PolyCSS, uses assets from the original Quake, and manages logic with TypeScript. Some gameplay differences remain, but the developer is working to fix them. In the comments, people are amazed to see Quake running in CSS, even if it’s not as smooth as on old PCs. Some explain why CSS is slow for this, and others point out small bugs. There are jokes about "hacks on top of hacks," and debates about whether CSS should be used for games. Some share nostalgia for the original Quake. Overall, people are impressed and entertained by the creativity and skill.

Next, StartupWiki is a new, free site for researching startups, aiming to be an alternative to Crunchbase. It lists information about many companies, especially in AI, biotech, and genetic engineering. The site uses AI agents to collect and organize data, and badges show which info is “verified.” Users can’t submit companies yet, but that feature is coming. There’s also an “agent ledger” showing how data was gathered. The main goal is to keep things open and simple. In the comments, many like the idea, but worry about data accuracy. Some find outdated or wrong info, bugs in forms, or missing sources for “verified” data. The developer replies to many comments, promising more updates and better fact-checking. Some suggest adding an API or letting users flag errors. There’s also debate about using AI and the need for regular manual checks. Most agree that accuracy is the biggest challenge, but are excited to see where StartupWiki goes.

Now, let’s talk about the cost of running AI language models. The article explains how to estimate the cost per word (token) when serving many users, using math based on GPU specs like FLOPs and memory bandwidth. Most of the work comes from matrix multiplication, and attention layers in the model. A trick called KV-Cache helps avoid repeating work, making things faster and cheaper. Using the NVIDIA B200 GPU as an example, the article shows you can serve hundreds of users at once, but memory limits the real number to around 40-60 active users, or more if chats are shorter or users are idle. The cost of buying a GPU is about $133 per user for the chip’s life, or $9.36 per user per month if renting. In the comments, people discuss if it’s better to buy or rent GPUs, the real costs including power and staff, and how these numbers can change with usage. Some like the simple math, while others want more detail on software tricks or smaller models. Overall, readers find the post helpful but know real-world costs can be unpredictable.

Next, Bun has opened a huge pull request to add shared-memory threads to JavaScriptCore, the JavaScript engine in WebKit. This would let JavaScript code use threads that share the same memory, unlike the usual message-passing model. The change is very big, with over 270,000 lines of code, much of it written or reviewed by AI tools. The main challenge is making garbage collection safe and fast across threads. Right now, it uses a "stop-the-world" GC, which can pause all threads. The PR is on Bun’s own fork, not the main WebKit project. In the comments, some are excited about the speed boost for heavy tasks, while others warn that shared-memory concurrency is hard and risky. Bugs can appear if two threads change the same object. Many worry about trusting such a big change, especially when much of it is AI-written and not well explained. Some wish for better documentation or a safer language like Rust. Others note JavaScript already has workers and shared memory at a lower level, but this is a bigger step. There’s debate about the risks and the future of JavaScript. Some are just amazed by what is possible now with new tools.

Finally, there’s a new tool that makes your PDF files look like they were scanned, either from the command line or in your browser using WebAssembly. The tool adds effects like gray colors, blur, noise, and small rotations to make clean PDFs look less perfect—just like a real scan. This is useful when someone asks for a “scanned” document, but you only have a digital file. The tool is free and open, and works without uploading files anywhere. In the comments, people share stories about needing scanned-looking PDFs for work or games. Some use other tricks, like taking photos of their screen or using ImageMagick. There’s talk about slow adoption of digital signatures and old company rules. A few worry that making fake scans could be fraud, but many joke about the world being stuck in the past. Some wish for tools to make real scans look clean again. People thank the creator for making the tool open, since many PDF tools are expensive or behind paywalls.
