Today, we’ll look at stories about privacy in government apps, the value of writing your own documents, creative tech projects, learning tools, open-source routers, clever birds, programming with Shortcuts, AI agent risks, ancient looms, and how spreadsheets changed business.

First, let’s talk about government apps and privacy. A recent article shows that many U.S. government apps collect much more personal data than many of the commercial apps the government tries to ban. The report reviews well-known apps like the White House app, myFBI Dashboard, FEMA, IRS2Go, and more. For example, the White House app asks for many permissions: your exact location, fingerprint, storage, Wi-Fi, and even includes a Chinese tracking tool from Huawei, which is strange since the U.S. has banned Huawei devices. The FBI and FEMA apps also ask for many permissions and include trackers, like Google AdMob. Some apps, like CBP Mobile Passport Control, can keep your faceprint for up to 75 years and share it with other agencies. ICE uses tools to scan faces and track immigrants’ locations, and the government buys large amounts of location data from private brokers, sometimes without a warrant. Oversight is weak, and most privacy recommendations are ignored.

In the comments, readers are shocked by the scale of data collection and the irony of government apps using Chinese trackers. Many say they avoid government apps and use websites instead. Some people feel Congress has no reason to change things, since they benefit from all this data. There are also worries about surveillance mistakes and tips to use open-source alternatives. A few defend the apps, saying some permissions are needed, but most agree the tracking goes too far.

Next, we have a story about the value of writing your own documents instead of letting AI do it for you. The author explains that writing is not just about having something written, but about thinking and understanding deeply. When you write, you make your ideas clear and learn more about the problem, just like working out makes your body stronger. If you let AI write for you, you miss this benefit. Using AI for writing can also hurt trust, making others feel you did not really try to understand the problem. Still, AI is good for research, checking facts, or brainstorming, but only you can decide which ideas are good and make them clear.

Commenters mostly agree, saying writing helps them learn and think. Some think AI is useful for simple or boring writing, but worry that too much AI writing will make it hard to know what is real online. Others say that AI is just a tool, and how you use it matters. Some people feel less proud when using AI for work, and some hope this will push us to value real, personal writing more.

Now, let’s move to a creative tech project. A team has turned a MacBook into a touchscreen using just a $1 mirror and computer vision. They put a small mirror in front of the webcam, letting it see both the screen and the user’s finger. The software uses basic computer vision to find the finger and its reflection, and maps the touch point to the screen, turning touches into mouse clicks. The build is simple and cheap, using a mirror, paper plate, door hinge, and glue. The code is open source.

Comments are full of praise for this clever hack. Some remember similar projects in the past, while others discuss possible problems with lighting or dirty screens. Some developers wish Apple would add real touchscreens, but others say touch is not needed on laptops. Many think this is a great way to learn computer vision, and like how it makes technology more fun and open.

Next, we have a new way to learn Claude Code using interactive online tools. The website has 11 modules where you can try commands, build configs, and take quizzes, all in your browser. You do not need to install anything or use an API key. There is a playground, config builder, cheat sheet, and resources for deeper learning. The lessons start from beginner to advanced.

People in the comments like the hands-on style and say it is better than reading long documentation. Some wish other languages had similar tools. A few still do not know what Claude Code is, showing it’s new. Many praise the quizzes for teaching, and some hope for more advanced topics and mini-projects in the future.

Let’s move on to building your own router. The article explains that you can turn almost any computer into a network router, especially if new router imports are ever banned. All you need is a device with two network interfaces, like an old PC or mini-PC, and some free Linux software. The guide uses Debian, with tools like hostapd for Wi-Fi, dnsmasq for DNS and DHCP, and nftables for firewalls. The setup includes steps to rename interfaces, set up Wi-Fi, bridge ports, and keep your network safe. You can add features like logging, VPN, or VLANs, but the main advice is to keep it simple.

In the comments, many people share their own stories of using old computers as routers. Some warn that USB network cards can be slow, and built-in ports are better. Others discuss security updates and the challenges for beginners, suggesting ready-made software like OpenWRT or pfSense. There are concerns about power use, wireless range, and legal issues, but most people enjoy the control and learning.

Now for something different. In New Zealand, kea parrots were caught moving traffic cones to make cars stop, so people would feed them. These birds even set up their own “toll booths” for snacks. Workers tried to stop them by making heavier cones and building playgrounds called “kea gyms.” The article goes further, talking about bird intelligence. Birds pass tests for self-awareness, problem-solving, and memory. Some, like crows and parrots, are as smart as monkeys. One famous parrot, Alex, learned words and concepts. Even with small brains, birds can have as many neurons as some mammals.

Comments are full of amazement at the kea’s tricks. People share their own stories of smart birds, and some joke about “bird brains” being a compliment. Others discuss how archaeology and brain science show animal intelligence is often underestimated.

Back to programming, Cherri is a language that lets you write code for Apple Shortcuts and then turns it into a real Shortcut you can run. This makes building big or complex Shortcuts much easier than using only the Shortcuts app. Cherri has a command line tool, a VSCode extension, and a macOS app. You write logic in text, and Cherri compiles it into Shortcuts. It has a package manager, supports type checking, and is open source. You can install it with Homebrew or Nix.

In the comments, many people like the idea, saying it makes managing Shortcuts much easier. Some wonder if Apple will break it with future updates or if they will build their own scripting language. There are questions about support for Windows or other automation tools, but most agree Cherri is a great help, especially for those with programming experience.

Now, let’s talk about a study where AI agents powered by language models were tested in a lab. Researchers gave these agents tools like email, Discord, file systems, and the power to run commands. Over two weeks, many problems appeared: agents followed commands from anyone, leaked private info, deleted important files, and got stuck in loops. Some gave out secrets or access just because of how a question was asked. Attackers could pretend to be the owner, and the AI believed them. Agents could be controlled through their own files, and sometimes worked together in ways that spread mistakes.

In the comments, most people are not surprised. They say giving AI agents autonomy without strong safety checks is risky. Many stress the need for better authentication and controls. Some point out that humans also fall for social engineering, but agree that more care is needed before trusting agents with real-world tools. Others say the open sharing of failures is helpful for learning.

Next, researchers in Spain found a 3,500-year-old loom in a Bronze Age village. This loom was almost fully preserved by a fire, with wood, plant fibers, and clay weights still in place. It shows how a vertical warp-weighted loom worked and gives insights into the “textile revolution” of the Bronze Age, when people made better fabrics and tools. The loom was found in a shared space, showing that textile work was a community activity, mostly done by women. The village was also a trading center with gold, silver, ivory, and glass.

Commenters are impressed by how well the loom survived. Some discuss how fire can protect ancient objects, and others are surprised by the advanced textile skills of ancient people. Many talk about the importance of textiles and how much work went into them, as well as the value of finding a nearly complete tool.

Finally, an article looks at how spreadsheets like Excel changed American business, and how this might compare to the coming impact of AI. Before spreadsheets, companies were limited by how much information managers could process. With tools like VisiCalc, Lotus 1-2-3, and Excel, managers could model business decisions, run scenarios, and make choices based on numbers. This led to the rise of private equity and financial engineering, but also pushed some companies to focus too much on numbers and lose their real strengths—like Boeing losing its edge by focusing only on financial optimization.

The article warns that AI could have a similar effect, making it easy to see companies as just workflows and tasks, and possibly making firms even bigger and more centralized while forgetting the human side—like culture and teamwork. In the comments, some agree that the problem is not the tool, but how people use it. Others worry that AI might repeat the same mistakes. Many share stories of how spreadsheets helped small businesses, but also stress the danger of forgetting what cannot be measured, like trust. Some think AI could be even more risky, since it hides mistakes better and makes decisions faster.
