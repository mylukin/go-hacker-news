First, let’s talk about DeFlock, a website that shows an interactive map of “Flock Cams.” These are cameras made by the company Flock, used to watch cars and collect license plate numbers in neighborhoods and cities. The map is built with Leaflet and OpenStreetMap, and you can see points for each camera location. Some points show just a few cameras, while others show thousands, like 363, 1069, or even 5045 in one area. You can zoom in and out to explore, and users can also report new cameras. The goal is to show how quickly these cameras are spreading and to raise awareness about surveillance in everyday life.

The comments are divided. Some people worry about privacy and how these cameras could be used to track where people go. Others think the map is helpful for showing just how many cameras are out there. Some say the cameras help the police solve crimes, but others point out they can catch innocent people too. There are technical discussions about Leaflet and OpenStreetMap, and some suggestions about adding more details to the map. A few people worry that it is too easy for neighborhoods to install these cameras. Many support the project for making surveillance visible, but some feel uneasy about how much public monitoring there is now.

Next up, Apple has announced the new MacBook Neo, starting at just $599. This is now the most affordable MacBook, aiming at students, families, and anyone wanting a Mac for less money. The Neo has a 13-inch Liquid Retina display, four colors, and is made from recycled aluminum, weighing only 2.7 pounds. It runs on Apple’s new A18 Pro chip, made for fast everyday work and AI features. Apple says this MacBook is faster than most others at this price, especially for things like photo editing or using AI. With up to 16 hours of battery life, it should last all day.

There’s a Magic Keyboard, a big trackpad, and a FaceTime HD camera with good microphones. The speakers support Spatial Audio and Dolby Atmos. It comes with two USB-C ports (one for displays), a headphone jack, Wi-Fi 6E, and Bluetooth 6. The Neo runs macOS Tahoe, works smoothly with iPhones, and is Apple’s greenest MacBook yet, with recycled materials and packaging.

Many commenters are impressed with the $599 price, saying it makes MacBooks much more accessible. Some are glad to see the headphone jack is still here. There are questions about the A18 Pro chip and whether 8GB of RAM is enough, especially for developers. People like the new colors, though some prefer a more “pro” look. Repairability is a big topic; some hope it will be easy to fix, while others expect Apple to keep it closed. Many are happy with the battery life and silent design, and some compare the Neo to cheap Windows laptops, saying the Neo might be a better value. There’s also discussion about Apple’s push for AI features, and some hope this low price will push other brands to make better, cheaper laptops.

Now let’s look at a story about everyday energy use. This article explains how much energy different things in our lives use, from LED bulbs and phones to kitchen gadgets and cars. For example, charging a phone takes about 20 Wh, while using a laptop uses about 20 Wh per hour. Watching TV can use 60–90 Wh per hour. Kitchen devices like kettles and ovens use a lot of power, and dryers are among the biggest users. Heating and cooling, like hairdryers and heaters, can use hundreds or thousands of Wh per hour. Electric vehicles are much more efficient than petrol ones, and electric garden tools use less energy too.

Commenters liked the clear comparisons. Many were surprised that streaming a video uses very little energy from the internet—the device itself uses much more. Some learned that using heat pumps or LED bulbs can save a lot. People shared tips about using eco modes on machines, and some checked the math against their own bills. A few wanted more data, like carbon emissions or more product choices. Overall, people found the tool useful and easy to understand, and it helped them think about ways to save energy.

Moving on, we have a story about NG Guard, a project working to bring Flash content back to Newgrounds. After browsers stopped supporting Flash in 2021, many games and animations were lost. NG Guard uses a tool called Ruffle—an open-source program that runs Flash in modern browsers without needing the old plugin. The goal is to keep classic Flash art and games alive and safe. The team checks files to avoid security risks and works to fix bugs, but not every old file works perfectly yet.

Comments are full of nostalgia. Many are happy to see Flash games return and thank the team. Some ask about how NG Guard works and how safe it is, while others worry about security with old files. A few suggest more ways to preserve web history, and many agree that open-source projects like Ruffle are important. There are also stories about favorite Flash games, and some hope other sites use similar tools. The main feeling is that Flash mattered a lot, and people want to keep it alive.

Next is news from Alibaba’s Qwen AI team. Just after releasing their new Qwen 3.5 models, several top researchers, including the lead Junyang Lin, suddenly quit. This has caused a lot of worry about the future of the project. Qwen 3.5 models are known for being small but powerful, working well on both language and vision tasks. Even with fewer resources, the team built strong models, but after Junyang Lin left—possibly due to management changes—other key members followed. Alibaba’s CEO held an emergency meeting, showing how serious this is for the company.

Commenters are surprised and worried. Some are sad that open-source AI in China may slow down. Others think the researchers might start a new company or join startups, which could be exciting. People share positive experiences using Qwen models, especially for coding, and compare them to models like Llama. Many talk about how hard it is to keep top AI talent and hope the team keeps sharing their work, wherever they go. The story shows both concern about losing a great team and hope for the future of open AI.

Another article discusses how software engineers get more praise and promotions for making things complicated, rather than keeping them simple. The main example is two engineers: one solves a problem with simple, clear code, and the other builds a big, complex system. Even if the simple code is better, the complex solution often gets more attention and rewards. This is not because managers want complexity, but because big work is easier to see and describe.

The advice is for engineers to make their simple choices visible—explain why you picked a simple design and what problems you avoided. For managers, the advice is to reward clear, easy-to-maintain work and ask people to justify added complexity.

Many commenters agree and share similar stories from their work. Some say company culture is the problem, and others point out that simple code is actually harder to write. There are tips on keeping things small, explaining your choices, and helping others see the value of easy-to-read code. In the end, good work does not always speak for itself, so you have to tell the story clearly.

Let’s talk about MOSS, a fun online painting tool where each brush is like a small program. Each brush does something different—some blend, drip, or grow like a vine, while others make patterns or glitch effects. The canvas is made of tiny cells, each holding more than just color. When you use a brush, it changes the data in the cells, so colors can mix in new and surprising ways. You can change how a brush works, even editing its code.

MOSS comes with over 50 brushes, and you can save and share your art. If someone opens your picture in MOSS, they get the same brushes and settings, so they can see exactly how you made it or make their own changes.

Commenters love the idea of programmable brushes and say it reminds them of old drawing tools, but with more power. Some think it’s a great way to learn programming, while others worry that too many complex brushes could confuse new users. There are requests for more features and ideas for using MOSS as a teaching tool. Overall, people are excited about MOSS as both a creative toy and a way to explore programming in a hands-on way.

Next, there’s a technical deep dive into how Rust calls functions. The author says Rust could be much faster if it used a better calling convention, instead of copying C’s old way. Right now, Rust often passes things by pointer, but could use registers for speed. The idea is to allow a fast calling convention, controlled by a flag, and choose which arguments go in registers. The post explains how to do this with examples and talks about challenges, like handling function pointers and debugging.

Commenters are interested and mostly agree that Rust could be faster with a better system, but they point out problems with debugging, mixing different Rust versions, and breaking compatibility with C. Some think this is most important for people writing libraries or performance-critical code, and wonder if the extra speed is worth the extra complexity. Many hope the Rust team will try these ideas, even though it won’t be easy.

Let’s look at NanoGPT Slowrun, a project to make AI models learn better with small data but lots of computer power. As computers get faster but data grows slowly, the team at Q Labs wants to find ways to train models that work well with limited data. In their open challenge, you train a model on 100 million tokens and use as much compute as you want, focusing on data efficiency instead of speed.

They found that a new optimizer called Muon, more epochs, larger models with strong regularization, and shuffling data all help. They have reached 5.5 times better data efficiency than before, and hope to get even more with new ideas. They invite others to join and share research.

Commenters are excited about the focus on data efficiency. Some like the open research approach, and others wonder if these tricks will help with bigger datasets or other tasks. There are questions about costs, regularization, and if the methods can work for things like images or robotics. Many agree this kind of research is important as AI moves forward.

Finally, we end with a fun science question: Does data have weight? The article explains that SSDs get a tiny bit heavier when full, because storing data means adding electrons, which have mass. For a 1TB SSD, the difference is only a few femtograms—too small to measure. Hard drives work differently, changing magnet directions, so their weight does not change except for an even smaller amount. The article is clear: while this is true in theory, it does not matter in real life.

The comments are full of playful science jokes. People joke about weighing their SSDs after copying files. Some explain more about how SSDs work, and others point out that batteries and powered-on computers also weigh a bit more, thanks to Einstein’s equation. Some ask if this matters for space travel or science, but the answer is always no—the effect is too small. People enjoy the fun fact and are happy to learn something new, even if it does not matter day-to-day.
