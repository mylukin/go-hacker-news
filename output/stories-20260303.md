## Intel's make-or-break 18A process node debuts for data center with 288-core Xeon

Intel has launched a new chip process called 18A, starting with a powerful 288-core Xeon processor for data centers. This is a big step for Intel, as the company wants to catch up with rivals like TSMC and AMD in making small, fast chips.

The 18A process means the parts inside the chip are even smaller and more efficient than before. This allows Intel to put more cores—basically, more brains—into one chip. The new Xeon chip is made for giant data centers, the places that run cloud services and big websites. Intel says the chip is faster and uses less power, which can save money for companies that use lots of servers.

The article explains that Intel has had problems before with making smaller chips, often falling behind competitors. This new process is important for showing that Intel can still lead in technology. The 18A process uses advanced tools, like new ways to build transistors (the tiny switches in chips) and new materials. For example, they use something called RibbonFETs and PowerVia, which help make the chips even better.

Intel hopes to sell not just its own chips, but also let other companies use its factories. This is called foundry business. If 18A works well, it could bring more customers to Intel’s factories.

People in the comment section have many opinions. Some are excited and say this is good news for Intel and for competition in the chip world. They think it could help lower prices and bring more new products. Others are skeptical, saying Intel has often announced new chip processes before, but then had delays or problems. They want to see real products and results before believing the hype.

Some commenters think 288 cores sounds impressive, but wonder if software will use all those cores well. A few say this chip is mainly for big companies, not regular people. There are also discussions about whether Intel can really catch up to TSMC, which has been ahead for years. Some remind others that making chips is very hard, and even small mistakes can cause big delays.

A few people talk about the foundry business. They wonder if other companies will trust Intel’s factories, since TSMC and Samsung are already strong in this area. One person points out that having more foundries is good for the world, so we don’t depend on just one company. Overall, commenters are hopeful but cautious, waiting to see if Intel can really deliver on its promises this time.

## Physics Girl: Super-Kamiokande – Imaging the sun by detecting neutrinos [video]

- 原文链接: [Physics Girl: Super-Kamiokande – Imaging the sun by detecting neutrinos [video]](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=B3m3AMRlYfc)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47233110)

This video is about Physics Girl (Dianna Cowern) returning after three years with a new science video, where she explains how scientists can take a picture of the sun at night using a huge detector called Super-Kamiokande in Japan. The main idea is that this detector can “see” neutrinos—tiny, hard-to-catch particles that come from the sun and pass through almost everything, even the Earth.

Dianna explains that most cameras need light to take photos, but neutrinos are not light; they are a different kind of particle. The Super-Kamiokande is a big tank of very pure water, deep underground, with many sensors around it. When a neutrino comes from the sun and hits a water molecule, it sometimes makes a flash of light called Cherenkov radiation. The sensors pick up this tiny flash, and by putting together many flashes, scientists can tell the direction the neutrinos came from—so they know they are from the sun, even if the sun is on the other side of the planet.

This means the detector can “see” the sun even at night, through the Earth, by looking at neutrinos. Dianna also explains why it is hard to catch neutrinos: they almost never hit anything, so you need a huge amount of water and very sensitive equipment. The Super-Kamiokande helps scientists study the sun, understand how stars work, and learn about the universe.

## Why payment fees matter more than you think

- 原文链接: [Why payment fees matter more than you think](https://cuencahighlife.com/why-payment-fees-matter-more-than-you-think/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47236582)

This article explains why payment fees, like the ones charged by Visa or Mastercard, are a bigger deal than most people think. It uses the example of taxi drivers and small shop owners in Cuenca, Ecuador, who now often use QR code payments that seem “free” to them.

In Ecuador, many digital payments go straight from one bank account to another with almost no fee. This is different from countries like the US or UK, where card companies take a fee from every transaction. These card fees might look small—maybe 4%—but if a shop makes only a $50 profit on a $150 sale, that 4% ($6) is actually 12% of their profit. It gets worse when sales tax is included, because the fee is charged on the whole price, including tax. For small businesses with slim profit margins, these payment fees can really hurt. They add up quickly, especially for cafés, small stores, and taxi drivers.

Card systems, like Visa and Mastercard, do make payments easy and safe for buyers. But their fees help pay for this, and those costs are often invisible to the customer. In contrast, QR code payment systems like Deuna in Ecuador, or Pix in Brazil, let people pay quickly with almost no fee. Money moves instantly, and the shop gets the full amount. These systems are simple: the bank just moves money from one account to another, skipping the big card networks and their costs. Many shop owners have accounts at several banks, so they can accept many different QR payments.

These new systems are changing how people pay in places like Brazil, where Pix is now used everywhere, from street vendors to supermarkets. Card companies now have to compete with these cheaper, faster systems. The article points out that while card companies are not “bad,” rising fees can force shops to raise prices or stop taking cards, which can hurt everyone.

In the comments, some people say they never realized how much these fees cost small businesses. Others explain that high payment fees are one reason some stores offer discounts for cash or set minimum amounts for card payments. A few say that in their countries, QR code systems are growing, but many people still like cards for the safety and rewards they offer. Some merchants join the discussion to say that fees really do make a difference, especially when their profits are already low. Other readers point out that while new systems are good for small stores, they may not offer as much protection against fraud. One person shares that they avoid cards when possible to help local shops, while another says they value the speed and safety of cards, even if it means higher prices. There is also debate about whether governments should limit card fees, or if the market should decide. Some worry that big banks could end up controlling the new payment systems too, but others feel that competition from things like QR codes is a good step forward. Overall, people agree that payment fees matter and can affect everyone, even if we do not see them.

## An Interactive Intro to CRDTs (2023)

- 原文链接: [An Interactive Intro to CRDTs (2023)](https://jakelazaroff.com/words/an-interactive-intro-to-crdts/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47237460)

This article explains what CRDTs are and how they help computers agree on shared data, even when working offline or at different times. CRDT stands for "Conflict-free Replicated Data Type," and it's a way to let many users change the same data and later combine their changes without errors.

The article first says there are two types: state-based and operation-based CRDTs. State-based CRDTs send the whole data state to others and merge them, while operation-based CRDTs send only the actions (like "add" or "remove"). This post only talks about state-based CRDTs.

A CRDT needs three things: a value (the data), a state (extra info for syncing), and a merge function (to combine states). The merge function must work the same no matter the order or how many times it's called.

The article gives a simple example: the Last Write Wins Register (LWW Register). This holds one value, and always keeps the newest value, using a timestamp. If two writes happen at the same time, it uses an ID to pick one. There's also a Last Write Wins Map (LWW Map), which is like a dictionary with many values. Each value in the map is a LWW Register. When you delete something from the map, it isn’t really gone; instead, it's marked as deleted (a "tombstone") so everyone can agree it was removed.

The code examples show how to set values, get them, delete them, and merge changes from other peers. The article says these ideas are the building blocks for real apps, like a shared drawing tool.

In the Hacker News comments, many users liked how clear and easy the article is. Some said the interactive examples helped them understand CRDTs better than math-heavy papers. Others pointed out that CRDTs can become large over time because of tombstones, and asked about ways to clean up old data. A few people said operation-based CRDTs are often more efficient, but harder to build. Some wondered if using real clocks instead of simple numbers might cause problems. Others shared that CRDTs are used in real apps, like collaborative editors, showing the idea is practical. Some users discussed how merging and resolving conflicts can be tricky in edge cases. A few people asked for more advanced examples, like lists or text editing. Others shared links to open-source CRDT libraries. Overall, most agreed that CRDTs are a useful and important tool for building collaborative software.

## Don't become an engineering manager

- 原文链接: [Don't become an engineering manager](https://newsletter.manager.dev/p/dont-become-an-engineering-manager)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47232727)

This article talks about why a friend decided not to take a promotion to Engineering Manager. In the past, the author would always suggest trying management for the experience, but now thinks it’s not the right time.

The article explains that moving into management means less time for hands-on coding and learning new tech. Managers spend most of their days in meetings and dealing with people problems, not building things. The tech world is changing fast, and it’s hard for managers to keep up with new tools and ideas. At the same time, companies are reducing the number of managers by having more engineers report to each manager. Because of this, there are fewer chances to move up to higher manager roles like Director or VP.

The article also says that being a manager does not always pay more. Some top engineers, like Staff Engineers, can earn as much or more than Engineering Managers, especially if they switch companies. It’s also easier to prove your value and get promoted as an individual contributor (IC) than as a manager, since managers can get stuck with the same team and little chance to grow.

The author does say that management can be a good job if you really enjoy helping people and leading teams. But if you just want to learn new tech and build things, staying an engineer is a better choice right now. The author suggests waiting a few years before moving into management, unless you feel a strong personal interest in it.

In the Hacker News comments, some people agree with the article and say they are happier as engineers than as managers. They like coding and learning new things, and don’t want to spend their days in meetings. Others say that management can be rewarding if you enjoy helping teams grow and solving people problems.

A few commenters point out that every company is different. In some places, managers still do technical work. Some people mention that being a good manager is a skill, not just a step up, and it isn’t for everyone. There are also comments about pay—some say managers are paid more, but others have seen top engineers with higher salaries.

Several readers share stories about moving into management, not liking it, and returning to engineering. Others say that management skills are useful, even if you go back to coding later. There are also concerns about job security: managers might be at risk if companies cut middle management, while engineers are always needed. Overall, many agree it’s important to think about what you enjoy, not just what seems like the next step.
