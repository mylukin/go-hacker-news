Next, we have news from Apple. Apple is changing how its "Hide My Email" and "Sign in with Apple" services work. From now on, new email aliases from these services will use the @private.icloud.com domain. Before, these aliases looked like normal iCloud emails, so it was harder for websites to block them. Now, if a website wants to stop people from using these private emails, they just need to block anything ending with @private.icloud.com. This makes it much easier for companies to block Apple’s privacy tool.

The article warns that this change will make Hide My Email less useful. Many websites already block temporary email services, and with this change, Hide My Email could be next. There is a tip: if you have iCloud+, you can still make some aliases with the old domain before the change happens. The writer hopes Apple will listen to feedback and not go through with this update.

Next, there’s a technical article about making HTTP requests from Bash using /dev/tcp when tools like curl or wget are missing. This is useful in Docker containers with just a basic shell. Bash can open a TCP socket by redirecting to /dev/tcp/host/port. You send HTTP requests with printf and read the response with cat. You should use the "Connection: close" header, or the command might hang. This method cannot handle HTTPS and does not support redirects or compressed responses. Not all shells support /dev/tcp, and it is not as safe or flexible as tools like curl.

In the comments, many are surprised by the /dev/tcp trick. Some have used it for quick debugging or in small Docker images. Others warn that it is not good for production, since it cannot handle HTTPS or complex needs. Some say it is useful for simple health checks. There are also comments about how this helps people understand networking basics. A few worry about using non-standard Bash features in scripts that might run in different places. Most agree it is a handy trick for special cases, but not a replacement for real HTTP tools.

Now for something different: an article about how a mechanical watch works. Mechanical watches use springs, gears, and careful engineering to keep time, with no batteries. The heart of the watch is the “movement.” You wind a mainspring, which slowly unwinds and powers a set of gears. A special part called the escapement lets the gears move step by step. The balance wheel swings back and forth, making the watch tick at a regular rate. Tiny jewels reduce friction, and shock protectors stop damage. You wind the watch by turning the crown, and some watches can wind themselves with your wrist movement. All these tiny parts fit into a space the size of a credit card.

In the comments, people loved the article’s clear explanations and interactive animations. Watch lovers praised the detail, and some shared stories about fixing or making watches. Others talked about how amazing it is that such small parts can work so well together. Some pointed out that mechanical watches are not as accurate as digital ones, but are more interesting as machines. There were questions about gear shapes and challenges of repairing old watches. Many now understand why mechanical watches are expensive—they are hard to build and fix. Some people just enjoyed seeing how things work. Many agreed that, even if mechanical watches are less practical today, they are a great example of human creativity and engineering.

Now, a security topic: the article says you should stop using JWTs (JSON Web Tokens) for keeping users logged in. JWTs were not made for session management, and using them this way is not safe. JWTs are for short-lived use, maybe five minutes or less, not for long sessions. Some people think JWTs are "stateless," but secure systems always need some state, like a database. Regular session cookies are better and easier. The article says security experts do not trust the JWT standard, and there have been problems in the past. PASETO is a safer choice for short-lived tokens, but not for sessions. Also, do not store tokens in localStorage—it is not safe. Most web frameworks make sessions easy, and you can use cookies for both web and mobile apps.

In the comments, many people agree. Some use Redis or other caches for sessions in big apps, but most apps do not need this. Some readers are confused about mobile sessions, but the answer is you can use the same tools as web. One commenter says JWTs make everything manual and hard, while sessions are simple. JWTs can help in microservices or single sign-on, but not for regular logins. Some argue that JWTs are fine if used correctly, but most agree cookies and server sessions are best for login. The main advice: use cookies and server sessions for login, and only use JWTs for special, short-lived tasks.
