- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48797916)

A new project called OpenPrinter wants to make a paper printer that is easy to repair, refill, and open source. The printer is made to last a long time, uses refillable ink, and works with standard cartridges you can buy almost anywhere.

With OpenPrinter, you can refill ink easily, lower your printing costs, and use less plastic waste. You get full control of how you use your ink: print only in black or color, and you can use cartridges separately—no more “can’t print black because yellow is empty” errors. The printer works with both standard paper sheets and paper rolls, letting you print banners or custom sizes. There’s even a built-in cutter for creative jobs. You can put the printer on your desk or mount it on the wall, and it’s very compact and easy to move.

Inside, it uses open source electronics like a Raspberry Pi and STM32 board. It runs CUPS, so it works with Windows, macOS, Linux, Android, and iOS, and you don’t need extra drivers. The printer is built from standard, open source parts—most plastic pieces can be 3D printed, and replacement parts will be available for a long time. You can buy it as a kit or ready to use. All the design files will be open source, but the creators have patents and licenses to keep the project non-commercial and collaborative.

The printer supports common ink cartridges from HP, and you can buy ink bottles to refill them. The team will provide tutorial videos, and the electronic boards will be sold by distributors. To scan documents, you can use your phone with free apps like Genius Scan or CamScanner instead of a traditional scanner.

## New AI tutor achieves 0.71-1.30 SD effect size in Dartmouth course [pdf]

- 原文链接: [New AI tutor achieves 0.71-1.30 SD effect size in Dartmouth course [pdf]](https://intextbooks.science.uu.nl/workshop2026/files/itb26_s1s2.pdf)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48796817)

This article is about Phosphor, an AI-powered digital learning platform tested in a Dartmouth College statistics course. The study looked at how using Phosphor affected student engagement and exam scores.

The platform adds AI-graded quizzes directly into online reading lessons. These quizzes use both multiple-choice and open-ended (constructed-response) questions. The AI grades open-ended answers using set rules from the teacher. Students can take quizzes as many times as they want, and the system was optional and ungraded. Still, over 90% of the students tried the platform, which is much higher than normal reading rates for textbooks (usually only 10–15%). Students completed nearly half of all possible quizzes, suggesting strong engagement. The study found that students who completed more Phosphor lessons and reviews did better on their final exams. The effect size was between 0.71 and 1.30 standard deviations, which is large for education. The best results came from students who did all the module reviews and lessons, who scored over 7 points higher on the final exam.

The study also tested different quiz formats. When quizzes included open-ended questions, student performance improved more than with multiple-choice only. In one module, removing open-ended questions made engagement rise, but learning gains dropped. The platform also had a chat assistant powered by AI, but students rarely used it, saying that other AI tools or the provided content were enough for their needs.

Most students found Phosphor more engaging than regular readings and said it helped them remember the material and prepare for class. Surveys showed that students liked having practice questions mixed with the content. However, since the study was not randomized, students who used Phosphor more might just be more motivated in general. The authors note this as a limitation and plan to run more controlled tests in the future.

In the Hacker News comments, many users were impressed by the high engagement and effect size. Some thought embedding practice questions into reading is a simple but powerful idea, and they liked that the quizzes were optional. Others worried that the most motivated students might just be the ones using the platform, so results could be biased. A few questioned if AI could grade open-ended questions as well as humans, especially for complex answers. Some users shared their own problems with students not reading textbooks and thought this approach could help. Others pointed out that too many quizzes or strict grading might lower engagement over time. Some praised the openness about the study’s limits and the call for more research. A few were skeptical about relying on AI in education, but most said this kind of tool could make learning more active and effective if used well.

## Completing a Computer Science Degree on Coursera

This article is about someone who finished a Computer Science degree online through Coursera, after working in tech for 20 years without a degree. The writer shares what it was like, what was hard, what was good, and gives advice for others thinking about doing the same.

The degree was 100% remote, run by University of London, and all lectures, assignments, and exams are on Coursera. You can join with a high school diploma or by passing two starter modules. The writer liked that you pay per module, and sometimes you can skip modules if you have other certificates, which also saves money. Even though the writer already had lots of tech experience, the classes and exams were tough and took a lot of work, especially near the end. The full degree took about three and a half years, with some very busy periods. The total cost was about £17,000, and the writer could claim some of it as a tax deduction because it improved their job skills.

There were many interesting projects, like making games, building a DJ simulator, and a final AI project for cancer detection. The writer enjoyed meeting students from around the world, who had all kinds of life stories—some even studied from war zones or refugee camps. But there were also problems: grades took a long time, group projects didn’t always work well, and the online platform had some bugs. Exams were stressful because of strict proctoring software, and support was slow to respond.

The writer gives tips: start assignments early, check your work before submitting, find a good study routine, finish important classes first, and pay attention to rules and advice shared in student forums. The rise of AI tools like ChatGPT changed how people studied and talked in the course, and the university had to make new rules about using AI in coursework.

Finally, a few people shared tips on balancing work, study, and life, and said that even though online learning is tough, it’s worth it for those who really want to learn.

## The future of Flipper Zero development

- 原文链接: [The future of Flipper Zero development](https://blog.flipper.net/future-of-flipper-zero-development/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48796552)

The article talks about the future of the Flipper Zero device and its software. There were rumors that the team had stopped working on the firmware, but they explain that they are still supporting it—just with new rules and less direct contact.

Flipper Zero began with a Kickstarter in 2020. At first, people doubted the project, but the team worked hard through problems like supply shortages and criticism. They kept their promises: every backer got their device, all main features were finished, and the device is sold in many countries. They also built tools for developers, so people could make their own apps and firmware for Flipper Zero.

The main limit is that Flipper Zero only has 700 KB of memory for the firmware. To get around this, they made it so extra features could be loaded from a microSD card as apps. In 2024, they released a big update—firmware 1.0—which made the device stable, the interface better, and the developer tools easier to use. After this, the team decided to focus more on making new devices, not just updating firmware.

But, because the community cared so much, the team will keep supporting the firmware. They will now use GitHub Discussions for all feature requests. The team will review requests with the most votes. They have new rules for code contributions and more strict tests to make sure new code does not break things.

The team cannot chat in real time anymore, because there are too many users now. All talks will happen on GitHub, and only the most important requests will be reviewed. They ask people to only suggest features that really help many users, not just ideas for one person.

From the comments, some people say they are happy the team listened to the community. Others worry that the new rules will slow down improvements because the team is small and busy. Some users like the GitHub voting system, while others feel it may ignore helpful but unpopular ideas. A few commenters praise how open the team is about their problems and choices. There are people who wish the team could still talk more on other channels, like forums or chat, but understand why it’s hard with so many users. Some discuss the memory limit—some think it’s a good way to focus on quality, others wish for more hardware power. Many agree that the Flipper Zero story is a rare example of a crowdfunding project that kept its promises. A few note that the move toward apps and community code is smart, while others worry about security risks if code is not checked well. In general, most comments support the new approach but hope the team will stay open to feedback and keep the device fun for everyone.

## Mr. Baby Paint and accidentally discovering a new cellular automata

- 原文链接: [Mr. Baby Paint and accidentally discovering a new cellular automata](https://tekstien-marginaalien-keskus.aalto.fi/residenssi/heikki/blog/004-december-2/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48770291)

This article is about making a simple drawing app for toddlers, called Mr. Baby Paint, and how the author found a new kind of cellular automata by accident while building it. The project started because the author's 3-year-old child wanted to use the computer, but normal apps like MS Paint were too hard and frustrating for young kids.

The main idea for Mr. Baby Paint was to have a fullscreen canvas with no menus or toolbars. The child could move the mouse and draw, while the parent could use keyboard shortcuts to help. Every action in the app gives feedback—drawing a mark, making a sound, or showing a visual effect—so nothing feels like a mistake. The app is designed for co-op play: the child draws, and the parent helps with harder controls.

The app solves a common problem: when toddlers move the mouse quickly, the drawing can look jagged because the computer only records points every few milliseconds. To fix this, the app uses a smooth curve (called a Catmull-Rom spline) between points, making lines look more natural.

Settings are changed by moving the mouse to the edges of the screen, which is easier for toddlers than clicking small buttons. For example, moving the mouse to the left edge changes the brush size or font, while the bottom edge changes the color.

A special part of the app is the fill tool. Instead of filling instantly, like in most drawing apps, the fill spreads slowly, inspired by the game Mario Paint. The author allowed multiple fills to run at once, and by accident, saw strange, organic patterns appear when fills collided—a new kind of cellular automata. These patterns can last for thousands of steps and look almost alive.

The app also includes custom fonts. The author made "pixel-fattening" scripts to thicken thin lines of bitmap fonts, testing many algorithms to make the letters chunkier but still clear. The process included experiments with distance fields, gap detection, and other graphics tricks, and the author shares images showing the results.

Mr. Baby Paint is sold for $4.99 and works on Mac, Windows, and Linux. The author was happy to see about 30 copies sold.

In the Hacker News comments, many people loved the idea of building software for toddlers and shared their own stories about making or using simple apps with kids. Some users praised the focus on removing distractions and menus, saying lots of modern tools are not friendly for children. Others were curious about the technical parts, like using Catmull-Rom splines and the discovery of cellular automata through overlapping fills. A few people discussed the "happy accident" of new features appearing during development and how important it is to keep these fun bugs in creative software. Some commenters wanted to see more educational tools that encourage kids to explore and experiment, while others noted how co-op play with a parent can make digital art much more fun for young children. There were also questions about the pixel-fattening methods and how these could be used in other software. A few users suggested making the app open source or adding more sharing features, but others thought the paid model was fair for a small, creative project.
