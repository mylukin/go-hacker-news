Hello everyone, this is the 2026-07-05 episode of Hacker News Daily Podcast. Today, we have a mix of stories about open source hardware, creative software, education, and even a bit of beer history. Let’s get started.

First, let’s talk about OpenPrinter, a new open source printer project. Many developers know the pain of dealing with commercial printers—locked down, expensive ink, DRM, and errors like “can’t print black because yellow is empty.” OpenPrinter wants to solve these problems. The printer is designed for easy repair and refilling. It takes standard, widely available HP ink cartridges, supports refillable ink bottles, and lets you print in black or color with full control. You can even print on both sheets and paper rolls, making banners or custom sizes, and there’s a built-in cutter for creative work. It’s compact, can sit on your desk or hang on the wall, and most of the plastic parts are 3D printable.

Inside, OpenPrinter uses a Raspberry Pi and STM32 board, running CUPS, so it works with almost any operating system without special drivers. You can buy it as a kit or ready to use, and all design files will be open source, though there are patents and licenses to keep it non-commercial. Scanning is handled by your phone, not a built-in scanner.

On Hacker News, people are excited about a repairable printer with no artificial limits. Many dislike how big printer companies use DRM or block refills. Some users are happy about standard cartridges and support for different paper. Others worry about patents and non-commercial licenses slowing adoption or community growth. Some ask about print quality and speed, since final specs are not set. A few like that parts are 3D printable, but others wonder if this makes it hard for those without a 3D printer. There are also questions about long-term support, since printers are hard to build and maintain. Still, most people hope OpenPrinter will push the industry toward more open and user-friendly designs.

Next, let’s look at Organic Maps, a free, open source offline maps and GPS app. It’s focused on privacy—no tracking, no ads, no data collection, and no push notifications. The app is great for hiking, biking, traveling, or driving. You can download maps for offline use, save battery, and keep your data private. Features include hiking and cycling trails, elevation info, voice navigation, CarPlay and Android Auto support, and offline search. It uses OpenStreetMap data, so maps are often very detailed, even for remote places. The app is fully open source and supported by community donations.

Hacker News users praise Organic Maps as a strong alternative to Google Maps, especially for privacy and offline use. Many like the simple, fast interface without distractions. Some wish for more frequent map updates or more points of interest, and a few miss live traffic or transit times. People say it’s especially good for hiking and cycling, but maybe less so for car navigation in cities. There’s a lot of love for the privacy focus, with some users sharing tips on downloading maps before trips and asking how to contribute new data. Many thank the developers for keeping it free and open.

Moving on, let’s talk about Phosphor, an AI-powered digital learning platform tested at Dartmouth College. Phosphor puts AI-graded quizzes right inside online reading lessons, with both multiple-choice and open-ended questions. Students can retry quizzes as much as they want, and the system is optional and ungraded. Despite this, over 90% of students used it—much higher than normal textbook reading rates. Doing more Phosphor lessons led to better exam scores, with strong effects for those who completed all reviews.

The study found that open-ended questions improved learning more than multiple-choice alone, though dropping them made more students engage. There’s also an AI chat assistant, but most students didn’t use it, preferring the main content or other AI tools.

Most students said Phosphor was more engaging than normal readings and helped them remember material. But the study was not randomized, so students who used Phosphor more may just be more motivated. The authors plan more research to test this.

Hacker News comments are mostly positive. Users are impressed by the high engagement and learning gains. Many like the simple idea of mixing quizzes with reading and that quizzes are optional. Some worry about bias—maybe only motivated students use the tool. Others wonder if AI can grade complex answers as well as humans. Some teachers share their own struggles getting students to read and think this could help. A few warn that too many quizzes or strict grading could hurt engagement over time. People praise the open discussion of limits and the plan for more research. Most agree this kind of tool can make learning more active if used well.

Let’s shift gears to online education. One article shares the story of finishing a Computer Science degree fully online through Coursera and the University of London, after 20 years working in tech without a degree. The program is remote, lets you pay per module, and sometimes skip classes if you have other certificates. The writer says the courses and exams were tough, taking three and a half years to finish, and costing about £17,000, with some tax deduction possible.

Projects included making games, a DJ simulator, and an AI project for cancer detection. Students came from all over the world, including some studying from war zones. Problems included slow grading, group projects that didn’t always go well, platform bugs, and stressful proctored exams.

The writer’s tips: start early, check your work, find a good study routine, focus on important classes first, and follow the advice in forums. The rise of AI tools during the program changed how people studied and forced the university to make new rules.

Hacker News users praise the effort to finish a hard degree while working. Others share their own online study stories, saying self-discipline is key. Some are surprised at the cost, but others note it’s still cheaper than in-person study in the US or UK. There’s discussion about whether online degrees are respected by employers, but many agree skills and projects matter more, especially in tech. Slow grading is a common worry. Some like the idea of skipping classes, but others wonder if it means missing key skills. Tips on balancing work, study, and life round out the discussion.

Now, let’s talk about Homegames, a free, open source platform to make and play games right in your browser—no account needed. You get a simple code editor with live preview, including multiplayer. You can upload images, sounds, draw, and record things in the studio. Games can be shared or kept private. The whole platform and games use the GPLv3 license, so you can read, change, or fork the code. Homegames can be self-hosted on your own machine or server, including the API, so your games are safe even if the main site disappears. The project started in 2018 and is mostly built by one person as a side project.

Hacker News users like how easy it is to create and share games, and praise the open source and self-hosting features. Some think it’s great for beginners to learn and be creative. There are questions about how easy it is to learn, the quality of tutorials, and how it compares to other platforms like itch.io or Pico-8. Some are curious about long-term support and suggest building a community or getting more contributors. Others ask about multiplayer support, game performance, and what kinds of games work best. Most users are supportive and excited to see new open projects like this.

Up next is Flipper Zero, a device that started on Kickstarter in 2020 and has become very popular. Recently, there were rumors that the team stopped working on firmware, but they explained they are still supporting it—just with new rules and less direct contact. Flipper Zero has only 700 KB of memory for firmware, so extra features are now apps you load from a microSD card. Firmware 1.0 brought a stable interface and better developer tools. The team will now focus more on new devices, but the firmware will still get support. All feature requests and talks happen on GitHub, and only the most voted requests are reviewed. Code testing is now stricter, and the team cannot chat in real time anymore because of the large user base.

Comments are mixed. Some users are glad the team listened to the community. Others worry the new rules will slow improvements. Some like the GitHub voting system, others think it may miss good but less popular ideas. Many praise the team’s openness and the fact that the project delivered on its promises. There’s also discussion about the memory limit—a good way to focus on quality, but some want more powerful hardware. Most agree the move to apps and open community code is smart but warn about security if code isn’t checked well. Overall, people hope the team stays open and keeps the device fun.

Let’s talk about Dungeon Proof Crawler, a web game that mixes fantasy adventure with real math proofs. You play to win back a stolen wedding ring, fighting monsters by solving logic puzzles. The game uses the Algae kernel and WebAssembly, so it runs smoothly in any browser. It’s made as a “love letter” to everyone who likes logic, not just math experts. Puzzles get harder as you go, and the story helps make proofs less scary.

On Hacker News, people like the mix of games and learning. Some say proofs were always hard for them, and this game looks like a fun way to practice. Others remember old “edutainment” games and think gamifying education helps motivation. Some worry puzzles might be too hard for beginners, and suggest adding hints or easier levels. The story is seen as charming and helps learning feel less serious. There’s interest in using games like this to teach other subjects, and some praise the technical choice of WebAssembly for smooth play. Overall, users are excited about using games to teach tough ideas in a friendly way.

Next, there’s a website listing computers that appeared in movies and TV. You can look up a computer model and see all the shows and movies it appeared in, sometimes with detailed info or links. The list covers everything from old mainframes to modern laptops, with brands like Apple, IBM, and Commodore, and includes rare and famous machines. Entries show where to spot them on screen, adding a fun layer for fans of movies and tech history.

Hacker News users love the nostalgia, sharing memories of their own first computers seen in films. Some point out mistakes, like computers shown before they existed or time mismatches. Many use the site to check what machines were in their favorite shows. There are requests for more pictures or the ability to search by movie. Overall, people thank the creator for a fun and useful resource.

Now for something different: an article about Burton upon Trent, the English town famous for beer. The writer visits to learn why it’s called the best place for a pint. The town is full of beer history, with old brewery signs, both big and small brewers, and even street names about beer. In the 1800s, Burton was the world’s beer capital, with 34 breweries by 1884. The secret is the local water, rich in minerals like gypsum, which gives Burton beer its special taste.

On Hacker News, people enjoy the beer history lesson. Some share stories of visiting Burton or trying its beer, and talk about how water changes beer flavor. There is sadness about closed breweries and the loss of the National Brewing Centre. Some users wish more small brewers could survive, while others are glad creative brewers still exist in Burton. The story makes some want to visit, and there’s curiosity about other towns with similar beer culture.

Finally, let’s talk about Mr. Baby Paint, a simple drawing app for toddlers, built because the author’s 3-year-old couldn’t use standard drawing programs. The app is full-screen, with no menus or toolbars, and lets kids draw with smooth lines using Catmull-Rom splines. Parents help using keyboard shortcuts, and settings are changed by moving the mouse to the screen edges—much easier for kids.

A unique feature is the fill tool, inspired by Mario Paint. Instead of filling instantly, fills spread slowly, and when they meet, they create strange, living patterns—a new kind of cellular automata found by accident. The app also features custom fonts, with the author developing scripts to “fatten” pixel fonts for better clarity.

Mr. Baby Paint is sold for a small fee and works on all desktop systems. On Hacker News, people love the focus on kids, sharing stories about making or using simple apps with their own children. There’s praise for removing distractions and making digital art friendly for young kids. Some users are interested in the technical details, like splines and cellular automata. Others talk about the value of “happy accidents” in creative coding. Many want more kid-friendly, experimental software, and some suggest making it open source, though most agree the small price is fair.
