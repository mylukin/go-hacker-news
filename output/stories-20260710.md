## QuadRF can spot drones and see WiFi through my wall

- 原文链接: [QuadRF can spot drones and see WiFi through my wall](https://www.jeffgeerling.com/blog/2026/quadrf-can-spot-drones-and-see-wifi-through-my-wall/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48861717)

QuadRF is a small device that uses a Raspberry Pi 5 and an FPGA to find drones and see WiFi signals through walls. The article explains how the QuadRF works, what it can do, and the experience of testing it.

The QuadRF uses a special radio antenna (phased array) and advanced signal processing. It can show where WiFi networks are, even if they are on the other side of a wall. It can also track flying drones by picking up their radio signals. The device is open source, and the author notes that if hobbyists can make this, governments probably have much more powerful versions already.

The software on QuadRF lets you see and analyze radio signals. You can use your phone or computer to connect to it and use tools like GNU Radio or custom programs. One fun tool is an augmented reality (AR) viewer that shows WiFi signals as colorful blobs through your camera. The device covers the 4.9–6 GHz frequency range, which includes many WiFi and drone signals.

The author and his dad tested the device by flying a drone and walking around with QuadRF. They could easily spot the drone and see different WiFi networks. The user interface is still a bit rough, and adjusting the gain (signal strength) was not easy while moving. The device is still in early development, but it already works well.

The QuadRF streams radio data really fast by using the Raspberry Pi’s camera connectors (MIPI), which can handle over 5 Gbps. This is faster and more reliable than USB. You can also link several QuadRF devices together for bigger experiments.

The author is impressed with the hardware and excited to see what the final version will be like. He warns that, like any crowdfunded gadget, it might take time to arrive and could change before shipping.

In the Hacker News comments, some people are amazed by the technology and think it is cool that individuals can now do things that used to be possible only for governments or big companies. Others are worried about privacy, since this means anyone could watch your WiFi traffic or spot drones near your home. Some users discuss the technical details, like how impressive it is to use MIPI lanes for high-speed radio data, and how chaining devices could help with big antenna projects.

A few commenters think the device could be useful for security research, WiFi troubleshooting, or even finding hidden cameras and devices. Others ask about the legal issues of scanning WiFi or tracking drones, since rules can be strict in some countries. Some people mention that the software interface needs improvement, while others share tips or ideas for new features.

Several users remember older radio projects and compare how much easier and cheaper it is now to experiment with SDR (software-defined radio). There is excitement about connecting multiple QuadRF modules and doing big science experiments, but also caution about possible misuse. Overall, people are interested, hopeful about what open hardware can bring, and curious to see what the community will build next.

## GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra produces proof of the Cycle Double Cover Conjecture [pdf]

- 原文链接: [GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra produces proof of the Cycle Double Cover Conjecture [pdf]](https://cdn.openai.com/pdf/04d1d1e4-bc75-476a-97cf-49055cd98d31/cdc_proof.pdf)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48863490)

This article says that GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra, an AI, has produced a proof for the Cycle Double Cover Conjecture in graph theory. The conjecture says every bridgeless undirected graph can have its edges covered exactly twice by a set of cycles.

The proof focuses on cubic graphs (graphs where each node has three edges) because it is enough to solve the problem for these graphs. The author explains earlier work, like how the conjecture is already true for planar graphs and some special cubic graphs. The core method uses something called a “nowhere-zero flow” from group theory, which is a way of labeling edges with numbers from a mathematical group so that the sum at each node is zero. The group used here is F32, which is a finite abelian group with 32 elements.

The proof uses these flows to assign two labels to every edge, making sure that at every node, each possible label appears zero or two times. This is similar to a 3-edge-coloring, but a bit more relaxed. The cycles covering the edges are built from these labelings. The author shows, using linear algebra, that this construction always works.

This result is important because the Cycle Double Cover Conjecture has been open for decades, and many smart people have worked on it. The article says the proof and writeup were both done by AI, not by a human.

In the comment section, some people are excited and amazed that AI could solve such a long-standing problem. Others are suspicious—some think this might be a joke or a clever April Fools’ post, since the date is 2026 and the PDF is short for such a big result. A few users debate if the proof is correct, noting that real mathematical proofs of this type are usually checked by experts over months or years. Some point out that the AI-generated writeup is clear and well-structured, even if the math is hard to follow. Others wonder what this means for the future of mathematics—will AI solve all open problems, or is human intuition still needed? ## The tech of 'Terminator 2' – an oral history (2017)

- 原文链接: [The tech of 'Terminator 2' – an oral history (2017)](https://vfxblog.com/2017/08/23/the-tech-of-terminator-2-an-oral-history/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48862365)

This article is about how the special effects team made the famous liquid metal T-1000 in the movie “Terminator 2” in 1991. The story is told by the people who worked at ILM, the studio that made the visual effects.

Back then, computer graphics were very new, and the team had to invent new tools and ways to do things because there wasn’t software for these effects yet. They used programs like Alias, and even wrote their own tools like “Make Sticky” and “Body Sock” to connect and move pieces of the digital character smoothly. The team worked with a lot of hardware, like expensive SGI computers, and even a gigabyte of storage cost $9,000. They had a small group—just a dozen or so people at first—but they managed to create the amazing effects with lots of work and testing.

To make the T-1000 look like liquid metal, they created special shaders and reflection tricks since there was no ray-tracing yet. For the morphing scenes, like when the T-1000 walks through bars or changes shape, they used hand animation and new software to blend real actor shots with digital models. They scanned the actor’s body, painted a grid on him, and animated his movements by hand, since there was no motion capture. They also had to solve small problems, like making sure textures “stuck” to the models as they moved, or hiding seams where digital body parts joined.

The team worked long hours, sometimes 80 hours a week, and everyone did many jobs—modeling, animating, rendering, and compositing. They had to move data between programs, write lots of scripts, and even change big computer discs by hand in the basement. Most effects were tested overnight, and sometimes shots had to be finished in low resolution because there wasn’t time to redo them. The group felt like a family, helping each other and finding ways to get things done under pressure.

In the Hacker News comments, many users praised the article, saying it shows how much creativity and problem-solving was needed before today’s tools existed. Some readers remembered seeing “Terminator 2” and being amazed by the effects, not knowing how hard they were to make. Others pointed out that back then, the artists had to understand both programming and art, while now jobs are more separated. A few users said they miss the “DIY” spirit of those days, when teams made new tools just for a project.

Some commenters talked about how expensive and slow computers were, and how working with film was much harder than with today’s digital cameras. There were also comments about how the mix of practical effects and digital work together made the movie special. Others mentioned that today’s movies use more effects, but sometimes the results feel less impressive because the process is less personal.

Overall, the comments agreed that the work on “Terminator 2” changed movies and inspired many people to get into computer graphics and programming. Some wished modern teams could have the same tight teamwork and excitement, even as technology makes things easier.

## New York City to become first in US to ban deceptive subscription practices

- 原文链接: [New York City to become first in US to ban deceptive subscription practices](https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/jul/10/new-york-city-deceptive-subscriptions-ban)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48863464)

New York City just passed a rule to stop companies from tricking people into paying for subscriptions they don’t want or can’t easily cancel. This is the first time a US city has made such a ban, and it will start in October.

The new rule says companies must let people cancel subscriptions simply and quickly—no more waiting on the phone for a long time, sending letters, or going to a store. If companies break this rule, they can be fined $525 for each customer, plus more in back fees. The city also wants to stop “junk fees,” which are hidden costs added to things like apartment rentals, sports events, or hotels. If the new fee rule passes, all extra charges must be shown in the price up front, so people know the real cost before they buy.

These rules are meant to help people save money and stop companies from hiding the true price of goods and services. For example, renters in New York often see a low price online, but later find out there are many extra fees, making the real rent much higher. The city says this is unfair and makes it hard for people to compare prices. The new law could save New Yorkers over $160 million a year by stopping unwanted renewals. The city will also listen to public comments before the final fee rule goes into effect.

These changes are pushed by the city’s consumer protection office, led by Sam Levine and Mayor Zohran Mamdani. They say this move fights the “worst kind of incentive,” where companies compete to hide costs instead of offering better prices. This is part of a bigger plan to make the city more affordable, especially for renters and working people.

People in the Hacker News comments mostly agree that hidden fees and hard-to-cancel subscriptions are a big problem. Many share stories about trying to cancel gym memberships or streaming services but facing lots of tricks and delays. Some say these tricks are common everywhere, not just in New York, and hope other cities or states will follow with similar rules. Others think companies will find new ways to get around the law, like adding more steps to online cancelation or using confusing language. A few worry that strict rules might make some services more expensive for everyone, since companies could raise prices to cover possible fines. Some commenters also wonder if these rules will be enforced strongly, since big companies have lawyers and money to fight back. But many feel this law is a good first step and shows the government is trying to protect regular people from unfair business practices. Overall, most agree that clear prices and easy cancelation are things everyone should have.

## Combustion Engine Web-Based Simulator

- 原文链接: [Combustion Engine Web-Based Simulator](https://combustionlab.net)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48795900)

This article talks about a new web tool called Combustion Lab, which lets you play with a car engine simulator in your browser. The simulator models how a real four-cylinder turbocharged engine works, showing how power, torque, and other things change as you adjust settings.

You can change things like how many cylinders the engine has, how big the cylinders are, the compression ratio, turbo boost, and more. The tool uses math from real engineering, like the first law of thermodynamics, to show how pressure and temperature change inside the engine at every step as the piston moves. Combustion (burning fuel) is modeled with a special curve called the Wiebe function that shows how fast the fuel burns. There are also models for how heat is lost to the walls, how friction slows things down, and how much energy is lost by moving air in and out of the engine.

The simulator warns you about "knock," which is when the engine burns fuel too quickly and can get damaged. It uses a scientific method (Livengood–Wu integral) to decide when knock happens, and you can try different fuels or settings to see how to avoid it. The tool explains different outputs, like IMEP (the average pressure doing work), BMEP (how much pressure is left after losses), and BSFC (how much fuel is used for each unit of work). It also carefully says it is just a teaching tool, not for designing a real engine—real engines need more complex modeling.

The app shows you a live graph and numbers as you change things, so you can see how each setting affects engine performance. It also explains the four strokes of the engine: intake, compression, power, and exhaust, making it easy to learn how engines work.

People in the comments liked how detailed and educational this simulator is. Some said it reminded them of using professional tools, but in a much simpler way. Many enjoyed seeing how changing compression or boost caused knock, and how rich or lean fuel mixtures changed power and efficiency. A few users pointed out that the tool makes complicated engine math easier to understand for students and hobbyists. Some wanted more features, like support for rotary engines or more advanced fuel models. Others warned that while the trends are realistic, the numbers are not good enough for real engineering work. Some users appreciated that everything runs in the browser with no downloads. A few mentioned small bugs or wanted more documentation, but most found the tool fun and helpful for learning engine basics.
