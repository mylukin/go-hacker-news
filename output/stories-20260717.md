## The Zilog Z80 has turned 50

- 原文链接: [The Zilog Z80 has turned 50](https://goliath32.com/blog/z80.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48951461)

The article is about the Zilog Z80 processor turning 50 years old and looks back at its history and impact. The Z80 was released in 1976 and became a key part of many early home computers, game consoles, and industrial systems. 

The article explains that the Z80 was built to be compatible with the Intel 8080 but added more features and was easier to use in systems. Before the Z80, Intel made the 8008 and then the 8080, which both had limits, like needing several power supplies and tricky wiring. The Z80 solved many of these problems: it used only one power supply, had clearer wiring, and added more control signals for easier design. It also included two new index registers, more instructions, and better support for interrupts. The Z80’s assembly language was easier to read and write than the 8080’s, and many programs could run on both chips.

The article also shares stories from the author’s own experience building a Z80 computer in school, using old chips and learning about computer engineering. There are fun stories about teachers and old computers, like someone building a computer in a Tupperware box to write a thesis.

Zilog, the company behind the Z80, was started by engineers who left Intel. They made the Z80 cheap and powerful, and it sold very well. The Z80 was used in the original GameBoy and many home computers. Over time, Zilog stopped making bigger chips and focused on Z80 variants for industry. The Z80 was finally discontinued in 2024, but its design influenced many later chips, including those from Intel.

Hacker News commenters shared lots of memories and opinions. Some people talked about building their own Z80 computers or writing games and programs for Z80-based machines. Many remembered learning programming with Z80 assembly and how simple and clear the chip was compared to modern CPUs. A few noted that the Z80 was still used in some systems until recently, showing how reliable and flexible it was. Others pointed out that the Z80’s instruction set and design made it easier for beginners to understand how computers work.

Some commenters compared the Z80 to other chips from the same time, like the 6502, and discussed which was better for different uses. Others mentioned that the Z80’s design helped shape the computer industry by making computers cheaper and easier to build. A few people shared stories about finding Z80 chips in odd places, like in cash registers or old printers. There were also discussions about the business side, such as why IBM chose Intel over Zilog for the PC. Overall, many commenters felt nostalgic and grateful for the Z80, saying it helped start their careers or hobbies in computing.

## First atmosphere found on Earth-like planet in habitable zone of distant star

- 原文链接: [First atmosphere found on Earth-like planet in habitable zone of distant star](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cy4kdd1e0ejo)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48947560)

Scientists found an atmosphere around a rocky planet called LHS 1140b, which orbits a distant star in the zone where life could exist. This is the first time an atmosphere has been found on a small, Earth-like planet outside our solar system, making it a big step in the search for life elsewhere.

The planet is 48 light-years away and circles a red star that is cooler than our Sun. Out of thousands of distant planets, only a few are both rocky and in the “Goldilocks zone”—not too hot, not too cold, but just right for water and maybe life. Before now, none of these rocky planets in the Goldilocks zone were known to have an atmosphere.

The scientists found helium in the upper atmosphere of LHS 1140b. Helium alone cannot support life, but it is possible that other gases, maybe ones needed for life, are present lower down. The study’s authors say this discovery is not proof of life or even proof that life could exist there, but it is a hopeful sign for future research.

Other planets have also made headlines in the hunt for life. For example, K2-18b once seemed to have signs of a gas linked to life on Earth, but later studies found those signs were too weak. The TRAPPIST-1 system, with seven rocky planets, is still being studied, but so far, strong evidence for Earth-like atmospheres has not been found.

In the comments, many people are excited about this discovery. Some say it is a huge step forward because it shows we can study atmospheres on distant, small planets, not just big gas giants. Others remind everyone that helium is not a sign of life, so we are still far from finding living things out there. A few users are cautious, saying science news sometimes gets too excited about small steps—reminding us that finding an atmosphere does not mean finding life. Some commenters discuss how hard it is to study these distant worlds, and they praise the technology and teamwork needed for this research. There are also people who talk about the importance of the “Goldilocks zone” and how rare it is to find rocky planets there. Finally, a few are hopeful, saying this work is a sign that we may one day find a planet like Earth—or even life itself.

## Learning a few things about running SQLite

- 原文链接: [Learning a few things about running SQLite](https://jvns.ca/blog/2026/07/17/learning-about-running-sqlite/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48950122)

This article is about using SQLite as the database for a Django website and what the writer learned from real-world use. The writer thought SQLite would be simple for a small site but found out that databases still have tricky parts, even for simple projects.

First, the writer turned on WAL (Write-Ahead Logging) mode as many guides suggest. Then, they ran into a slow query using SQLite’s full-text search (FTS5) on a table with 4000 rows—it took 5 seconds. After running the ANALYZE command, the query became much faster, almost instant. ANALYZE helps the database make better choices by collecting stats about tables. The writer admits they don’t fully understand query plans yet but saw a big improvement.

Cleaning up the database (like deleting many rows) turned out to be hard. When deleting lots of rows, the operation took over 5 seconds, which caused other processes to fail because they couldn’t write to the database during that time. As a fix, the writer started deleting in small batches. This made them appreciate why some people use databases like Postgres that allow more than one writer at once.

Using Django’s ORM (Object-Relational Mapper) to make database queries mostly worked fine, since the database was small (about 10,000 rows), except for the earlier issue that ANALYZE fixed.

For backups, the writer tried two ways. One is using restic, which backs up the database file after making a copy with VACUUM INTO and compressing it. Sometimes the backup process used too much memory and failed. The second way is using Litestream, which does incremental backups and is more memory-efficient. They also mentioned the pain of dealing with AWS credentials.

The writer noticed you can use more than one SQLite database file in a project. In another project, they split tables into three separate database files, which worked well.

They finish by sharing that learning about these “basic” database features took time, and expect to keep learning more in the future.

In the Hacker News comments, some people agree that SQLite is good for small sites and admire its simplicity. Others warn that, while SQLite can work in production, it has limits—especially with many users or lots of writes, since only one write can happen at a time. A few readers share tips, like using batch deletes, running ANALYZE regularly, or moving to Postgres if you need more power. Some say they did not know about ANALYZE either and thank the writer for the tip. Others point out that using SQLite’s full-text search is powerful for small projects. There are jokes about learning these basics only after years of use. Some share their own SQLite stories, both good and bad. One person suggests testing backups by actually restoring them. Another says using multiple database files can be risky if you need transactions across them. Overall, many people like SQLite for small, simple projects but agree it’s important to know its limits and quirks.

## Lobste.rs is now running on SQLite

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48899847)

Lobste.rs, a popular tech news site, moved its main database from MariaDB to SQLite. The team waited for a busy Monday to see how the new setup would handle heavy traffic, and it worked very well—CPU and memory usage both dropped, the site felt faster, and costs are now lower.

The migration took a long time and had several steps. At first, the team thought about switching to PostgreSQL, but later decided on SQLite because the main volunteer had experience with it, and SQLite is simpler for their needs. The first try at switching failed because of performance problems, but after fixing some slow queries and adding better tests, the second try worked great. The team learned a lot about differences between MariaDB and SQLite, like missing features, different ways to handle text and numbers, and how search works. They also had to change some database columns and use special scripts to move all the data. Rails (the web framework) helped by setting good defaults for SQLite, but some things still needed manual work.

The team shared some lessons: good communication was key, realistic test data is important, and it’s hard to do a database migration without access to the real, live site data. They also wished for easier ways to spot slow queries before launching.

In the comments, many people cheered the successful migration and liked the clear write-up. Some were surprised that SQLite could handle a busy site, and noted how fast the site felt now. Others discussed technical details, like how SQLite allows many readers but only one writer at a time, and how to set up good defaults for better performance. There were debates about strict data types, with some preferring SQLite’s flexible approach and others wanting more safety. Several people discussed backup strategies, pointing out that while a daily backup is used, tools like Litestream could give even better protection. Some worried about losing data if the server crashed right before a backup, but others said this risk isn’t unique to SQLite. There were also questions about how updates and server moves work with SQLite—answers explained that since it’s just a file, moving or backing up is simple, but concurrency and migrations need care. A few users found minor bugs after the switch, but the team fixed them quickly. Overall, most commenters agreed that SQLite was a good, simple choice for Lobste.rs, but reminded that more complex or larger sites might still need bigger databases like PostgreSQL.

## Three ways people respond to a problem (other than solving it)

- 原文链接: [Three ways people respond to a problem (other than solving it)](https://improvesomething.today/responses-to-problems/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48947490)

This article talks about how people act when they face problems at work, especially in big companies. The writer is a consultant and says that often, people don’t really solve problems—they react in other ways. Besides trying to solve a problem, people usually do one of three things: push the problem somewhere else, work to keep the problem around, or create new problems.

First, pushing problems around means making things better in one place but worse in another. For example, one team fixes their part, but the trouble just moves to another team. This happens a lot in big groups, and the writer says the real answer is to change how the whole system works, not just blame workers.

Second, preserving problems is about people or groups who need a problem to exist because their job depends on it. This idea is called the Shirky Principle. Some companies or teams keep a problem alive because, without it, they are not needed. So, when you try to fix a problem, it’s smart to ask who might lose out if it goes away.

Third, promoting new problems happens when solving one issue creates another. The writer quotes Jerry Weinberg, who says once you fix your top problem, the next one just takes its place. There will always be problems to solve, so it’s important to choose which ones are really worth your time.

The writer’s tip is to make diagrams so everyone can see all the problems together and pick the most important ones to fix. This helps teams agree and not waste time on less important issues.

In the comments, many people say they see these behaviors in their own companies. Some agree that moving problems around is common and sometimes the only thing you can do in a big system. Others point out that leaders often don’t want to fix root causes because it’s hard or could hurt their position. A few people argue that preserving problems is not always bad—sometimes, problems keep people busy or give purpose to a group.

Some readers share stories where fixing one problem made bigger problems later. They say you should think carefully before trying to “solve” everything. Others mention that ignoring small problems can be wise, so you have energy for the big ones. A few comments talk about how changing incentives or company rules is the best way to stop these patterns. Overall, most readers agree that understanding these three ways people handle problems makes it easier to work in teams and get real results.

## The state of open source AI

- 原文链接: [The state of open source AI](https://stateofopensource.ai/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48947825)

This article talks about the state of open source AI in 2026 and how it is changing the tech world. It explains that many groups and countries now use open source AI models for different uses, like language, medicine, farming, and business, often to keep control over their own data.

The article says open source AI models are now almost as good as the best closed ones, especially for coding and following instructions. Inference costs (the price to use AI) have dropped a lot, from $20 to just $0.40 per million tokens in three years. Open models now handle most of the work on platforms like OpenRouter, especially for coding and automation tasks. However, closed models are still better at hard reasoning, long-context tasks, and handling many types of data at once. For most jobs, open models are now good enough.

Many developers use both open and closed models together—about half do this. Open models are more popular in China and East Asia, where almost 90% of developers use them. In places like South America and Western Europe, closed models are more common. More developers use open models, but fewer teams finish building real products with them. This is because open models are harder to run in production: teams struggle with performance, integration, maintenance, and documentation, not with the models themselves.

The open AI stack has many parts that work well, but is still weak when it comes to operations, standardization, and being ready for big companies. Still, open AI is now big business, with billions of dollars invested in companies like DeepSeek, Mistral, and Databricks. Open source AI has five proven ways to make money, including hosting, enterprise tools, and fine-tuning services.

One big reason for open source AI's growth is cost and control. Closed models charge by the token, which can get expensive fast. Big companies like Microsoft and Uber have had issues with surprise costs. With open source, companies can run models themselves, save money, and avoid being locked into one vendor. For governments or big groups, having their own copy of the model means they cannot lose access suddenly due to politics or business changes.

China leads in releasing open weights, and many countries now see open AI as a matter of independence, not just tech choice. Moving away from closed, vendor-owned tools is seen as important for both cost and control. At the same time, the next big challenge is building better “harnesses”—the systems that manage, control, and connect these AI models to real-world tasks. The “write” problem—making sure only safe and approved actions happen—is still unsolved for open models.

Top Hacker News comments had different views. Some users were excited about open source AI progress, saying it gives more people a fair chance to use and improve AI, just like with open source software before. Others worried that open AI models are still hard to run for small teams, and that the real gap is not in the models, but in the tools and support around them. A few pointed out that while open source is good for trust and independence, closed models may stay ahead in cutting-edge tasks because they can use more data and get feedback from millions of users.

Some commenters noted the shift of power: as more countries and companies use open source, the big US tech companies might lose their lead, especially if China and others keep pushing out high-quality open models. There was also talk about the risk of open models being misused, but many felt that open models can be just as safe if the right controls are built. A few worried about “lock-in” returning if open models end up dominated by a single provider, like China or a big company.

Many agreed that the next “battle” will be in who builds the best harnesses for agents and tools, not just the AI models themselves. The article’s point about owning your own data and memory (not just renting a model) also got support. Overall, the comments showed hope for open AI, but also reminded readers not to ignore the real work needed in operations, support, and keeping things truly open.
