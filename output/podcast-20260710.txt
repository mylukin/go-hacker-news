Our first story is a big AI showdown. An article compared twelve popular AI models, including GPT-5.6 Sol, Terra, and Luna, Grok 4.5, several Claude models, Muse Spark, and some open-source models. For each app, every model was tested five times to see how well and how often it could finish the job.

Claude models did not do as well as people hoped, and open models often failed to make a working maze. Muse Spark was sometimes great, but not always.

For the Rubik’s Cube, Claude Fable 5 was the only model to get it right every time. GPT-5.6 models were less stable and sometimes failed, while Grok 4.5 was simple but worked. Open-source models had big problems here, with most not making a working cube.

For the calculator, most models did fine. Claude’s models were best for style and always gave a working calculator. GPT-5.6 Sol sometimes made things too fancy, which hurt usability. Open models could do basic math, but sometimes failed with things like negative numbers.

For Conway’s Game of Life, even open models did well. This was the simplest task, and since there’s lots of sample code online, most models made a working app quickly and at low cost.

There were also two bonus SVG drawing tasks. Claude Fable 5 made the best images, while GPT-5.6 models were a bit weaker here. Grok 4.5 was good too.

Speed and cost were also compared. GPT-5.6 models were the fastest and cheap. Qwen 3.7 Plus was fast and affordable. Some open models were slow, especially for bigger tasks.

Next, let’s talk about hardware. QuadRF is a new small device that uses a Raspberry Pi 5 and an FPGA to find drones and see WiFi signals—even through walls. It uses a special phased array antenna and smart signal processing. With the right apps, you can see where WiFi networks are, or even spot drones by picking up their signals. The device is open source, and the creator says that if hobbyists can do this now, governments probably have much more powerful tools.

You can use your phone or computer to connect to QuadRF and view or analyze radio signals. There’s even an AR viewer that shows WiFi as colorful blobs through your camera. The device covers the 4.9–6 GHz range, which is used by many WiFi networks and drones.

The author tested the device with his dad by flying a drone and walking around. They could easily spot the drone and see different WiFi networks. The user interface is still rough, and adjusting the gain while moving is not easy yet. But the device already works well, even in this early stage.

The QuadRF streams radio data really fast, over 5 Gbps, using the Pi’s camera connectors instead of USB. You can chain multiple QuadRF devices together for bigger experiments.

Commenters were amazed that this tech is now possible for individuals. Some worried about privacy, since anyone could use this to watch WiFi or spot drones near your home. Others discussed the technical details, praised the use of MIPI lanes, and liked the idea of connecting many devices. Several saw uses for security research, WiFi troubleshooting, or finding hidden devices. Some raised legal questions about scanning WiFi or tracking drones. Others remembered older radio projects and liked how cheap and easy SDR is now. Most are excited to see what the community will build next, but some warn about possible misuse.

Next, a big story from math and AI. GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra, an AI, has produced a proof for the Cycle Double Cover Conjecture in graph theory. This is a famous open problem that says every bridgeless undirected graph can have its edges covered exactly twice by a set of cycles. The proof focuses on cubic graphs, since that’s enough for the full problem. It uses group theory and something called a “nowhere-zero flow” with the group F32. The AI assigns two labels to every edge so that every node has each label zero or two times, then builds the cycles from these labelings. The proof uses linear algebra and earlier results.

This is big news because many smart people have tried and failed to solve this problem for decades. The article says the AI wrote the proof and the writeup itself. In the comments, some people are amazed, while others are suspicious and wonder if this is a joke or an April Fools’ post, since the result and writeup are short for such a big problem. Some debate if the proof is real, since big results are usually checked by experts for months or years. Others say the writeup is clear even if the math is hard. Some wonder what this means for the future—will AI solve all open math problems, or do we still need humans for intuition and explaining results? A few worry about jobs in math, but others think new work will appear. Some users discuss the group theory details, trying to check the proof themselves.

Now, a story from movie history. An article told the inside story of how the team at ILM made the liquid metal T-1000 effects in “Terminator 2” in 1991. Back then, computer graphics were new, so the team had to invent tools and write their own software. They used programs like Alias, but also built tools like “Make Sticky” and “Body Sock” to make the digital character move smoothly. They worked on expensive SGI computers, with storage costing $9,000 per gigabyte. The team was small—just a dozen people at first—but they made the amazing effects with long hours and lots of testing.

To make the T-1000 look like liquid metal, they made special shaders and reflection tricks, since there was no ray-tracing yet. For morphing scenes, they used hand animation and blended real actor shots with digital models. They scanned the actor’s body, painted grids on him, and animated by hand because there was no motion capture. They also had to solve problems like making textures stick to moving models and hiding seams. The team worked 80 hours a week, did many jobs, and moved data by hand in the basement. Most effects were tested overnight, and some shots were finished in low resolution.

In the comments, users praised the creativity and problem-solving of those days. Many remembered watching “Terminator 2” and being amazed, not knowing how hard it was to make. Some said that artists back then needed both programming and art skills, while now jobs are more divided. A few missed the “DIY” spirit of that time. Others talked about how expensive and slow computers were, and how mixing practical and digital effects made the movie special. Some noted that today’s movies use more effects, but the results can feel less personal. Most agreed that the work on “Terminator 2” inspired many people to get into graphics and programming. Some wished modern teams could have the same tight teamwork and creativity.

Next, some consumer protection news from New York City. The city just passed a rule to stop companies from tricking people into paying for subscriptions they don’t want or can’t easily cancel. This is the first such ban in a US city and starts in October. Companies must let people cancel subscriptions simply and quickly—no more long phone calls, letters, or visits to a store. If companies break the rule, they can be fined $525 per customer. The city also wants to stop hidden “junk fees” on things like rentals, sports events, or hotels, making sure all extra charges are shown up front.

These rules are meant to save people money and make prices clear. For example, renters often see a low price online but find many extra fees later. The city says this is unfair and makes it hard to compare prices. The new law could save New Yorkers over $160 million a year by stopping unwanted renewals. The city will listen to public comments before the final fee rule goes into effect.

Commenters mostly agree that hidden fees and hard-to-cancel subscriptions are a big problem. Many shared stories about trying to cancel gym or streaming services but facing tricks and delays. Some hope other cities or states will follow. Others warn that companies may find new ways around the law or that strict rules could raise prices. Some wonder if the rules will be strongly enforced, since big companies have lawyers and money. But most feel the law is a good first step and that clear prices and easy cancelation are things everyone should have.

Our last story is about a new web tool called Combustion Lab, which lets you play with a car engine simulator in your browser. You can change engine settings like cylinder count, size, compression, turbo boost, and see how power and torque change in real time. The math is based on real engineering, using the first law of thermodynamics, and models for fuel burning, heat loss, friction, and airflow.

The tool also warns you about “knock,” when the engine burns fuel too quickly and can get damaged. You can try different fuels or settings to see how to avoid knock. The tool explains outputs like IMEP, BMEP, and BSFC, and says it’s for teaching, not for real engine design. The app shows a live graph as you change things and explains the four strokes of the engine: intake, compression, power, and exhaust.

Commenters liked how detailed and educational the tool is. Many enjoyed seeing how changing settings affects knock, power, and efficiency. Some said it makes engine math easy for students and hobbyists. A few wanted more features, like support for rotary engines or advanced fuel models. Others warned that while the trends are realistic, the numbers are not good enough for real engineering work. Most found the tool fun and helpful for learning engine basics.
