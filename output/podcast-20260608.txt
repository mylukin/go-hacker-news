On to China, where Xiaomi has launched MiMo-V2.5-Pro-UltraSpeed, an AI model with 1 trillion parameters that can generate up to 1,000 tokens per second. This makes responses nearly instant, helping users in real time for things like coding, decision-making, and even medical situations. The article says speed is now a key part of AI, and this model’s speed lets it check its own work and improve its reasoning quickly. Xiaomi worked with TileRT to make this happen, using regular GPUs with smart software, FP4 quantization, and a new method called DFlash to guess many tokens at once. The model is available through a two-week API trial for businesses and developers with real needs, and parts of it are open-sourced on HuggingFace.

Hacker News users are impressed by the technical achievement, especially using regular GPUs. Some ask if most people really need such speed, while others worry about the higher cost and limited trial. Developers discuss how the new methods might affect quality, and some hope the tech will be more open and available outside China. People see new uses for such speed, like running many AI agents at once or real-time games, but warn that quality and cost still matter. There’s a mix of excitement and questions about real-world impact.

Now, let’s talk science. Why are cells so small? The article says it’s all about physics and biology. Small cells have a large surface area compared to their volume, which helps them take in food and remove waste quickly. If a cell gets too big, its inside grows faster than its outside, so it can’t get what it needs fast enough. Diffusion, or how molecules move inside the cell, also works better in small cells. Some cells, like red blood cells, are small and shaped for their job. Others, like oocytes, are bigger because they store food and work more slowly. Some cells get around the rules in clever ways, like the giant bacterium Thiomargarita magnifica, which pushes most active parts to the edge. In the comments, people liked the clear math and examples, and discussed exceptions like neurons and special plant cells. Some talked about how evolution shapes cell size, and others wondered about the limits for artificial cells or nano-machines. Most agree that physics gives strong limits, and life finds ways to work within them.

Switching to food safety, a new report shows many foods in Europe—like rice, tea, and spices—have pesticides in them that are banned in the EU. Tests found that 45 out of 64 products had these banned pesticides. The problem is that while the EU bans some chemicals, European countries can still sell them to others. Food grown with these chemicals is sometimes shipped back to Europe, so people end up eating the same banned pesticides. The article calls this the “toxic pesticides boomerang.” Some foods, like paprika and chili, had many banned chemicals, some above the legal limit. The article warns that the EU wants to change food safety rules quickly, which could make things worse.

Hacker News users are shocked and worried about food safety. Some say it’s not fair that banned chemicals come back in imported food. Others think the real problem is weak checks at borders and poor enforcement. Some argue that banning pesticides in one place doesn’t work if they’re still used elsewhere, and discuss if organic food is truly safer. There are calls for better labeling and for the EU to ban imports with these chemicals, but others warn this could cause trade problems. Some people worry that banning too many pesticides will make food more expensive or harder to grow. The main feeling is that safety rules should be the same everywhere.

Back to Apple: they just announced Core AI, a new framework for running AI models directly on Apple devices with Apple silicon. It’s built for Swift and helps developers add smart features to their apps, using the CPU, GPU, or Neural Engine for fast AI. Developers can prepare, convert, and debug models with new tools, and the framework supports new model types and lets you control how models are compiled and cached for each device. Core AI works across all Apple platforms, but if you want to use other model types like decision trees, you should stick with Core ML.

Some developers on Hacker News are excited, saying this is a big step for making AI apps on Apple devices. They like the on-device processing for privacy and speed, but others say the beta feels early and documentation is thin. There are questions about support for bigger models and if it will work with current AI tools. Some are worried about lock-in, since this only works on Apple devices, but most agree it shows Apple is serious about AI.
