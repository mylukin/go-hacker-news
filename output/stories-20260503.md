## BYOMesh – New LoRa mesh radio offers 100x the bandwidth

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47999636)

A new hardware device called BYOMesh was announced, promising to bring much faster speeds to LoRa mesh radio networks. The main idea is that this device combines two chips: the SX1276 for sub-1GHz LoRa and the SX1281 for high-speed 2.4GHz LoRa, all on a very small board.

The creators say BYOMesh is the smallest and most powerful LoRa companion board so far. It can use both the usual long-range, low-speed LoRa frequencies and the newer, much faster 2.4GHz LoRa. This means you can get up to 100 times more bandwidth than before, which could be very useful for sending more data over long distances—like between mountaintops.

The device is still being finished, with new software patches coming for it. The team shared test results of 2.4GHz LoRa using software-defined radio (SDR) and showed excitement about the much higher speeds possible without needing WiFi or more complex systems. They also say this board is good for mesh networks, where many radios connect together, and think it will be useful for hobbyists and people who want to build their own networks.

In the comment section, people shared mixed thoughts. Some were excited about the higher bandwidth and asked if the board comes with an antenna or if it’s just the chip. A few asked if one antenna works for both frequency bands, and if there are ways to optimize it for both. One person said LoRa at 2.4GHz is still much slower than WiFi HaLow—about 30 KB/s versus 40 MB/s—but admitted LoRa is better for long range.

Another commenter wondered if the 2.4GHz LoRa could send bigger packets, which would help move lots of small messages at once. Someone else asked if this board is a good starting point for Meshtastic, a popular secure mesh radio project. Overall, many were interested in trying BYOMesh, but some had technical questions about antennas, speed, and use cases. The main feeling was excitement for more options in DIY mesh radio projects.

A new study from Harvard shows that OpenAI’s o1 AI diagnosed emergency room patients more correctly than human triage doctors—67% accuracy for the AI, compared to 50–55% for doctors. The test gave both the AI and the doctors only basic patient information, like vital signs and short nurse notes, to see how well each could make fast decisions in stressful situations.

The AI did especially well when time was short and there was not much data. When given more health details, the AI’s accuracy went up to 82%, while doctors reached 70–79%. The AI also did better than doctors in making longer-term treatment plans, scoring 89% compared to the doctors’ 34%. One story from the study showed the AI noticing a rare cause for a patient’s lung problem that the doctors missed, leading to the right treatment.

However, the study authors say this does not mean AI will replace doctors. The AI only saw text records, not things like patient distress or how they looked, which human doctors use to make decisions. The AI acted more like a second opinion, not a full doctor. The researchers believe AI will work together with doctors, not alone, to help patients in the future.

Many doctors already use AI tools to help with diagnoses. Still, some are worried about mistakes and who is responsible when AI is wrong. There is no clear rule for who is at fault if AI gives bad advice. Also, patients may still want a human to help with big health choices.

Top comments on Hacker News are mixed. Some users are excited, saying this could make emergency care faster and safer. Others warn that AI can make mistakes doctors would not, and that over-trusting AI could be risky. A few point out that the test was limited, since it did not use real-life patient interactions. Some doctors on the thread share that AI can help with paperwork and ideas, but real care needs a human touch. Others worry about bias in AI and how it might struggle with certain groups, like older people or those who speak little English. Many agree that AI can be a great tool, but should not fully replace human doctors, at least for now. ## US–Indian space mission maps extreme subsidence in Mexico City

- 原文链接: [US–Indian space mission maps extreme subsidence in Mexico City](https://phys.org/news/2026-04-usindian-space-mission-extreme-subsidence.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47974682)

A new US–Indian space mission, called NISAR, has used radar from space to measure how much Mexico City is sinking. The city, home to 20 million people, is built on an old lakebed and loses ground every year because people pump too much water from underground.

The NISAR satellite uses strong L-band radar to see small changes in the ground—even when there are clouds or many trees. Between October 2025 and January 2026, some parts of the city sank over two centimeters each month. Over many years, this slow sinking has broken roads, pipes, and buildings. One famous statue, the Angel of Independence, now has 14 extra steps because the ground dropped so much.

Scientists have watched Mexico City sink for almost a century, but NISAR’s new radar can check the ground often and in more places. The radar works at night and in bad weather, making it easier to watch cities, farms, and glaciers anywhere in the world. The early data also show some yellow and red areas, but these may just be noise, which will get better with more data.

Experts say NISAR will help people track sinking land in other places, especially where cities are near the coast and also face rising sea levels. The mission is a big step for both NASA and India’s space agency, showing how countries can work together to solve big problems.

In the comments, some people are amazed at how fast the city is sinking and worry about the future of its buildings and roads. Others talk about how groundwater use is a problem in many cities, not just Mexico City. Some readers are impressed by the technology and the clear images from the radar, while a few wonder if this data will help city leaders make better choices. A few people share stories of visiting Mexico City and noticing uneven streets themselves. Others ask if there are ways to stop the sinking or if it is already too late. One commenter points out that monitoring is good, but fixing the problem will be much harder. Some are hopeful that sharing this data will push for smarter water use around the world. ## Why TUIs Are Back

- 原文链接: [Why TUIs Are Back](https://wiki.alcidesfonseca.com/blog/why-tuis-are-back/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48000028)

This article is about why Terminal User Interfaces (TUIs) are becoming popular again. It looks at how desktop apps have changed and why many people are now choosing TUIs over traditional graphical apps.

First, the author explains that native GUI apps on Windows, Linux, and macOS are becoming less useful and less consistent. On Windows, Microsoft keeps making new GUI libraries, but none work well for long. On Linux, different teams use different toolkits, so apps look and work differently. On macOS, Apple once had clear design rules, but now breaks its own rules, making things harder for users.

The article also talks about Electron apps. These are popular because they are easy to make and run on many platforms, but they use a lot of memory and often don’t work well with the keyboard. Many apps today use Electron, but they don’t feel like part of the system.

Some teams have tried to solve these problems by building new UI toolkits from scratch, like Google with Flutter or Zed with their own GPU renderer. However, these new toolkits often don’t fit well with the operating system either.

TUIs, on the other hand, run in the terminal and are fast, easy to automate, and work on almost every system. You can use them remotely without problems. Because desktop apps are now less consistent, TUIs fill the gap. They give users a simple and reliable way to work, no matter what OS they use.

The author says we need to focus on making UIs simple and easy to use. Developers should study what makes a good UI and not treat design as something unimportant.

In the Hacker News comments, many people agree that modern GUI apps are confusing and inconsistent. Some say they miss the old days when apps looked and felt the same. Others like TUIs because they can be used with only the keyboard and are easy to script.

A few commenters point out that TUIs are not always easy for beginners, and can look outdated or hard to learn. Some people mention that many tasks still need a good GUI, especially for complex work or new users. Others say that TUIs are great for experts, but regular users still want simple visual apps.

Some users discuss Electron apps, saying they are slow and don’t fit well with the system, but also admit it’s hard to make cross-platform apps any other way. There are also comments about how companies avoid making native Linux apps because it’s too much work, so they choose Electron or just support Windows and macOS.

A few people hope in the future we’ll have better tools for both TUIs and GUIs, and that developers will care more about making software easy to use. Some are just happy to see TUIs getting attention again.

## Make Your Own Microforest

- 原文链接: [Make Your Own Microforest](https://ambrook.com/offrange/environment/a-forest-in-your-pocket)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48000507)

This article is about how people are planting small, dense forests, called microforests, using the Miyawaki method, even in cities or on farmland. The Miyawaki method lets trees grow faster and create habitats quickly, helping nature in places where there usually isn’t much green.

The Miyawaki method was started by a Japanese botanist, Akira Miyawaki, in the 1970s. He wanted to restore land using native plants, planting them very close together so they grow quickly and help each other. At Horn Farm in Pennsylvania, they used this method to make a 12-foot-wide forest by a busy road. They planted more than 500 native trees, and now, only a few years later, the trees are already 30 feet tall and full of birds, insects, and other wildlife.

This method works well in small urban spaces and on farms. It uses many different tree species, packed tightly, which makes them compete and also help each other. The trees grow faster because they fight for sunlight and water, but also make the soil healthier and stop water from washing away the earth. Microforests can help with flooding, reduce pollution, and bring animals back. The soil is prepared first, then covered with local leaf litter to help the young trees.

The Miyawaki method is different from usual tree planting, where trees are spaced far apart and grow slowly. This new way helps forests grow much faster and supports more life. People have planted these forests in many countries, and they are showing success. For example, in Los Angeles, one small Miyawaki forest had 51% more types of plants and animals than a regular area nearby after only two years.

These microforests can also help capture carbon from the air, which is good for fighting climate change. They give people hope and a way to help nature, even if they don’t have much space. But it costs more up front—sometimes $25,000 to $35,000 for a tiny forest—and needs good fences to keep animals out until the trees are bigger. Some people have trouble getting money for these projects, because they are small and not everyone believes they make a big difference yet.

In the Hacker News comments, many people liked the idea and found it inspiring, especially for cities and schools. Some said the Miyawaki method is a good way for communities to help the environment and learn together. Others pointed out the high cost and wondered if it could be cheaper if people did more of the work themselves.

A few commenters worried about long-term care, saying that some microforests might need extra help at first, like watering or weeding, especially in dry places. Some liked that the forests use local tree species, which is important for birds and insects. Others discussed whether these small forests really fight climate change or if they are too small to matter. Some experts in the comments explained that every bit helps, and these forests can be a “gateway” for people to care more about nature.

There was also debate about using land for forests versus for parks or playgrounds in cities. Some felt that since microforests are so dense, people can’t walk inside them, so they should be balanced with open spaces. Others thought the beauty and benefits to air and water quality made them worth it, even if you can’t walk through. Many agreed that making even a small forest is better than doing nothing, and that the Miyawaki method brings people together and makes a visible difference fast.

## Bad Connection: Global telecom exploitation by covert surveillance actors

- 原文链接: [Bad Connection: Global telecom exploitation by covert surveillance actors](https://citizenlab.ca/research/uncovering-global-telecom-exploitation-by-covert-surveillance-actors/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47998449)

This article explains how secret groups are using global mobile networks to track people without their knowledge. The researchers found two main ways these groups spy: by sending special messages through the phone network, and by sending hidden commands to SIM cards through SMS.

The first way uses weaknesses in old and new mobile network protocols (SS7 for 3G, Diameter for 4G/5G). Attackers pretend to be real phone companies, sending messages to find out where a person is. They often change which country or network they appear to come from. They use many tricks, like rotating network identities, to get around security and avoid being caught. The attacks can pass through phone companies in many countries, making them hard to trace. The second way involves sending a secret SMS to a phone’s SIM card. This SMS does not show up in your messages. It tells the SIM to send back the phone’s location, turning it into a secret tracker. This method is linked to a known attack called SIMjacker.

The article shows that many mobile operators do not use strong security in their networks. Some operators allow outside companies to use their systems, making it easier for attackers to blend in. Attackers use special knowledge of telecom rules and documents to make their messages look real. The spying can last for years, targeting people in many countries, including high-profile business leaders.

The researchers worked with several security and mobile firms to track these campaigns. They matched attack patterns, message details, and routing paths to identify two main attacker groups. One group used network protocol tricks; the other used SIM card exploits. Both groups were able to reach many targets around the world.

In the Hacker News comments, some users said they were shocked at how weak global telecom security is, even after years of warnings. Others were not surprised, saying that the mobile network was never designed for security, only for trust between operators. A few commenters pointed out that adding proper authentication and encryption is possible, but phone companies often avoid it because it’s expensive or hard to coordinate worldwide. Some users worried that regular people cannot protect themselves, since these attacks happen deep in the network, not on the phone itself.

Several people noted that governments and commercial vendors both take part in this kind of spying, not just hackers. A few shared stories about SIMjacker and similar attacks being used by law enforcement or for political reasons. Some commenters discussed possible technical fixes, like better firewalls and strict controls on who can send network messages, but they doubted these would be applied soon. Others suggested that until there is stronger global regulation, these problems will continue. A few users said the report was excellent and praised the technical depth and clear examples. Overall, the discussion showed worry and frustration about how hard it is to stop this kind of hidden surveillance.
