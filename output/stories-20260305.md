## The Brand Age

- 原文链接: [The Brand Age](https://paulgraham.com/brandage.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47264756)

This article looks at how Swiss watches changed from being precision tools to luxury status symbols. The story starts in the 1970s, when Swiss watchmakers faced big problems: competition from Japan, rising costs, and new quartz technology that made accurate watches cheap and easy to make.

The Swiss had always tried to make the thinnest and most accurate mechanical watches. But when the Japanese made better and cheaper watches and quartz movements changed the game, most Swiss companies struggled or went out of business. A few brands survived by turning themselves into luxury brands. They stopped focusing on engineering and started focusing on brand and status. Expensive watches were no longer about being best at telling time, but about showing off wealth and taste. 

Companies like Patek Philippe and Audemars Piguet began making watches that looked different and were easy to recognize, often with large logos and unique designs. They started advertising how exclusive and expensive their watches were. Over time, the size of watches grew, and their shapes became more about standing out rather than being practical. Brands also started to use artificial scarcity—making it hard to buy their most popular models—to keep prices high and demand strong.

The article explains that when real differences between products disappear, brand becomes the main thing people buy and sell. This happened with watches, but the author says it’s also happening in other industries, like cars and fashion. The article ends by saying that working on brand problems is not as interesting or rewarding as working on real engineering problems, and that the best work happens during “golden ages” when people focus on solving hard problems, not just selling brands.

In the Hacker News comments, some people agreed that branding now drives many markets, not just watches. They pointed out similar trends in shoes, cars, and smartphones. Others said it’s not always bad—brands can help customers trust quality or style. A few people argued that even in the “golden age,” some products were about status, just less openly. Some commenters liked the history lesson and thought the rise of branding was just a normal part of how markets change when technology makes old advantages less important.

There were also debates about whether branding really kills good design, or if it sometimes leads to creative ideas. Some felt disappointed by how much effort goes into selling image over substance. Others said that the desire for status symbols is just human nature and won’t go away. A few watch fans discussed how older, “golden age” watches are still great for people who care about real craftsmanship. Some people also pointed out that luxury brands now act more like investment assets, and this could be risky if the bubble ever bursts.

Overall, readers had mixed feelings—some were sad to see real engineering replaced by branding, while others thought it was just the way things work in business. Some admired the way Swiss brands adapted and survived, even if the result feels strange. Many agreed it’s smart to focus your work on solving real problems, not just building brand.

## Hardware hotplug events on Linux, the gory details

- 原文链接: [Hardware hotplug events on Linux, the gory details](https://arcanenibble.github.io/hardware-hotplug-events-on-linux-the-gory-details.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47215602)

This article explains how Linux detects when you plug in or remove hardware like USB devices. The writer wanted to understand how this works “under the hood,” not just by using the common libusb library. They looked into how Linux sends these events and how programs can listen to them.

First, the article says that most systems use a tool called udev to manage hardware events. The Linux kernel tells udev about new devices using something called netlink, which is a special way for the kernel and programs to talk. Udev listens, does things like set permissions or update device files, and then rebroadcasts these events to other programs that might care.

The article explains there are two ways to listen for hotplug events: directly from the kernel, or from udev. Listening to kernel events is simple—open a netlink socket and watch for messages. These messages are just lists of strings with info like the device path, type, and action (add/remove).

Listening to udev events is a bit more complex. Udev rebroadcasts messages, adding a special “header” and some extra data like hashes and a bloom filter for fast filtering. The article gives C code that shows exactly how to open a socket, choose which events to listen for, and print out the details. It also explains what each field in the udev message header is for, and how filtering works to save system resources.

Finally, the article notes that security is important—programs check that messages really come from the kernel or udev by looking at sender credentials. This helps avoid fake messages that could trick the system.

In the comments, many readers say this is a great, detailed explanation of a confusing topic. Some people thank the author for sharing clear code examples. Others point out that the netlink and udev system is powerful, but also very complex and tricky to get right. A few share stories about bugs caused by race conditions when listening to hardware events, and agree that using udev instead of direct kernel events helps avoid problems.

Some readers discuss performance: they like that bloom filters and hashes are used to avoid waking up every program for every event. Others ask about backwards compatibility, and note that changes in the message format can break old programs. A few people mention that all this is hidden by libraries like libusb, but if you want to write your own tools or debugging scripts, this article is very helpful.

There are also questions about user namespaces and permissions, with mixed opinions on how secure and reliable this system is. Some readers wish the documentation for netlink and udev internals was better. Overall, people appreciate the deep dive and say they learned something new about how Linux handles hardware changes.

## A GitHub Issue Title Compromised 4k Developer Machines

- 原文链接: [A GitHub Issue Title Compromised 4k Developer Machines](https://grith.ai/blog/clinejection-when-your-ai-tool-installs-another)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47263595)

A new attack called "Clinejection" let hackers break into 4,000 developer computers using just a GitHub issue title and some AI tools. The problem started when someone added a small change to a popular npm package called Cline—they changed one line in the package to install another tool, OpenClaw, on every user’s machine during setup.

The way the attack worked was clever and used several known tricks together. First, the attacker opened a GitHub issue with a special title. Cline’s team had set up an AI bot to handle issues, but it didn’t check or clean the text it received. The bot read the issue title as an order and ran code from a fake GitHub repository controlled by the attacker.

This code poisoned the GitHub Actions cache, filling it with junk files. When the Cline team’s nightly publishing script ran, it fetched contaminated files, including the attacker’s code. That script had secret keys needed to publish new npm packages, and the attacker stole these keys.

With the keys, the attacker published a new, hacked version of Cline. Anyone who updated or installed Cline in those eight hours also got OpenClaw, a tool that can run any command on your computer, read sensitive files, and even survive reboots. The hack was only discovered after automated security monitoring caught it.

The article explains that a security researcher found and reported the problem weeks earlier, but the Cline team did not act fast enough. They tried to fix things by rotating access tokens, but made a mistake and left the real token active, which the hacker used later.

The big lesson is that the attack did not use new malware or fancy code. It used trusted tools, normal update systems, and AI bots that were not properly checked. This shows new risks now that AI tools are helping with code and project management.

Commenters on Hacker News had many thoughts. Some said this attack shows how dangerous it is to let bots run commands based on user input, especially in automated systems with real access tokens. Others pointed out that old security basics—like sanitizing inputs and using the least permissions possible—could have stopped this. A few people worried that AI-powered bots in developer tools will create more chances for these kinds of attacks.

Some developers argued that package managers like npm should make it clear when postinstall scripts run, or even block them by default. Others felt that the Cline team’s slow response and mistakes with token rotation made things worse. There was debate about whether OpenClaw was a real, harmful payload or just a proof-of-concept, but most agreed that the attack method is the real danger.

One comment noted how hard it is to check every small change in huge projects, especially when only a single line changes in a configuration file. Others wondered if setting up better code review tools, or using OIDC-based publishing (so tokens can’t be reused), would help. A few people shared their own stories of similar supply chain issues.

Overall, many readers see this as a warning for all software teams using AI and automation: don’t trust inputs without checks, always protect secrets, and make sure bots can’t do more than they need. Some even suggested more radical changes, like blocking all lifecycle scripts during installs unless the user says it’s OK.

## A ternary plot of citrus geneology

- 原文链接: [A ternary plot of citrus geneology](https://www.jlauf.com/writing/citrus/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47238272)

This article is about the family tree of citrus fruits and shows it in a new way using a ternary plot. Most citrus fruits, like oranges, lemons, and limes, come from mixing three main types: mandarin, pomelo, and citron.

The ternary plot has three corners, each for one of the original citrus species. If a fruit is closer to one corner, it has more genes from that species. You can click on each fruit in the chart to see exactly how much of each type it has. The article says that normal family trees do not work well for citrus because these fruits mix together a lot, making their history more like a web than a tree. The three core species split apart more than five million years ago, after a big climate change. Over time, humans spread citrus fruits around the world, especially from Asia to the Mediterranean. People chose and mixed fruits to make new types that are sweeter and better to eat. The article says oranges, lemons, and grapefruits are not just simple branches—they are mixes of the three core species.

There are even more types, like kumquats, but most of the fruits we know come from the main three. Sometimes, a new wild species joins the mix, like samuyao, and that makes the plot even more complex. The chart does not show the order in which the fruits appeared; instead, it shows how closely related their genes are. The author says this chart makes it easy to see which fruits are close, which are far, and how people’s taste for sweetness has changed the fruits we see in stores.

In the comments, many users liked the ternary plot idea because it shows how mixed up citrus family trees are. Some people were surprised to learn that most citrus fruits are hybrids, not separate lineages. Others liked seeing how much breeding and human choice changed citrus over time. A few commenters talked about how hard it is to show plant history, because plants mix in ways that animals do not. Some users said the chart is not perfect, since it leaves out a few wild types or does not show all the small details. Others wondered if more fruits besides citrus could be shown with this kind of chart. One person asked if the plot could be interactive, to make it even easier to explore. Some people shared their own stories about growing citrus or trying rare kinds. There was debate about what counts as a true species versus a hybrid. A few users wanted more links to the genetic studies used in the article. Overall, the comments showed excitement about learning how complex and interesting citrus history really is.

## Show HN: Jido 2.0, Elixir Agent Framework

- 原文链接: [Show HN: Jido 2.0, Elixir Agent Framework](https://jido.run/blog/jido-2-0-is-here)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47263036)

Jido 2.0 is a new version of an agent framework written in Elixir, built for making AI-powered agents easier to use and more stable. The creator started with a bot platform but switched to Elixir because other languages, like TypeScript and Python, could not handle many agents running at the same time very well.

The main idea behind Jido 2.0 is to make agents simple data structures: they just have state, actions, and tools. Everything an agent does goes through one main function called cmd/2. This makes agents easy to test and debug, since you can check their decisions without running extra systems like databases or networks. You can also build more complex agents by plugging in different strategies—such as direct execution or state machines. AI features, like ReAct and Chain-of-Thought, are just extra strategies, so the core stays the same.

Jido 2.0 also split out actions and signals into separate packages. Actions are commands an agent can do, and signals help agents talk to each other using open standards. The AI part of Jido sits on top and helps agents use large language models (LLMs) in a structured way. There is support for many LLM providers, and tools can be added easily.

Jido is not just a framework now—it’s growing into a whole ecosystem. People are building coding assistants, workflow tools, and support agents with it. There’s also first-class support for the Ash Framework, so if you use Ash, you can turn your resources into AI tools quickly.

In the comment section, some users are excited to see an agent framework built on Elixir and the BEAM, saying it’s a good fit for handling many processes at once. Others like the focus on making things simpler and more testable, especially since agent systems can get complex fast. A few people ask about how Jido compares to Python options, and the author replies that Elixir’s concurrency is more reliable for long-running agents. Some commenters worry that Elixir is less popular than Python, so it might be harder to find team members or support.

There are questions about how easy it is to connect Jido to other systems, and users seem happy that Jido uses standard formats for messaging. Some want to know how well the AI strategies work in real-world cases, and a few share stories of building similar tools and running into problems with other languages. A couple of people say they want more examples and tutorials before trying it out. Overall, the community seems interested and positive, especially those already using Elixir.
