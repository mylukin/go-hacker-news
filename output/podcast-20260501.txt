Hello everyone, this is the 2026-05-01 episode of Hacker News Daily Podcast. Today, we have a wide range of stories, from credit card security to AI water use, new developer tools, and even a fortune-reading plugin. Let’s get started.

First, let’s talk about credit card security. An article shared a personal story about how someone’s credit card was stolen, even though the company followed PCI DSS security rules. According to these rules, websites can only show the first six and last four digits of your card, along with your name and expiration date. The most sensitive details like the full number or the CVC should never be shown. But the problem is, many companies only do the minimum to pass the rules and do not take extra steps to keep your card safer.

The writer’s card was saved at a big store. One day, attackers tried to use it. They got to the bank’s 3D Secure page, which told them that the card was still active, which bank it belonged to, and some masked card details. With this info, the attackers then guessed the missing numbers—only six digits were left to guess, which is about 100,000 options. Using special programs, they tried different combinations slowly so they would not get blocked. Some payment systems even reveal if the problem is with the card number, expiration date, or CVC, making it easier for attackers to guess each part.

Some stores do not need the CVC or do not use 3D Secure, so attackers can use stolen cards more easily. The comments were full of concern. Many people think PCI DSS is not enough and merchants should do more. Others are shocked that payments can go through without all the card details. Some suggest always using virtual cards or cards with low limits online. A few point out that even printed receipts can leak too much information, and many agree that both users and companies need to be more careful. The hope is that payment industry standards will get better in the future.

Next, we have the new TI-84 Evo graphing calculator from Texas Instruments. This model is aimed at students and teachers. It is faster, with a processor three times quicker than before, a bigger color screen, and a new icon-based home screen. The keypad is simpler, menus are better organized, and it uses USB-C for charging. It also comes in many colors like white, pink, mint, and more.

The Evo supports Python and TI-Basic programming and comes with a free four-year license for the online version of the calculator. Schools get free licenses when buying teacher packs. On Hacker News, opinions are mixed. Some users think the new features are good, but the price is still too high, especially compared to cheap smartphones or computers. Some say these calculators are only popular because they are allowed on exams, not because they are the best tool. Teachers like that the calculator cannot go online, which keeps students focused. Many feel that exam rules are the real reason these calculators remain popular, not their technical edge.

Now, let’s look at a new study about lucid dreaming. Researchers showed that people can talk and practice skills while dreaming, as long as they know they are dreaming and can control their actions. During the study, people answered questions by moving their eyes or face. Some could even practice math or music in their dreams, and sometimes their real skills improved after waking up.

Commenters are excited and share personal stories about practicing in dreams. Others are more careful, saying not everyone can control dreams or know if dream practice helps. Some worry about using dreams too much for work or learning. Many agree this research is interesting and could change how we think about sleep and learning.

Moving on, there’s a new Claude Code plugin called “destiny.” This plugin gives you a daily fortune reading based on East Asian metaphysics, using your real birth data. It is not just random text—it uses real calculation methods based on the Four Pillars, the lunar calendar, and the I-Ching. The plugin works locally, does not use outside servers, and is for fun only.

In the comments, people enjoy the creativity and technical detail. Some say it reminds them of old Unix “fortune” programs, but smarter. Others like that it is private and open source. Some joke about using their daily fortune to decide if they should fix bugs. Most people see it as a light and fun tool, as long as it is not taken too seriously.

It’s also the start of a new month, so the “Who is hiring?” thread is back on Hacker News. Companies and startups share job openings for software engineers, data scientists, designers, and more. There are jobs for remote and office work, from junior to senior positions, with a wide range of tech stacks. Some companies offer visa sponsorship, while others focus on skills rather than degrees. Applicants and company reps share tips, ask questions, and talk about their experiences. Many people say this thread is a helpful resource, even though the tech job market can change very fast.

Next, let’s talk about a free open house for a course called Whimsical Animations. The creator, Josh, made some lessons public for a short time so people can preview his teaching style. The lessons focus on creative animations for the web, and users can click through at their own pace. There are no hidden catches—just a chance to try the course before buying. Commenters like the honest approach and clear navigation. Some ask if the lessons cover both CSS and JavaScript, and others hope more courses offer free previews. Overall, the mood is positive and grateful.

There’s also a story about Flock, a company that sells surveillance cameras and software. In Dunwoody, Georgia, Flock employees used cameras in sensitive places like children’s gyms and playgrounds to show demos to police buyers. This was found out by a local resident, Jason Hunyar, who looked at camera access logs. Flock says they had city permission and only used footage for demos, but many people are upset, especially since parents did not know their kids were being watched. The city renewed Flock’s contract, but Flock promised to stop using cameras in private areas for demos. The comments show a lot of anger and worry about privacy, with some saying this is an example of “surveillance creep.” A few defend Flock, but most agree more care and rules are needed.

On the tools side, there’s whohas, a command-line utility for searching software packages across many Linux and BSD distributions. Written in Perl, it queries package lists and shows which distros have a package, what version, and more. You can filter using grep or cut, and it’s good for package maintainers or power users. Comments say it’s helpful but sometimes out of date, since it scrapes web data. Some wish it supported modern package systems like Flatpak or Snap. Others mention web tools like Repology and pkgs.org as easier for casual users, but many power users still prefer command-line tools like whohas.

Another new tool is Understand Anything, which turns any codebase or knowledge base into a graph you can explore, search, and ask questions about. It works with many AI coding tools and helps people see how everything in a big project connects. You can explore the code, follow guided tours, and even map business logic to code. The tool makes it easy for new team members to learn a project and can share the graph with others. Comments are mostly positive, saying it fills a big need, but some worry about very large projects or privacy issues. Many like the idea of sharing a “mental map” of the project with the whole team.

Finally, let’s talk about AI and water use in California. Many people are worried that AI data centers use too much water, but the real numbers are much smaller than most think. The article explains that even if all data centers used only water cooling, they would use less than 0.1% of California’s total water each year. Farming and other industries use far more. The writer says we should look at the real data before worrying. In the comments, people agree, but some point out that local water shortages can still be a problem. Others dislike how the media reports on these topics without good numbers. Some joke that humans themselves waste a lot of water. The main lesson is to check the facts and keep things in perspective.
