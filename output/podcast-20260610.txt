Let’s start with πfs, a funny and clever idea for a filesystem that claims to store your files in the digits of pi, not on your hard drive. The idea comes from the theory that every possible sequence of numbers can be found somewhere in pi’s infinite digits. So, in theory, every file you make is already in pi—if you can find where. When you use πfs, your data is not really saved to disk. Instead, the system searches for each byte of your file in pi’s digits and saves the locations as metadata. The real trick is, you always need this metadata to get your file back. The article explains how to install and use πfs on Linux, and jokes about storing even the metadata in pi, though that would be even harder.

Most people in the comments see πfs as a parody and enjoy its humor. They discuss the limits of the idea, since searching for long files in pi is not practical, and the normality of pi—the theory that every possible sequence can be found in it—has not even been proven. Some joke about copyright and security, saying if everything is in pi, so are all secrets and copyrighted works, but you can’t really find them. Others talk about the huge size of the metadata compared to any space you “save.” Overall, readers like the creativity and the way πfs makes people think about data, storage, and the strange side of math.

Next, we move to Mars and the story of how NASA’s Curiosity rover is still doing science after 13 years. Curiosity has driven almost 37 kilometers, drilled 42 rocks, and taken over 763,000 photos. The rover faces cold, dust, and rough ground, but the JPL team keeps it running with only software updates. When one of the rover’s two computers had memory problems, the team switched to the other and even used small parts of memory meant for other tasks. The wheels have been damaged by sharp rocks, so engineers started making Curiosity drive backwards to protect them. The nuclear battery is getting weaker with time, so they save energy by turning off computers and heaters when not needed.

The team watches every part that can wear out, like the robotic arm, and does some tasks less often to make things last. Lessons from Curiosity help newer rovers like Perseverance, which now drive by themselves with better software. The main limits for Curiosity’s future are power and maybe budget, but the team hopes it can work until at least 2035. In the comments, people are amazed at how long Curiosity has lasted. They praise the careful engineering and smart problem-solving. Some compare the old rover computer to modern phones and wonder why our own devices do not last as long. Others say the story is inspiring and shows the value of learning from every mission.

Now let’s talk about PgDog, a new open-source tool for scaling Postgres databases. PgDog just raised $5.5 million and can handle over 2 million queries per second. The main idea is simple: PgDog puts a smart proxy in front of Postgres, letting you split data across many servers—this is called “sharding.” You can run PgDog on your own servers or in the cloud, and just need to change your database connection. It already has over 1.4 million Docker pulls, and the team has experience from big companies.

Many commenters are excited about PgDog, happy to see Postgres scaling so well and with a simple setup. Some ask about migration and the added complexity of sharding, especially with joins and transactions. A few worry about trusting a small team, but the open-source code helps build trust. Users want to see more benchmarks and real-world examples. Some hope PgDog will help smaller companies avoid costly managed databases. There are also questions about backups, failover, and upgrades. Overall, people see PgDog as a promising tool for scaling Postgres, and are watching to see how it works in more situations.

Next is HelixDB, a new database made for building AI tools. HelixDB combines graph, vector, document, and relational data models into one system. You can install it easily, start projects with a tool called helix chef, and write queries in Rust or TypeScript. It supports cloud storage, full-text and vector search, ACID transactions, and scaling with high availability. There’s also commercial support and clear documentation.

In the comments, readers like the idea of one database for many data types, especially for modern AI needs. Some are cautious, saying that mixing so many features could make things complex or hard to maintain. Others praise the speed and safety of Rust, while some want to know more about real-world performance and comparisons to other databases. The quick start and good documentation are strong points, but people are waiting for more feedback from real users before trying it in important projects.

Let’s move to hardware. Mercedes-Benz has started making a new kind of electric motor, called an axial flux motor, at its Berlin factory. This motor is smaller, lighter, and more powerful for its size. It uses two flat rotors and a stator in the middle, making it very compact. To build it, Mercedes invented new ways to bend wires and weld parts, even using AI to check quality. The first car to use it is the Mercedes-AMG GT 4-door coupe, which has three of these motors and can go from 0 to 100 km/h in just over 2 seconds.

In the comments, readers are excited about lighter, stronger electric cars. Some note that axial flux motors have been known for years, but now Mercedes can make them at scale. Others wonder about repair and cost, and if these motors will come to cheaper cars. Some like that this keeps jobs in Germany and uses old factories for new tech. There are questions about efficiency and how these motors compare to Tesla’s designs. Most agree it’s a smart move to stay ahead in electric car technology.

Now let’s talk about GeoLibre 1.0, a new cloud-based map tool. GeoLibre lets you view, explore, and analyze map data on the web or desktop. You can load many types of map data, see tables, change how layers look, and use tools like measure or minimap. Plugins add more features, such as street view or time sliders, and you can use it inside Jupyter notebooks. It works fully in your browser for privacy and can be embedded in other websites.

Many commenters are impressed by the features in this first release, especially the support for modern formats and privacy. Some compare it to bigger tools like QGIS, but find GeoLibre easier to use. There are questions about handling large datasets, plugin support, and performance. Some want more examples and tutorials. Overall, people are positive and hope the project keeps growing.

Let’s take a look at a story from space engineering. On the International Space Station, a chemical called siloxane started causing big problems in the water system. Astronauts needed to recycle water to save money, but sensors found rising levels of organic carbon from an unknown contaminant. After a long search, engineers found the cause: dimethylsilanediol (DMSD), from siloxanes in items like deodorant and lotions. These chemicals broke down in space and got into the water, building up in filters and then spiking into the drinking water.

NASA tried new filters and ways to catch siloxanes before they entered the water, but often fixing one problem caused another, like mold in the filters. The story shows how small, boring problems can become big headaches in space, and how hard it is to test for every risk. In the comments, readers are surprised by the problem and how hard it is to solve. Some joke about “devious” siloxanes, while others worry about these kinds of issues on longer missions. Many agree that space engineering is often about solving very human, everyday problems.

Now for some philosophy. The article “What is it like to be a bat?” by Thomas Nagel talks about the problem of consciousness. Nagel says that we cannot really know what it is like to be another creature, like a bat, because consciousness has a “subjective character.” Science can study brains and behavior, but this does not explain the feeling of being someone. The article suggests that science may never fully explain consciousness, and that new ways of thinking may be needed in the future.

In the comments, many people find the article deep and thought-provoking. Some agree that science has trouble explaining consciousness. Others think technology might help us understand other points of view, like blind people using sound or virtual reality. Some are more hopeful that science will solve the problem one day, while others think philosophy is still important. A few share stories about trying to imagine being an animal or having different senses. Overall, the article makes people think about what it means to be conscious and how little we know about other minds.
