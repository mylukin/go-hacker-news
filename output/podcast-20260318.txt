Let’s start with OpenRocket, a free and open source simulator for model rockets. OpenRocket lets you design rockets on your computer, test how they will fly, and see how changes affect things like height, speed, and stability. The tool uses advanced flight modeling and shows over 50 different variables during a test flight. You get detailed graphs and can export your results. There’s a built-in CAD system so you can pick parts and materials, or even make your own. You can export PDF designs to help when building the real rocket. OpenRocket also has an AI assistant that suggests ways to improve your rocket, for example, to fly higher or stay more stable. As you adjust your design, the data and graphs update instantly, showing the effect of every change. The software supports multi-stage rockets and motor clusters, with a large database of rocket motors for easy selection. There are guides for both beginners and developers, and the project is open source with an active Discord group for community support.

On Hacker News, many people praised OpenRocket as a great tool for students, hobbyists, and teachers. Some shared stories about using it in science classes or with kids, saying it’s much easier to learn than professional engineering software. While a few users noted bugs or missing features, most agreed the software is getting better. Some wanted better packaging for easier install on all platforms, and a few wished for more advanced rocket types or better 3D support in the future. Most commenters liked the big motor database and real-time feedback, and many were excited about the AI assistant and the open developer community. Overall, users were happy to see a strong open source tool for rocketry.

Next up is Wander, a simple tool for exploring personal websites. With Wander, you can browse random pages from a group of websites in the Wander network. To join, you just download two files and add them to your own site, following simple instructions. Once set up, you share your Wander link, and if others add it, your site becomes part of the network. Wander is decentralized—there’s no central company, just many small sites linking together. It’s designed to bring back the feeling of surfing the “small web,” where people make personal pages for fun.

People on Hacker News love Wander because it reminds them of the early days of the internet, before big social media took over. They say it’s easy to set up, even for beginners. Some worry about how to keep the network alive or stop spam, but most like that Wander is open source and easy to check or change. Many hope Wander will help more people make and visit personal websites again.

Our next story is about Tom, a man who repairs both machines and software for farmers. With new AI tools, most farm software is now created and fixed by typing instructions, not by traditional code. Tom’s job is to help farmers when their software tools don’t work, usually because the original instructions were not clear or because outside data changed. For example, Margaret lost money when her harvest-timing tool gave the wrong advice because of a change in weather data. Tom fixed it by adding a warning for future changes. Ethan had a group of custom tools that broke when he updated one of them, so Tom showed him the need for all tools to work together and helped him hire someone to organize the system. Tom also helped Carol, an older farmer, by adding a physical switch to her AI irrigation system so she could turn it off and keep control when needed.

The article shows that even with smart AI tools, human experience and clear instructions are still needed, and maintenance saves money in the long run. On Hacker News, many readers said the story felt true to life, especially for people working in IT or with complex systems. They agreed that writing good instructions for AI is hard and that people often don’t want to pay for maintenance, only for repairs after something breaks. Some liked the idea of a physical switch to keep control, while others wondered if this will be enough as tech gets more complex. Overall, the story was seen as a reminder that as software gets easier to “regenerate,” the need for experts who understand the real world will only grow.

Now let’s talk about Rob Pike’s five rules for good programming. Pike says you can’t guess where your code is slow, so don’t try to make it faster until you know what’s actually slow. Always measure first. The third rule is to avoid fancy algorithms unless your data is very big—simple code is better for most cases. Fancy code is hard to write and has more bugs, while good data structures make the rest of your code easier. Many commenters agreed, sharing stories where they wasted time optimizing the wrong part of a program. Some noted that in special cases, like games or databases, complex algorithms are needed, but for most work, simple code is best. The rules are guides, not strict laws, but following them helps teams work together and fix bugs faster. Many said these rules made them better programmers over time.

Next is Nvidia NemoClaw, an open source tool to run OpenClaw AI assistants in a secure way. NemoClaw uses OpenShell to create a sandbox, meaning the AI runs in a locked-down space with strong rules to protect your files and network. It checks your hardware and software, and helps you set up the sandbox, connecting to Nvidia’s cloud for AI models. You can interact with the AI by chat or terminal, and all requests are controlled by OpenShell. If the AI tries to access something it shouldn’t, you get to allow or block it. The project is still in alpha, so not every feature is finished, and the team asks for feedback.

On Hacker News, many users liked the strong security focus, saying sandboxing is important for always-on AI agents. Some were happy to see Nvidia open source their tools, while others worried about high hardware needs or wanted to use their own GPU instead of Nvidia’s cloud. Some found bugs but were able to work around them. Most people are hopeful but cautious, wanting more real-world tests and good documentation before using NemoClaw for important work.

Now, let’s look at a new book about machine learning benchmarks. The book explains that while benchmarks—splitting data and ranking models—have driven progress in machine learning, they also have problems. Static test sets and narrow metrics can make research less creative, and some people “game” benchmarks without real progress. Benchmarks can keep biases in data and depend on low-paid workers. Big companies with more resources have an advantage. Even so, benchmarks have helped push fields like deep learning forward. The book covers technical issues like overusing test sets and statistical problems, and explains that benchmarks work better for ranking models than for measuring true accuracy. It also highlights new problems with large language models, where it’s hard to know what data they saw, and with multi-task benchmarks.

On Hacker News, some users agreed that benchmarks are both helpful and flawed. While benchmarks are great for learning, real products need more than good scores. Some pointed out the ethical issues—like bias and low pay for data labelers. Others said benchmarks help keep research open and competitive. Many hope the book helps the field think more carefully about what progress really means.

For our last story, the 2025 Turing Award was given to Charles Bennett and Gilles Brassard for their work in quantum information science. They created the foundation of quantum cryptography, making it possible to share secret keys safely using the laws of physics. Their work also led to quantum teleportation and helped build the base for the global quantum internet. Their research connected physics and computer science in new ways.

On Hacker News, most people said the award is well deserved. Some were surprised it took so long for them to win, while others pointed out that quantum cryptography is still hard to use in real life. Many commented on the teamwork between an American and a Canadian, showing how science crosses borders. While a few worry about hype in quantum computing, most agree that Bennett and Brassard’s achievements are real and lasting.
