## The electromechanical angle computer inside the B-52 bomber's star tracker

- 原文链接: [The electromechanical angle computer inside the B-52 bomber's star tracker](https://www.righto.com/2026/04/B-52-star-tracker-angle-computer.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47817132)

This article is about how the B-52 bomber used a special analog computer, called the Angle Computer, to help navigate by tracking stars before GPS existed. The Angle Computer was part of a bigger system called the Astro Compass, which helped the bomber know its direction and position by using the positions of stars in the sky.

The Angle Computer did not use digital electronics. Instead, it was electromechanical, using gears, arms, and motors to solve hard math problems about where stars should appear in the sky. To use it, the navigator needed to enter details about the star, like its position from a special book called the Air Almanac. The Astro Compass tracked a star using a telescope with a light sensor and a stable platform, even while the plane was moving. The navigator would input time and star data using knobs and displays. The system could remember information for three stars, which helped make position fixes more accurate.

The main job of the Angle Computer was to turn the star’s position on the celestial sphere into the local angles needed to point the telescope: azimuth (direction along the horizon) and altitude (angle above the horizon). This required changing from a global coordinate system to the local one, using a process called solving the “navigational triangle.” The Angle Computer physically modeled this triangle inside a small sphere, moving a pointer to show where the star should be seen from the plane. Gears and arms inside the computer worked together to make this happen, and electrical parts sent the results to other instruments. Navigators could use the system to find the aircraft’s heading or to draw “lines of position” on a map by comparing expected and measured angles to stars. By crossing lines from two or three stars, the navigator could figure out the plane’s position.

The article explains how this mix of mechanical and electrical technology was chosen because digital computers were too slow and unreliable in the 1960s. Other possible solutions, like using resolvers or early digital computers, were rejected for being too big or expensive. The Angle Computer worked together with vacuum tubes, transistors, and servos, making it a complex device that was soon replaced by digital systems.

People in the comments find the system very interesting and impressive. One person remembers that Gary Kildall, an early computer pioneer, was inspired by trying to help with similar sea navigation problems. Another person is amazed that such a complex mechanical system actually worked and wants to see a demonstration. The article’s author replies that they have managed to power up the Angle Computer and get the motors running, but getting the full star tracker system working is much harder and will take more time. Readers show respect for the engineering skill needed, and some are surprised at how much effort went into navigation before modern computers and GPS. Others point out how this kind of problem-solving led to new ideas in computing and technology. There is also a sense of nostalgia and wonder about the creativity and precision involved in building machines like the Angle Computer. Some think it is a good reminder of how people used clever mechanical solutions before digital tools became common.

## Any Color You Like: NIST Scientists Create 'Any Wavelength' Lasers

- 原文链接: [Any Color You Like: NIST Scientists Create 'Any Wavelength' Lasers](https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2026/04/any-color-you-nist-scientists-create-any-wavelength-lasers-tiny-circuits)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47819453)

NIST scientists have made a new kind of chip that can create laser light in almost any color, using very small circuits. This could help make big, expensive laser systems much smaller and cheaper, helping things like quantum computers and atomic clocks work outside of labs.

The chip is built by stacking special materials onto normal silicon wafers, like layers in a cake. One layer is lithium niobate, which helps change the color of light. Another is tantalum pentoxide, called tantala, which is very good at creating many colors from just one laser. The chip also has metal parts that let scientists control the light, turning it on and off quickly or changing its color. By putting all these materials together, the chip can make and control many different colors of light, moving them between layers. With this method, NIST made about 50 chips on one wafer, and each chip has about 10,000 circuits, each making a different color. This is important because quantum computers and atomic clocks need lasers at very exact colors, which are hard and expensive to make today. If these chips work well, they could make these quantum machines smaller, cheaper, and much easier to use in real life—for example, in medicine, navigation, or studying the Earth.

The new chips are not just for quantum tech. They could help with faster, better communication between computer chips, make AI tools more powerful, or improve displays in virtual reality. NIST worked with a startup company to help make more of these chips in the future.

Others are curious about the limits—asking how easy it is to control the color, how stable the lasers are, or if the chips can be made in large numbers. A few mention possible new uses, like better sensors or more accurate time-keeping. ## Optimizing Ruby Path Methods

- 原文链接: [Optimizing Ruby Path Methods](https://byroot.github.io/ruby/performance/2026/04/18/faster-paths.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47819369)

This article talks about making Ruby's path handling methods faster, especially for big projects that run many tests in parallel. The main focus is on how tools like Bootsnap help Ruby applications start quicker by speeding up file loading and path operations.

The author explains that when running large test suites, the time it takes to start each worker matters a lot. In systems with hundreds or thousands of workers, even saving one second during setup can save many minutes overall. Bootsnap helps by caching which files exist in the load path, so Ruby does not have to check the file system every time it needs a file. This is done by building a map of all possible files at startup, making future lookups much faster.

However, keeping the cache accurate is hard because adding or removing files should refresh the cache. Bootsnap checks the modification time (mtime) of directories to know if something changed, but this still requires many system calls, which can be slow. Also, tools like git do not update mtimes, so the cache often has to be rebuilt each run in CI systems.

The article then looks at the code that scans directories. The standard way in Ruby uses a lot of system calls, one for each file or folder. This is slow, especially on systems like macOS. The author wanted a better way and suggested changing Ruby to provide the file type directly when scanning directories. This change (Dir.scan) will be in Ruby 4.1 and doubles the speed of scanning.

Next, the article reviews File.join, a common Ruby method for joining path parts. The author found it was much slower than just putting strings together, mainly due to handling special text encodings and extra checks. By optimizing for simple cases, like standard UTF-8 paths, and avoiding unnecessary checks and allocations, File.join became more than 7 times faster in tests. Similar improvements were made to other path methods like File.basename and File.dirname.

From the comments, many readers were impressed by the detailed investigation and happy to see Ruby getting faster. Some pointed out that these kinds of low-level improvements matter a lot in big codebases and CI pipelines. Others shared that they often ignored path methods, assuming they were already optimized, and learned to check for "slow mushrooms" in their own code. A few mentioned worries about backward compatibility when changing method signatures, but liked the careful approach of adding new methods (like Dir.scan) instead of changing old ones.

Some developers wished that similar directory scanning improvements could be brought to other languages or older Ruby versions. There was also interest in how file system details, like directory mtimes and syscall costs, can have a big impact, especially on macOS compared to Linux. A few commenters joked about how every Ruby performance win seems to involve learning about Japanese text encodings.

Finally, people appreciated that the author worked with Ruby maintainers, explained his reasoning, and shared benchmarking details. There was praise for the open-source process, with several hoping these changes would help make Rails and other Ruby projects feel faster soon.

## Migrating from DigitalOcean to Hetzner

- 原文链接: [Migrating from DigitalOcean to Hetzner](https://isayeter.com/posts/digitalocean-to-hetzner-migration/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47815774)

This article is about moving a company’s servers from DigitalOcean to Hetzner to save money and get better performance, all without any downtime. The company was paying $1,432 per month for a big server on DigitalOcean, but by switching to Hetzner, they now pay only $233 a month and have a stronger server.

The old server had 192GB RAM, 32 vCPUs, and over 2TB of storage, and it hosted 30 MySQL databases, 34 websites, GitLab, Neo4j, and more. The new Hetzner server has a faster CPU, more RAM, and fast NVMe storage. The main reason for moving was cost: the Turkish Lira lost value, making US dollar costs too high. The company also wanted to upgrade from CentOS 7 (no longer supported) to AlmaLinux 9.7.

To have zero downtime, the migration was split into six steps. First, they installed all software on the new server and matched every setting. Second, they copied all web files using rsync. Third, they set up MySQL replication so the new server got real-time updates from the old one. Fourth, they lowered DNS TTLs to make changing IP addresses quick. Fifth, they made the old server’s nginx act as a reverse proxy, sending any late traffic to the new server. Last, they switched DNS to the new server and kept the old one as backup for a week.

MySQL migration was the hardest part. They used a tool called mydumper to export and import data in parallel, which saved a lot of time. Upgrading from MySQL 5.7 to 8.0 caused a small problem with system tables, but dropping and re-creating the sys schema fixed it. They also found that some users had too much privilege (“SUPER”), which let them write even when the server should have been read-only; revoking this fixed the issue.

They tested everything by editing their own /etc/hosts file to point to the new server before making the switch for everyone. Scripts were used to change DNS records, update nginx configs, and fix GitLab webhooks, making the process faster and less risky.

The result: No downtime, big savings ($14,388 per year), and better performance. The article ends with advice: use MySQL replication for zero-downtime moves, check user privileges, script as much as possible, use mydumper for big databases, and consider dedicated servers over cloud for steady workloads. All the scripts used are open source on GitHub.

In the comments, many people praised the detailed write-up, saying it was both clear and helpful for anyone planning similar migrations. Some shared their own stories of moving from cloud to dedicated servers and saving money. A few warned that Hetzner’s support is not as strong as DigitalOcean’s, so it may not be right for everyone, especially if you need lots of help. Others pointed out that being able to script and automate tasks makes these migrations much safer. There was also talk about how the falling value of local currencies can make cloud prices unaffordable outside the US or Europe.

Some users discussed technical choices, like the risks of big “all-in-one” servers versus splitting services over several smaller servers, which makes upgrades easier but can add complexity. Others asked about backup and disaster recovery, wondering how the company plans to handle hardware failures now that it’s on a single dedicated machine. A few commenters said that for very dynamic or scaling workloads, the cloud still makes sense, but for steady use, dedicated servers are much cheaper.

Lastly, some people thanked the author for sharing all the scripts, saying this was a rare and generous move. Others suggested that open-sourcing automation like this helps the whole community, making similar moves less scary for everyone.

Each semester, she brings in old typewriters for her German language students. There are no screens, no spellcheck, and no delete button. Students must type their essays or poems by hand, making mistakes and fixing them with “X” marks or pencil. The teacher started this in 2023, after noticing students used AI and online translators to make perfect work that was not their own.

Phelps says the main goal is for students to write on their own, without digital help. She teaches students how to use the typewriter, like feeding paper and using enough force on the keys. Students are surprised by how hard it is and by the need to return the carriage at the end of each line. Some students even needed help from the teacher’s young children, who acted as “tech support.”

Students say the experience is very different from using a laptop. There are no distractions from notifications, so they focus better. They also talk more with classmates for help, instead of searching online. Many students find they need to think more before writing, because fixing mistakes is not easy. One student with a broken wrist found it extra hard, but she enjoyed trying and kept all her messy pages.

This way of teaching is part of a larger trend. More teachers are using pen-and-paper or oral tests to make sure students do their own work, without AI.

Other readers share mixed feelings—some think it’s a fun challenge, while others wonder if it really helps students in today’s digital world. ## Floating Point Fun on Cortex-M Processors

- 原文链接: [Floating Point Fun on Cortex-M Processors](https://danielmangum.com/posts/floating-point-cortex-m/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47804423)

This article explains how floating point math works on Arm Cortex-M microcontrollers, like the nRF52840 and ESP32-S3. It looks at how different compiler options control if floating point calculations use hardware (the FPU) or software, and how this affects how your code runs.

There are three main floating point ABI (Application Binary Interface) options: `soft`, `softfp`, and `hard`. With `soft`, all floating point math is done in software, and numbers are passed using normal CPU registers. With `hard`, the CPU uses special floating point registers and does the math in hardware, which is faster if the chip has an FPU. `softfp` is in the middle: it uses hardware when possible, but passes numbers using the normal registers. If you mix code built with `soft` and `hard` settings, you get linker errors, because they use different ways to pass data.

The article gives real examples using the Zephyr RTOS on the nRF52840 board. By changing build options, you can see how the compiler changes the way it passes arguments and does the math. With `soft` ABI, the compiled code calls a software function to add floats. With `hard`, it uses FPU instructions and floating point registers. With `softfp`, it passes numbers using normal registers, but still uses hardware instructions for the math.

The author also shows how the FPU is enabled in Zephyr using special configuration options. If you try to use floating point instructions without turning on the FPU, the CPU will crash with a usage fault. But you can also turn on the FPU just before you need it, or even handle faults to enable it only when needed (though this is tricky and risky).

In the Hacker News comments, many people share their own problems with floating point ABIs on embedded systems. Some say mixing libraries with different settings is a common source of bugs. Others point out that documentation is often unclear, and it’s easy to make mistakes with build flags. Several readers mention that choosing between `soft`, `softfp`, and `hard` can be confusing, especially when using third-party libraries or RTOSes.

Some developers suggest always using the `hard` ABI if your chip has an FPU, to get the best performance. Others warn that if you use libraries built with the wrong ABI, things will break and errors can be hard to debug. A few people talk about problems with context switching and saving FPU state in multi-threaded programs, saying you need to be careful when letting more than one thread use the FPU.

One commenter likes the idea of enabling the FPU only when needed, as it can save power on battery devices, but others say this adds complexity and possible bugs. Some users share tips for checking ABI settings with tools like `readelf`, while others wish that toolchains and build systems made all this easier to understand. Overall, the comments agree that floating point handling on embedded Arm chips can be tricky, and mistakes can be hard to find, so it’s important to understand how your compiler, build system, and chip work together.

## Michael Rabin has died

- 原文链接: [Michael Rabin has died](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_O._Rabin)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47782925)

Michael O. Rabin, a famous computer scientist and mathematician, has died at age 94. He was known for many important ideas in computer science, especially in theory and cryptography.

Rabin was born in Germany in 1931 and moved to Israel as a child. He showed strong interest in math early on and studied at top schools. He got his PhD from Princeton University under Alonzo Church. In his career, Rabin worked at many big universities like Hebrew University, Harvard, and others. One of his most famous works is with Dana Scott: together, they created the idea of nondeterministic automata. This work is a key part of complexity theory and won them the Turing Award in 1976.

Rabin also invented or helped invent many things used in computer science today. He worked on polynomial time, which is important for understanding how hard problems are. He made the Miller–Rabin primality test, which is a fast way to check if a number is prime, and this is used in cryptography. He created the Rabin cryptosystem, Rabin signature algorithm, and helped with the Rabin–Karp string search algorithm. Rabin also worked on probabilistic automata and oblivious transfer, which are important for privacy and security. Over his life, he won many awards, like the Israel Prize, the Dan David Prize, and the Dijkstra Prize.

He taught many students, some of whom became leaders in computer science. His daughter, Tal Rabin, is also a well-known computer scientist.

In the comments, many people shared their sadness and respect for Rabin. Some called him a giant in the field, saying his work changed how we think about computers and math. Others told stories about meeting him and said he was kind and helpful. People listed his ideas, like the Rabin–Karp algorithm and Miller–Rabin test, and said they use these things in their work even today. Some said he did not get as much public attention as he deserved, but his influence is huge in the computer science world.

A few commenters discussed how Rabin’s ideas, like randomness in algorithms, made a big difference in practice and theory. Others pointed out how his work on automata helped explain what computers can and cannot do. Some remembered his teaching and humor, saying his classes were both hard and fun. Overall, the comments show that Rabin inspired many people and his ideas will be used for a long time.
