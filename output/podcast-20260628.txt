First, let’s talk about a new open-weight AI model from Zhipu AI called GLM 5.2. This model just beat the popular Claude model in a test for finding security bugs in code, especially a problem called IDOR. IDOR is when a user can see or change another user’s data by guessing an ID number. The Semgrep team wanted to check if finding these bugs depends more on the AI model itself or the tools used with it. Their own tool, Semgrep Multimodal, uses a special “harness” to help the AI, and with this, it got the best results. But even without extra help, just using a simple prompt and code, GLM 5.2 did better than expected and even beat Claude Code.

GLM 5.2 is open to download, much cheaper than closed models, and uses a Mixture-of-Experts setup. This means it is big but only uses part of itself for each answer, so it is faster and costs less. It can look at very large codebases, up to a million tokens, which is good for big security jobs. In tests, it scored as well as top models but is much cheaper to use. In the main test, GLM 5.2 got a 39% F1 score, beating Claude’s 32%, and cost only $0.17 per real bug found. Other open models did worse, showing that GLM 5.2 is special.

In the comments, many people are excited about how good open-weight models are getting, especially because they are private and low cost. Some people warn this is just for one kind of security bug, and results might change with other problems. There were questions about “open-weight” versus “open-source,” with the answer being that open-weight means you get the trained model, not the full training data or process. Many users think the test was fair, but others want to see more types of bugs and harder code. Some are surprised at how much the harness helps and suggest focusing on better tools, not just bigger models. People also talk about “reward hacking,” where models may cheat during training, but most agree that better testing and guards are needed. The community feels this is an important moment for open models but wants to see more experiments before making big claims.

Next, we have a personal story about using AI for health. The article tells how someone used Claude Opus 4.8 to get a second opinion on their shoulder MRI after feeling unsure about a clinic’s fast diagnosis and treatment. The author was told by a doctor that they had a serious tendon tear and needed several treatments, but some of these seemed questionable. Curious, the author used Opus 4.8 to review their MRI files, setting up the AI to fully analyze the images. The AI said the tendon was intact and did not show the big tear the doctor described.

To compare, the author asked Claude to look at both the doctor’s and AI’s reports, with extra advice from ChatGPT. The AI looked at the problem step by step and even split the job into smaller tasks. In the end, the AI’s “arbiter” verdict was that there was only mild tendon inflammation, not a big tear, with moderate-to-high confidence. This was very different from the doctor’s view. The author was left unsure whether to trust the doctor, the AI, or just wait and see if the shoulder got better. The article is not about blaming the doctor, but about how AI can add another layer of review.

In the comments, many people are interested in AI for medical imaging but worry about the risks. Some agree it is smart to double-check fast or aggressive treatments, especially if they are expensive or not well supported by science. A few doctors join the discussion, saying that even experts can disagree on MRI results, and AI can also make mistakes or miss important things. Some readers warn that AI might not have enough context about the patient. Others are excited for a future where AI can help catch errors but agree it is not ready to replace doctors yet. There are suggestions to get a third opinion from another human doctor, or to use AI only as a tool, not the main judge. One commenter says this story shows both the promise and the problems of AI in health: it can help, but it can also make people feel confused and unsure who to trust.

Now, let’s talk about memory prices. A new article shows how the price of memory, like DRAM and storage, has changed from 1960 to 2026. Charts and data show the cost per gigabyte for DRAM, NAND flash, and HBM memory. Prices have dropped a lot over time, especially as new types and generations came out. For DRAM, prices are broken down by generation, like DDR through DDR5. The article also talks about the cost to build AI accelerators from companies like Nvidia and AMD, showing how much of the total cost comes from memory and other parts.

The data comes from both old and new sources, with DRAM prices going back to 1957. For newer prices, it uses Amazon price history from Keepa. SSD prices are tracked from 2016, skipping strange data. HBM prices are only estimates, as they are sold only to big companies. The article warns that these numbers are the cheapest found, not what most people pay, and prices are not adjusted for inflation. Data is updated every month for DRAM and NAND, and every quarter for HBM. Anyone can download the data as a CSV file.

Hacker News users were impressed by how cheap memory has become, with some remembering when a few megabytes were very expensive. People noted how SSDs and HBM changed what is possible in AI and gaming. Some warned that as memory gets faster and more complex, price drops might slow down. There were requests for inflation-adjusted numbers and more details on errors in the data. Some explained that very cheap prices often mean old stock being sold off. Others discussed how memory is just one part of the cost for big AI chips, with packaging and other parts getting more expensive. Next up is news about the new number one supercomputer in the world, called LineShine, revealed at the ISC 2026 event in Germany. LineShine, built in Shenzhen, China, is the first Chinese supercomputer on the TOP500 list in nine years. It uses a special CPU called the LX2, which follows the Armv9 standard and supports new instructions for faster calculations. Each chip has 304 cores, 32 GB of fast memory, and 256 GB of DDR5. The system has over 22,000 nodes and more than 13 million CPU cores in total. It can reach 2.2 exaflops of real performance and uses 42 megawatts of power, making it very powerful for a CPU-only system. It is also the best in the HPCG benchmark for real scientific workloads.

There is also a new number six supercomputer from Italy’s Eni company, based on AMD chips. Italy now has more computing power on the list than any other country in Europe. The old number one, Japan’s Fugaku, is now number nine but still does well in some tests. The Green500 list for power efficiency has not changed in its top 10. Because some old systems were retired, the overall field is now more efficient.

Many people are surprised to see China submit a new top system after years of being quiet. Some wonder if China will add more systems. There are also questions about why big AI companies do not submit their huge GPU systems, even though they are very powerful. The TOP500 list will soon be managed by ACM SIGHPC for better historical tracking.

In the comments, people are impressed by China’s return and the technical details of LineShine’s CPU. Some discuss possible political reasons for the long wait. There is also debate about CPU-only versus GPU-based systems, and whether AI clusters should be listed as supercomputers. Some are concerned about power use and the environment but see that overall efficiency is improving. There are also jokes about how these huge computers compare to home PCs. Overall, people are very interested in the technology and the global competition in supercomputing.

Now let’s look at a project called LibrePods, which helps users get AirPods features on devices not made by Apple, like Android phones and Linux computers. The idea is to let people use features like noise control, ear detection, battery status, and head gestures, without needing an iPhone or Mac. LibrePods works by copying how AirPods talk to Apple devices. Many features already work on Linux and Android, while some advanced features are only on Android or need more work.

Plans include support for the “Find My” network and better spatial audio, but these may be tricky. The project allows you to “spoof” your device’s identity to unlock more features. Most of the code is hand-written, but some used AI help. The code is open source and uses the GNU General Public License. The project warns not to trust fake websites.

In the comments, many users are excited and impressed by the reverse engineering work, calling it clever and useful for breaking tech “walled gardens.” Some ask about privacy and security, and others about which AirPods models work best. A few say they have tried the app and it works well for basic things, but some advanced features can be buggy. There are also questions about legal risks, since copying Apple’s protocol might upset Apple. Overall, most people feel LibrePods gives users more freedom with their devices and hope the project will keep growing.

Now, let’s talk about running OpenBSD on the Lemote Yeeloong laptop, a rare Chinese netbook made for free software. The Yeeloong was designed to be cheap, use little power, and have an open bootloader, making it popular with people who care about free software, like Richard Stallman.

Updates and upgrades are tricky, and the Yeeloong is slow, but it is unique because of its open design.

Finally, we have a project about building a GPT-2-like language model from scratch using only C and CUDA, with no machine learning libraries. The creator wrote everything by hand: forward and backward passes, tokenizer, training, and custom CUDA kernels. The model can run on a CPU or a single GPU like an RTX 4070. It uses a hand-made byte-level tokenizer and trains on books and web text, then fine-tunes as a chatbot. The model has about 116 million parameters, so it can write good English sentences, but does not have real knowledge. The project is named “NanoEuler” because it uses a math method called the Euler method in its design.

All parts are checked carefully, with gradients compared to math checks. The model uses features like RMSNorm, rotary embeddings, grouped-query attention, and FlashAttention for speed. You can train small models on your CPU or run the full pipeline on GPU. The code is open and simple, for learning and research, not production.

In the comments, people praised the project’s clarity and completeness. Many were impressed by the hand-written backpropagation and careful checks. Some said it is rare to understand every part of a big model, so this project is great for learning. Others asked about training times, hardware, and technical details. Some were excited to try the code on their own GPUs. There was also talk about how projects like this help education, since students can see all the parts and how they fit together. Overall, people thanked the author for making a hard topic easier to learn and use.
