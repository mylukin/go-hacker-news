First, let’s start with an unusual story from Uganda’s Kibale National Park. Scientists have watched a group of chimpanzees split into two and start fighting a “civil war” that has lasted eight years. These chimps used to live together peacefully, even holding hands, but a series of events broke their social ties: many adult chimps died in 2014, a new leader came in 2015, and a disease hit in 2017. After the split, the Western group started attacking the Central group, resulting in 24 deaths, including 17 infants. Researchers say this shows that animal group conflicts can start from social changes, not just over land or food. The study may help us understand why humans sometimes fight over new group identities, even without politics or religion.

In the comments, some readers were surprised by the violence, while others said chimps have shown this before. There was discussion about how animal behavior can look very human, with alliances and power struggles. Some worried about chimp safety and conservation. Others joked about “chimp politics” and how the story sounds like human history. Some wanted to know if scientists should step in, but others said it’s important to let nature take its course. One user pointed out that violence is not just a human problem, but something deeper in our past. Another found hope, saying that by understanding animal conflict, maybe we can avoid future wars. Overall, the story made people think about both nature and society.

Next, we have a look at a new way to play chess: 1D-Chess. It’s a simple version played in a straight line, not a square board. You play as white against AI, using only King, Knight, and Rook. The King moves one space, the Knight jumps two spaces, and the Rook moves any number of spaces left or right. The goal is still to checkmate the enemy King. Games can end in a draw by stalemate, threefold repetition, or if only Kings are left. This variant was first described by Martin Gardner in 1980.

Many in the comments found 1D-Chess fun and surprisingly tricky, even though it looks simple. Some think it’s a great way to practice new chess ideas or teach beginners. There are questions about whether White always wins with perfect play, and some users discuss the power of the Knight in this version. Others enjoy the simple rules, and a few joke about “zero-dimensional chess.” Many remember Martin Gardner and say it’s nice to see his influence continue. Overall, people enjoyed this clever twist on a classic game.

Now for some software news: WireGuard has released new Windows versions after fixing a Microsoft driver signing issue that had blocked updates. The new versions bring bug fixes, better performance, and drop support for old Windows versions, making the code faster and safer. There are new features, like better handling of allowed IPs and low MTUs, and an easier update process. The team tested the update even on very old Windows builds and asks users to report any problems.

The background: Microsoft had blocked WireGuard’s developer account, stopping driver updates. This caused worry online, but after public discussion, Microsoft fixed the issue. The developer says it was just bureaucracy, not bad intent. In the comments, many thank the WireGuard team and are glad to see updates. Some are frustrated with Microsoft’s strict rules, while others understand the need to protect users from malware. There is talk about the value of dropping old Windows support, and many praise WireGuard’s security and performance. People are happy the issue is fixed and the project is moving forward.

Next, Keychron has published industrial design files for over 100 keyboard and mouse models on GitHub. The files are free for personal, educational, and some commercial accessory use. You can find CAD assets in formats like STEP, DXF, and PDF, and use them to study real hardware, design your own cases, or 3D print parts. The license allows making and selling original accessories, but not copying or branding products as “Keychron.” The company also offers guides and a Discord for support.

In the comments, people praised Keychron for being open, saying it’s rare for hardware companies to share like this. Some think it will help with custom parts and repairs, and could grow Keychron’s brand by making their products more customizable. A few note that the license is not fully open source, and some worry about legal confusion if people try to sell clones. Still, most see this as a win for hobbyists and students, and hope other companies will do the same. The community is excited about having more freedom to make and learn.

Moving on, the JSON Formatter Chrome plugin, once open source, has moved to a closed, paid model. The creator says this is to add more features, but the old open source version is still on GitHub. The plugin makes it easy to read JSON in the browser, with features like dark mode and fast loading, but some users are upset because the new version shows ads or acts like adware. Many say this is common when open source plugins go closed and try to make money—often by showing ads or selling data. Some think users should be careful and check permissions before updating extensions. There are suggestions to use forks or older versions, and calls for better browser controls. A few thank the developer for leaving the old version online, but most agree it's important to review what runs in your browser.

Next, we have “watgo,” a new toolkit in Go for working with WebAssembly. With watgo, you can read and write WebAssembly code using only Go, no extra tools needed. It can turn WAT text files into binary WASM, and the other way around. There’s a command-line tool and a Go programming interface. The main part, “wasmir,” lets you see and change the inside of a WebAssembly module. Watgo comes with many tests and uses the official test suite. The author says it works well and is fast enough for most use cases.

In the comments, people were happy to see a pure Go toolkit for WebAssembly, since other tools need C++ or Rust. Some note that watgo does not yet support every feature, but it covers the basics well. Developers are excited to use it for backend work with Go. Others ask for more features and wonder about performance. Some had trouble testing with other tools and liked that watgo uses Node.js for testing. Overall, the community is interested and supportive, looking forward to future updates.

In the world of CAD, FluidCAD is a new parametric tool that lets you design 3D models by writing JavaScript code. You type code and see the 3D shape update right away. You can draw, extrude, add fillets and shells, and change steps at any time. If you like, you can also use the mouse to shape objects and then save those changes as code. FluidCAD supports importing and exporting STEP files.

Many commenters liked the idea of coding CAD models in JavaScript, saying it gives more control and makes changes easier. Some compared it to OpenSCAD, but said FluidCAD is easier to use because of real-time updates and the popular language. Others liked mixing code and mouse editing. There were questions about how well it handles complex models and if it’s good for professional use. Some want more documentation, and others ask about pricing and licenses. Overall, people are interested and have ideas for making FluidCAD better.

Next, let’s talk about helium. The article explains that helium is hard to replace in many industries—medical, tech, aerospace—especially after recent supply problems because of the war in Iran and the closure of the Strait of Hormuz. Helium is rare and mostly comes from the US and Qatar. It is used in MRI machines, chip making, fiber optics, welding, and more. Sometimes, other gases like argon or hydrogen are used instead, but they have problems. Recycling helium helps, but in many cases nothing else works as well.

In the comments, some worry that selling off the US helium reserve was a mistake, making shortages worse. Others say recycling should be used more, especially in labs and hospitals. There is hope for new technology that could use less helium, like high-temperature superconductors. Some say helium should be treated like oil and kept in reserves. There are also questions about using helium for party balloons. Many agree: helium is hard to replace, and shortages cause trouble for science, medicine, and industry.

Now, about RISC-V—a free, open CPU standard that anyone can use or change. The article explains why Canonical, the company behind Ubuntu, cares about RISC-V. Big companies like Qualcomm, NVIDIA, and Google already use RISC-V. It is easy to add new features, which helps in AI, security, and special hardware. Ubuntu has supported RISC-V since 2021, and Canonical promises long-term support.

Hacker News users are excited about RISC-V because it is open and could lead to more innovation and lower costs. Some worry that too many custom versions could hurt software compatibility. Others want to see better tools and more mature support. There are comments about “technology sovereignty,” meaning countries or companies can avoid relying on foreign chip makers. Some users have tried RISC-V and find it easier for new ideas than closed platforms. Many see RISC-V as a big step for hardware freedom, even if it takes time to match Intel or ARM in speed and price.

Finally, a warning about security. CPUID’s website, known for tools like HWMonitor and CPU-Z, was hacked for six hours. During the attack, download links were changed to serve malware. The real programs were not changed, but users who downloaded during the hack got fake installers with harmful files. The malware tried to steal browser data and used tricks to avoid being noticed.

In the comments, people were worried and upset. Some say this shows that even trusted sites can be risky if their backend is not safe. Others point out that digital signatures are not enough if attackers change the links. There is talk about using hashes and checking downloads. Some only trust open source tools or build software themselves. Others want better ways to check software, like sandboxing. Some defend CPUID, saying it’s hard for small teams to protect everything, but others blame them for not having better security. Many agree this is a wake-up call to always be careful, even with well-known sites.
