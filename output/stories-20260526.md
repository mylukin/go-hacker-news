- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48271448)

This article talks about new pixel fonts that are inspired by old tech, like VCRs and early TVs. It explains how designers are updating these classic styles to work better on modern computers.

First, it mentions Analog Mono, a font made by Andrew Gleeson. The old VCR OSD Mono font had a problem: letters with tails, like "g" or "y," were pulled up and looked strange. Analog Mono fixes this, making the letters look more normal.

Next, there’s Coral Pixels by Kumiko Yoshida. This font adds colorful edges, just like the visual "fringing" you saw on screens in the 1990s and 2000s. The fringing used to be a mistake, but now it’s a fun, nostalgic look.

Another font, Two Slice by Joseph Fatula, is only 2 pixels tall. Even though it’s tiny, you can still read it—kind of impressive!

All of these fonts are vector fonts, not real pixel fonts. That means they can be used on any modern computer and scale to any size, but they pretend to be old pixel fonts.

The last font is Geist Pixel, made by Vercel. The creators say it’s not just for fun or decoration. It’s made to work well in real products, fixing common problems with pixel fonts. For example, most pixel fonts don’t scale well or don’t fit with other fonts; Geist Pixel tries to solve these issues. The team behind Geist Pixel worked hard on not just the letters, but also the details like spacing, extra symbols, and how the font fits in with others.

A few users discuss how hard it is to make pixel fonts look good at different sizes, and they praise the work that went into Geist Pixel’s spacing and layout. Some still prefer the "real" pixel look from old bitmap fonts, saying that vector versions don’t always feel the same.

One commenter likes the colorful Coral Pixels font, saying it brings back memories of old computer screens. Another person wonders if there’s a real need for pixel fonts today, or if it’s just a style trend.

A few people talk about the technical side, noting that good kerning and metrics are important, and they appreciate that Geist Pixel focuses on these details. Some wish for even more creative or playful pixel fonts in the future.

## The Ballad of TIGIT

- 原文链接: [The Ballad of TIGIT](https://www.owlposting.com/p/the-ballad-of-tigit)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48281367)

This article tells the story of TIGIT drugs, a new type of cancer treatment that looked very promising but ended up failing after years of research and billions of dollars spent. The author compares TIGIT drugs to past disappointments like amyloid-beta drugs for Alzheimer’s, explaining that sometimes, even the best ideas in medicine don’t work out.

TIGIT is a protein found on certain immune cells. Scientists thought that blocking TIGIT would help the immune system fight cancer better, just like the successful cancer drug Keytruda, which blocks a different “brake” on the immune system. Drug companies were excited and invested a lot. Roche was the first to make a TIGIT drug called tiragolumab. Their first tests looked good, so they started many big trials for different cancers. Other companies like Merck, BMS, BeiGene, Arcus, Gilead, iTeos, and GSK also made their own TIGIT drugs and started testing them.

At first, everyone hoped TIGIT drugs would be the “next Keytruda.” But as larger trials finished, the results were disappointing. Roche’s main studies showed that tiragolumab did not help patients live longer. In some trials, patients on the drug even did worse than those on standard treatment. One trial had a small success, but it did not change the overall outcome. Other companies had similar failures. Merck’s drug, vibostolimab, was stopped because it was causing too many side effects and didn’t help patients live longer. Arcus and Gilead tried a new version of TIGIT drug that was supposed to be safer, but it also failed. Smaller companies gave up quickly when results were bad, and some, like iTeos, even shut down.

The article explains that almost 49,000 patients joined TIGIT drug trials, costing over $3 billion. This “herding” effect happened because companies saw others investing and wanted to join in. At the end, only AstraZeneca is still testing a TIGIT drug, but hope is low. Experts now avoid talking about TIGIT, and it’s seen as a bad bet in cancer research.

In the comments, some people said this story shows how drug discovery is very hard and full of risk, even when the science looks good. Others pointed out that the drug industry often chases trends, spending too much on the latest idea instead of exploring different paths. A few commenters worried about the cost to patients who joined these trials and might have missed out on better treatments. Some defended the companies, saying that testing new ideas is important, and failure is part of progress in medicine. People also debated whether there should be better ways to decide when to stop big trials if early signs are not good, to save money and protect patients. Some users were surprised that so many companies followed each other without waiting for stronger evidence. Others felt that this was a lesson in humility for both scientists and investors: even smart people with good data can be wrong. A few commenters were hopeful that the research might still help in the future, maybe with better understanding or for a special group of patients. Finally, some readers said they appreciated the honest review, even if it was a sad story for cancer research.

## Launch HN: Minicor (YC P26) – Windows desktop automations at scale

- 原文链接: [Launch HN: Minicor (YC P26) – Windows desktop automations at scale](https://www.minicor.com/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48280729)

Minicor is a tool for automating tasks on old Windows desktop apps that do not have APIs. It helps companies connect their new AI systems to these legacy apps, which are common in fields like healthcare and finance.

The main idea is to let teams build robots (RPAs) that can use desktop programs just like a human does—clicking buttons, reading text, and filling out forms. Many old systems don’t let outsiders connect through an API, so Minicor acts as a “robot user.” The special part is Minicor’s “self-healing agents.” If something on the screen changes, or a pop-up appears, the robot notices, fixes itself, and keeps working. This means users don’t have to rewrite scripts every time the app gets an update.

Minicor can run these robots anywhere—on Windows machines, in the cloud, or even on Citrix systems. You start automations with just one API call. It’s already handling work for thousands of patients per day in production.

For debugging, Minicor records videos of every automation run and sends alerts if something fails. Users can watch these videos or check screenshots to see exactly what happened when something goes wrong.

Minicor works by recording a video of a human doing the job, then turning that into a computer program. It claims to be more accurate than other “computer vision” tools because it saves the steps as code and only uses AI to recover when something unusual happens. It reports 93–96% accuracy, which is higher than other approaches.

Security is important: Minicor is SOC 2 Type II certified and HIPAA compliant. It can run fully on-premise, so data never leaves the company.

The system promises to get companies from zero to production in weeks, instead of months. It works with many types of old business software in healthcare, finance, and logistics.

## C64 Basic: Game Map Overhead “Camera View”

- 原文链接: [C64 Basic: Game Map Overhead “Camera View”](https://retrogamecoders.com/overhead-camera-view/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48279842)

This article explains how to make a game map with an overhead, “camera” view in C64 BASIC, like in old games such as Ultima. The writer wanted to answer a question about how to show a moving window into a big game world, and how to make this work fast on the Commodore 64.

The main idea is to have a big 2D map in memory, but only show a small part—the viewport—on screen. The player moves around the map, and the camera follows, keeping the player in the center. The visible area is just a small slice of the whole map. At first, the writer shows simple code: for each frame, copy map tiles from memory to the screen, centering on the player. But this first version is very slow because it does lots of math and “POKE” commands for every tile, every frame.

To make it faster, the writer tries several tricks. First, they use a “lookup table” to avoid multiplication. Instead of calculating screen positions on the fly, they pre-calculate them once and use the results over and over. Then, they flatten the 2D map into a 1D array, using more lookup tables to remove more slow math. Next, they “unroll” the drawing loop, writing out each row by hand, instead of using a FOR loop, because loops also slow down BASIC on C64. Each step makes the game faster, but startup (loading) gets a bit slower because of the setup work.

The writer also shares ideas for further speed-ups. For example, they say using PRINT instead of POKE is faster for drawing, or calling assembly code to draw tiles super quickly. Using “meta-tiles”—bigger blocks of tiles—can help build maps faster and use less memory. Other ideas are to add color, only redraw changed rows or columns, use hardware scrolling, and use custom graphics for better looks.

At the end, the writer sums up the lessons: always separate the game world from what you show on screen. Use lookup tables to save time. Find and speed up the slowest part of your code. And, measure first—sometimes, an optimization might not help as much as you think.

In the comments, some people are happy to see C64 BASIC code and feel nostalgic. They like how the article explains each step and shows real code. A few say that using assembly would be better for speed, but agree that BASIC is good for learning and demos. Some users share their own tricks from past C64 games, like building the map as strings or using color memory. Others point out that “partial redraws” (only drawing what changed) can be a huge speed-up. There are also tips about using PRINT and cursor control codes for faster drawing, and a few people remember using meta-tiles for their own games. Some discuss how these old tricks still help in modern systems, not just retro ones. A few wish there were more BASIC tutorials like this, and thank the writer for the clear, step-by-step guide.
