## Virginia bans sale of geolocation data

- 原文链接: [Virginia bans sale of geolocation data](https://www.hunton.com/privacy-and-cybersecurity-law-blog/virginia-bans-sale-of-geolocation-data)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48767347)

Virginia just passed a law that will ban the sale of geolocation data starting July 1, 2026. This means companies in Virginia cannot sell your location information for money to others.

The law updates the Virginia Consumer Data Protection Act and says “sale” only means trading data for money—not for other things of value. Other states like Maryland and Oregon already have similar bans, but they include trades for both money and anything valuable, which is a broader rule. Virginia’s law follows growing worry about how companies buy and sell people’s location. Some states, like California and Washington, are also looking at making these bans. There have been investigations and fines about selling location data, like a big case in California and a ban from the Federal Trade Commission on a data broker last year. The point of these new rules is to protect people’s privacy and stop companies from trading sensitive data without clear permission.

In the comments, many people say this is a good step for privacy. Some wish the law was even stronger and covered trades for things other than money, since companies can still swap data for other benefits. Others are worried about how companies will try to work around the law or find loopholes. A few think it will be hard to enforce, since companies can just change how they describe these deals. Some wonder if the law will really stop apps from sending your location to advertisers or trackers. There are also comments saying a federal law would be better than a patchwork of state rules. Others point out that even with this ban, companies can still collect location data for their own use—just not sell it. Some are hopeful this will lead to more privacy laws in other states. A few people think users should have full control over their own data, not just geolocation. There is also talk about how location data can be dangerous if misused, like tracking people without their consent. Finally, some commenters say that simple privacy settings and clear choices for users are just as important as new laws.

## Since Linux 6.9, LUKS suspend stopped wiping disk-encryption keys from memory

- 原文链接: [Since Linux 6.9, LUKS suspend stopped wiping disk-encryption keys from memory](https://mathstodon.xyz/@iblech/116769502749142438)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48763035)

Linux 6.9 caused a problem where, after suspending a laptop, the encryption key for the disk stayed in memory instead of being wiped. This is important because many people use disk encryption (like LUKS) to protect data if their laptop is lost or stolen. Normally, you expect the key to be removed from memory when the laptop sleeps, so that it cannot be stolen with a cold boot attack or similar method. The bug appeared because of a small but important change in the Linux code that had an unexpected effect on how encryption keys were handled. A simple one-line fix was found, but the author notes that formal checks are still needed to be sure it’s perfect. There is now an automated test to help catch such bugs in the future, and a warning will be shown if the key is not properly wiped. The post also talks about a project to securely wipe keys on suspend, even for the main (root) filesystem, and how this idea can be used in other Linux versions.

People in the comments had a lot to say. Some worried about how real the security risk is, saying newer computers have memory protections and cold boot attacks are rare. Others replied that law enforcement sometimes does use cold boot attacks, especially in countries like Germany. Some said that most users just close their laptop lid to sleep, not fully shut down, so this bug could affect many people. There was debate about how annoying it would be to have to enter your disk password every time you wake your laptop, and if that might make people stop using full disk encryption. Some users pointed out that many systems (like Ubuntu and Debian) do not lock the disk on suspend by default, and people might wrongly believe their data is safe when it is not. Others talked about how hard it is to be sure the key is wiped at exactly the right time before suspend, not after resume, and how good testing is needed. Some suggested using virtual machines to check if the key is still in memory after suspend. There was also surprise that there were not already good automated tests for this kind of security problem. Overall, people agreed it’s a tricky issue and needs better tools, more testing, and clear communication about what is actually protected.

## Lightning Memory-Mapped Database Manager (LMDB) 1.0

- 原文链接: [Lightning Memory-Mapped Database Manager (LMDB) 1.0](http://www.lmdb.tech/doc/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48766598)

This article is about LMDB, a fast, memory-mapped database library used for storing key-value data. It explains how LMDB works, its features, and some things to watch out for if you use it.

LMDB uses a B-tree structure and is modeled after BerkeleyDB, but it is simpler and faster. The whole database is mapped into memory, so when you read data, it comes straight from memory, not from a cache or extra copy. This makes reading very fast and uses less memory. LMDB is transactional and supports ACID rules, which means your data stays safe and correct, even if something goes wrong. If you open the database in read-only mode, your data cannot be corrupted by bugs in your program. LMDB allows many readers at the same time without locking, but only one writer can change data at once. Writers do not block readers, so the database stays fast even with many users. Unlike other databases that use logs or keep writing new data, LMDB reuses space inside its files, so the database does not get bigger and bigger over time. You can use LMDB in read-only or read-write modes; read-write is faster for writing but can be unsafe if your program has bugs.

The article gives advice for fixing problems, like stale locks or database files not being cleaned up after a crash. It tells you to check for old reader processes and to avoid using LMDB on network drives. There are also details about using LMDB with different operating systems, and reminders that only one user should use the database at a time on some systems. Some older versions of LMDB may write garbage data to disk, but new settings can control this. Each transaction belongs to a single thread, and you should not open the same database twice in the same process. Long-running transactions can cause the database to grow quickly, so it is better to keep them short. Suspending or killing a process with an open transaction can also cause issues.

Many Hacker News readers say LMDB is very fast and reliable, and they like how simple it is compared to other databases. Some people mention they use LMDB for projects where speed is very important. Others warn that, because LMDB uses memory-mapping, it can be hard to use on network file systems or in Docker containers. A few users say they had problems with locking or stale readers, especially after crashes, and recommend running cleanup tools. Some commenters like that LMDB does not need background processes or log cleanup, unlike databases like LevelDB or SQLite. Others point out that you must set the maximum database size when you create it, which can be hard to guess. Some users say that, with very large databases, LMDB can run into system limits or memory address space problems. There are also comments about the risks of using write mode if your program has bugs, because it can corrupt the data. A few suggest using LMDB only for read-heavy workloads or when you need to share a database between processes. Many users agree that, if you understand its caveats, LMDB is a great choice for embedded databases.

Podman v6.0.0 is a new, major release of a tool for managing containers on Linux and other systems. The update brings big changes to networking, security, and user experience.

The networking part now uses Netavark, Pasta, and nftables. This should make things easier to fix and add new features in the future. There is also a new, experimental feature called Pesto, which helps with port forwarding and keeps the right source IPs when running containers without root.

“Podman Machine” is better for working with virtual machines (VMs). It now works well with different VM providers and has a new command to update the VM’s operating system. This should make using and updating Podman machines much easier for everyone.

Another part called “Quadlet” got a major upgrade. Now it supports REST APIs, makes it easier to manage related files, adds more features for volume units, and lets package makers find files more easily.

Big changes also came to Podman’s configuration files. Managing settings and users should be smoother and more reliable now, especially for people who run Podman on servers for many users. If you want all the details, there’s a separate blog post about config file changes.

Podman keeps getting better at working like Docker. The Docker API support and the output from Podman commands are now closer to what Docker users expect. This makes moving from Docker to Podman simpler.

If you want to see every change, the release notes on GitHub list them all. The Podman team thanks all contributors and encourages users to try the new version and give feedback.

Setting up your own PeerTube site is possible, and there are guides for different ways to install it, including using Docker or YunoHost. Documentation is available for users, admins, and developers, and PeerTube’s code is open source under the AGPL license.

The author describes how they got Mesa’s Lavapipe driver, which lets Vulkan run on the CPU (no GPU needed), to build, install, and register on NetBSD 10.1 amd64. The process is automated: you just run some scripts on a fresh NetBSD install, and the driver ends up in the right place. The project documents every step, including past problems and fixes, so others can follow or improve the process. Prebuilt binaries are coming soon, so in the future, people can just download and install, not build from source.

Right now, the driver is ready, but running Vulkan apps needs the Vulkan loader (`libvulkan.so.1`), which is not yet finished. There is also a workaround for a compiler issue that will be fixed later. The project’s scripts handle setup, building dependencies, compiling Mesa, installing the driver, and packaging binaries. If something fails, rerunning the scripts will continue from where you stopped. All work is shared on GitHub with MIT licensing for the scripts, while other software keeps its own license.

In the comments, some people are excited that NetBSD is getting Vulkan, saying this helps make NetBSD more usable for modern software. Others point out that Lavapipe is a CPU driver, so it’s slow and not for games, but it is useful for testing and running basic graphics apps. ## Postgres transactions are a distributed systems superpower

- 原文链接: [Postgres transactions are a distributed systems superpower](https://www.dbos.dev/blog/co-locating-workflow-state-with-your-data)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48765639)

This article talks about why keeping your workflow state and your app data together in the same Postgres database is a strong idea for building reliable systems. The main point is that using Postgres transactions for both your workflow and your data lets you handle hard problems like idempotency (doing something only once) and atomicity (making sure two things happen together or not at all) much more easily.

When your workflow and app data are in the same database, you can update both in one transaction. This means you can avoid problems where only part of your work gets saved if something fails. For example, if you have a step that adds money to an account, you want to make sure you don’t accidentally add the money twice if your system crashes and restarts. Usually, people have to build extra tables to keep track of what has already been done (like an “applied_payments” table). But if you use one transaction for both the workflow checkpoint and the account update, you don’t need this extra logic—the database makes sure either both are saved, or neither is.

The article also explains the “transactional outbox” pattern for sending messages to other systems. Normally, if you want to update your database and also send a message (like telling a warehouse about a new order), you have to make sure both things happen together. The common way is to write both the update and the message to an “outbox” table in one transaction, then have a separate process send the message. But this adds more moving parts. With everything in Postgres, you can use a user-defined function to do both steps at once and let a worker process the workflow later, making things simpler and more reliable.

In the comments, some users really like this approach because it makes systems simpler and safer, reducing the need for extra code. Others point out that putting both workflow and app data in the same database can make the database more complex and harder to scale, especially for large systems. A few say that while this works well for many cases, sometimes strict separation is needed for big teams or for security reasons. There are concerns about locking, performance, and database bloat if everything is in one place. Some mention that this idea is not new, and similar tricks have been used before with other databases. Others ask for more real-world examples and benchmarks. A few people worry about vendor lock-in to Postgres or the risk of relying too much on database-specific features. But overall, many agree that for small to medium systems or for teams that want to move fast, this “co-located” approach is very practical and useful.

## How to ask for help from people who don't know you

- 原文链接: [How to ask for help from people who don't know you](https://pradyuprasad.com/writings/how-to-ask-for-help/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48761118)

This article explains how to ask for help from people you don’t know, whether you need advice, a job, or anything else. The author says that asking for help is a skill, not just luck or personality.

First, the main idea is to think about the person you’re asking, not just about yourself. When you contact someone, show that you are serious and worth helping. You can do this by showing “proof of work”—for example, sharing a project you’ve completed or something you’ve written, not just saying you want to learn. Another way to build trust is by mentioning a mutual contact, but be careful because if that person is not trusted, it can backfire. You can also share your school or company, but this is not very strong and can sound like you are just showing off.

Next, explain your situation quickly and clearly. Don’t give too much background or tell your life story. Instead, connect your request to something the helper already cares about or knows. For example, if you are writing to a scientist, mention their paper you studied, not just your childhood love for science.

Then, make your request easy to accept. This means asking for something small and clear, like “Can I get 20 minutes of your time?” instead of a big favor. Be specific and make it simple for them to help—write your question in an email, or give them a short blurb to forward if you want an introduction. Keep your request limited, not open-ended.

Finally, make it easy for the person to say no. Don’t make them feel guilty or pressured. If they say no, thank them and move on. If you push too hard, you can damage the relationship. Help that is given freely is better and can lead to a real connection.

In the Hacker News comments, some people agree that showing proof of work is very important—it shows you are not just asking for free help without any effort. Others share that short, clear requests are more likely to get a response, especially from busy people. Some users say that mentioning a common contact can help, but only if you are sure that person is respected. A few comment that people sometimes ask for too much too soon, like asking to be mentored for a long time, which is overwhelming.

Others point out that being honest is key—if you lie or exaggerate, people can sense it and will not want to help you. Some add that even a “no” can be useful, because it keeps things honest and lets you move forward. One commenter says that making it easy to say “no” actually makes people more likely to say “yes” because they feel no pressure. Lastly, several people remind others to always thank someone for their time, even if you don’t get the help you wanted, as it leaves a good impression for the future.
