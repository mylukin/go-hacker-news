First, the Zilog Z80 processor has turned 50 years old. Released in 1976, the Z80 became a key part of many early home computers, game consoles, and even industrial systems. It was designed to be compatible with the Intel 8080 but added many new features, like easier wiring, a single power supply, and more readable assembly language. Many programs could run on both chips, but the Z80 was cheaper and easier to use. Zilog, the company behind it, was started by engineers who left Intel. The Z80 powered the original GameBoy and many classic home computers. Although Zilog later focused on industrial chips, the Z80’s design influenced many later processors. It was finally discontinued in 2024, but its legacy lives on.

The author of the article shared personal stories about building a Z80 computer in school, learning computer engineering, and even writing code in a computer built inside a Tupperware box. Commenters on Hacker News shared their own memories—some built Z80 computers or learned programming with Z80 assembly. Many praised the chip’s simplicity, saying it helped them start their careers. Some compared the Z80 to other chips like the 6502 and discussed which was better. Others found Z80 chips in unexpected places, like cash registers and printers. There were also business discussions, such as why IBM chose Intel over Zilog for the PC. Most people felt grateful for the Z80 and its role in their lives.

Now, let’s look far beyond Earth. Scientists have found an atmosphere around a rocky planet called LHS 1140b, orbiting a star 48 light-years away. This is the first time an atmosphere has been found on a small, Earth-like planet outside our solar system, making it a big step in the search for life. The planet is in the “Goldilocks zone”—not too hot, not too cold, just right for water and maybe life. Scientists detected helium in the upper atmosphere. Helium alone cannot support life, but other, life-friendly gases could be present lower down. The authors say this is not proof of life, but it gives hope for future research.

Other planets, like K2-18b and those in the TRAPPIST-1 system, have also been studied, but strong evidence for Earth-like atmospheres is still missing. In the comments, people were excited about the progress. Some reminded us that helium is not a sign of life, but it’s a big technical step. Others praised the teamwork and technology needed for such research. Some were hopeful that one day we might find a planet like Earth, or even life itself.

Now, let’s move to databases. One article shares lessons from using SQLite as the database for a Django website. The writer expected things to be simple for a small site but found some tricky parts. They turned on WAL mode, but then a full-text search query on 4000 rows took 5 seconds. After running ANALYZE, the query became almost instant. Cleaning up the database was also tough—deleting many rows at once blocked other writes, so the writer switched to batch deletes. The Django ORM worked well for most things. For backups, they tried both restic and Litestream, with Litestream being better for memory use. The writer also learned that using multiple SQLite files can work, but has risks.

In the comments, people praised SQLite for small sites, but warned about its limits—only one write at a time can happen. Many shared tips like using batch deletes, running ANALYZE regularly, and switching to Postgres if you need more power. Some joked about learning these basics after years of use. Others shared their own SQLite stories, both good and bad. There were tips about testing backups by restoring them. Overall, people liked SQLite for simple projects, but agreed it’s important to know its quirks.

Another story is about someone who wrote their own X server for Linux in Assembly language, called "frame". The writer also replaced other programs with their own small Assembly tools, cutting the total code size from millions of lines to about 100,000. The main goal was to save battery and CPU, and in tests, "frame" used much less CPU than Xorg. Lobste.rs, a popular tech news site, moved its main database from MariaDB to SQLite. The team waited for a busy Monday to test the new setup, and it worked very well—CPU and memory use dropped, the site felt faster, and costs went down. The switch took a long time and needed many changes, like fixing slow queries and writing special scripts to move data. They learned about the differences between MariaDB and SQLite, and had to adjust some database columns. Good communication and realistic test data were important for success.

In the comments, people cheered the migration and liked the clear write-up. Some were surprised SQLite could handle a busy site. Others discussed technical points, like how SQLite allows many readers but only one writer at a time. There were debates about data types and backup strategies. Some worried about losing data if the server crashed, but others said this risk isn’t unique to SQLite. A few users found bugs after the switch, but the team fixed them quickly. Most agreed SQLite was a good choice for Lobste.rs, but bigger sites might still need more powerful databases.

Next, there’s an article about how people act when facing problems at work. The writer, a consultant, says people often don’t solve problems—they push them elsewhere, keep them alive, or create new ones. Pushing problems means fixing one area but making things worse in another. Preserving problems is when a group needs a problem to exist, because their job depends on it. Promoting new problems happens when solving one issue creates another. The advice is to make diagrams to show all problems and pick the most important ones to fix.

In the comments, many said they see these patterns at work. Some agreed that moving problems is common in big systems. Others noted that leaders may not want to fix root causes, or that preserving problems can sometimes be useful. Some shared stories of solving one problem only to create bigger ones later. Others said it’s wise to ignore small problems and focus on the big ones. Many agreed that understanding these behaviors helps teams get real results.
