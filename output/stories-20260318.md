## Wander – A tiny, decentralised tool to explore the small web

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47422759)

Wander is a small tool that helps people find and explore personal websites made by others. It works by letting you browse random pages from a group of websites in the Wander community.

To use Wander, you just download two files—index.html and wander.js—from a ZIP file. You put these files in a folder called /wander/ on your own website. Then, you open wander.js and follow simple steps to set it up. There are clear directions online if you get stuck.

Once your Wander console is working, you can share your link with the community. If other people add your link to their Wander consoles, your site becomes part of the network. This makes it possible for more people to find your personal website.

Wander is decentralized. This means there is no big company in control—just many small sites linking to each other. It is a simple way to discover new, creative websites instead of only seeing the most popular sites on the web. Wander wants to bring back the feeling of exploring the “small web,” where people make personal pages for fun.

In the Hacker News comments, some people love Wander because it reminds them of the early days of the internet. They say it feels like web “surfing” before big social media took over. Others point out that Wander is simple and easy to set up, so even beginners can join.

A few people worry that it might be hard to keep the network alive if not many people join. Some ask if there are ways to stop spam or bad websites from joining. Others talk about how Wander is different from big platforms like Reddit or Twitter because it is just people sharing their own sites.

## Rob Pike’s Rules of Programming (1989)

- 原文链接: [Rob Pike’s Rules of Programming (1989)](https://www.cs.unc.edu/~stotts/COMP590-059-f24/robsrules.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47423647)

Rob Pike’s article shares his five simple rules for good programming. The rules help programmers write better, faster, and easier-to-fix code.

First, Pike says you can’t guess where your program is slow—bottlenecks often appear in places you don’t expect. So, don’t try to make things faster until you know what’s actually slow. The second rule is to always measure your code’s speed before trying to make it faster. Only change things if one part is much slower than the rest.

The third rule warns about using “fancy” or complex algorithms. These only help if your data, called “n,” is very big. Most of the time, data is small, and simple code is better. Even with big data, you should measure first before using complex solutions.

The fourth rule says fancy algorithms are hard to write and often have more bugs. Simple code and data structures are easier to write and fix. The fifth rule is about data: if you pick the right way to organize your data, the rest of your code will be much easier to write. Good data structures matter more than tricky algorithms.

Pike’s first two rules are like Tony Hoare’s famous saying: “Premature optimization is the root of all evil.” Ken Thompson jokes that rules three and four mean “when in doubt, use brute force.” Keeping things simple is often called “KISS”—“keep it simple, stupid.” Rule five comes from Fred Brooks’ book, which also says to focus on smart data, not smart code.

In the comments, many people agree that simple code is best and that you should measure before making things faster. Some share stories where they wasted time speeding up the wrong part of a program. Others say that simple data structures saved them hours of work.

However, a few commenters point out that sometimes, in special programs like games or databases, you do need complex algorithms. Some people note that measuring speed can be hard, especially early in a project. Others warn that “brute force” can be slow if you ignore the problem size.

A few users remind everyone that these rules are guides, not strict laws, and every project is different. Some mention that simple code is easier for teams to work on and fix bugs. Others say that focusing on good data structures helped them find fast solutions by accident.

Several commenters like the idea of “smart objects, stupid code,” meaning you should put effort into modeling data well. A few users mention that these rules are old but still true today. Finally, some people say that following these rules made them better programmers over time.

## Nvidia NemoClaw

- 原文链接: [Nvidia NemoClaw](https://github.com/NVIDIA/NemoClaw)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47427027)

Nvidia NemoClaw is an open source tool to help people install and run OpenClaw AI assistants in a safe way. It uses something called OpenShell to create a sandbox—this means the AI runs in its own locked-down space, with strong rules to protect your files and network.

The main idea is to make it easy to launch always-on AI agents that cannot reach data or the internet they’re not supposed to. NemoClaw checks your hardware (you need at least 8GB RAM, but 16GB is better) and your software (Linux, Node.js, npm, Docker, and OpenShell are needed). To install, you just run a simple command in your terminal, and NemoClaw will guide you through setting up a sandbox, connecting to Nvidia’s cloud for AI models, and adding security policies.

Once setup is done, you can connect to your AI agent using a chat interface (TUI) or by sending commands in the terminal (CLI). The agent’s answers come from an Nvidia cloud model, and all requests go through OpenShell, so they’re checked and controlled. The sandbox only lets the AI access certain folders and blocks any network calls that are not allowed. If the AI tries to go somewhere it shouldn’t, it gets blocked, and you get a choice to allow or deny the request.

NemoClaw gives you commands to check the agent’s status, see logs, and start or stop services. The project is still in “alpha,” so things may break or change, and not all features are finished yet. The team asks for feedback and says not to use it in production right now.

In the Hacker News comments, some users like the idea of strong sandboxing for AI agents, saying security is very important if you want agents running all the time. Others are happy to see Nvidia open sourcing their tools, as this could help more people use and trust the technology. There are questions about how easy it is to use on different Linux setups and if it will work with other AI models besides Nvidia’s own.

A few users worry about the high hardware needs, pointing out not everyone has 16GB RAM or a fast computer. Some people want to know if they can use their own GPU instead of the Nvidia cloud, or if everything must go through Nvidia’s servers. There’s also talk about the “alpha” warning—some wish the project was more stable before release, but others like being able to test it early.

One commenter mentioned they found some bugs during install but were able to work around them. Another said the strong network and filesystem controls are a good step, but they hope it’s easy to update these policies. A few users wonder if sandboxing will block useful things by accident, and ask for good documentation and troubleshooting guides.

Overall, most comments are hopeful but cautious. People want to see more real-world tests and examples before trusting NemoClaw for important work. Many are interested in following the project as it grows.

## Book: The Emerging Science of Machine Learning Benchmarks

- 原文链接: [Book: The Emerging Science of Machine Learning Benchmarks](https://mlbenchmarks.org/00-preface.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47380714)

This article is about a new book that looks deeply at how and why machine learning benchmarks work. It explains that benchmarks—splitting data into training and test sets, then ranking models—have always been central to machine learning, but they also have big problems.

The book says many experts criticize benchmarks. They argue that static test sets and narrow metrics make research less creative. Benchmarks can be “gamed,” meaning people find ways to get good scores without real progress. Over time, models may get better at the test set but not at real-world tasks. There are also ethical problems: benchmarks can keep biases in data and depend on low-paid workers to label data. Big tech companies also have more resources to win at benchmarks, giving them an advantage.

Still, benchmarks have driven progress. For example, the ImageNet competition helped push deep learning forward, and language model benchmarks are now important for both companies and even stock markets. The author says that while benchmarks have clear flaws, they have also worked well for progress, even if it wasn’t planned.

The book discusses technical ideas like the “holdout method” (splitting data for testing) and problems like “adaptivity,” where people use test sets too much, making results less reliable. Statistical problems, like Freedman’s paradox and Goodhart’s law, show how measuring progress can go wrong when people compete just for high scores. In practice, however, model rankings (which model is best) tend to be more reliable than the actual accuracy scores.

The book explains that while scores don’t always repeat on different datasets, the order of the best models often does. This means that benchmarks work better for ranking than for absolute measurement. The author also covers newer challenges, like evaluating large language models (LLMs). With LLMs, we can’t always know what data they saw, which makes fair benchmarking hard. Multi-task benchmarks (testing many tasks) have their own problems, such as rankings changing if you add or remove models.

Another big problem is “performativity”—when models are used by millions, they change the world and the data, so it gets hard to measure them fairly. As AI models get better, it’s even tough to find ways to test them. The book says we need new science to make sure benchmarking keeps working. It’s written for students, teachers, and researchers, and uses both theory and real-world examples to explain its points.

In the Hacker News comments, some people agree that benchmarks are both helpful and flawed. One user points out how benchmarks sometimes push everyone to focus on tiny gains, instead of big, new ideas. Another says that benchmarks can hide real-world problems if everyone just tries to get a better score. A few users mention that benchmarks are useful for beginners to learn, but real products need more than just good scores.

Some commenters highlight the ethical issues—like bias in datasets and low wages for data labelers. Others argue that benchmarks help keep research open and competitive, rather than secret and private. There’s also debate about whether new types of benchmarks, especially for LLMs, are really measuring what matters.

A few users think the book’s focus on social norms in the research community is important. They say how people use benchmarks may matter more than the benchmarks themselves. Some worry that “leaderboard chasing” is wasteful, while others appreciate that benchmarks let anyone compete, even with small resources. Several hope the book helps the field think more carefully about what progress really means.

Overall, the comments show that while benchmarks are far from perfect, many in the community see them as necessary—at least for now.

## 2025 Turing award given for quantum information science

- 原文链接: [2025 Turing award given for quantum information science](https://awards.acm.org/about/2025-turing)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47423694)

The 2025 Turing Award was given to Charles H. Bennett and Gilles Brassard for their work in quantum information science. They are known for creating the foundation of quantum cryptography and secure communication.

Bennett and Brassard worked together for over 40 years. In 1984, they made the first practical quantum cryptography protocol, called BB84. This method lets two people share a secret key that is safe even if someone has a very powerful computer. Their work means the safety comes from the laws of physics, not from hard math problems. If someone tries to listen in, the act of measuring changes the system, and both people can see that someone is spying. This is different from normal cryptography, which could be broken by new quantum computers.

Their ideas go beyond just keeping secrets. They also helped invent quantum teleportation, which means sending information about a quantum state from one place to another using something called entanglement and classical communication. This idea is now used in real experiments and is a big part of making quantum networks. Their work on entanglement distillation showed how to make weak entanglement stronger, which helps with building a global quantum internet.

Their research joined physics and computer science, opening new fields in both. Bennett and Brassard's discoveries led to many new studies in cryptography, algorithms, and quantum computing. Thanks to them, there are now working quantum communication networks in the world, using fiber and satellites.

In the Hacker News comments, many people say this award is well deserved, as Bennett and Brassard’s work changed how we think about information and security. Some users remember learning about BB84 in school and are surprised it took so long for them to win the Turing Award. Others point out that quantum cryptography is still hard to use in real life, but the science is important and will help keep information safe in the future. A few comments talk about the difference between quantum and classical cryptography, and how current systems may become unsafe as quantum computers get better. Some users wonder if quantum communication will ever be easy and cheap enough for everyone to use. Others celebrate how this prize shows that computer science and physics are now more connected than ever. There are also comments highlighting the teamwork between an American and a Canadian, showing that science often crosses borders. A few worry about hype in quantum computing, but most agree that Bennett and Brassard’s work is a real and lasting achievement.
