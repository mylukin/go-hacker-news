First, let’s talk about a major Linux security issue called “Dirty Frag.” This new bug lets attackers get root access on almost any Linux system. It is made of two separate bugs, one in the ESP network code and another in the rxrpc kernel module. Attackers can use these together to change important files, like replacing the “su” program or editing the password file, so they can become root. The article explains the technical steps in detail and even shares full C code to show how the exploit works. The code patches files in memory, bypasses normal security checks, and opens a root shell. There are no official fixes or CVE numbers yet, because the bug was made public before patches were ready.

The author says the secret got out, so they released the details early, breaking the normal process for reporting bugs. All major Linux distributions are affected. For now, the only quick workaround is to disable the ESP and rxrpc modules, but some users warn this could break VPNs or other programs. In the comments, developers are worried about how easy it is to get root using this bug. Many are surprised it works on all big Linux versions, and some say it’s hard for normal people to check if their systems are safe. There is also debate about how the news was released—some think early disclosure helps users, while others say it puts everyone at risk before a fix is ready. Finally, people agree that Linux needs faster patches and better security review, while some joke about switching to other operating systems.

Next, let’s look at the MOOP Map at Burning Man, which tracks how much trash people leave behind after the event. Every year, 150 volunteers walk the whole area and record all the “Matter Out of Place,” like screws or cigarette butts, to make a color-coded map. Yellow means a moderate mess, and red means the worst spots. The U.S. government checks the land after the event, and if there’s too much trash, Burning Man could lose its permit. Most years, the festival passes, but sometimes it comes close to failing.

In 2025, the biggest problem was lag bolts, which are small and hard to find in the dust. The MOOP Map helps camps see their impact, with reports sent to those who leave too much trash. Repeat offenders can lose their favorite camping spots. The map also leads to some public shaming online. Over 20 years, the map has helped the community get better at leaving less trash, following the “Leave No Trace” rule.

In the comments, people admire the careful and organized cleanup process. Some wish other big events were this strict. There is debate over public shaming—some think it’s helpful, others think it’s unfair. Many users say the MOOP Map should be a model for other festivals, and some share their own stories of working hard to pick up even tiny pieces of trash at Burning Man.

Let’s move on to software agents and language models. An article this week says that agents using language models need clear control flow, not just longer or more complex prompts. Relying only on prompts makes behavior hard to predict, especially for complex tasks. The author compares this to writing code in a language where commands are just suggestions, saying good software is built from smaller, predictable modules.

Prompt chains can help with simple jobs, but are not enough for reliable, checkable tasks. The important logic should be in real code, not just in natural language instructions. The article lists three ways to handle errors: check results each time, do a full check at the end, or just hope for the best. The first two are safer.

In the comments, many agree that prompt-only solutions break down as tasks get complex. Some share stories about real failures when using just prompts. Others note that adding clear control flow is how software has always managed complexity. There is interest in tools that mix code and prompts, but most agree agents should use code for control and treat language models as helpers, not the main brain.

Now, let’s look at AlphaEvolve, an AI coding agent from Google DeepMind that uses Gemini. AlphaEvolve has made a big impact in science, business, and technology after one year of work. It improved DNA analysis by cutting errors 30%, made energy grids run from 14% to 88% success, and improved weather predictions by 5%. It also helped with hard math problems, even working with famous mathematician Terence Tao. For quantum computers, AlphaEvolve made circuits with 10 times fewer errors, and it improved Google’s hardware and databases, saving time and money.

Commercial users like Klarna and Schrödinger use AlphaEvolve to speed up AI training, run bigger simulations, and improve delivery or marketing. In the comments, some people are excited about the progress, while others worry it could take jobs from engineers. There are questions about transparency and how Google checks for errors or bias, since big companies have an advantage using this tool. Some hope for faster science and better tools for everyone, but also want more open access and clear rules. A few remind us that human insight is still needed to guide and check AI’s work.

Another new AI tool comes from Anthropic—Natural Language Autoencoders, or NLAs. NLAs help explain what big AI models like Claude are “thinking” in plain English. Normally, AI turns words into numbers, but NLAs train the AI to explain its own activations using text. Two copies of Claude work together: one turns activations into text, and the other tries to rebuild the original activation from that text. If the reconstructor can do a good job, the explanation is likely accurate.

NLAs have helped researchers see why Claude does certain things, like planning rhymes in a poem or acting strange in tests. They also help with AI audits, letting people find hidden goals or sneaky behaviors. But NLAs can sometimes make things up, and they need a lot of computer power. In the comments, some people are excited about making AI more understandable, while others warn that AI could still invent false explanations. There is debate about cost and if NLAs will work for even bigger models. Most agree NLAs are a step forward, but need careful testing and clear limits.

Let’s turn to ds4.c, a small program that helps you run DeepSeek V4 Flash, a large AI model, on your Mac using Apple’s Metal system. DeepSeek V4 Flash is very fast, with up to 1 million tokens in its context window, and is strong in both English and Italian. The tool saves memory by using a smart cache that can be stored on disk, so you can continue chats after a restart. It supports 2-bit quantization for RAM savings, and comes with scripts to download and run the model. The focus is on making one model work really well locally, but it needs a high-end Mac with a lot of RAM.

In the comments, users like the project’s focus on a single model and the clever use of disk for memory. Some wish for Linux or CUDA support, while others are happy Apple Silicon is getting more AI software. People also discuss the quality of DeepSeek’s output, and many thank the author for being honest about using AI help in the code. Some question if it’s wise to invest in one model as AI changes fast, but others say focus brings stability.

Now for something about graphics. An article shows how to add colored shadow penumbra to Unreal Engine 5. This makes the soft edge of shadows show more color, which can look better in games or art. The steps are simple: you edit a shader file, set the PenumbraSaturation value, and recompile. The effect works for dynamic lights and is easy to adjust, but all colored shadows use the same setting and it does not work for baked lighting.

In the comments, people are excited about the visual effect, saying it looks more realistic or artistic. Some warn that engine updates might overwrite the change, and a few wish for more control per light or scene. There are questions about performance, but the author says it is very low cost. Some share links to similar effects in other tools, and many thank the author for clear instructions.
