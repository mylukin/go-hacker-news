## Show HN: I replaced a $120k bowling center system with $1,600 in ESP32s

- 原文链接: [Show HN: I replaced a $120k bowling center system with $1,600 in ESP32s](item?id=48968606)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48968606)

A developer replaced a bowling center’s expensive $120,000 management system with a much cheaper setup using ESP32 microcontrollers for only $1,600. The old system controlled things like scoreboards, lanes, and lights, but it was outdated and hard to maintain.

The article explains how the new system works. Instead of one big computer, each bowling lane now runs on its own small ESP32 chip. These chips are cheap, easy to program, and connect to Wi-Fi. The developer created custom software to handle scores, display them on screens, and control the lights and sounds for each lane. The new system is easier to fix because each lane is separate—if one breaks, the others still work. The hardware uses simple touchscreens and regular TVs as displays. All the data, like player scores, goes to a central server that tracks everything. The setup also lets people control things from a phone or tablet. The code is open source, so others can use or change it. In the end, the bowling center saved a lot of money and now has a system that’s easy to update and repair.

People in the comments were impressed by the low cost and the engineering skill. Some said it was a great example of how modern tech can replace expensive old systems. Others pointed out that the project needed a lot of custom work, so it might not work for every bowling alley. A few worried about long-term support, saying that big companies offer support contracts that small setups can’t match. Some liked that the code is open, which means others can help improve it. Others wondered about safety and reliability, since a commercial system needs to work all the time. Some readers shared their own stories of replacing expensive tech with DIY projects. Many people said this kind of project is inspiring and shows what’s possible with cheap hardware and some programming skills.

## The Zen of Parallel Programming

- 原文链接: [The Zen of Parallel Programming](https://smolnero.com/posts/the-zen-of-parallel-programming)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48907390)

This article talks about parallel programming, where many processors work together to solve big problems. The writer compares how computers need good communication between processors to how people need good communication inside themselves and with others.

The writer explains that just adding more processors to a computer does not always make things faster. First, you must split the problem into smaller parts. Each processor must work on a part, and they must talk to each other, share the work, and not get stuck waiting for one busy processor. If they do not share well, the whole system slows down. The real challenge is not more power, but better coordination.

The article then connects this idea to people. A person can have intelligence, energy, feelings, and creativity, but if these parts do not work together, the person feels tired and confused. Sometimes, feelings from the past stay in our minds, using up our energy like an unfinished computer task. The writer brings up the Zen idea of "wholehearted activity"—doing something completely and letting it go, instead of holding onto old experiences.

Honest communication, the writer says, helps us synchronize our feelings, thoughts, and actions. If we hide things from ourselves or others, we become anxious and burnt out. Both parallel programming and Zen ask how many parts can work together as one without losing their own special qualities. Maybe, the writer suggests, our problem is not lack of power, but power that fights itself.

In the comments, some people like the comparison between computers and people. They say it helps them think about teamwork and personal growth. Others think the analogy is interesting but warn it should not be stretched too far, since computers and people are very different. Some mention that real parallel programming is even harder than the article says, because of unexpected bugs and slowdowns. A few readers share stories about struggling to balance their own "internal processors"—their thoughts, feelings, and actions.

One comment says the Zen idea of letting things "burn out" is helpful for mental health. Another person jokes that sometimes their brain feels like a system with too many unfinished tasks. Some like the focus on honest communication, both in code and in daily life. Others think the article is a bit philosophical, but still useful. Overall, readers seem to enjoy the mix of technical and personal ideas, and many say it made them reflect on how they work with others and themselves.

## HomeLab #1: MikroTik as a Home Router

- 原文链接: [HomeLab #1: MikroTik as a Home Router](https://justsomebody.dev/blog/mikrotik-home-router)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48970772)

This article explains how to set up a MikroTik router as your main home router, with special focus on getting it working with your internet provider and getting good WiFi. It starts by showing you how to figure out what kind of internet connection your provider uses—either IPoE or PPPoE—and what setup steps each needs on a MikroTik device. If your ISP uses IPoE, you just add a DHCP client to the WAN port. If it uses PPPoE, you might need to set up a VLAN and then create a PPPoE client with your username and password.

The article also explains the difference between having a public or private (CGNAT) IP address from your ISP. If you have a public IP, things like port forwarding and remote access work easily. If you have a private IP, those things are much harder or need extra services.

You get step-by-step commands for connecting the MikroTik to your fiber box (ONT), including how to clone your old router’s MAC address if your ISP checks for that. Setting up VLANs and PPPoE clients is explained with real MikroTik commands. Once the internet is working, the guide helps you set up good WiFi using MikroTik’s CAPsMAN system. This part includes how to power a wireless access point using PoE, and how to set up WiFi security, channels for 2.4GHz and 5GHz, and centralized management.

The article then talks about a common problem called bufferbloat, where internet speed is fine but latency (lag) gets bad when the network is busy. It explains how to use FQ-CoDel, a smarter queue system, to keep gaming and voice calls smooth even when someone is downloading or uploading a lot. You get commands for setting up FQ-CoDel and for turning off a default MikroTik feature called fasttrack, which can break this queue system.

In the comments, many people say MikroTik is a great value for home labs, offering pro features at a good price. Some mention the setup is not easy for beginners—there are many settings and the interface can be confusing. Others share tips about using other brands, like Ubiquiti or OpenWRT, and compare their experiences. Some warn that MikroTik’s documentation is not always clear, so you might need to search online or use forums. A few users say they like how powerful the firewall and routing options are, while others wish the WiFi hardware was better, especially for big homes or fast speeds. There are also comments about bufferbloat, with several people saying FQ-CoDel really works for gaming. Some mention that ISPs can make things tricky, like locking connections to a single MAC address or using CGNAT, which can limit what you can do. Lastly, a few people share that after using MikroTik, they learned a lot about networking, which helped them in their jobs or other tech projects.

## Minecraft: Java Edition now uses SDL3

- 原文链接: [Minecraft: Java Edition now uses SDL3](https://www.minecraft.net/en-us/article/minecraft-26-3-snapshot-4)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48967256)

Minecraft: Java Edition just changed its windowing and input system from GLFW to SDL3 in the latest snapshot. This means the way the game handles windows, keyboard, and mouse is now different and should help Minecraft work better on more systems.

The update brings technical changes: mouse input now always uses relative mode, and key bindings use physical keys instead of layout-specific codes. The game now defaults to borderless fullscreen and makes switching fullscreen modes easier. Exclusive fullscreen mode is no longer supported on macOS, and Linux now prefers Wayland if available. The minimum window size is now 320x240. The debug overlay can have its own GUI scale, and shows new info like player speed and display refresh rate.

There are new features too: spectator mode players can now use portals to teleport. Armadillos won’t try to roll up in water anymore. The creative inventory has improved sorting for minerals and blocks to make it easier to find items. Technical users get many changes for data packs, loot tables, advancements, and world generation, such as new ways to define fuel, mob spawns, and how blocks and items work. Many bugs are fixed, including problems with key bindings, spectator portals, and display issues.

Some users in the comments are happy about SDL3 because it should give better support for Linux, Wayland, and input devices. People using non-US keyboard layouts are glad that key bindings are now based on physical keys, as old bugs with weird key mapping are fixed. Some worry about bugs in exclusive fullscreen, especially on Windows and Wayland where crashes can happen. Mac users are disappointed that exclusive fullscreen is gone for them.

Modders and data pack creators have mixed feelings. Some love the new flexible ways to reference items and blocks in loot tables and advancements. Others are frustrated because old formats and tags were removed, so they have to update their mods again. A few think the technical changes will make maintaining mods harder at first, but better in the long run.

Linux users in the thread are excited to see native Wayland support, hoping for better performance and fewer input bugs. Some are worried that SDL3 might bring new bugs, but are hopeful that this move will make Minecraft more stable across platforms. Overall, the community sees this as a good technical step, but expect some bumps as everyone adjusts to the new system.

## Blender 5.2 LTS

- 原文链接: [Blender 5.2 LTS](https://www.blender.org/download/releases/5-2/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48911021)

Blender 5.2 LTS is a big new release of the free 3D software, now with long-term support until July 2028. The update brings many new tools, changes, and fixes for making 3D art, animation, and video.

The main change is to Geometry Nodes. Now, you can make animations that move with sound, add details with new bevel controls, and use new nodes for cloth and hair physics. There’s a new way to control which objects affect each other, and bundles help move data around your scene. Lists, attributes, and strings are easier to use in nodes, and you can even work with “empty” objects for special effects. Curves, node tools, and performance got better, too.

Cycles and Eevee, Blender’s render engines, have new features. Texture Cache saves memory by loading smaller images, and a Thin Wall mode helps you render things like paper or glass more realistically. Eevee also fixes some old problems and adds new ways to handle lighting.

The Asset Library is bigger, but Blender’s download is not. You get more materials and effects online, and you can add your own or use libraries from others. The compositor now supports more node types, is faster, and lets you animate directly with gizmos.

Grease Pencil is improved—fills are smarter and faster, and new brushes are available. Animation gets new playback modes, better graph editor controls, and more options for bones and constraints. LoopTools operators are now built in for modeling, and the video sequencer is stronger, with GPU support for effects and better text styling.

The user interface has many small changes, like flipping render resolution with one click, better sidebars, easier navigation, and more icon options. The Outliner and editors are easier to use, and VR support is better for scouting scenes. Import and export for all major formats is improved, with new support for color spaces, compression, and point clouds.

Blender Extensions now has over 1100 add-ons and themes. The Python API changed—scripts that use Geometry Nodes need updates, and there are many new ways to handle data and UI in scripts. Hundreds of bugs are fixed, too.

People in the comments say the update is huge and exciting, especially for artists and studios needing stable tools. Many like the node-based physics and the improved Asset Library. Some are happy about long-term support, saying it will help teams and big projects. Others point out the new Python API will break some old scripts, so they warn to check your add-ons.

There are also comments that praise the faster compositor and better memory use in Cycles. Some users are glad LoopTools are native now, as they used to install these by hand. A few people wish there were more core modeling features, but most are happy with the step-by-step progress.

A few users are a bit lost with all the new features and want better documentation or more tutorials, but they still like how Blender keeps growing. People using VR or video editing are glad for those updates, too. In general, users say Blender 5.2 LTS is a strong release and keeps Blender a top choice for 3D work, both for hobby and pro.

## What I learned selling 2,500 MIDI recorders: Hardware is not so hard

- 原文链接: [What I learned selling 2,500 MIDI recorders: Hardware is not so hard](https://chipweinberger.com/articles/20260719-hardware-is-not-so-hard)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48966713)

This article talks about how the author made and sold 2,500 Jamcorder MIDI recorders. The main idea is that building hardware was much easier than expected, especially compared to writing the software.

The Jamcorder is a simple device that records everything played on a piano without needing any help from the user. The author, who worked in software before, thought hardware would be the hardest part—because people always say “hardware is hard.” They expected problems with electronics, manufacturing, or not being able to get parts. But those problems never really happened.

To start, the author made the first 500 units by hand, and it went smoothly. There were no big surprises or major changes needed. Even things like tariffs almost caused trouble but did not. The hardest part was actually the software for Jamcorder. It took over three years and 200,000 lines of code for the firmware, app, and tools.

The author points out that Jamcorder is a simple device by design. It uses only 25 unique parts. Most of these are easy to buy, and the design avoids things like USB-C ports or extra features. The assembly uses just one screw, and the plastic case is simple. The author also says that 2,500 units is not a huge amount, but even so, hardware was not the biggest challenge.

The author gives advice for people who want to build hardware: keep the parts list simple, avoid complex assembly, use suppliers in China, aim for good profit margins, avoid hiring too many people, have a plan to stop counterfeits, do quality checks yourself, ask for samples, write clear guides, and use small packaging.

In the comments, many people agree that hardware can be simple if you keep the design simple and control the process. Some readers say small projects are much easier than large ones. Others warn that things can get hard if you try to scale up, add more features, or enter crowded markets like smartwatches or cars. A few people point out that making simple hardware is possible today because there are more tools, suppliers, and information online. Some ask about customer support, returns, or problems after selling, which the article does not cover much. Others share stories of their own hardware projects, both good and bad. Some are surprised that nothing went wrong, while others say luck and careful planning both matter. A few readers think software is always the hardest part, while others believe hardware only seems easy until something goes wrong at scale. Overall, most people like the practical tips and feel encouraged to try building hardware themselves.
