First, let’s talk about the growing competition between China and the US in AI. A recent article points out that China is winning in AI by making its AI models open to everyone, while many American companies keep their models private. The author explains that the true value in AI is not just the models, but the services and systems built around them. Since engineers can easily swap out AI models using APIs, trying to lock in customers does not work very well. The US government also restricts GPU exports and data sharing, making it harder for Chinese companies to provide global AI services like OpenAI or Anthropic. But China’s open approach means anyone can use, change, and host their AI models, helping more people to build new things.

Open AI models from China are improving fast and are already cheaper than US models. Many startups are now using Chinese models, and some believe these could soon be the best in the world. The author finds it ironic that China, known for tight control, is now more open with AI than the US. US companies, once leaders in open technology, are now focusing on profit and keeping tech private. The warning is clear: if the US does not change, it could lose its lead in AI and harm its economy.

In the comments, some readers support open models, saying they help more people build new ideas. Others worry about trusting Chinese models, especially because of government control and possible censorship. Some people defend US companies, saying they need to protect their business. There are also concerns about security and safety with open models. A few users hope that competition between open and closed models will make AI better for everyone, but others warn that if the US falls behind, it could lose power in tech and the world.

Another article also discusses China’s rise in AI, focusing on the business side. It explains that running AI models costs real money—known as COGS, or cost of goods sold—each time you use them. Even if a model is open, using it needs expensive computer power. Some models use more tokens to get the same answer, making them more expensive to run. The author says intelligence is becoming a commodity, and companies that give the same results for less money will win. China is catching up fast, helped by “distillation,” where they use results from top US models to train their own, saving time and money. The author suggests the US should allow similar shortcuts, or risk falling behind.

In the comments, people agree that the real issue is cost, not just openness. Some see China’s open models as a way to spread innovation, while others worry about security if the US ends up using Chinese models for important tasks. There is debate about whether intelligence can really become a simple commodity, and if open models really help small companies or just big players. Some suggest the US should loosen rules for cybersecurity, while others warn about risks if dangerous tools become too open. Overall, the comments show mixed views and a sense that the situation is changing quickly.

Now, let’s move to Kimi Work, a new desktop AI agent for knowledge workers. Kimi connects deeply with your local files and automates office tasks or data analysis, working all day and night to boost productivity. It has a scheduler for running jobs at set times—like making reports in the morning or running scripts at night. Kimi’s WebBridge lets it act like a person online, browsing websites, clicking, and collecting data with a simple goal. For big tasks, Kimi uses several smart agents together to break work into smaller parts. It can turn research into PowerPoint slides or Excel tables, and it comes with built-in data for stocks in China, Hong Kong, and the US—making it easy to get earnings reports or market analysis in plain language.

Kimi is different from web chat bots because it works directly with your files, automates web tasks, runs code, and can schedule jobs. It always asks for your permission before changing files, so you stay in control. Kimi can check websites for new info, summarize news, and collect stock data into spreadsheets, running jobs even overnight if you want.

In the comments, people are excited about Kimi’s automation and web browsing skills. They like its connection to local files and that it runs jobs without the cloud. Some users are interested in its finance features, seeing it as a time-saver for analysts and traders. The use of many agents together is seen as clever, but some worry about privacy and ask how safe it really is. Others question if Kimi will work well on all operating systems or if it will slow down their computers. Some compare it to other automation tools and ask how it stands out. There are requests for open-source options or better controls. Overall, most agree Kimi Work is ambitious and could change busy office work, but they want to see more real-world tests.

Next, let’s look at Jelly UI—a new library that brings soft, bouncy, jelly-like effects to HTML form controls using Web Components. With over 40 elements like buttons, sliders, switches, and cards, each control moves or wobbles when you use it. Jelly UI is built for accessibility, using native HTML with ARIA roles, keyboard support, and screen reader-friendly labels. It supports dark mode, right-to-left languages, and color choices that meet accessibility standards. The API lets you customize themes, accents, and sizes, and the documentation is full of examples.

In the comments, many are excited by the fun and tactile UI, saying it’s refreshing compared to the usual flat designs. Others like that it’s dependency-free and focuses on accessibility. Some joke about wanting to “squish” all their buttons now. There are worries about performance if too many jelly elements are used, and some ask for a way to reduce the animation. Questions about browser compatibility and using it with frameworks like React or Vue also come up. Some mention that custom elements can sometimes break form submissions, but others reply that Jelly UI keeps normal form behavior. Overall, it’s seen as a creative and well-documented project.

Switching to AI in mathematics, there’s an article about how AI is now better than humans at finding counterexamples in math. Recently, AI disproved the famous Erdős’ Unit Distance conjecture, and another AI tool turned the proof into Lean code—a way to check math proofs. This used to take humans years, but now AI can do it in weeks, writing over a million lines of code. At a workshop, AI found mistakes in papers and solved a 60-year-old question by Grothendieck. AI also found a counterexample to the Jacobian Conjecture, open for 100 years. The author says AI is now a must for serious math work, and students at big schools pay for AI access or get it free.

Comments are full of strong feelings. Some are amazed that AI can solve problems humans struggled with for years, predicting faster progress in math and science. Others worry that people may stop understanding deep math, relying too much on AI. Some say AI proofs are too big to be fully trusted. There’s sadness for mathematicians who feel left behind, but hope that AI will help people learn more. Some joke that soon, “AI will solve math” while humans just watch. Most agree this is a big change for anyone who loves hard problems.

Now, let’s discuss a serious cybersecurity story. A hacker broke into Romania’s land registry agency and deleted the whole land database after failing to get ransom money. This stopped all real-estate deals in Romania, as notaries couldn’t work and people couldn’t get proof of land ownership. The hacker used real login details, learned the system, and erased both main data and backups. Some stolen files, like passwords and internal documents, were put up for sale online. Officials restored the website and found an offline backup, so not all data was lost, but the damage was big. The hacker, known as “ByteToBreach,” is linked to attacks on other governments. Romania is not alone—other countries have had their land registries hacked in recent years, causing chaos.

Hacker News users were shocked that such important data could be wiped out by one person. Many said it’s too risky to rely only on digital systems without strong, offline backups. Some suggested keeping paper records. There was talk about better password security and the danger of insider threats. Many noted that hackers often delete backups first, so offline backups are vital. There was debate about paying hackers, and worries about restoring old records causing legal fights. Some hope this will push governments to take cybersecurity more seriously.

Now, let’s look at Nativ—a new open-source app that lets you run advanced AI models on your Mac with Apple Silicon, without using the cloud. Nativ is free, does not need an account, and keeps all your data on your device. You can choose from a library of open models from companies like Google and Cohere, and Nativ helps you pick the right one for your computer. The chat interface is simple and supports streaming answers, code highlighting, and image input. All processing is local—nothing goes online.

Nativ shows live stats on tokens per second, memory use, and speed. It is made for Apple Silicon chips and supports tasks like chat, image captions, video summaries, code autocomplete, and audio. There are no subscriptions or hidden fees—Nativ is free under the MIT license. You can also link it to other coding agents, and it acts as a local server.

Comments are very positive. Many like that Nativ is open source and free, and that it really works locally, so it is more private. Some are happy to see a tool made just for Apple Silicon. There are questions about RAM and which Macs work best—Nativ works best on M1 and newer, with more RAM. Some worry about big model sizes and ease of setup. Some users would like to see Windows or Linux versions, but the team is focused on Apple Silicon for now. Overall, people are excited to try Nativ.

Next, an article describes building complex software using many AI agents together—a method called an agent swarm. The idea is to split big tasks into smaller jobs using planner agents, with smart (and expensive) models for planning and cheaper models for work. This tree-shaped system lets the swarm handle big projects, like making a database from scratch, without getting lost. They ran into problems, like normal tools not keeping up, so they built a new version control system. They added rules to handle merge conflicts, big files, and agent communication. Agents keep notes for each other, like a “Field Guide,” and review agents check for errors.

Tests show the swarm builds software better and cheaper, especially by using smart models only for planning. The system produces smaller, cleaner codebases. The article says the hard part now is writing clear specs, since the swarm can do the rest.

Comments are mixed. Some are excited and think agent swarms could change software development. Others wonder if it works for all types of projects, or only those that can be split up well. There are worries about errors if agents misunderstand specs or each other, and about the cost of using top AI models. Some want more real-world examples. There’s also interest in the new version control system and debate about splitting roles between planning and doing. Many wonder what jobs humans will have if swarms can build software from specs, but most agree humans will still need to review the results.

Now, we turn to a study about AI writing in research papers on arXiv. The authors tested over 12,000 papers and found that about one third of recent submissions look machine-written. They used a special detector and checked papers from 10 fields since 2023. In computer science, about 65% of new papers look machine-written, while in math it’s much lower. The study warns that the real share might be higher, as the detector might miss some cases.

A flagged paper does not prove it was written by a bot, only that the style looks machine-like. Some readers are surprised by the high numbers, while others point out that academic writing has always sounded robotic. Some question if AI is used for the whole paper, or just for grammar help. There are worries that more AI writing could make research less clear. Others like the open approach of the study and suggest new ways to measure AI writing. Overall, there are mixed feelings about what this means for science.
