## ChatGPT serves ads. Here's the full attribution loop

- 原文链接: [ChatGPT serves ads. Here's the full attribution loop](https://www.buchodi.com/how-chatgpt-serves-ads-heres-the-full-attribution-loop/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47942437)

This article explains how ChatGPT now shows ads and tracks users when they click on them. When you chat with ChatGPT, the system can add ad objects into your conversation stream. These ads are structured data, including information about the advertiser, the ad message, and special tokens for tracking. 

For example, if you ask about traveling to Beijing, you might see an ad for Chinese food delivery. If you ask about NBA playoffs, you might see tickets for sale. The ad content matches the topic of your conversation. Each ad contains four encrypted tokens: one checks for fake clicks, two track your actions after you click, and one is for the server to match clicks with ads. These tokens use Fernet encryption, which hides their content but allows the system to know when the ad was created.

When you click an ad, ChatGPT opens the advertiser’s site inside its own browser window. This lets OpenAI see everything you do on the site, on top of the advertiser’s own tracking. The advertiser’s website also loads a script called OAIQ. This script reads a tracking token from the URL, saves it in your browser as a cookie for 30 days, and sends data back to OpenAI every time you look at a product or take an action.

If you want to block this tracking, you can filter the domains bzrcdn.openai.com and bzr.openai.com, and watch for cookies named __oppref and __oaiq_domain_probe.

People in the comments had mixed feelings. Some are surprised and worried about how much tracking happens when using ChatGPT. They say OpenAI is now like other big tech companies, focused on advertising and user data. Others say this is normal for free internet services—they need to make money, and ads are how it works. A few technical commenters are interested in the use of Fernet tokens and how the system links actions from ChatGPT to the advertiser’s site. Some users dislike ads inside their AI chats and wish for a paid, ad-free option. Others discuss privacy, asking if OpenAI shares too much data with advertisers or tracks more than needed. Some note that opening ads inside ChatGPT’s own browser makes it easier for OpenAI to watch what you do, which feels intrusive. There are also suggestions on how to block the tracking, like using ad blockers or disabling cookies. Finally, some people accept ads as a trade-off for a free service, but want better controls over tracking and privacy.

## Before GitHub

- 原文链接: [Before GitHub](https://lucumr.pocoo.org/2026/4/28/before-github/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47940921)

This article talks about what open source was like before GitHub and how things changed after GitHub became popular. The author explains how, in the past, people often ran their own servers and tools like Subversion and Trac to host their projects. Later, sites like SourceForge and Bitbucket were popular, but GitHub became the main place for open source. GitHub made it much easier to create and find projects, track issues, and connect with other developers. The site also became a huge library of software, keeping old code and discussions online even when projects were abandoned.

Before GitHub, projects were harder to find and trust depended on knowing people and their reputations. Adding a dependency was a big decision because you needed to know where it came from and who ran it. People often “vendored” code by copying it into their own projects to avoid losing access. Running your own server meant you had to be part system administrator, and if you stopped, your project might disappear from the web. With GitHub and npm, it became much easier to share small code packages, but also easier to lose track of what you depended on.

Now, the author is worried that GitHub is getting worse. Many people complain about product changes, bugs, leadership problems, and too much focus on AI features like Copilot. Some big projects are starting to leave GitHub for other sites like Codeberg. The author says this might be good, because it is not healthy for one company to control so much of open source. But moving back to many small sites has a cost: important history and discussions can be lost easily, just like in the old days.

The article ends by saying we need a real, independent archive for open source, one not controlled by a single company or person. This would protect the history and knowledge of open source for the future. The author wants open source to keep its memory and not depend on one website, but also not go back to the days where everything could disappear overnight.

In the comments, some people agree that GitHub made open source much more open and easy to join. They remember how hard it was to contribute to projects before, and how mailing lists and old tools were confusing for new people. Others worry about losing so much history if GitHub fails or deletes old projects. Some developers say that centralization makes things easier, but it also creates big risks, like when a company changes its rules or goes away. A few people think moving to smaller sites or self-hosted tools is good for freedom, but they admit it is a lot more work and not everyone can do it. One commenter points out that the Internet Archive is already doing some of this work, but it cannot save everything. Another person asks if we really need to keep every old project, or if it is okay for some things to disappear. Some hope for new tools that make it easy to copy or move projects, including all their issues and discussions, so no one company can control open source. Many agree that open source needs to learn from the past and find a better way to keep its history safe.

## Claude system prompt bug wastes user money and bricks managed agents

- 原文链接: [Claude system prompt bug wastes user money and bricks managed agents](https://github.com/anthropics/claude-code/issues/49363)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47942492)

A bug in Claude Code is causing its agents to refuse normal code edits because of a confusing system reminder about malware. The problem started after a past fix did not hold, and now in version 2.1.111 (and newer), every time a file is read, a reminder is injected saying agents must refuse to improve or change code—without clearly saying this only applies to malware.

The reminder text says agents should analyze malware but must not improve code, but the “must refuse” part is not just about malware. This causes many Claude subagents to think they have to refuse any code edit, even on safe, user-owned projects. In tests, about half the agents stop working, which breaks workflows that use multiple agents in parallel. The reminder is put into every file read, so it wastes tokens, fills up the context window, and costs users more money. It even happens when Claude reads files it created itself, or plain config files.

The author suggests three fixes: remove the reminder, make the warning clearer (only refuse to edit if the file is malware), or only show the reminder on the first file read. The bug makes Claude Code much less useful for coding tasks, especially for longer jobs or background agents, since users get no chance to correct refusals.

People in the comments shared their own problems. One person saw the bug even with Claude reading its own files, and another said background tasks could waste hours and money before refusing to do anything. Some said changing prompts or models could sometimes help, but others said these workarounds did not always fix the problem and made the agent more suspicious. Many users confirmed the bug still happens in newer versions, and they are frustrated because it gets in the way of work and costs extra tokens. Some people switched models or downgraded as a workaround, but this is not a real fix. A few users joked about Claude treating every file as “not malware” again and again, but still refusing to make changes. Most commenters ask for a fast fix, say it is hurting productivity, and that it makes Claude Code unreliable for code editing tasks. Some called out Anthropic for being slow to respond or for not testing enough before releasing updates.

## We decreased our LLM costs with Opus

- 原文链接: [We decreased our LLM costs with Opus](https://www.mendral.com/blog/frontier-model-lower-costs)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47942903)

This article is about how a team reduced their AI costs by using a smart system with two types of language models (LLMs): a cheap one for easy tasks and an expensive one for hard problems. They process lots of CI (continuous integration) logs to find software build failures.

First, most failures (about 80%) are repeats of known issues, so the team built a system where a cheap AI agent (Haiku) checks if a failure is already known. If it is, the process stops there. Only new or unclear problems go to the expensive AI model (Opus), which saves money. Haiku uses two ways to find matches: exact matching for known errors and semantic search to find similar errors with different words.

When the expensive model is needed, it plans the investigation but does not read all the logs itself. Instead, it tells the cheap model what parts to look at and what to report back. The system also does not send huge logs to the AI upfront. Instead, the agent can ask specific questions using SQL to get just the needed data from the database. This avoids bias and saves on token costs.

The expensive agent (Opus) makes a plan, like forming a hypothesis, asking for error messages, checking trends, and looking for changes in code or workflow. Each step is handled by a cheap sub-agent with a clear, small task. This step-by-step approach keeps context clean and focused, so the AI makes better decisions and costs do not spiral out of control.

Six months ago, this was not possible because the older models were not good enough at writing database queries or handling complex tasks. Now, the new models (Opus 4.6 and Haiku 4.5) are much better, so the team can use this layered approach to save money and solve problems faster.

From the comments, many readers liked the clear explanation and the smart use of cheap and expensive models together. Some were surprised that upgrading to a more powerful model actually lowered costs, since usually "frontier" models are more expensive. Others pointed out that this triager pattern (using a light-weight filter before the heavy AI) is common in other areas, like security or support, but is still not used enough in AI workflows.

A few people asked about the challenges of tuning the system, especially deciding when to use sub-agents or handle things in the main agent to avoid overhead. Some wondered about the risks of missing real problems if the cheap agent makes a mistake, and how the team checks for false positives or missed issues. There was also interest in the SQL approach, with readers noting that letting the agent pull context instead of pushing it avoids bias and saves on costs.

Other commenters discussed generalizing this pattern to other high-volume data tasks, like analyzing financial or IoT data. Some warned that the approach only works well if the cheap agent is accurate enough, and that too many layers can make systems hard to debug. Overall, people agreed this is a clever, practical way to use LLMs efficiently in real-world systems.

Overall, most agree this is a big step for AI in the cloud, but there are risks and open questions.

## Carrot Disclosure: Forgejo

- 原文链接: [Carrot Disclosure: Forgejo](https://dustri.org/b/carrot-disclosure-forgejo.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47941590)

This article talks about serious security problems found in Forgejo, a software platform now used by Fedora for hosting code. The author looked into Forgejo’s code and found many vulnerabilities, including SSRF (Server-Side Request Forgery), missing security headers, weak authentication, cryptography issues, and easy ways to attack or leak information.

The author says it only took one evening to find these problems, and they could even chain them together to get Remote Code Execution (RCE), steal secrets, or take over accounts. The RCE only works if open registration is allowed and a certain setting is changed from the default, but this is true for some real Forgejo setups.

Instead of reporting the bugs directly, the author uses something called “carrot disclosure.” This means they show proof that the software can be hacked, but do not give out all the details. The idea is to push the Forgejo team to do a full security review themselves, or risk users leaving the platform. The post includes proof that the exploit works, like creating a backdoor admin and running commands on the server.

The author thinks fixing just these bugs is not enough, because more problems are likely. They also mention trying to send fixes in the past, but it did not go smoothly. The main point is that Forgejo needs a bigger, more careful security check.

In the comments, some people are worried about Fedora’s move to Forgejo and question if it was tested enough. Others defend Forgejo, saying security problems happen in all projects and need time to fix. A few users agree with the “carrot disclosure” idea, thinking it’s better than hiding or dumping all the details.

Some commenters argue that showing only a redacted, working exploit helps push for change without making things easy for attackers. Others think it might scare users or harm the project’s reputation. There are also people who believe the real problem is that open source projects often lack enough people to review code and keep it safe.

A few think the bugs found show deeper issues in Forgejo’s design, not just small mistakes. Some suggest the Fedora team should help fix the problems, since they now depend on Forgejo. Many agree that a public security audit is needed soon. Finally, one commenter says this is a good reminder that all software, especially new or fast-growing projects, need regular and serious security checks.

## I won a championship that doesn't exist

- 原文链接: [I won a championship that doesn't exist](https://ron.stoner.com/How_I_Won_a_Championship_That_Doesnt_Exist/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47940389)

This article is about how someone tricked AI chatbots into saying he won a world championship for the card game 6 Nimmt!, even though no such championship exists. The writer did this as an experiment to show how easy it is to fool AI systems that search the web for answers.

He made a fake press release on a new website he bought for $12, saying he won the championship. Then, he edited the Wikipedia page for 6 Nimmt! to mention his fake win and used his own website as the source. Now, both Wikipedia and the website said the same thing, but it was all fake. AI chatbots that search the web found these sources and confidently told users he was the world champion. The experiment only took him about twenty minutes.

The writer explains that this kind of trick is a big problem. AI models and tools often trust what they find online, especially if it comes from Wikipedia or similar sources. If someone puts fake information in the right place, the AI will likely believe it and pass it on. He points out that once fake facts are in Wikipedia long enough, future AI models trained on that data may keep those false facts for years, even if the Wikipedia edit gets removed later.

He warns that this attack could be used to do much more harm, like changing important or dangerous information. He also gives some advice: don’t trust single sources, watch out for citations that only point to new websites, and LLM (AI chatbot) makers should show where their answers come from and make it easy to see if the sources are really independent.

In the comments, people had different opinions. Some were impressed by how easy the attack was and agreed we should be careful trusting AI answers. Others pointed out that this is not a new problem—search engines and Wikipedia have always had trouble with false information. A few thought Wikipedia needs better ways to spot suspicious edits, especially when new websites are used as sources. Some users worried this could be used for more serious attacks, like spreading fake news or scams. Others said AI tools should be more transparent about their sources so users can check them. Some commenters joked about trying similar pranks, while others said this shows why humans still need to check facts, not just trust AI or Wikipedia. Overall, most agreed that as AI gets used more for search and decision-making, these trust problems will get worse unless we find better ways to spot and stop fake information.

## Intel Arc Pro B70 Review

- 原文链接: [Intel Arc Pro B70 Review](https://www.pugetsystems.com/labs/articles/intel-arc-pro-b70-review/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47885644)

The review talks about the Intel Arc Pro B70 GPU, a new graphics card from Intel made for professional and AI work. Intel did not just add more memory to an old card—they made a new model with double the Xe cores and VRAM compared to the B50, now with 32 GB of memory. The B70 has faster clock speeds and much higher power use, and it costs about 2.7 times more than the B50. It is also more expensive than some similar NVIDIA and AMD cards, but it offers more VRAM for the price.

In real-world tests, the B70 did well in some programs but not all. In Lightroom Classic, it was a bit faster than the B50 but slower than NVIDIA and AMD cards. In Premiere, it was faster than the B50 and the NVIDIA 2000 Blackwell but slower than AMD’s R9700 and more powerful NVIDIA cards. For After Effects, the B70 was better than AMD’s R9700 but much slower than NVIDIA cards. In DaVinci Resolve, the B70 was good but not the fastest. For Blender, Intel’s B70 beat the AMD R9700 but was still slower than NVIDIA’s 2000 Blackwell. In Unreal Engine and Unigine (rendering tests), it was in the middle—faster than the B50 but not as fast as the top AMD and NVIDIA cards.

The B70 did best in AI tasks using MLPerf, being the fastest of all tested, thanks to its big 32 GB VRAM. For engineering software like Revit, Inventor, and SOLIDWORKS, the B70 was decent but was not always the top choice. Sometimes, NVIDIA cards were better.

Looking at the Hacker News comments, many people like that Intel is trying to compete in the GPU market, saying that more choices are good for everyone. Some users notice that Intel’s drivers have improved but still need more work, especially for pro and creative apps. Others say the B70’s high VRAM is great for AI models, but for regular graphics or video work, NVIDIA and AMD might be faster or a better deal. A few users worry about power use and heat, since the B70 uses much more power than the B50. Some ask if Intel will make a cheaper version with less VRAM for people who do not need so much memory. Many are hopeful that Intel will keep getting better and might soon catch up to NVIDIA and AMD for both games and work software. A few are waiting to see if Intel releases a consumer version of this GPU, hoping for good prices. Some say that for now, if you do a lot of AI work and need lots of VRAM, the B70 is a smart pick, but for most other jobs, NVIDIA or AMD may still be better. Overall, the comments show people are happy to see Intel making progress, even if there is still room to grow.
