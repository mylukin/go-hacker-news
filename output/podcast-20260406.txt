First, let’s look at Ghost Pepper, a new speech-to-text app for macOS. This app works fully offline—no internet needed, and your voice data never leaves your computer. You just hold the Control key, talk, and when you release, your words are typed and pasted where you want. Ghost Pepper uses open-source models like WhisperKit for speech recognition. There are several models: some are small and fast for English, others are bigger and support more languages. After converting speech to text, a local language model helps clean up your text by removing “um” and other filler words, or fixing mistakes. You can control how much clean-up you want. The app sits in your menu bar and starts at login by default. It never saves your voice or text to disk, and debug logs only stay in memory. Setup is easy: download, drag to Applications, and give permissions. You can also build it yourself with Xcode. The app is open-source and free, and IT admins can pre-approve permissions for company setups.

On Hacker News, many users are happy to see a privacy-first speech tool that does not use the cloud. Some say it’s great for people worried about privacy or handling sensitive work. Others like its speed and that it works without internet, unlike Apple’s own dictation. Some wonder about support for accents or noisy places and hope for Windows or Linux versions and more languages. Many praise the easy install and open-source code, which builds trust. There are questions about transcription accuracy and future features like custom hotkeys. Some worry about the size of the models, but most agree the privacy is worth it. Many are excited to see more local AI tools and say open-source is catching up with big tech. Others just like that the app is free and not collecting data.

Next, we cover Freestyle, a tool for running and managing AI-generated code in special sandboxes, or safe spaces. These sandboxes are actually full Linux virtual machines (VMs) that start very fast and can be controlled with an API. With Freestyle, you can create VMs from templates, fork VMs to do many tasks at once, and review code changes using AI. For example, one agent can build an API, another can make the UI, and a third can write tests—all at the same time, in different VMs. The VMs can be paused to save money, then resumed later. Code is managed with Git, and you get features like webhooks, GitHub sync, and easy deployments. Unlike other sandboxes that use containers, Freestyle gives you full root access, so you can run almost anything. Security includes user isolation and full networking. The service is free to start, and you only pay when VMs are running.

On Hacker News, users like Freestyle’s speed and full-VM approach. They say it’s useful for running many agents and offers more power than CodeSandbox or GitHub Codespaces. Some ask about security, as root access can be risky, but the team says they have strong isolation. There is some worry about costs if you need many VMs, but pausing helps save money. People also discuss how easy it is to connect Freestyle to other services, and whether it’s good for teaching or research. Overall, the community thinks Freestyle is a strong tool for advanced users who want to build, test, and deploy AI code quickly and safely.

Now, let’s talk about peptides. Peptides are small chains of amino acids, important in biology and medicine. They are like short pieces of proteins and do important jobs in the body, such as sending signals or fighting germs. Scientists can make peptides in the lab for medicine or research, but making them is not easy—they can stick together, fold wrongly, or break down. Some peptides are helpful for treating diseases, but it’s hard to get them into the body, as they can be destroyed in the stomach or blood. Scientists are working on ways to make them last longer and work better. Some new peptide drugs are already helping people, for example, with diabetes and cancer, but developing safe and useful peptide drugs takes a lot of work.

In the comments, people say peptides are great tools for research and medicine, but they are hard to make and use. Tricks exist to help peptides survive in the body, but none are perfect. Some share lab stories, saying working with peptides can be very frustrating. Others are hopeful about new peptide medicines. One person worries about people using peptide supplements without knowing if they work. Another says peptides are a big topic with much to learn. Overall, everyone agrees peptides are important, but working with them is full of challenges.

Moving on, let’s discuss quantum-resistant cryptography. A cryptography engineer warns that we must switch to quantum-safe cryptography sooner than expected. New research from Google and Oratomic shows that quantum computers might break current encryption by 2029. The new findings lower the quantum power needed to crack 256-bit elliptic curves, which means attacks could come sooner and with fewer resources. Hardware is improving, quantum algorithms are getting better, and error correction is less of a problem.

The author says we cannot wait for perfect solutions and must start using post-quantum tools now, like ML-KEM and ML-DSA, even if they are not perfect. He warns against using hybrid approaches, as they add complexity. Symmetric encryption like AES is still safe with 128-bit keys, but big changes are needed for other systems. Not all systems will break at once, but some, like hardware security modules, are not ready and may be left behind. People using modern cryptographic identities and file encryption must act fast.

On Hacker News, many agree that the timeline is moving up and it’s wise to prepare. Some say that, even if the risk is small, the cost of being late is too high. Others are more skeptical, worrying about bugs in rushed new systems. Several remind us that deploying new cryptography takes years, so starting early is necessary. Some worry about the size and speed of post-quantum cryptography for Web certificates and embedded devices. There is debate about trusting experts, how much risk is acceptable, and how slow past migrations have been. Most agree it’s better to face some pain now than a crisis later, and the comments show a mix of urgency, caution, and real worry about making the switch.

On Hacker News, many like the idea since finding these listings is a pain. Some share stories about getting great deals, but warn that auctions can be risky if you don’t know the item’s condition. People ask how often listings are updated, if the site covers auctions outside the US, and about future features like price history or tips. Next is a story about German police revealing the identity of "UNKN," the leader behind Russian ransomware groups REvil and GandCrab. His real name is Daniil Maksimovich Shchukin, a 31-year-old Russian, who worked with another Russian to attack companies and demand ransom. Together, these groups caused over 35 million euros in damage in Germany between 2019 and 2021. GandCrab and REvil used “double extortion”—locking files and threatening to leak data. The US government found a crypto wallet linked to Shchukin with over $300,000 from ransoms. GandCrab first appeared in 2018, paid hackers to break into companies, and updated their malware often. After GandCrab, REvil started with a big show of money to prove they were serious. Experts think REvil was just GandCrab with a new name. "UNKNOWN" even gave interviews about rising from poverty to becoming a millionaire through hacking. These gangs ran like businesses, hiring help and targeting companies with cyber insurance, since insurance would pay the ransom. In one case, REvil hacked Kaseya, affecting 1,500 businesses. The FBI broke into REvil's servers and gave a free decryption key to victims, which helped destroy the gang. Police think Shchukin still lives in Russia. They confirmed his identity using photos from a birthday party.

In the comments, some joke that insurance makes ransom payments more likely. Others point out that insurance is not free—the costs get passed on. There’s sarcasm and debate about who really loses when ransom is paid. Some link to old videos that guessed Shchukin’s identity years ago. Users talk about how fast ransomware groups change and how hard it is to catch leaders in Russia. There’s debate about whether public naming stops criminals or just makes them more careful. Many are surprised at how organized these gangs are, running like real companies, and are worried about the effect on regular people and businesses.

Now, let’s talk about a problem with Claude Code, an AI tool for coding, after a big update in February. Before the update, Claude could follow detailed instructions, remember project rules, and make careful code changes. After the update, Claude started making mistakes and became less helpful. The model’s “thinking” was reduced, so it stopped checking code carefully and made careless changes. The team tracked sessions and found that as “thinking tokens” dropped, quality went down. Claude started editing files without reading them, making repeat mistakes, ignoring rules, and asking for permission instead of working on its own. Users had to correct Claude much more often, and even the AI admitted its work was “lazy” or “sloppy.” The team built tools to watch for these problems, but engineers became frustrated and stopped trusting Claude. The cost of using Claude went up, even if each request was cheaper, because of wasted time and corrections. The author asks Anthropic for clear information about model “thinking” and suggests a premium tier for deeper reasoning. Analysis shows quality now changes during busy hours, which did not happen before.

In the comments, many agree that AI models sometimes get worse after updates, as companies try to save costs. Some say “thinking depth” is important for all serious AI work. Others suggest Anthropic should offer better quality for a higher price, or at least be honest about changes. Some share similar stories with other AI tools and worry about relying on AI if quality can drop suddenly. Tracking tool behavior and user sentiment is seen as smart, and more teams should do this. There are also comments about the difficulty of balancing cost, quality, and user needs. Some defend Anthropic, saying scaling AI is hard. Most want more transparency and control over model “thinking.”

Let’s end with a book review: “There Is No Antimemetics Division,” based on the SCP Foundation stories. The book is about “antimemes”—ideas that erase themselves from people’s minds, making them impossible to remember or fight. The story follows Marion Wheeler, leader of the Antimemetics Division, whose job is to fight threats everyone else forgets exist. She and her team take special drugs to remember things their brains want to forget, but the drugs cause pain. The enemy is a dangerous creature that kills anyone who understands it; even knowing about it is risky. The book suggests information is more real than physical things, and strange creatures live in the space of ideas.

The review says the book is scary in a way that feels familiar to computer workers, because it’s about losing information and not knowing it’s gone—like silent software bugs or forgotten backups. The main character even erases her own memories to stop the enemy, losing her identity and relationships. The story has a sad but hopeful message: even if someone is forgotten, the love they leave behind can still be felt. The book’s style matches its theme—chapters start in the middle of scenes and information is missing, so readers feel the same confusion as the characters.

In the comments, some say they love the book and the SCP Foundation, calling the story unique and clever. Others say the idea of antimemes is interesting and makes them think about how information is lost in real life. Some like the mix of horror and computer science ideas. A few say the style is confusing, but it fits the theme. There are comments about the pain of working in jobs where your work is invisible, like open source. Not everyone loves the book, but most agree it’s creative, deep, and worth reading if you like strange stories about memory, information, and horror.
