## Can you stop beans from making you gassy?

- 原文链接: [Can you stop beans from making you gassy?](https://www.seriouseats.com/how-to-reduce-bean-gas-tested-11883862)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47904224)

This article is about why beans make people gassy and if there are ways to stop it. The writer worked with Harvard scientists to test 17 popular remedies and see which ones really work.

Beans cause gas because they have special sugars called oligosaccharides (like stachyose) that our bodies can’t digest. When these sugars reach our gut, bacteria eat them and make gas. Many people say you can reduce gas by soaking beans, throwing away the water, cooking with bay leaves or kombu, or using canned beans. The team tested all these methods in the lab.

They found that none of the usual ways—like soaking beans, using bay leaves, or cooking with kombu—really make beans less gassy. Surprisingly, beans cooked with bay leaves had even more gas-causing sugars. Pressure cooking and blanching also did not help. Canned beans had a little less gas than dried beans, but not by much. Rinsing canned beans did reduce gas by about 20%, but it also made the beans taste bland because the flavor is in the liquid you throw away.

The only big winner was using Beano, a pill with an enzyme called alpha-galactosidase. When added to bean puree (like bean dip), Beano broke down most of the gas-causing sugars and made the beans much less gassy. But for whole beans, Beano mostly worked on the liquid, not the beans themselves, so the effect was smaller. The Harvard “Fart Squad” also tested this on real people. People who ate bean dip with Beano reported less gas than those who ate regular dip.

In the comments, many Hacker News users were surprised that soaking beans does not help with gas. Some people said they always rinse canned beans, while others cared more about flavor and didn’t want to lose the tasty bean liquid. A few users joked that everyone has their own “secret trick” for less gassy beans, but science doesn’t support most of them. Some people pointed out that eating beans often can make you get used to the gas, or just not notice it as much. Others talked about Beano and wondered if there are cheaper or more natural ways to solve the problem. Several commenters liked the careful, scientific approach of the article and thought the “Harvard Fart Squad” was funny. A few worried that removing all the gas-causing parts might also remove some of the health benefits of beans. And some users just accepted that beans are a little gassy and said it’s a small price to pay for a healthy, tasty food.

## The Free Universal Construction Kit

- 原文链接: [The Free Universal Construction Kit](https://fffff.at/free-universal-construction-kit/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47860198)

The article talks about the Free Universal Construction Kit, which is a set of 3D-printable adapters that let you connect different brands of building toys, like Lego, Tinkertoy, K’Nex, and more. The kit includes about 80 adapters, so you can mix and match pieces from ten popular toy sets.

The creators made these adapters because many kids (and adults) want to join their different toys together, but the companies don’t make parts that fit with each other. Now, with 3D printing, people can just print their own connectors at home. The kit files are free to download, and you can print them yourself or use a 3D printing service. The adapters were designed very carefully, using high-precision tools to make sure they fit tightly and work well with the real toys. There’s even a special “universal” brick that connects all the systems at once.

The article points out that toy companies usually want their toys to be used alone, so they don’t help people connect sets from other brands. This kit goes against that, letting people be more creative and not waste old toys. The project also wants to show that it’s possible to “hack” or improve physical products, just like people do with software. The creators think this is good for learning, creativity, and fighting against business “lock-in”—when companies make it hard to use products together on purpose.

There are some legal issues, because companies own patents and trademarks on their toys. But the kit mostly supports older toys, where the patents have expired. For toys with active patents, those adapters are not yet released. The kit is free for personal use, but not for selling. The creators say this is fair use, and they want people to share and remix the designs for fun or education.

Looking at the Hacker News comments, many people think the kit is a great idea. They remember wanting to connect their toys as kids, and wish this had existed back then. Some users talk about how 3D printing is changing what’s possible, letting regular people make parts that companies won’t make. Others are excited by the idea of “interoperability,” not just for toys but for all kinds of products. A few commenters worry about how well the adapters will actually fit, since home 3D printers might not be as precise as commercial molds. Some talk about legal risks, guessing that big toy makers might not like people sharing these files, but others think the project is safe because it’s non-commercial and helps kids learn.

Some users share stories of making their own adapters by hand or with glue, saying this kit is a big step up. There’s discussion about patents and copyright, with some people surprised that making adapters is allowed as long as you don’t sell them. A few people ask for more adapters for other toys, or hope for updates as 3D printing gets better. Overall, most comments are positive, seeing this as a clever, useful, and even playful hack that opens up new ways to play and build things.

## New 10 GbE USB adapters are cooler, smaller, cheaper

- 原文链接: [New 10 GbE USB adapters are cooler, smaller, cheaper](https://www.jeffgeerling.com/blog/2026/new-10-gbe-usb-adapters-cooler-smaller-cheaper/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47899053)

This article talks about new 10 Gigabit Ethernet (10 GbE) USB adapters that are now smaller, cooler, and less expensive. The author compares these new adapters to older Thunderbolt models, which were big, expensive, and got very hot.

The new adapters use the Realtek RTL8159 chip and work over USB 3.2. One example, the WisdPi adapter, costs $80—cheaper than past 10 GbE options, but still more than 2.5 or 5 GbE adapters. The main point is that if you need 10 Gbps speeds and use RJ45 connectors, these new adapters are a good choice, but you must have a computer with the right USB port. Only computers with USB 3.2 Gen 2x2 (20 Gbps) ports can get the full speed. Most laptops and Macs only get about 6–7 Gbps, even if the adapter says "10 Gbps." Sometimes, it’s hard to know what USB version your port is, because Windows just calls everything “USB 3.0,” and port labels are rare. Mac computers show more details in the system info.

If you don’t need the highest speed, the author says 2.5 or 5 GbE adapters are a better value. The WisdPi 5 Gbps adapter gives about 4.6 Gbps on a MacBook Air, and costs less than half of the 10 GbE version. For even faster connections or SFP+ support, Thunderbolt adapters are still needed, but they are bigger and hotter.

The new USB adapters also use less power and do not get as hot as older 10 GbE adapters. The author found the new adapter stayed at about 42°C, while older models could get almost too hot to touch. If $80 is too much, similar adapters can be found for less on other sites, or as PCIe cards for desktops.

In the comments, some people are happy these adapters are finally getting smaller and cheaper. Others warn that USB port confusion is a big problem—many people will buy these adapters and not get full speed, because their computer ports are too slow. A few users say it’s hard to find out what USB version a laptop actually has, and that manufacturers should label ports better. Some think Thunderbolt is still better for real 10 Gbps speeds, even if the adapters are bigger and more expensive. Others like that these new adapters do not get as hot, making them safer and easier to use. A few commenters point out that for most home or office uses, 2.5 or 5 Gbps is enough, and those adapters are cheaper and work well. There are also tips about checking driver support, especially on Windows, where you may need to download drivers for the adapter to work. Some users share that on Linux and Mac, the adapters often work out of the box. Lastly, a few people discuss using these adapters in small home servers or for quick backups, saying the smaller size is very helpful.

## Simulacrum of Knowledge Work

- 原文链接: [Simulacrum of Knowledge Work](https://blog.happyfellow.dev/simulacrum-of-knowledge-work/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47902987)

This article talks about how people judge the quality of knowledge work, like reports or code, by looking at surface details instead of checking if the work is actually good. The author gives examples, like a market report with small mistakes—typos, wrong dates, and bad graphs—which make people stop trusting the whole report, even if the main ideas are correct. People use these small mistakes as shortcuts, called proxy measures, because checking the real quality takes too much time and effort. 

The article says this problem is everywhere in knowledge work. Instead of carefully checking if something is true or useful, managers and workers often just check if it looks professional and well-written. With large language models (LLMs) like ChatGPT, this gets worse. LLMs can create reports or code that look perfect on the surface, but may not be deep or correct underneath. People can use AI to write and review code, and everything looks good—even if no one actually checks if it works well.

The author says this is a "simulacrum"—an imitation—of real work. Workers want to be judged well, so they use tools that make their work look good, because that is what is measured. LLMs themselves are trained to make things look good based on what seems likely or what makes a judge happy, not on truth or usefulness. As a result, companies spend lots of money making more and more of this surface-level output, but no one has time to check if it's actually right. The article ends by saying we have automated ourselves into a problem where only what looks good matters, not what is truly good.

In the comments, many people agree that surface-level checks are common, but some say this has always been a problem in offices—even before AI. Some users worry that this trend will lower the real quality of work everywhere, making it harder to know what to trust. Others think that using AI for reports or code reviews is helpful, as it saves time and energy, even if it sometimes misses deeper problems. A few people point out that good managers or experts still take time to check important work, so not everything is fake. Some commenters wonder if new tools will be made to check real quality, not just the surface. Others joke that soon, even the people checking the work will be AIs, and no one will look deeper anymore. A few suggest that this could make real, careful work more valuable in the future, since it will be rare. Some people also say that the problem is not new, but AI makes it much easier to hide bad work behind a good-looking surface.

## Mine, an IDE for Coalton and Common Lisp

- 原文链接: [Mine, an IDE for Coalton and Common Lisp](https://coalton-lang.github.io/mine/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47903173)

Mine is a new IDE made for Coalton and Common Lisp, and it works on Windows, macOS, and Linux. The article explains what Mine does and how it helps people write code in these languages.

Mine has two versions: a full app for Windows and macOS that works out of the box, and a “core” version for all platforms that you use in your terminal. You can use Coalton, which is a statically typed functional language, or Common Lisp, which is dynamic and object-oriented. You can even mix both in your projects. The IDE includes a built-in REPL, so you can send code from the editor to the REPL and try it out right away. When you make mistakes, an interactive debugger pops up to show errors and a stack trace, and you can fix issues on the spot.

Mine also shows errors, warnings, and even optimization hints right inside the editor as you code. If you use Coalton, you see type hints and can auto-complete function names easily. There are built-in lessons to help you learn structural editing, which is a way to edit code more efficiently, but you don’t have to use it if you don’t want to. All code is compiled to native binaries for speed—there are no virtual machines or interpreters.

In the Hacker News comments, some people like that Mine brings modern IDE features to Lisp and functional programming. They think the built-in debugger and instant feedback will help new users and make coding faster. Others are happy that the IDE is cross-platform and works on Linux, since many tools skip Linux these days. Some users ask about how well Coalton and Common Lisp work together, and if mixing them in one project is easy. A few people wonder about the “no dependencies” claim for the full app, and if it really is stable.

Other commenters wish more languages had this kind of easy setup and strong tooling. Some are interested in the structural editing lessons, saying it’s nice to have a gentle way to learn a new editing style. A few mention that native code compilation is rare in IDEs for these languages, so this is a welcome surprise. There is also some discussion about the REPL integration, with people saying that sending code directly from the editor makes a big difference when experimenting. ## Desmond Morris has died

- 原文链接: [Desmond Morris has died](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c51y797v200o)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47841141)

Desmond Morris, a British zoologist, author, artist, and TV presenter, has died at 98. He was best known for his 1967 book "The Naked Ape," which suggested that, deep down, humans are still very much like other apes.

The article explains how Morris looked at humans as animals, not just as people with culture and history. He studied animal behavior (ethology) and used these ideas to explain why people act the way they do. Morris wrote "The Naked Ape" in only four weeks, and the book quickly became a bestseller, selling 20 million copies. He talked about why humans have little body hair, why men show off their brains but hide their sex organs, and why women’s breasts may have evolved for more than just feeding babies. The book explained things like fighting, eating, and sex as animal behaviors. Morris even did art experiments, giving paintbrushes to chimps to see if only humans could make art. He became a TV personality, running shows about animals and later about people’s gestures and body language. Some of his ideas, like men being “risk-takers” and early humans as hunters, were very controversial. Feminists and some scientists disagreed with him, saying he ignored culture and language. Still, Morris always said he was just looking at humans as he did with animals, without judgment. He wrote more books, painted surreal art, and stayed curious until the end of his life.

In the Hacker News comments, many people remembered reading "The Naked Ape" when they were young and said it made them think differently about human behavior. Some people admired Morris for making science popular and easy to understand. Others felt his ideas were too simple, especially when talking about gender roles or ignoring culture. A few commenters pointed out that modern science knows more about genetics now, and some of Morris’s ideas might not hold up. One commenter said they liked how he made people ask big questions, even if not everyone agreed with the answers. Others mentioned being inspired by his TV shows and art experiments, like chimps painting. Some commenters thought Morris was too quick to explain everything by biology, and wished he had included more about human society and history. Still, most agreed that Morris helped people see themselves as part of nature, and that was a big gift.

Martin Galway, a famous music composer for Commodore 64 games in the 1980s, has shared his old music source files on GitHub. These files include the code and samples used in games like "Wizball" and "Game Over," allowing people to study and even modify the original music.

The files are written in assembly language, which was common for making music on the C64 because it gave direct control over the SID sound chip. Galway explains in the README that the "Wizball" music used his first-generation player, and later games used an updated version. He also mentions that the code includes special tricks, like putting digital drum samples right into the music source. Galway encourages people to read, learn, and even create new music from these files, but asks for credit if they use his work. He notes that he owns the copyright now, though he had to get the rights from Infogrames after the 1980s.

The GitHub repository has files for different games, a license (GPL-3.0), and some notes about the unique assembler used at the time. Galway warns that some files may be hard to assemble because of special formatting, but he hopes to improve them later. The project is popular, with many stars and forks, showing that a lot of people are interested in this piece of game history.

Commenters on Hacker News are excited and call this a "treasure." Many share memories of hearing Galway's music as kids, saying the tunes are still stuck in their heads. Some discuss the technical side, like how making music on the C64 needed clever code because of hardware limits. People praise Galway’s creativity and say his work pushed the SID chip to its limits.

Some users ask about how to play or assemble the files today, and others point to tools and emulators that can help. A few comment on the importance of preserving digital history, while others wonder if more game composers will share their old source code too. There's also discussion about the legal side, with people glad Galway got the rights to his own work. Overall, the comments show deep respect for Galway’s skills and excitement at seeing this code made public.
