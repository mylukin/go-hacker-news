First, YouTube is making it easier for users to see when videos use AI. Now, if a video is photorealistic and made or strongly changed by AI, YouTube will show a clear label. For long videos, this label appears just below the video player, above the description. For YouTube Shorts, the label is overlaid right on the video. If a video is only a little changed by AI or is animated, the AI label will be in the expanded description instead.

YouTube is also starting to use automatic AI detection. Before, creators had to say if they used AI. Now, if YouTube’s system thinks a video uses a lot of realistic AI, it will add the label even if the creator does not. If creators think the label is a mistake, they can change it in YouTube Studio. But for videos made with YouTube’s own AI tools, or with special AI metadata, the label cannot be removed.

These changes do not affect how videos are shown or how they make money. YouTube says it wants viewers to know what is AI and also wants creators to have some control.

In the comments, users had mixed feelings. Some like the new labels and think it helps with trust. Others worry that automatic detection might make mistakes. A few say creators might try to avoid the label, so YouTube should keep improving its AI detection. Some people think the labels will help viewers know what is real, while others feel many viewers will just ignore them. One person pointed out that AI is now so common in video that soon, almost every video might get a label. Others ask if these labels will help with deepfakes or fake news. Some want the labels to be even bigger, but others worry about losing control. Most agree AI in video is growing fast, and YouTube is trying to keep up.

Next, let’s look at the latest in AI business. Anthropic and OpenAI have finally found product-market fit, thanks to their coding agents like Claude Code and Codex. The article explains that big companies are now paying much more for API usage, moving from cheap flat fees to billing based on actual usage. Both Anthropic and OpenAI changed their pricing recently—now, enterprise customers pay by API tokens, which can get very expensive. At the same time, new and more expensive models were released.

The main reason is that coding agents are now very useful for professionals, especially developers, and companies are willing to pay a lot. Consumer subscriptions are not enough to make these AI labs profitable, but enterprise API usage is. This change also led both companies to hire more sales and support people for big clients.

There are stories of companies like Uber and Microsoft getting surprised by high AI bills. The article explains these are not failures but a sign of real use and value. AI labs are also spending huge amounts on computing power. For example, Anthropic spends $1.25 billion a month just for extra capacity from one vendor.

The comments show mixed reactions. Some agree that charging by API usage makes sense and shows these tools are valuable. Others worry that high costs could push out smaller companies or limit access. Some users are excited by how useful coding agents have become, but others warn companies to watch their budgets. There are concerns about vendor lock-in, with some people comparing this to the early days of cloud computing and AWS. A few mention open-source AI as future competition. Others question if this level of spending is sustainable or just a hype cycle. Many agree that while enterprise deals are making money now, consumer AI use is still not profitable, so these companies must keep growing their business customers.

Now, to something for retro gamers. One article shares how to get SimCity 3000, the classic city-building game, running well in 4K resolution on Windows 10. The writer explains that the game is their favorite, but it has many problems on new computers: old screen resolutions, no widescreen, slow scrolling, missing music, and broken graphics.

To fix this, they use a special EXE from GOG.com, change config files, install a D3D Wrapper for 4K and graphic fixes, adjust settings for true fullscreen and VSync, and add a 4GB patch for more RAM. They also disable the auto-updater and use a patch to get all the music tracks from the CD. After all these steps, SimCity 3000 works in 4K with good performance and music.

Commenters praise the guide and share their memories of SimCity 3000. Some use similar tricks for other old games. A few warn that patching and downloading files can be risky for beginners. Others mention running the game on Linux with Wine. There are questions about Mac or Steam versions, and some people thank the writer for helping keep old games alive.

On the topic of hardware hacking, another article describes how someone jailbroke a Kindle Paperwhite and ran Rust code with a Slint GUI. The author wanted to use the Kindle as a clock and ended up making custom apps. They explain how to cross-compile Rust for ARMv7 with musl libc, use Zig and cargo-zigbuild, and set up SSH access with USBNetwork. After getting a simple app to run, they used Slint to draw graphics, writing image data directly to the Kindle’s framebuffer file and refreshing the e-ink display. Touch input is handled by reading from the touchscreen device file.

After debugging, they got a simple counter app running. The author also released their Kindle backend for Slint as open source.

Many commenters are impressed and happy to see old devices get new uses. Some suggest more e-ink hacks, like dashboards or note-taking apps. Others ask about battery life or risks of jailbreaking. There’s praise for direct hardware control, and some share similar projects. A few warn about screen burn-in. Overall, readers are enthusiastic and share tips on Rust, cross-compiling, and e-ink programming.

Some worry DuckDuckGo might change as it grows, and point out it still uses Bing for some results. Many are glad to have more choices and hope for more privacy-focused search engines. Some people miss the “old Google.” Overall, users care about privacy, choice, and the ability to avoid AI summaries.

Switching to social trends, there’s an article about Labubu, a cute plush toy from POP MART, and why so many adults now carry and share photos of these toys online. Labubu started as a book character and became popular worldwide, especially after a K-pop star was seen with one. Many adults find comfort in these toys, especially after the loneliness of the pandemic. People post photos of Labubu doing daily things to feel connected and show their identity online.

Labubu is sold in “blind boxes,” which makes collecting exciting and sometimes addictive, like a lottery. The article talks about “object fetishism”—the idea that things can become special because people agree they are special. Labubu lets people escape daily problems but also hides the emptiness of buying things just to feel better.

In the comments, some agree that Labubu is a symbol of loneliness and comfort. Others think it is just a fun trend. Some worry about the gambling side of blind boxes, while others enjoy collecting with friends. There’s talk about fast social media trends and marketing tricks. Some say that as long as Labubu brings joy and connection, it is not a bad thing.

Next, an important change for developers and marketers: Apple and Google now control almost all push notifications on your phone. Over time, both companies have taken more control over what notifications are delivered, how they look, and when they appear. They use AI to filter, group, and even rewrite notifications. Users can now set importance levels, and opt-in rates for push have dropped, especially for games and news apps.

This means apps and marketers have less power, and the platforms decide what is important, often using secret models. There is no inbox for old notifications, and senders cannot track what happens on the device. Both Apple and Google hold patents for these filtering systems.

User research shows people care about only a few notifications each day. Too many alerts make users turn off push or delete apps. The article suggests saving push for important updates and using in-app messages for everything else. Looking forward, Apple and Google plan to let AI assistants act on notifications for users, so senders need to make messages simple and useful for both people and machines.

In the comments, some people agree that strong controls are good for users. Others worry Apple and Google have too much power and can block important messages by mistake. Developers say it is now harder to reach users or know if notifications work. Some like AI summaries, while others think this hurts smaller apps. There are also questions about privacy and missing important updates. Many agree that personal, user-requested messages should always get through, but there is debate on where to draw the line.

All accounts, listening history, privacy settings, and Pro subscriptions are unchanged. The company promises to focus more on building features for music fans and the community, and says it will share more future plans soon.

Most comments are positive, with users congratulating the team and hoping for more improvements. Some share memories of using Last.fm for many years, and a few worry about the future but feel reassured by the announcement. Developers are glad the API will continue to work. Some users ask about ways to support Last.fm. Overall, the mood is hopeful.

On the world stage, Canada has decided to buy new military planes from Sweden instead of the US. The planes, called GlobalEye, are made by Saab and will help Canada watch over its Arctic land and sea. This comes after problems with US planes and rising costs. Saab will also invest in Canadian research and jobs. The move is seen as a way for Canada to be more independent and build ties with Sweden, now a NATO ally. Canada may also buy fighter jets from Saab in the future, though it still has F-35s from the US. The decision comes after the US put tariffs on Canadian goods.

In the comments, many support buying from Sweden, saying it helps Canada be more independent and brings jobs. Some worry about problems with new suppliers or the time needed to deliver the planes. There is praise for Sweden’s technology and comments about the politics behind the decision. Some are surprised Canada did not do this sooner, while a few think it may cause tension with the US. Overall, most support the move.

The GitHub team posted updates as they worked on the problem and promised a full explanation later. Many parts of GitHub were affected, and many developers could not work as usual.

In the comments, some people were frustrated, while others said outages like this are rare but still a big problem. Some shared tips for working offline with git. A few thanked the GitHub team for quick updates and asked for more technical details. Others joked about taking a break during the downtime. Some said we should expect these problems sometimes since running big services is hard. A few discussed moving projects or keeping backups just in case.
