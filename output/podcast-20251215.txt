First, Let’s Encrypt is making some big changes to how it gives out security certificates for websites. The main updates include new “Generation Y” root and intermediate certificates, ending support for TLS client authentication, and making certificates valid for a shorter time. Most websites will get the new Generation Y certificates starting in May 2026. If you need a little more time to switch, you can still use the old roots until May 2026 with a special profile, but after that, everyone will be moved to the new system. The new roots will not support TLS client authentication because of new rules, and Let’s Encrypt will stop supporting this from February 2026. Another change is that you can now get short-lived certificates for IP addresses, not just for domains. Certificate lifetimes are also getting shorter: soon, you can try 45-day certificates, and by 2028, this will become the default.

In the Hacker News comments, some people are worried that shorter certificate lifetimes mean more work and more chances for something to break if auto-renewal fails. Others believe shorter lifetimes make the web safer, because stolen certificates can’t be used for long. Many users trust Let’s Encrypt’s automation and have never had problems with renewals. Some mention that older devices might not trust the new roots, but for most people, things should work fine. There is praise for Let’s Encrypt’s free and easy-to-use service, and a few people say supporting certificates for IP addresses is a nice bonus. Most agree that these changes are normal as security standards get stricter, and almost everyone thanks Let’s Encrypt for making the web safer and simpler.

Next, let’s talk about Umbrel, a home server and operating system that turns your hardware into a private cloud. Umbrel Home is a plug-and-play device with up to 4TB of storage, starting at $499, but you can also use their free umbrelOS on your own hardware. With Umbrel, you can store files, stream movies, block ads using Pi-hole, run smart home devices, and even set up a Bitcoin node or host AI models like Llama 3. There’s an app store with one-click installs for apps like Plex, Nextcloud, LibreOffice, and more. The focus is on privacy and making it easy for people to keep control of their data instead of using big tech cloud services.

On Hacker News, many people are excited about how simple and user-friendly Umbrel is. They like that it helps users take back control of their files and devices. Some compare it to building a similar system with a Raspberry Pi, but find Umbrel easier for beginners. A few worry about the price and wonder if you can build something cheaper yourself. There are also questions about updates and data safety, but others point out that Umbrel is open source. Some users want even more advanced features or better documentation. Still, most agree that Umbrel makes self-hosting much more accessible, especially for people who do not want to deal with complex setups.

Now, let’s move to something for students and engineers. A new open book gives a clear and simple introduction to the physics of semiconductors, the materials behind chips and transistors. The book starts with the history of semiconductors, explains energy bands, band gaps, and the difference between metals, insulators, and semiconductors. It describes different types of semiconductor materials and their properties, and why silicon is so widely used. The book covers the difference between direct and indirect band gaps, and why this matters for devices like LEDs. It also explains doping—adding other atoms to make semiconductors conduct better, which is the key to making electronic devices.

Hacker News readers praise the book for being clear, easy to read, and not needing advanced math. Many wish they had such a resource in school and find it useful even for software engineers who want to understand the hardware. Some discuss technical points, like how certain values are measured or why silicon is preferred. There’s a lot of support for open educational resources, and people say this book helps connect the basics from physics and chemistry to real-world technology. A few suggest adding more real-life examples or simple experiments to help students. Overall, readers think this book is a great introduction for anyone interested in how computers and electronics work.

Switching gears, we have a story about a tough bug in the Linux kernel. A user found that his machine kept freezing when using async-profiler to check QuestDB’s performance. After a lot of testing, he found the problem started after upgrading to Linux kernel 6.17. The bug was in the kernel’s perf_events feature, which uses high-resolution timers. A recent change caused a deadlock: when the timer fired, the kernel tried to cancel it while still running its own callback, making the system freeze. The fix was to use a non-blocking cancel function. The author found a workaround by changing the profiler’s options and even learned to debug the frozen kernel using QEMU and GDB, managing to unfreeze the virtual machine by hand.

In the comments, many users praise the write-up and the author’s persistence. People say they learned a lot about kernel debugging. Some share similar stories about small kernel changes causing big problems. There are questions about why kernel tests did not catch the bug, and praise for the quick fix from kernel developers. Some users talk about staying safe by avoiding kernel 6.17 or sticking to LTS kernels. A few joke about “playing God” with the kernel, but most agree this should be done only in test environments. Overall, it’s a helpful and inspiring story for anyone interested in Linux internals.

Now let’s look at a big security failure. Freedom Chat, a messaging app claiming to be very secure, actually leaked users’ phone numbers and PIN codes. The app was made by people with no experience in mobile apps, but they promised privacy and special encryption. A security researcher found many problems: the app collected lots of data, used normal servers, had weak encryption, and made a huge mistake—when you opened a chat channel, it sent back a list of all members, showing their phone numbers and PINs. The researcher wrote a script and was able to collect all users’ phone numbers in about a day. The company was told and later claimed it was fixed.

People on Hacker News say this is a good example of why it’s hard to make secure apps, and you shouldn’t make big promises if you lack experience. Others note that privacy is about the whole system design, not just encryption. Some are surprised by the simple mistakes, like exposing sensitive data in an API response. There are jokes about the app’s political branding, and worries that people might think it is safer than WhatsApp when it is not. Many agree that responsible disclosure is important, but companies also need to fix problems quickly.

Moving on to space science, there’s a new model explaining how a past supernova helped create planets like Earth. These star explosions gave our Solar System special radioactive elements, which gave heat to small rocks and helped form dry, rocky planets. The new “immersion model” says a nearby supernova sent cosmic rays to the young Solar System, creating new elements inside the disk of gas and dust, while also adding some elements directly. This matches what scientists see in meteorites and doesn’t require the supernova to be dangerously close. The model is flexible and fits the data well.

Hacker News commenters are excited that Earth-like planets might be common, but some remind us that there are still many unknowns. Some discuss the technical side, like how cosmic rays make new elements, or the timing of supernovae near young stars. Others wonder if this changes how special Earth is, or if it just means we need more data. Many like that the model brings together different ideas, and a few say it could guide future searches for life. Overall, the mood is hopeful but cautious, with lots of curiosity about what new research will show.

Next up, a fun tool for the command line: Chafa. Chafa lets you see images and even animations inside your terminal window, using special symbols and colors. It supports many formats and standards, works with different terminals, and can use different fonts and color modes. Chafa is fast, uses parallel processing, and is open source. There are also APIs and bindings for Python and JavaScript, so developers can use it in their own projects.

On Hacker News, many users love Chafa for making the terminal more fun and useful. Some are impressed by the balance between speed and image quality. A few say not all terminals support every feature, but most like that Chafa is open source and easy to use. Some share creative ideas like making terminal-based slideshows or art. A few worry about accessibility, but overall, the tool is seen as both fun and practical for developers.

Finally, we end with a bit of nostalgia. An article explains how to land your plane in the NES game Top Gun. To land, your altitude must be between 100 and 299, speed between 238 and 337, and you need to be lined up with the carrier. The game shows you the center of these numbers, and after about a minute, it checks if you landed correctly. If you crash, you still go to the next level. The article also shows the game’s landing check code and even a cheat code for perfect landings.

The comments are full of people remembering how hard it was to land when they were kids. Some thought the game was broken because they always crashed. Others enjoy seeing the real code and learning how the numbers are checked. Some talk about how old games were often much harder than today’s games. There are jokes about using cheat codes and people wanting to try landing again with this new knowledge. The comments mix nostalgia, technical details, and appreciation for the deep dive into old game code.
