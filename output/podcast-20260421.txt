First, we look at a serious security problem at Vercel, the company behind popular web app deployment tools. Attackers broke into Vercel’s systems by abusing a third-party OAuth app trusted by Vercel employees. Once inside, the attackers could read environment variables, which often contain passwords and secret keys for many other services. The main risk was how Vercel managed these secrets—unless developers marked a variable as “sensitive,” it was not well protected. Many did not, so a lot of secrets were left exposed. The attackers could use these secrets to get into databases, cloud accounts, payments, and more.

This is not an isolated case. Similar attacks happened before at Codecov and CircleCI, where attackers stole secrets from environment variables. What makes this breach worse is the timeline: the attack started in June 2024, but Vercel only told users in April 2026. That means attackers had almost two years of secret access. One customer even got a warning about a leaked secret nine days before the news became public.

OAuth was supposed to make apps connect safely, but if attackers break into a trusted OAuth app, they can move through company systems without needing passwords. This is hard for security teams to detect. Also, Vercel’s CEO thinks AI tools helped the attackers move faster and smarter than before. Experts now worry that AI will help more attackers in the future.

To fix these problems, the article suggests using secret managers instead of plain environment variables, checking which OAuth apps are trusted, rotating secrets often, and acting quickly on leaked-credential alerts.

In the comments, many developers said the breach was not a surprise, since storing secrets as environment variables is common but risky. People blamed Vercel’s default settings for making it too easy to leave secrets unprotected, and said this is a general problem in the industry. There was debate about whether OAuth is just too dangerous, or if the real problem is how companies manage trust and secrets. Some people said their own companies also have weak controls but changing them slows down work. Many were upset that Vercel took 22 months to disclose the breach, showing how hard these attacks are to catch. Users agreed companies must take leaked-credential warnings seriously and change how they protect secrets now, especially as AI makes attacks faster. Many hope this breach will push more teams to use secret managers and review OAuth apps more often.

Next, California’s government has found it has more money than expected, thanks to two mistakes in the state budget. These mistakes involved payments to CalPERS, California’s retirement system, adding up to $2 billion. At first, the governor’s team thought there was a $2.9 billion budget shortfall, but now it should be much less. The first mistake was double-counting money needed for CalPERS, causing a $1.6 billion error. The second mistake was using the wrong numbers for future payments, adding another $450 million. California’s Legislative Analyst’s Office found these errors in February, but the public did not hear about them until a memo was leaked.

The LAO says mistakes happen because the budget is huge and complex, and it is their job to find them. They expect things will be fixed in May. The governor’s team says this was not an error, just a change in how they estimate payments, and the update will be in the next budget revision. Some lawmakers are upset about the lack of openness, while others say this is normal for a big budget. Some comments worry that hiding mistakes can hurt trust in government, while others note that budgets are complicated and errors happen. People suggest better systems and clearer communication would help. A few joked that finding extra money is better than being short, but wish the process was less confusing.

Moving on, we have news about Britannica11.org, a website that puts the entire 1911 Encyclopædia Britannica online in a searchable, easy-to-read way. This classic encyclopedia is famous for its expert articles and writing style. On Britannica11.org, you can search topics, click cross-references, read extra notes, and see who wrote each article. The design is simple and focused on reading, but some pages may load slowly.

Comments show people are happy to see the 1911 Britannica made easy to use online. They enjoy the old writing style and sometimes find old ideas funny or interesting. Others warn that some information is outdated, especially on science or history, and suggest using it only as a starting point. Developers ask about the technology behind the site and like its clean, fast design. Some discuss copyright, noting the 1911 edition is now public domain. There is hope for more projects like this for other old books. Overall, people are excited and curious, but warn to check facts with newer sources.

A different article discusses a software maintainer who no longer wants to accept pull requests from others. The main reason is that reviewing code from strangers is risky and takes more time than just making changes themselves, especially with AI tools like LLMs that can write code quickly. The maintainer says code from unknown people might hide bad things, and everyone has their own style, so merging PRs means extra work. In the past, good PRs were worth the effort, but now maintainers can just ask an LLM to make changes, review them, and move on, without waiting.

The maintainer also says the hardest part is not writing code, but understanding the project, designing solutions, and reviewing code. They suggest other ways to help, like giving feedback, reporting bugs, or sharing small code examples. If you want to make big changes, just fork the code.

In the comments, some agree that reviewing unknown code is hard, especially with fast-moving tools. Others worry this attitude will make open source less friendly and limit new contributors. Some maintainers say they feel the same way, while others argue PRs are still important for growing projects and communities. Some point out that AI still makes mistakes and needs human review. Many say open source is a gift, and maintainers can choose how to accept help. The discussion shows many feelings about how AI is changing code collaboration.

Today, we also remember Stephen’s Sausage Roll, a puzzle game released ten years ago that changed how people think about puzzles. The game looks simple—you use a fork to roll sausages onto grills—but the puzzles are very hard and deep. It only uses a few elements (player, fork, sausage, grill, block, ladder), but these create endless challenges. The rules never change, but the game keeps surprising you.

The creator, Stephen Lavelle, also made PuzzleScript, a tool for making puzzle games. Both the game and tool have inspired many other puzzle games. Developers say Stephen’s Sausage Roll made them rethink puzzles and even start making their own games. Some compare its impact to punk music for games—simple and raw, but powerful. Games like “A Monster’s Expedition” and “Patrick’s Parabox” were inspired by it.

In the comments, people say the game is very hard, but they respect its design. Some did not finish it, but learned from it. Others praise its fairness and creativity, even if the graphics are simple. Many say it made them think differently about games, and thank it for inspiring them to make games too. Some share tips for new players, like not giving up and taking breaks. Most agree it is a special game that changed puzzle design.

Next, we have the new Framework Laptop 13 Pro, made for developers who want strong hardware, long battery life, and easy repairs and upgrades. It uses Intel Core Ultra processors, can run up to 20 hours on battery, has modular memory, and a strong aluminum body. You can buy it pre-built with Windows or Ubuntu, or as a DIY kit. The 13.5-inch touchscreen is sharp and bright. You can pick your own ports by swapping cards—USB, HDMI, Ethernet, and more. Every part is easy to replace with one tool, and upgrade guides are included. Much of the hardware is open source, with design files on GitHub. There are privacy switches for the webcam and mic, and the laptop is shipped with a clean OS and lots of recycled materials.

In the comments, people are happy to see better battery life and Linux support. Many praise Framework’s focus on repair and upgrades, saying it is rare in modern laptops. Some already own older Framework models and are glad they can upgrade. Others think the price is high, but most agree it is worth it for repairability. Some want more AMD or ARM options, but overall, people are impressed with the new features and focus on sustainability.

Now, Cal.diy is a new open-source, community-driven version of Cal.com for scheduling meetings and events. The big news is the switch to the MIT license, so anyone can use, modify, or host it without restriction. The project started as a fork, removed all enterprise-only features, license checks, and branding, and is now lighter and focused on the community. Documentation, setup files, and Docker images are cleaned up for easier self-hosting, and a new welcome bot helps new contributors. The API is better organized, and the project is focused on being simple and open.

Commenters are excited about the MIT license, saying it is easier to build on or use Cal.diy for business. People are happy there are no more license checks or hidden limits and hope self-hosting is now simpler. Some worry about long-term support, but most see this as a good move for developer freedom. There are questions about upgrading from Cal.com, and people discuss the importance of open tools for privacy and control. Many think this will be useful for small businesses, schools, and freelancers.

Another article lists 56 well-known “laws” and principles for software engineering, such as Conway’s Law, the Boy Scout Rule, and YAGNI. Each law covers a key topic: team structure, project planning, coding best practices, and system design. Some laws warn about common problems, like the Sunk Cost Fallacy and Goodhart’s Law, while others give advice, like KISS (Keep It Simple) and DRY (Don’t Repeat Yourself). The list also mentions the Law of Leaky Abstractions and the CAP Theorem.

In the comments, people liked having all these laws in one place and called it a good cheat sheet for new developers. Some point out not every law fits every situation and that some are just opinions. There is debate over Conway’s Law and if changing company structure really matters for software. Many agree these principles are useful reminders, but real software is always more messy. Some liked the links for deeper reading and plan to share the list with their teams.

Next, there is an article about a new map that shows all possible ways to make cheese by mixing different milks, textures, rinds, molds, and aging methods. The map also points out “holes”—cheese types that do not exist yet—and explains why. Some gaps are impossible because of science, while others are only missing because of tradition or geography. For example, Gruyère-style cheese with yak milk, or Brie from buffalo milk, are possible but not common. The map suggests new ideas, like Spanish-style cheese with buffalo milk and thistle rennet, or hard cheese from reindeer milk.

Comments are excited about the map and want to explore it. Some think new cheeses come from mixing old styles in new ways, while others note that taste and local habits play a big role. People wonder if food safety laws or economics keep some cheeses from being made. Some share stories about rare local cheeses, showing the map might miss some. Others think the map could help small dairies and indie cheese makers find new products. A few warn that even if a new cheese is invented, it takes time before people accept it. Some want to try the ideas at home, while others are just hungry after reading.

Finally, Zindex is a new tool for making and managing diagrams, especially for software agents like AI. It treats diagrams as structured data, with every change saved, like a database. Agents define nodes and edges, and Zindex arranges everything automatically. Each part has a stable ID, so you can update just one part without breaking the rest. It supports different styles and formats, like SVG and PNG, and always gives the same result for the same input.

Zindex is made for teams or agents working together. It supports revision history and diffs, and works for system architecture, workflows, org charts, and more. It is not just for drawing, but understands what you are drawing. It has validation rules, secure storage, and features like user accounts and rate limits.

In the comments, people like the idea of treating diagrams like code. They say this could help manage complex systems over time. Some wonder how easy it is for humans to use and compare it to PlantUML or Mermaid, asking what makes Zindex different. Some like the support for collaboration and stable IDs, while others think it may be too complex for simple uses. Many want to see real-world examples or open source options and are curious about importing and exporting to other tools.
