## Writing by hand is good for your brain

- 原文链接: [Writing by hand is good for your brain](https://nealstephenson.substack.com/p/writing-by-hand-is-good-for-your)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=49022152)

This article talks about why writing by hand is good for your brain, and gives tips on how to do it well. The author, a writer who uses fountain pens, explains that writing by hand uses more parts of your brain than typing, and helps you think better.

When you write by hand, you solve little problems with every word—like how far apart to space letters and where to cross a “t” or dot an “i.” Your whole arm works together with your brain to do this. The author says that some schools are going back to hand-written exams because of AI, but many students don’t know how to write by hand anymore, or have poor handwriting. He has written thousands of book pages by hand and never gets sore or cramps, unlike when he was a child. He explains that pain usually comes from pressing too hard, which happens more with pencils or old ballpoint pens. Fountain pens or gel pens need less force, so your hand does not get tired.

Writing on glass with a stylus has too little friction and can actually make you more tired. The best is a balance: fountain pen and good paper, not too rough and not too smooth. The author tested different pens and papers, and found that cheap printer paper is bad because it makes the ink spread and look messy. Good notebooks or paper with some cotton are much better. He says you should not worry about using too much paper—just use one side and write comfortably.

Cursive writing is much easier and less tiring than printing letters one by one. Most people today only write for themselves, so perfect penmanship is not needed. He shares some tips about fountain pens: keep the pen nib up on airplanes to avoid ink leaks, and don’t worry about getting ink on your fingers—it washes off. For left-handed people, he says he has never had trouble with smudging, especially if you use the right paper.

He suggests starting with simple pens and paper, trying what works best for you, and not spending too much money at first. Cheap gel pens work almost as well as fountain pens. More expensive pens are only fancier, not really better at writing.

In the comment section, some readers agree and say writing by hand helps them remember things better, and it feels more personal. Others say they prefer typing because it’s faster, easier to edit, and their handwriting is hard to read. A few mention that their hands get tired or cramped, and they blame poor pen or paper quality, or not writing often. Some people talk about their favorite pens and notebooks, sharing tips on what works best for them. There are comments from teachers who say students struggle with handwriting now, and others who wish schools would teach cursive again. A few readers say that writing by hand is slower, but that is a good thing because it makes you think more. Some left-handed people agree with the author, but others say smudging is still a problem for them. One person says that writing by hand helps with creativity, while someone else thinks it is mostly nostalgia. Overall, most people seem to agree that writing by hand has benefits, but it is not easy for everyone.

The treatment was meant to fix a small mistake in Mei’s DNA, using a new technique called base editing, but sadly, Mei died one week after receiving the therapy due to a severe immune reaction.

The article tells how Mei’s parents, Jason and Linda, learned about the gene-editing trial led by neuroscientist Zilong Qiu at a respected hospital in Shanghai. Mei’s condition was not deadly but affected her development, and her parents hoped the treatment would help her live a more normal life. They paid for much of the research and preparation, including direct payments to researchers and labs. The therapy used viruses to deliver gene-editing instructions into Mei’s brain, aiming to fix the genetic mistake found in only a few hundred people worldwide.

Although animal studies in mice and monkeys seemed promising, some experts later said these studies were not fully convincing or safe. Doctors warned the parents about possible side effects, but the risk of death was not clearly stated in the consent forms. After the treatment, Mei developed a fever, kidney problems, and blood issues, all known risks of this kind of therapy. She died in the hospital a few days later.

The hospital admitted that Mei’s death was directly caused by the treatment. However, the event was not made public, and the hospital only received a small fine. The researchers did not face serious punishment, and the hospital’s ethics board had not even reviewed all safety data before approving the trial. The parents were upset by the lack of transparency and accountability, especially when they saw that the research was still published in a top science journal without mention of Mei’s death.

Some said this story highlights problems with medical ethics and transparency in China, while others warned that similar issues can happen anywhere when hope and money mix with experimental medicine. ## The Beam Engine

- 原文链接: [The Beam Engine](https://glinscott.github.io/beam-engine/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=49007221)

The article explains how the beam engine changed the world during the Industrial Revolution by turning steam into useful power. The author walks through how the engine works, starting from basic ideas about steam and showing how each part fits together.

First, the article tells how heating water makes steam, which can expand to 1,700 times its original size. This steam creates pressure inside a container. If you control the steam, you can use it to push things, like a piston inside a cylinder. Early engines used wide pistons to get more force with low-pressure steam, since making strong boilers was hard. The article also explains how the weight of air (atmospheric pressure) can help move the piston when you create a vacuum under it. Thomas Newcomen used this idea to make the first successful steam engine for pumping water out of mines. His engine used a lot of coal because it wasted heat, but it worked better than teams of horses.

James Watt improved the engine by adding a separate vessel called a condenser, which made the process more efficient and saved fuel. He also closed the top of the cylinder and used steam to push in both directions, making the engine stronger and smoother. The article describes how a slide valve and an off-center disc called an eccentric help control the flow of steam in and out of the cylinder. Stopping the steam early (cutoff) uses less fuel while still giving most of the power.

To turn the back-and-forth motion into spinning power, the engine uses a crank and a big flywheel. The flywheel keeps the engine from stopping at certain points called dead centers. Special linkages, like the beam and parallel motion, keep the piston moving in a straight line so it doesn't break its seal. The engine also has a small pump that keeps the boiler full of water.

The spinning shaft from the engine can be used to drive machines in a mill, using leather belts and iron pulleys. Friction and tension in the belts let them move a lot of power without slipping. Early mills needed to be near rivers, but with steam engines, factories could be built anywhere. The load on the engine changes as machines are turned on and off, so Watt added a governor—a device with spinning balls—to keep the speed steady by adjusting the steam.

The article ends by showing how later engines became even better, using new designs and materials. Over time, engines got more efficient, and electricity finally replaced the big shafts and belts in factories.

Commenters on Hacker News really liked the interactive diagrams and clear writing. Some said the article helped them understand steam engines for the first time. Others shared stories about seeing old engines working in museums. A few people pointed out that Newcomen's and Watt's engines were huge steps forward, but they were not very efficient compared to modern machines. Some explained how dangerous early boilers were—there were many accidents until better materials and safety rules were made.

One commenter explained more about the governor and how it is like the control systems we use today. Another person talked about how the flywheel idea is still used in cars and other machines. Some readers asked about building small engines at home, and others talked about the importance of the beam engine for factories, ships, and trains. There was discussion about how the move from water power to steam changed where people could build factories, making cities grow quickly. Some people debated whether steam power or electric power did more to change the world. Finally, readers thanked the author for making a hard topic easy and fun to learn.

## Building on ATProto

- 原文链接: [Building on ATProto](https://lukekanies.com/writing/building-on-atproto/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=49025984)

This article talks about building apps on ATProto, the protocol behind Bluesky. The writer went to a conference and wanted to use ATProto to build review apps that could replace things like Yelp or Goodreads, but found problems with how ATProto is designed.

The author wants apps that let people keep control of their own data. He wants to write reviews and keep them private, share them with some friends, or make them public—whatever he chooses. His wife, for example, would only use an app if she could keep her reviews totally private. Most review apps today are built for people who want to be influencers and share everything publicly. But the author believes most people don't want that. Instead, they want a mix of public and private, or even just private.

ATProto does a good job with identity. It lets apps easily handle logins, friends, and tracking followers. But right now, ATProto only supports public data—everything you do is for the whole world to see. The community is working on something called "permissioned data" (the author prefers calling it "private data"), but this is designed as a totally separate system from public data. This means, if you build an app, you have to handle public and private data in two different ways, even though users just see "reviews." If someone wants to change a review from private to public, it's not simple—you have to delete the private version and make a new public one, which loses likes and links.

The author also thought ATProto would work like Git, letting you have a local copy of your data and work offline, but that's not how it works. ATProto stores your data on a server. If you want offline support, you have to build your own system for that, and also deal with different protocols for public and private data. This makes things much harder for app developers who want to give users full control and offline access.

In the comment section, some people agree that managing public and private data should be simpler and unified. They say most users want more control, not less, and dislike the influencer-focused model. Others think that keeping public and private systems separate does make sense for security and privacy, and that mixing them could be risky. A few comment that ATProto is still new and early, and hope the community will listen to feedback and improve it. Some developers say that having to write two systems will slow down adoption, while others point out that identity features alone are already very useful. There are also people who miss the “old internet” with open standards, and agree with the author’s wish for protocols that give users real power over their data. Some technical commenters discuss possible ways to make local-first and permissioned data work better together, but say it's a hard problem. Overall, the discussion is hopeful but notes that right now, ATProto is not easy for developers who care about privacy and offline use.

## A solid-state “atomic channel” for separating rare earth elements

- 原文链接: [A solid-state “atomic channel” for separating rare earth elements](https://pme.uchicago.edu/news-events/news/cleaner-route-purifying-rare-earth-elements)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=49025831)

This article talks about a new, cleaner way to separate rare earth elements, which are used in many things like electric car motors and LED lights. The usual method for separating these metals is dirty and uses a lot of toxic chemicals, but this new method uses water and electricity instead.

The researchers at the University of Chicago, with help from Northwestern University and Argonne National Lab, used a special kind of manganese oxide—a material with layers that can hold ions. They found that these layers can separate rare earth metals by taking advantage of their small differences in size and water shells. Lighter elements have bigger water shells, and heavier ones have smaller shells. When they put a mix of rare earth elements into the manganese oxide, the heavy ones stick in the channels, and the light ones push the layers apart.

To make the separation even better, they added magnesium ions, which work like a scaffold and hold the channels at the right size. This “pins” the channels so only small ions can fit tightly. Now the method can separate even rare earth elements that are very similar, like neodymium and lanthanum. After two rounds, they got neodymium that was 97% pure.

The team checked their results using computer simulations and X-ray tests to see exactly how the ions behaved. The process uses only water—no harsh chemicals—so it’s safer for the environment. While the method is not ready for big factories yet, it may change how and where rare earth metals are processed in the future.

In the Hacker News comments, some people were excited about the idea of a cleaner way to separate rare earth elements. They said it could help the environment and make processing cheaper and safer. Others pointed out that even if the method uses water, mining for rare earth metals is still bad for the planet. A few people wondered if this new process could work for other metals, not just rare earths. Some were unsure how easy it would be to use this method in large factories, but others thought it was a good step forward.

A few commenters discussed how rare earth processing is mostly done in certain countries, and this method could help other countries set up their own processing plants. Some people liked the science behind using the size of ions and water shells to separate elements. There was also talk about the importance of using computer models to understand and improve the process. Finally, some users hoped this research would lead to more new ideas for cleaner industry in the future.
