## Why so many control rooms were seafoam green (2025)

- 原文链接: [Why so many control rooms were seafoam green (2025)](https://bethmathews.substack.com/p/why-so-many-control-rooms-were-seafoam)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47518960)

This article explains why many old control rooms, like those in nuclear plants, used seafoam green paint. The writer visited the Oak Ridge site from the Manhattan Project and noticed the green color everywhere in the control rooms. She wanted to know why that color was so common, so she did some research.

A man named Faber Birren, who studied color, helped make rules for using color in factories during World War II. He found that certain colors could make people feel different ways and even help them work better. For example, white walls made meat look bad, but a green or blue background made it look fresh. Companies started to use his ideas to sell more products.

During the war, factories became more dangerous and busy. Birren worked with DuPont, a big company, to create a color safety code for factories. Each color had a special meaning: red for fire and emergencies, yellow for caution, orange for machine dangers, blue for information, green for safety, and light green for walls to help people’s eyes. Light green was chosen because it was soft, not too bright, and helped workers feel calm and less tired. This was important in places where people had to pay close attention, like control rooms.

Birren wrote that color should be useful, not just pretty. His rules became standard in many places, like the Hanford nuclear site and other factories. Photos show that the color code was used exactly as he suggested: green for walls, gray for machines, red for fire, and so on. Even in Germany, a similar seafoam green was used for bridges.

People in the Hacker News comments had many thoughts. Some said they noticed the same green in old hospitals, schools, and even submarines, showing it was a popular choice everywhere. A few people explained that green is easy on the eyes, especially under harsh lights, and it helps reduce stress and mistakes. Others shared stories about working in places with this color and agreed it made long hours easier.

Some readers wondered if modern offices should use more color theory, not just white walls. A few questioned if the seafoam green was also chosen because it hid dirt and wear well, making cleaning easier. Several commenters liked learning about Faber Birren and suggested other design books about color. There was also debate about whether color choices still matter as much today with computers and digital screens. A few people joked about how seeing the color now reminds them of old movies or video games. Overall, most agreed that color in work spaces is important, and they enjoyed learning the history behind seafoam green control rooms.

## CERN to host a new phase of Open Research Europe

- 原文链接: [CERN to host a new phase of Open Research Europe](https://home.cern/news/news/cern/cern-host-europes-flagship-open-access-publishing-platform)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47534629)

CERN will soon host the next phase of Open Research Europe (ORE), which is Europe’s main open access publishing platform for science. This move is backed by the European Commission and many European research funders.

The ORE platform lets researchers publish their work for free if they are from supported countries or have European Commission funding. It uses a “publish–review–curate” model: first, articles are checked for quality, then published openly, and then reviewed by peers in public. The reviews are also open for everyone to read. If an article passes, it gets added to special collections by topic. This model helps keep research open, fair, and high quality.

ORE started in 2021 to let anyone see research paid for by public money. In just five years, more than 1,200 articles and 6,300 authors from over 3,000 places have used it. CERN will now give ORE its technical base, using open-source software called Open Journal Systems. The main decisions about ORE will still be made by the group of funders and researchers, not by CERN alone.

CERN has a long history with open science, running other big projects like Zenodo and SCOAP3. Leaders at CERN and the European Commission say this is a big step for open science in Europe. They hope it will help more people trust science and make it easier to share new knowledge.

Outreach will also grow, with groups like OPERAS helping to spread the word and get more authors involved. The goal is to keep growing the number of research articles published every year and to set a good example for fair, open publishing, both in Europe and worldwide.

In the Hacker News comments, some people are happy with this news and say open access is the right direction for science. They like that researchers will not need to pay to share their work, and that all results will be available for free to everyone. Other users are interested in the technical side, asking about the use of Open Journal Systems and how it will scale at CERN.

Some commenters warn about possible problems. They worry about how to keep quality high when there are no fees, and if this model can last for a long time. A few say open review is good but wonder if some reviewers might be less honest if their names are public. Others point out that having a trusted group like CERN is important for keeping the platform neutral and safe from commercial interests.

A few users compare ORE to other publishing models, like “diamond open access,” and say this is a sign Europe is taking the lead in open science. Some are excited to see if this will pressure other publishers to change. Still, some are cautious, noting that big commercial publishers may push back or try to slow down open access. Overall, the mood is hopeful, but people know there will be challenges ahead.

## My minute-by-minute response to the LiteLLM malware attack

- 原文链接: [My minute-by-minute response to the LiteLLM malware attack](https://futuresearch.ai/blog/litellm-attack-transcript/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47531967)

This article is about a real-time response to a malware attack through the Python package litellm, which was spread on PyPI. The author, an engineer, describes how they discovered, analyzed, and reported the attack as it happened.

The incident started when the author noticed their laptop was slow and had thousands of Python processes running. They saw strange commands using Python’s exec and base64 decoding, which is sometimes normal, but in this case led to suspicion. At first, the author thought the problem was a bug in a tool, but after more checks, they found a hidden file (litellm_init.pth) in the litellm package. This file ran every time Python started, stealing secrets like SSH keys, cloud credentials, and API keys. The malware sent the stolen data to a fake server and tried to install itself for persistence. It also tried to spread to Kubernetes clusters by creating new pods, but this failed on the author’s Mac.

The attack spread because someone uploaded a new, fake version of litellm (1.82.8) directly to PyPI, not through the usual GitHub release. Anyone who installed or updated litellm could get infected. The malware caused a “fork bomb,” making endless Python processes, which slowed or crashed affected computers. The author quickly reported the issue to PyPI and the main litellm project, wrote a blog post, and shared the news on key subreddits to alert others. They also listed steps for people to clean their systems and rotate all secrets that might have been stolen.

In the Hacker News comments, many people thanked the author for acting so fast and sharing such a detailed report. Some developers were surprised at how easy it was for malware to spread through a single package update. Others worried about how common these supply chain attacks are becoming, especially as more projects depend on open source packages. A few said they now plan to pin package versions and audit dependencies more carefully. Some security experts in the thread discussed how .pth files can be abused, and suggested package managers and Python itself should add more warnings or checks for these files. There was also debate about how much AI tools helped or slowed down the investigation, and if package repositories like PyPI should require more security checks before allowing uploads. Finally, a few commenters shared tips for rotating secrets and recovering from such attacks, while others called for better security education for all developers, not just security experts.

## DOOM Over DNS

- 原文链接: [DOOM Over DNS](https://github.com/resumex/doom-over-dns)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47490705)

Someone made it possible to play DOOM by downloading all the game data from DNS TXT records, using just PowerShell and public DNS servers. The project splits the DOOM game files into nearly 2,000 pieces and stores them in DNS records on Cloudflare, then puts the game together in memory and runs it—without saving the game to disk.

Normally, DNS is used to match names to IP addresses, but here, DNS TXT records are used to store chunks of the game data. Cloudflare’s global system helps make the data fast to access. The project uses PowerShell to fetch each chunk from DNS and then runs the game engine and data directly in memory. There is no need to download a big file or install extra software, just PowerShell 7 or higher. To play, you run a script, and it grabs everything from DNS automatically. For uploading your own copy, you need to split the game files and upload them to your own DNS zones using Cloudflare’s API.

The project explains that free Cloudflare zones can only hold about 185 data chunks, so if you want to upload the full game, you need several domains unless you pay for a better Cloudflare plan. There are also ways to resume uploads if they get interrupted, and the game engine has been changed to work better in this setup. The code uses open-source tools, and there are no releases—just the source code on GitHub.

In the comments, many people say this is a clever and fun hack, showing how far you can push basic internet tools. Some think it’s a creative way to avoid network blocks, since DNS is often allowed when other traffic is blocked. Others point out that using DNS like this is not what it was meant for and could cause problems for DNS services if many people start doing it. A few remember other projects that used DNS for tunneling or data transfer, but agree that running a full game is unusual. Some wonder about the speed and stability, since DNS is slow and not made for big data. Others joke about how people keep finding new ways to play DOOM almost anywhere. A few worry that if projects like this become popular, DNS providers might block or restrict TXT records. Overall, most commenters are impressed by the technical skill and the fun spirit behind the project.

## Fermented foods shaped human biology

- 原文链接: [Fermented foods shaped human biology](https://press.asimov.com/articles/culture-shift)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47457407)

This article explains that fermented foods did not just come from human invention—our bodies actually changed over time because of them. It says eating fermented foods, like sauerkraut, changes our immune system and can lower inflammation, because special molecules in these foods talk directly to our cells.

Researchers found that a molecule called D-PLA from sauerkraut can trigger a unique receptor in the human immune system called HCA3, giving us health benefits. This HCA3 receptor is only found in humans and great apes, suggesting our bodies evolved alongside fermented foods. Fermentation is very old, maybe even older than cooking, and could have helped our ancestors survive by making food safer, easier to digest, and more nutritious. For example, fermentation lets us eat foods that would otherwise be toxic, like wild cassava, and may have helped early humans get enough calories to grow bigger brains.

The article also discusses how, in the last hundred years, Western diets lost many fermented foods when food safety laws pushed for more sterile, long-lasting products. But some scientists, like Ilya Mechnikoff, still promoted yogurt and fermented dairy as healthy. Modern research, including studies at Stanford, shows that eating more fermented foods increases gut bacteria diversity and lowers inflammation, but most people don’t eat enough to see these benefits.

Science still doesn’t fully understand exactly how fermented foods help us. Many studies only look at one food at a time, but new projects are trying to map out all the microbes and chemicals in hundreds of fermented foods to learn more. The article ends by saying we are just now learning the science behind what people have done for thousands of years—using fermentation to help our health.

In the comments, some people are excited, saying this shows how important traditional foods are and that we should eat more fermented things. Others are curious but skeptical, asking for more proof and saying that eating too much fermented food could also have risks, like for people with certain health problems. A few highlight that not all fermented foods are the same—some are full of healthy microbes, but others, like wine or chocolate, might not have the same benefits. Some users share stories about making their own fermented foods at home and feeling better, while others point out that modern diets are too focused on shelf life and profits, not health.

One commenter worries that focusing only on “good” microbes might ignore the complex balance in our guts. Others want more big studies before changing their diets. A few point out that in some cultures, fermented foods are still common and people seem healthier, but it’s hard to know if fermentation is the main reason. Some users discuss how hard it is to add more fermented foods to their daily life, while others hope that new science will make it easier to know which foods are best for gut health. Overall, the discussion is lively, with lots of interest and some healthy skepticism.

This article is about a free online book called “The Little Book of C.” It helps people learn C programming from the basics to more advanced topics.

The book starts with what C is and why learning it still matters. It explains that C is close to how computers work, so learning it teaches you about memory, how computers run programs, and why C is used in many important systems. You learn how to install a C compiler (like gcc or clang) on Linux, Mac, or Windows. There are step-by-step guides to write, compile, and run your first program, including the famous “Hello, world!” example. The book shows the structure of a C program, how to use headers and the preprocessor, and how the build process works (compiling, linking, and running). You get tips on reading and fixing common errors and warnings. There’s a section on basic command-line tools, like `ls`, `cat`, and `gcc`, which are useful for C developers. The book also explains how to organize your project files using folders and Makefiles, making bigger projects easier to manage.

In Chapter 2, you learn about C’s building blocks: data types (like int, float, char), variables, constants, and enums. The book covers operators (arithmetic, logical, bitwise), control flow with if/else and switch, and loops (for, while, do-while). You study how to write and use functions, understand variable scope and lifetime, and see how functions can return values. There’s an explanation of static and dynamic linking, and a practice project to build a simple calculator.

Chapter 3 starts talking about memory. You learn about how C programs use memory: code, data, stack, heap, and what each part does. The book uses small code examples to show how local variables, global variables, and dynamically allocated memory live in different places. There’s a focus on mastering pointers—variables that hold memory addresses—and how arrays and pointers are connected. You see how strings in C are really arrays of characters, and you get practice with dynamic memory functions like malloc, calloc, realloc, and free.

A few advanced programmers point out that while the book is good, it doesn’t cover every detail (like undefined behavior or tricky pointer bugs), but agree that it’s a solid place to start. Some commenters like the practice exercises, saying hands-on work is the best way to learn. Others hope the book will keep getting updates, since C itself changes over time. ## Show HN: Turbolite – a SQLite VFS serving sub-250ms cold JOIN queries from S3

- 原文链接: [Show HN: Turbolite – a SQLite VFS serving sub-250ms cold JOIN queries from S3](https://github.com/russellromney/turbolite)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47534283)

Turbolite is a new tool that lets SQLite databases live in S3 or similar storage, while still giving fast query times—even for JOINs when the data is not cached. The project is written in Rust and aims to make it easy to run many separate databases (like one per customer) in the cloud, without needing a disk volume for each.

Turbolite works by acting as a custom “VFS” (virtual file system) for SQLite. It fetches groups of database pages from S3, instead of reading one small page at a time, to make the most of each network call. Pages are stored in types: for example, important index pages are bundled, loaded, and cached first. This makes point lookups and JOINs faster, since the most-used data is ready right away. All data is compressed using zstd and can be encrypted with AES-256 for safety. The tool also tries to predict what data will be needed soon and prefetches it in the background. You can use Turbolite from Python, Node.js, Rust, or Go, and it works with any S3-compatible storage.

Performance numbers look good—point lookups and JOINs can be under 250ms even when nothing is cached, and big scans are faster if you have enough CPU threads. There’s a small delay on the very first query, but later requests are much quicker. Turbolite is best when you have many small to medium databases, and you only need one machine to write at a time (it does not support multiple writers). It’s also experimental, so some bugs may exist.

Commenters on Hacker News had mixed feelings. Many people are impressed by the speed and the clever design that deals with S3’s slow round-trips. Some users think this is perfect for apps with lots of customers, each with their own database, and for serverless setups where you want quick cold starts. Others worry about reliability, since the project warns about possible data loss and is still new. Some think the single-writer limit is a problem for teams wanting full database sharing. There are questions about how Turbolite compares to other projects, like Litestream or LiteFS, which focus more on backup or replication. A few readers ask about costs—since S3 charges per request, batching helps, but heavy workloads might add up. Some wonder if the tool could work with other cloud storage, or even for offline use, since it supports local mode too. Overall, the community sees Turbolite as an interesting step in bringing SQLite to the cloud, but they want to see more testing and real-world cases before trusting it for important systems.
