First, let’s talk about how AI is changing company organization, especially in tech. For 30 years, most software companies had a simple structure: a few leaders at the top decide the purpose ("why"), a middle group plans what to build ("what"), and a larger group at the bottom does the hands-on work ("how"). The middle layer was mainly about translating ideas into specs, tickets, and code. Many roles and processes were built around this translation.

Now, with AI agents doing much of this translation work faster and cheaper, the need for a big middle layer is shrinking. AI is not just taking away job titles, but replacing the whole translation-type work common for project managers, engineering managers, and even some engineers. The hardest and most creative work—deciding "why" and "what"—is now more important, while AI handles the easier "how" tasks.

Managers who only coordinate, without adding real value, are at risk. Some are holding onto old habits, while others are moving back to hands-on work like design or coding. In the new setup, there are fewer people overall. The group making key decisions about what to build is larger, while the group doing technical work is smaller but focuses on the most complex systems. AI agents do most of the repetitive tasks.

Being "hands-on" now means making big decisions and working close to the real problems, not just running meetings. Companies will hire fewer people, and roles will change. Engineers should focus on work that AI cannot do, like defining quality or solving tough problems.

In the comments, reactions are mixed. Some agree that AI will shrink the middle, making teams smaller and more focused. Others worry that removing too many coordinators could cause chaos, as someone still needs to keep things together. Not all translation work can be automated, especially in unclear or messy cases. "Why" and "what" work is hard to scale and needs human judgment. Some are worried about job losses or big changes, and wonder how companies will help people adapt. One person thinks work will be more interesting for those who stay, but harder for those who liked routine tasks. Another warns that AI can speed up damage from bad decisions at the top. Overall, many agree that teams will look very different soon, and everyone will need to learn new skills or become more flexible.

Next, let’s look at Apertus, a new open AI model from Swiss universities and tech partners. Apertus gives everyone full access to its data, code, and methods, aiming for full transparency. It is not just open-source code—it shares its training data, weights, and how it aligns with ethical rules. Anyone can see and check every step of how it was made. The team also works hard to follow EU AI rules—removing private information and respecting people’s wishes about data.

Apertus comes in two sizes, with 8 billion and 70 billion parameters, and is trained in over 1000 languages. The main goal is to help others build "sovereign AI," letting countries or groups run their own models without depending on big tech. The team promises to keep the community updated.

In comments, people are curious but cautious. Some are excited for transparency and trust. Others wonder if truly open models can compete with private giants like OpenAI or Google. There’s talk about making sure all parts are open and reproducible, not just code. Some question if the model can really protect personal data, and how well it works in so many languages. Many hope this leads to more open AI work in Europe, but some worry about misuse of open models. Overall, people welcome the idea but say it needs careful watching as it grows.

Switching topics, we have an article about using JSON-LD to add structured data to your personal website. JSON-LD helps search engines and web crawlers understand your site better, which could improve your search results and previews. You add a special script in your site’s head, using JSON format to describe your site’s info, following schema.org rules. The article gives ready-to-use code and tips for adding nodes like WebSite, Person, Blog, and more. Linking nodes and using unique IDs is important for best results.

In the comments, many are skeptical about the real benefits of JSON-LD today. Some say Google uses this data to train AI models, then shows answers above your site, so tags may not help traffic. Others remember adding microdata in the past and only saw Google use it to keep users on the search page. Still, some think structured data is useful, especially for appearing as sources in LLMs or for business sites. There’s debate between using semantic HTML versus JSON-LD; many agree they serve different goals. Some prefer older formats like RDFa, but most say JSON-LD is easiest now. The advice is to add the most important metadata and not overdo it.

Now, let’s talk about a math article that says many things in math are like logarithms, even if we don’t always notice. The author shows how ideas from vectors, derivatives, and dimensions can all be connected by thinking about logarithms. For example, a "baseless" logarithm is like an abstract object, only turning into a number when you compare two of them—similar to how vectors need a reference. Changing the base of a log is like changing the basis of a vector. The article also links logarithms to derivatives and dimension functions.

Comments are few, but show both excitement and confusion. Some like seeing math ideas linked together, while others want more concrete examples. One person hopes this could help teaching math. Others say treating everything as a logarithm might not always help, but looking for patterns across math is a good way to find new ideas.

Moving on, we have a story about why copying code is sometimes better than making the wrong abstraction. The author says trying to remove duplicate code too early can lead to bad abstractions that do not fit future needs. Over time, shared code can fill up with special cases and become hard to maintain. When this happens, it’s often best to "go backward": bring code back to each place, remove extra parts, and keep things simple. This lets you see what is really the same and what is different, making it easier to find a better abstraction later.

In comments, many agree but say you need to balance. "Single source of truth" is important, but too much abstraction can cause problems. Some say wait until code is repeated three times before sharing it. Modern tools and AI might help with duplication, but could also make it worse. The main lesson is: do not rush into abstractions. Wait until you understand the problem, and be ready to undo a bad abstraction if needed.

Next, we have a tool called Recall, designed to help you save and summarize your Claude Code sessions locally. Recall works fully offline, never sends data out, and keeps your information private. It solves the problem of "cold starts" in Claude Code, where every new session forgets your project context. Recall keeps a history and a summary on your own computer, using simple algorithms and no large models or APIs. You control the tool with simple commands, and can adjust settings as needed. Recall scrubs secrets from logs, keeps data in your project folder, and lets you share team memory if you want.

In comments, people like the idea of saving time and tokens by not repeating themselves between AI sessions. Some wonder about security and if it works with other tools. Others suggest adding encryption. Many like the offline, private design, and want to try it for solo or small-team coding.

A few defend Anthropic, saying they must follow government rules. Some warn that ID checks could be used for tracking or censorship, or could lead to data leaks. Others think most users will accept the change, but hope for more competition. Some doubt this will really block bad actors.

Next, let’s talk about Beyond All Reason (BAR), a free and open-source real-time strategy game inspired by Total Annihilation. BAR features huge battles with thousands of units, deep strategy, and supports Windows and Linux. You can play against people online, fight AI, or try co-op and single-player modes. There are three main factions, each with their own style. The game is moving to Steam with a publisher, but the core will stay free and open.

Hacker News users praise the game’s technical side and how it feels like a true successor to classic RTS games. Many enjoy the big battles and detailed controls. Some say it’s harder than old RTS games but more rewarding. Others like the co-op and single-player content. Many comments focus on the multiplayer community—some say the main lobbies can be toxic, especially for new players, while others say smaller games and co-op are friendly. The BAR community manager says they take reports seriously and recommend relaxed modes for new players. Some feel the focus on "meta" strategies makes games less creative, but others say this is normal for competitive games. There are also debates about the move to Steam and about paid content, but developers say the core game will stay free and open source.

Now, an article from Peter Norvig shows how to write a simple interpreter for the Lisp language Scheme, using Python. Norvig explains Scheme’s simple syntax and shows how to build a small Scheme-like language called Lispy. He guides you through breaking code into tokens, parsing it, and evaluating it in Python. He adds features step by step, including variable environments, quoting, assignment, and lambdas. He shows how even a small interpreter can do useful things like defining functions and recursion.

In the comments, people say building a Lisp interpreter is a valuable and eye-opening project. Some recommend other resources like Make-A-Lisp. There’s discussion about Lisp’s parentheses—some dislike them, but others enjoy the simplicity. Many agree that building an interpreter is a great way to learn, even if AI can now do it for you. There’s nostalgia for when programming was more about learning and fun. People share their own Lisp projects and say the learning process is rewarding.

Last, we have a story about the choice between buying software or building your own, now that AI can help build tools faster and cheaper. The author gives an example: replacing Jira (at $400/month) with a home-built tool using AI. But even with AI, building and maintaining your own tool takes time and work. If an engineer earns $200,000 a year, spending more than four hours a month maintaining a Jira clone already costs more than just paying for Jira. For very expensive tools, building your own might make sense, but for most, buying is better. The author calls this the "zone of viability"—when software is priced right, it is better to buy.

In comments, people agree that even with AI, building is still hard and takes motivation. AI can help code faster, but making good, easy-to-use software is still a challenge. Changing needs mean code often needs to be rewritten. Buying is usually safer for companies, but some warn that building big tools like Jira or Google Docs is harder than it looks. Others say company rules can slow down buying, making small purchases harder to approve. Some developers prefer building for fun or control, but with experience, many prefer to buy and save time. The core value is in software that helps the business make money, not just saves it. Community-built software can be great, but can also lack focus. Most agree that the build vs. buy decision is still tough, and AI only moves the line a little.
