First, let’s talk about Ada 2022, the latest version of the Ada programming language standard, now released as ISO/IEC 8652:2023. The Ada Resource Association has published the full reference manual, an overview of new features, and even an annotated manual with extra notes for compiler writers and testers. These documents are free to download in PDF, HTML, or text formats. The site also has links to older Ada versions, free tools, libraries, and training resources. Ada is well known for its strong focus on safety and reliability, and is often used in critical fields like aerospace and defense.

On Hacker News, many people were happy to see Ada still moving forward. Some praised its power for building safe systems, and shared stories of Ada’s use in important projects. Others wished Ada was more popular or taught in schools, saying its strict design helps people write better code. However, some users felt Ada is hard to learn, or not flexible enough for fast, small projects. A few said Ada’s strictness and style feel slow compared to new languages, but others think these rules are what make Ada special. There were also positive comments about Ada’s support for multi-core programming and the clear, well-organized documentation. Some people wondered if Ada will get more attention outside its usual fields, but most agree that it’s good to see the language still improving.

Next, we have a serious story about airport security and the TSA. A woman who had just had cancer surgery was made to go through a body scanner at a US airport, even though she told the TSA it was not safe for her. She asked for a pat-down, which is allowed by TSA rules for people with medical needs, but the workers did not listen and forced her through the scanner. Afterward, she suffered pain and needed more surgery. The article points out that this is not the first time the TSA has failed to follow its own policies, and many people are afraid to fly because of such mistakes. The woman is now asking for answers and wants the TSA to change.

The comments were full of anger and worry. Many called this abuse and shared their own bad airport stories. Some said TSA workers are not well trained, while others believe the scanners are not safe for anyone. People agree that airport safety is important, but rules must be followed, and the TSA often seems rude and uncaring. A few defended the TSA, saying the job is hard, but most people felt sad for the woman and hope for change.

Now, let’s look at the US tech job market. Last month, 12,000 tech jobs were lost, and over the past year, 57,000 jobs disappeared. This drop is now worse than the 2008 or 2020 recessions, and almost as bad as the big downturn in 2024. Many companies are cutting staff or freezing hiring, and growth has slowed because of weak demand, high interest rates, and fears about jobs moving overseas. Some say the tech industry is just maturing, while others blame the economy.

Hacker News users are worried. Many say it is now harder than ever to find a tech job, even for skilled people. Some think pay is still high, but others believe companies simply hired too many people before and are now correcting this. There is talk about AI making some jobs less needed, and how remote work lets companies hire from cheaper places. Some people remember past downturns and hope things will improve, but most agree it’s a tough time right now.

Let’s move on to a project from the gaming world. Moongate v2 is a new Ultima Online server emulator, made in C# using .NET 10. This project is built from scratch, with a modular structure, clear code, and support for Lua scripting, which allows easy custom actions and events. The server uses AOT compilation for better speed, chunk-based world loading for efficiency, and MessagePack for fast, small saves. There’s a built-in email system, good test coverage, Docker support, and tools for easy hosting and monitoring.

On Hacker News, many users felt nostalgic about Ultima Online and liked seeing new projects for such an old game. People praised the modern technical choices like Lua scripting and chunk-based loading. There were questions about compatibility with the original game client, legal risks, and how hard it is to support all game features. Some users shared their own stories about running UO servers, while others talked about the project’s clean code and good documentation. Many showed interest in helping out, and some hope that Moongate can help keep the old MMO spirit alive.

In AI news, Anthropic worked with Mozilla to use their AI, Claude, to find security bugs in Firefox. In just two weeks, Claude found 22 bugs, 14 of which were high severity—almost a fifth of all serious bugs fixed that year. Claude was tested on both old and new Firefox code and even found a tricky bug in the JavaScript engine in only twenty minutes. Most of the bugs were quickly fixed by Mozilla.

Although Claude is good at finding bugs, it was less successful at creating working attacks, or exploits. Out of hundreds of tries, it made only two simple exploits, and only in special test setups. Anthropic shared tips for using AI in security, such as using tools to check if AI bug fixes really work, and sending clear, reproducible steps in bug reports. They warn that AI will soon get better at making real attacks, so defenders have a short window to fix software before attackers catch up.

On Hacker News, people were both impressed and worried. Many liked seeing AI help with defense, but others feared it could soon help attackers. Some worried AI would flood teams with too many bug reports, including false alarms, and said human experts are still needed. Others discussed if open source projects can handle so many bug reports. Some users liked Anthropic’s idea of sharing clear steps with each report, and hope AI will help with fixing bugs next. Most agree that AI will be a key tool for both making and breaking software security in the future.

Next, let’s talk about another big business deal. Warner Bros. Discovery (WBD) is being bought, this time by Paramount’s Skydance, backed by the Ellison family. The article says this deal will likely fail, just like so many past media mergers. The history includes the failed Time Warner and AOL merger, where AOL’s value crashed, and AT&T’s failed buy of Time Warner, which also lost billions. Now, WBD and Paramount have $79 billion in debt and most of their money comes from old-style TV, which is shrinking.

The article warns that promised savings will likely mean layoffs, and that selling off assets or combining streaming services may not help much. Compared to Disney, which has theme parks and strong brands, WBD is in a weak position. The advice to TV news stars is to start their own shows online, as old media jobs are not safe.

Hacker News users agree that most media mergers fail, usually hurting workers more than helping. Some say these deals mainly help bankers and CEOs, not regular people. There is talk about how streaming has changed the business, and if AI or big data can save old media. Some give credit to Netflix for avoiding the deal and getting a breakup fee. Many users fear that if big companies keep merging, viewers and workers will have fewer choices. They remember past failed deals and see history repeating itself.

Let’s look at an open source tool for Android users. Open Camera is a free, open source camera app that gives more control than most default camera apps. It supports manual controls, timers, noise-activated photos, grids, crop guides, HDR, panorama, RAW files, and privacy features like removing metadata. The app needs Android 5.0 or higher, and is free with no ads. The source code is open for anyone to check or improve.

On Hacker News, many people praise Open Camera for being free, open, and not tracking users. Some use it on old or cheap phones where the default camera is weak, and many like its privacy features. A few wish for more manual controls or a simpler interface, and some report small bugs on certain phones, but most agree it is a powerful and reliable app. Users say good open source Android apps are rare and hope more developers will help projects like this. Some wonder if similar apps exist for iOS, but most agree Android is more open for this kind of tool.

Apache Otava is a new project that just joined the Apache Software Foundation as an incubating project. Incubation is a process where new projects learn to follow Apache’s ways and improve their infrastructure, but the code is not yet considered stable or fully supported. The project is open source under the Apache License.

On Hacker News, people were curious about what Otava actually does, since the announcement gave no details. Some guessed it might be related to programming languages or other Apache projects. There was discussion about the value of the Apache incubation process, and some users were hopeful the project would bring new ideas. Others were cautious, saying many incubating projects never become fully supported. Overall, people want to know more about what Otava is and what problem it solves.

Palus Finance is a new company helping startups and small businesses make better use of their unused cash. The service lets companies put extra money into short-term U.S. Treasury bills, which are safe and give higher returns than normal bank accounts. The system connects directly with business bank accounts and makes it easy to move money in and out, so cash is still available for bills when needed. Palus charges a small fee, but says the extra earnings are much higher. The service is starting in the U.S. and may expand later.

On Hacker News, many people liked the idea, saying it makes sense for startups. Some were surprised banks do not offer this directly. There were questions about risks, fees, and the safety of connecting bank accounts to new software. Some pointed out that large companies already have treasury managers, but small startups do not, so Palus fills a real need. Many agree that making idle cash work harder is smart, as long as safety and easy access are not a problem.

Finally, we have a bit of fun with Payphone Go, a game where people hunt for working payphones in California and use them to score points. Players find payphones using an online map, call a special number, and check in for points. The first person to use a payphone gets the most points. The game uses payphone numbers to track calls, and the creator updates the list if mistakes are found. Payphone Go is meant to celebrate and preserve memories of payphones, which are becoming rare.

Hacker News users found the idea creative and nostalgic. Some shared childhood stories about payphones, while others liked the “real-life video game” feel of the point system. There were questions about how many payphones are left, and some discussed the technical details of matching phone numbers. Many praised the project as a mix of nostalgia, hacking, and adventure, and some hope for versions in other places.
