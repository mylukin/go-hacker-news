## Noise infusion banned from statistical products published by Census Bureau

- 原文链接: [Noise infusion banned from statistical products published by Census Bureau](https://desfontain.es/blog/banning-noise.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48517377)

The article talks about a new rule: the U.S. Census Bureau cannot use "noise infusion" (adding randomness) to protect privacy in its published data. The writer explains why this is a big problem for both privacy and usefulness of census data.

In the past, the Census used different tricks to hide private data, like suppression (not showing small counts), coarsening (making data less precise), swapping (mixing up data), and adding noise (random numbers). From 1990 to 2010, they mainly used swapping, but it turned out people could still figure out private info. So, for the 2020 Census, they picked "differential privacy," which uses carefully added noise to make sure data is useful but private. This method was not perfect—numbers became less exact, and some users got upset because they had to work with noisy data. Still, it was better than other options.

Now, with noise banned, the Census can only use tools like coarsening and suppression. These are not good for big, detailed datasets like the Census. If you use them too much, data becomes almost useless, especially for small or minority groups. If you don't use them enough, privacy is at risk. The article says that banning noise could mean future Census data is either very unsafe or not helpful at all. Also, almost every privacy method (even sampling and imputation) adds some randomness, so this rule might break more than expected.

The writer wonders why this rule was made. Maybe it's to help with political tricks, like gerrymandering, or maybe to hide unfair differences in the population. Or maybe people just didn't like dealing with the problem of privacy versus usefulness and hoped banning noise would make it go away.

In the comment section, many people worry that banning noise will make it easier to find private info in Census data. Some say this change could help bad actors, like those trying to gerrymander voting districts, because they can use more exact data. Others think the rule will force the Census to publish very weak data, making it hard for researchers and planners to do their work. A few point out that almost all statistical work involves some form of randomness or noise, so this rule is confusing and hard to enforce. Some comments say the real issue is not privacy, but politics, and that the rule may have been pushed for political reasons. Others are sad that important privacy research, like differential privacy, is being ignored. A handful of people say that working with noisy data is hard, but it is better than putting people's information at risk. Overall, most commenters agree this ban is a step backward for both privacy and good data.

This story is about a person who wanted to run DOS on a Behringer DDX3216 audio mixer, which uses a real 386 processor. The writer could not find a working BIOS for the machine, so he decided to write his own x86 BIOS from the beginning.

First, he studied the hardware: the AMD Elan SC300 CPU, RAM, ROM, LCD, UART, and other chips. He learned how the x86 boot process works, starting from the reset vector at memory address 0xFFF0. He wrote assembly code so the CPU could start running, and created a linker script to put this code in the right place in ROM. He used a Raspberry Pi Pico to emulate the ROM chip, which made testing much faster than using real chips.

Next, he got the serial port working for debugging, which meant learning about the external UART chip and how to turn on the right signals. Then, he worked on getting the LCD up and running. He learned about x86 memory segments and had to write special code to write to the video memory. The display needed a font, so he made a full 8x8 pixel ASCII font with the help of AI.

He added support for more hardware: setting up the interrupt vector table and BIOS data area, initializing the timer and keyboard, and working with the CF card slot. Accessing the CF card was tricky because of memory and mode problems, but he solved them by changing the card mode and using ATA commands.

To boot DOS, he had to implement many BIOS interrupts (like disk, video, keyboard, timer) because DOS uses these to talk to hardware. He tried to boot MS-DOS 6.22 but ran into problems—DOS would start but then get stuck. He then tried FreeDOS 1.4 and it worked! He could boot into FreeDOS on the audio mixer.

He also explored controlling the mixer’s LEDs by writing to special addresses. He learned about shift registers and how to turn LEDs on and off in patterns.

## Appreciating Exif

- 原文链接: [Appreciating Exif](https://brentfitzgerald.com/posts/appreciating-exif/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48467437)

This article is about Exif, which is the metadata inside image files, like photos from your phone or camera. The writer explains where Exif lives in files, what it stores, and why it matters for developers working with images.

Exif (Exchangeable Image File Format) stores details such as when a photo was taken, camera settings, GPS, and image orientation. In JPEG files, Exif sits in a part called APP1 and starts with a marker showing it is Exif data. The data is shaped like an old TIFF file, which is a type of image file from before digital cameras. Exif is optional—some images have it, some do not, and it can be fake or stripped out during editing. The most common Exif tags are date, camera make, lens type, exposure, GPS, and especially orientation.

Orientation is important because cameras do not always rotate the pixels; they just set the orientation tag, and viewers rotate the picture for you. There are eight possible orientation values, from normal to mirrored or rotated. If you are editing images, you should fix the orientation and then clear the tag. Tools like exiftool help read, change, or remove Exif data. Exiftool is very complete and shows all tags, including camera-specific and GPS data. Stripping Exif does not remove all metadata—other data like color profiles or XMP can remain.

Exif is not the only metadata format. There is also XMP, IPTC, ICC color profiles, and new standards like C2PA for content signing. Each file format (JPEG, PNG, WebP, HEIC, etc.) may store Exif or other metadata differently. When you upload or download images, do not trust that metadata is removed unless you check or strip it yourself. For developers, there are many libraries in different languages to handle Exif, but for simple needs, you can just read tags yourself.

In the comments, many people say exiftool is their go-to tool for any metadata work. Some share stories where wrong Exif data caused rotated or upside-down images in apps and websites. A few users warn that Exif can leak private data, like location, if not removed before sharing images. Others mention using Exif to find out how a good photo was taken, or to sort thousands of photos by date. Some point out that not all viewers or browsers respect orientation tags, which leads to confusion. A few commenters discuss how metadata can be faked or misused, so it should not be fully trusted. People also debate how much metadata should be kept or stripped when processing images. There are questions about support for Exif in new image formats and how some apps handle metadata badly. One person likes that Exif is boring but works well for most needs. Others wish metadata standards were simpler or more secure. Finally, some share “Exif tricks,” like hiding jokes in fields, or embedding fake camera names for fun.

## The adder at the heart of Intel's 8087 floating-point chip

- 原文链接: [The adder at the heart of Intel's 8087 floating-point chip](https://www.righto.com/2026/06/intel-8087-adder-reverse-engineered.html)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48519011)

This article is about how Intel made the fast math adder inside the 8087 floating-point chip, released in 1980. The adder helped the 8087 do hard math operations, like square roots and logarithms, much faster than before.

The adder is 69 bits wide and is the main part of the chip’s math engine. The chip splits each floating-point number into two parts: exponent and fraction. The adder works on the fraction part, and if it needs to add exponents, it copies them over. To make the adder fast, Intel broke it into 4-bit blocks. The main problem is the “carry” that happens when two numbers are added; normally, carries move slowly from one bit to the next. Intel used a trick called the Manchester carry chain, which lets the carry move very quickly through the adder, almost as fast as electricity in a wire.

The Manchester carry chain uses three ideas: Generate (a carry is created), Propagate (a carry moves through), and Delete (a carry is stopped). These can be calculated at the same time for all bits. Each 4-bit block in the adder checks if all bits will let the carry pass through; if yes, it uses a “carry-skip” circuit to move the carry quickly to the next block. This design made the adder much faster, but it still took two clock cycles to finish one addition.

The article also explains how the adder was built with NMOS transistors, which pull signals low quickly but are slow to pull them high. So, before each addition, the carry lines are “precharged” high and then pulled low as needed. The adder also handles special cases for rounding and negative numbers, making it slightly larger than the 64 bits needed for normal math. The logic is packed tightly on the silicon chip, with special circuits for multiplication, division, and square roots, all using the adder over and over.

In the comments, many people were impressed by how much Intel could do with so few transistors and clever tricks. Some said the Manchester carry chain is simple but very smart for its time. Others pointed out that newer chips use even faster adders, like the Kogge-Stone adder, but those need a lot more hardware. A few readers enjoyed the detailed chip photos and how the author took apart the chip layer by layer. Some asked technical questions about NMOS design and why Intel made certain choices, like precharging signals or using carry-skip. Others remembered using the 8087 and said it was a big step forward for math in computers. A few people debated if the chip’s design could be improved today, and some shared links to other articles about old chip designs. Overall, readers praised the deep technical look and clear explanations, saying it helped them understand how math works inside a computer chip.
