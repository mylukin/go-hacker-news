Hello everyone, this is the 2026-03-02 episode of Hacker News Daily Podcast. Today, we have news about secure phones from Motorola, Apple’s new iPad Air, a special stem cell therapy, the progress of LFortran, a new tool for better government transparency, British Columbia changing its time rules, a story from Switzerland’s streets, a creative Linux tool for music keyboards, and a filter for cleaning up Instagram.

First, at Mobile World Congress, Motorola announced a partnership with GrapheneOS Foundation to make their phones more secure. GrapheneOS is known for a very private version of Android. Together, they plan to build new devices, software, and security features, mixing Motorola’s safety knowledge with GrapheneOS privacy and Lenovo’s ThinkShield system. They promised more updates soon.

Motorola also introduced Moto Analytics, a tool for companies to check phone health in real time, like app crashes and battery life. It helps IT teams fix problems fast and keeps workers happy. Another feature, Private Image Data, can remove private info from new photos, like location or device details. This runs in the background and will be part of the Moto Secure app.

On Hacker News, people are excited Motorola is working with GrapheneOS and hope it will help more users care about privacy. Some wonder if Motorola will let everyone install GrapheneOS, or just on special phones. Others worry about Motorola adding bloatware or tracking, and hope GrapheneOS can stay strong. There are also questions about long-term updates, since Motorola has dropped support early in the past. Most agree this could push other brands to care more about privacy, but remind us to watch for the details.

Next, let’s talk about Apple. They just launched a new iPad Air with the M4 chip. The tablet comes in two sizes, adds more memory, and has strong AI and wireless features. The M4 chip is much faster than before, with a better CPU and GPU, and supports ray tracing for improved games and graphics. It now has 12GB of memory and a faster Neural Engine for AI tasks. New networking chips bring Wi-Fi 7 and faster 5G, and iPadOS 26 brings a new design and better multitasking. There’s support for the latest Apple Pencil and Magic Keyboard, and the starting price is $599 for 11-inch, $799 for 13-inch.

People in the comments like the speed and new multitasking, but some wish the iPad could run macOS or better desktop apps. There are jokes about the high price of accessories, and some feel the upgrade is only big if you use AI features. Others are happy about faster wireless, but worry about Apple making features only for the newest models and keeping repair rules strict. Overall, the new iPad Air gets mixed reviews: fast hardware, but still some limits.

Staying with Apple, there’s a popular article about problems with macOS. A long-time user says Apple’s software is getting worse, with bugs in Time Machine, Finder, Spotlight, and more. The writer says Apple spends too much time on new looks and not enough on fixing old problems. Many people agree in the comments, sharing stories about bugs and missing features, like the loss of Rosetta 2. Some defend Apple, saying software is hard, but most hope Apple will fix the basics and listen to long-time users. Some even wish they could run other systems on the good Mac hardware.

Now, some good news from medicine. A new study shows that stem cell therapy during fetal surgery for spina bifida is safe. Doctors placed a patch with stem cells from donated placentas over the baby’s exposed spinal cord inside the womb. All six babies in the study did well, with no major problems, and early signs of healing. The FDA will let the study continue with more patients, and doctors will follow the children to see if they have better movement as they grow.

Comments are hopeful, with many amazed by the progress. Some remind us this is just the first phase and we need to watch long-term results. People ask about cost and access, and praise the use of placenta stem cells. There are questions about how the cells help healing, and some share personal stories of hope. Most agree this is a big step, but it’s too early to celebrate fully.

Next, a new website called Govbase helps people track government bills from their full text, through news stories, to social media discussions. It shows how bills change and how the story is told by different sources, making it easier to check facts and spot bias.

Comments show a lot of interest. Some like how Govbase could fight misinformation and help people make up their own minds. Others worry about keeping the site updated and ask if it works outside the US. There are questions about privacy and what happens if the site labels news as biased. Most agree it’s a good idea, as long as it stays fair and up-to-date.

Now to British Columbia, which will stop changing clocks twice a year and use daylight saving time all year. The province is waiting for nearby US states to do the same, to keep business and travel easy. Most people want the change, hoping for less confusion and better health. Some worry about dark mornings in winter. Comments mostly support the move, though some say they’ll miss the tradition or prefer standard time all year.

We also have a story from Basel, Switzerland, about a man named Ortiz who lives near a drug house. He and his friends watch the strange people—called Svenoids—on the street, sometimes yelling or throwing things from their balcony. The story is full of weird scenes, fights, and odd people, but ends with Ortiz making a friendly connection with one of the Svenoids, showing hope even in a tough place.

Commenters find the story both real and dreamlike, enjoying the writing and the mix of fear, humor, and kindness. Some compare it to their own cities, and most like how it shows real life without too much judgment. Many see hope in the ending, with people helping each other even in hard times.

For something technical, Pianoterm is a new tool that lets you use a USB MIDI piano keyboard to run shell commands on Linux. You can assign any command to any key, making the piano act like a giant custom keyboard. Users can control music, scripts, or even smart home devices. Set up is simple—just edit a config file. Comments are excited, with ideas for live coding, accessibility, and fun uses. Some warn about pressing the wrong key by accident, and a few ask about Mac or Windows support. Overall, people like the creativity, even if it’s a bit strange.

LFortran, a modern Fortran compiler, can now build and run fpm, the Fortran Package Manager, which is a complex project. The team added support for many modern Fortran features, fixed bugs, and now runs all tests automatically. LFortran is faster than gfortran on some tasks and is close to its first beta. Comments are positive—many are happy to see Fortran getting better tools and remember when it was seen as old. There are requests for more features and better integration, but most cheer for the LFortran team and say they might try it soon.

Finally, someone made a uBlock Origin filter list that hides Instagram Reels, videos, and posts from people you do not follow. The goal is to see only posts from friends. The filter is simple but will also hide some good videos and may break if Instagram changes its code. Comments are mixed: some like more control, others find it sad that a filter is needed, and some want even better tools. A few warn that filters can make sites harder to use, but most are glad to have options.
