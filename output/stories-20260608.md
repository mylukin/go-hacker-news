## MiMo-v2.5-Pro-UltraSpeed: 1T model with 1000 tokens per second

- 原文链接: [MiMo-v2.5-Pro-UltraSpeed: 1T model with 1000 tokens per second](https://mimo.xiaomi.com/blog/mimo-tilert-1000tps)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48446639)

Xiaomi has launched MiMo-V2.5-Pro-UltraSpeed, an AI model with 1 trillion parameters that can generate up to 1,000 tokens per second, which is a new record for models of this size. This makes responses nearly instant, helping users in real time for tasks like coding, decision-making, and even medical situations.

The article says that speed is now a key part of what makes an AI smart. With this new speed, the model can try many answers at once, check its own work, and improve its reasoning in real time. For developers, this means AIs that write code much faster—no more waiting for slow output. In areas where timing is critical, like trading or medical analysis, the AI can now make decisions almost instantly, which could help save lives.

To achieve this, Xiaomi and TileRT worked together closely. Instead of using special hardware, they used regular GPUs but improved both the model and the system. They used FP4 quantization to make the model smaller without losing much accuracy, and a new decoding method called DFlash to guess many tokens at once, making everything faster. The TileRT system also runs the code in a way that removes delays, so the GPU is always working as efficiently as possible.

MiMo-V2.5-Pro-UltraSpeed will be available through a limited-time API trial, with applications open mainly to companies and developers who have real business needs. The trial is only for two weeks, and only approved users will get access. Xiaomi also open-sourced parts of their model on HuggingFace, so the community can use and test it.

In the Hacker News comments, some users are impressed with the technical achievement, saying that reaching 1,000 tokens per second on a trillion-parameter model is amazing—especially using regular GPUs. Others ask about the real-world value of such high speed, wondering if most users actually need this. There are a few concerns about the higher cost (three times regular MiMo), and some wish the trial was open to more people, not just businesses.

A few developers discuss technical details, like how FP4 quantization and the DFlash method might affect output quality, especially for complex tasks like code or math. Some praise Xiaomi for open-sourcing the model, saying it’s good for the community and research. Others hope the technology will be available outside China and become more open in the future.

Some users point out that very high speeds could bring new uses, like running many AI agents at once, or using AI in real-time games. But some are cautious, saying that speed is not everything—quality, safety, and cost still matter. Overall, there is a mix of excitement about the speed breakthrough and questions about how it will change real-world applications.

## Why are cells small?

- 原文链接: [Why are cells small?](https://burrito.bio/essays/what-limits-a-cells-size)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48450065)

This article looks at why cells are so small and what keeps them from getting bigger. It explains that the size of a cell is not random—there are strong reasons for it, based on both biology and physics.

Most cells need to be small to work well. Small cells have a large surface area compared to their volume, which helps them get nutrients and remove waste quickly. If a cell gets too big, its inside grows faster than its outside. This means it cannot take in food or get rid of waste fast enough for its size.

The article talks about diffusion, which means how molecules move around inside the cell. In a small cell, molecules quickly bump into each other and react. In a big cell, it takes much longer for molecules to find each other, so important cell processes slow down.

Red blood cells are an example. They are small and shaped like a donut, giving them extra surface area to carry more oxygen. Oocytes, or egg cells, are much bigger because they store food and do not need to work as fast as other cells.

Some cells get around these rules in special ways. The giant bacterium Thiomargarita magnifica is very big, but it fills most of its space with an empty area, pushing its active parts close to the edge. This way, things inside still move quickly enough.

Cells are not always the same size, even in one kind of animal. Sometimes they grow bigger when they need to make more proteins or split into two new cells. But physics still sets strong limits.

In the comment section, many people liked the clear explanation of surface area and volume. Some said the math was easy to follow, and it helped them remember what they learned in school. Others pointed out that some cells, like neurons or muscle cells, can be very long, but they are still thin to keep a good surface area for their size.

A few commenters talked about exceptions, like the giant bacteria and special plant cells, showing that life sometimes finds clever ways around the usual rules. Some people wondered how viruses and very small cells work, and others asked about the limits for artificial cells or nano-machines.

One person said they enjoyed the historical quote from D’Arcy Thompson, and another added that cell size is also shaped by evolution and the needs of each organism. Someone else linked to more studies about how cells move things around using “active transport,” not just diffusion.

There were also questions about how big or small a cell could get if we change the laws of physics, like in science fiction stories. Most agreed that, in our world, the rules of physics are very strong, and they shape almost everything about cell size.

## EU-banned pesticides found in rice, tea and spices

- 原文链接: [EU-banned pesticides found in rice, tea and spices](https://www.foodwatch.org/en/eu-banned-pesticides-found-in-rice-tea-and-spices)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48447062)

A new report shows that many foods in Europe, like rice, tea, and spices, have pesticides in them that are banned in the EU. The tests looked at products from the Netherlands, France, Austria, and Germany, and found that 45 out of 64 products had these banned pesticides.

The article explains that even though some pesticides are not allowed in the EU, European countries can still sell them to other countries. Sometimes, food grown with these pesticides is then shipped back to Europe, so people end up eating the same chemicals that were banned. This is called the “toxic pesticides boomerang.”

The lab tests found that all paprika powder, chili, and cumin samples had banned pesticides. One paprika sample had 22 different pesticides, and 6 of them are not allowed in the EU. Some foods even had more pesticide than the legal limit, so they should not be sold in stores. The most common banned chemicals found were Chlorfenapyr, Bifenthrin, Spirotetramat, Clothianidin, Thiametoxam, Imidacloprid, and Isoprothiolane.

The article warns that the EU government in Brussels wants to change food safety rules quickly, which could make things worse. They want to pass many new laws at once, making it easier for unsafe food to enter the market. The article asks people to support food safety and tell lawmakers to stop these rule changes.

In the comments, some people are shocked and worried about food safety in Europe. They say it is not fair that banned chemicals can come back in imported food. Others think the real problem is weak checks at the borders and poor enforcement of rules. A few commenters argue that banning pesticides in one place does not work if they are still used elsewhere.

Some users ask if organic foods are safer, but others warn that even organic products can sometimes have pesticide residues. There are also comments saying that food safety rules should be the same everywhere, so this “boomerang” problem stops. A few people think that banning so many pesticides will make food more expensive or harder to grow, and they want to see more studies on the real health risks.

Others discuss how companies move production to countries with weaker laws, then sell the food back to Europe. Some users think the EU should ban imports with these chemicals, but others point out this could start trade problems. There are also calls for better labeling, so shoppers know what’s in their food. A few comments say that the EU needs to balance safety with making sure people can still get enough food.

## Apple Core AI Framework

- 原文链接: [Apple Core AI Framework](https://developer.apple.com/documentation/coreai/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48449665)

Apple just announced Core AI, a new framework for running AI models directly on Apple devices with Apple silicon. This tool helps developers add smart AI features to their apps, using the CPU, GPU, or Neural Engine for fast performance.

Core AI is built for Swift and focuses on making on-device AI easy but powerful. Developers can prepare their models with Core AI Optimization, convert them with Core AI PyTorch Extensions, and debug or inspect them using the Core AI Debugger app. The framework supports running new model types and lets developers control how models are specialized, cached, and compiled for each device. There are tools to monitor and profile AI performance in Xcode, and models can be compiled ahead of time to speed up app startup. Core AI works across all Apple platforms, including iOS, macOS, watchOS, tvOS, and more. If you want to use other model types like decision trees, Apple recommends sticking with Core ML.

Key parts of the API include AIModel (the main model object), AIModelAsset (for unspecialized models), InferenceFunction (runs predictions), InferenceValue (inputs and outputs), and features for managing arrays, caching, and performance. There are also tools for debugging and profiling and error handling built in.

From the comments, some developers are excited about Core AI and see it as a big step for making AI apps on Apple devices. They like that it uses all parts of Apple silicon and that everything can happen on-device, which is good for privacy and speed. Others say the beta is still early, and documentation is a bit thin, so they want to wait and see how easy it really is to use. Some people wonder about support for bigger models and if it will work with existing AI tools and workflows, especially outside the Apple ecosystem. A few users point out that Core ML is still needed for other kinds of models, which could be confusing. There are also some concerns about lock-in, since this only works on Apple devices. Still, many agree it shows Apple is serious about AI and is giving developers new tools to build smarter apps.
