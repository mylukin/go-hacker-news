## ChatGPT Won't Let You Type Until Cloudflare Reads Your React State

- 原文链接: [ChatGPT Won't Let You Type Until Cloudflare Reads Your React State](https://www.buchodi.com/chatgpt-wont-let-you-type-until-cloudflare-reads-your-react-state-i-decrypted-the-program-that-does-it/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47566865)

This article explains how every time you use ChatGPT, a hidden program from Cloudflare runs in your browser to check if you are a real user. The writer decrypted these programs and found that they do much more than normal browser fingerprinting.

The program checks 55 different things about your browser, your network, and how the ChatGPT website is running. For the browser, it looks at your graphics card, screen size, fonts, and even creates a hidden box to measure how text is shown. For the network, it reads your city, IP address, and region from Cloudflare headers. The most special part is that it checks the ChatGPT React app itself. It looks for certain data that only appears if the real ChatGPT website finished loading and running JavaScript.

This means that fake browsers and bots that only pretend to be browsers or skip loading the full ChatGPT app will be detected and blocked. The program is sent to your browser in encrypted form, but the encryption is quite simple: the key to unlock it is hidden right inside the message. The writer shows how to decrypt it step by step using information from the HTTP request and response. Once decrypted, the program runs instructions in a custom virtual machine, collecting all 55 data points, and builds a special token sent with every ChatGPT message.

Cloudflare also uses two other checks. One watches how you type and move your mouse, looking for real human actions. Another makes your computer do a short math puzzle (proof of work) to slow down bots. The writer says the encryption is not strong; it just hides details from quick checks, but anyone determined can still see the fingerprinting process.

In the comments, some people are worried about privacy and how much data is collected without users knowing. They say this kind of deep fingerprinting feels like spying and could be overkill. Others think it’s clever because it really stops bots that would otherwise abuse ChatGPT. A few programmers are impressed by how the detection works at the application level, not just the browser level. Some users point out that the encryption is weak, so it’s just keeping regular people out, not real attackers. There are also comments from people who wish OpenAI would be more open about these checks, as users deserve to know what happens in their browser. A few note that this kind of system can block people who use privacy tools or non-standard browsers, making access harder for some. Others discuss technical ways this fingerprinting could be bypassed or why it’s still better than using CAPTCHAs for everyone. Some are concerned about how these methods could be used by other websites to track users even more closely in the future. Finally, a few commenters share that they have noticed ChatGPT sometimes not letting them type, and now they understand why.

## Voyager 1 runs on 69 KB of memory and an 8-track tape recorder

- 原文链接: [Voyager 1 runs on 69 KB of memory and an 8-track tape recorder](https://techfixated.com/a-1977-time-capsule-voyager-1-runs-on-69-kb-of-memory-and-an-8-track-tape-recorder-4/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47564421)

Voyager 1 is a spacecraft launched in 1977 that is now more than 15 billion miles from Earth, running on only 69 KB of memory and an 8-track tape recorder. It is the farthest object humans have ever sent into space, still sending back scientific data almost 50 years after launch.

The main computer on Voyager 1 is much weaker than the phone in your pocket—your phone has about a million times more memory. The craft sends data very slowly, at just 160 bits per second, using only 22.4 watts of power for its transmitter (that’s like a small bulb). Its famous “8-track” tape isn’t like the music tapes from old cars. Instead, it’s a strong, space-ready tape machine that could last for decades without breaking down. This tape system worked perfectly until 2007, when it was finally turned off to save power—not because it broke.

Voyager 1 made huge discoveries, like volcanoes on Jupiter’s moon Io and a thick atmosphere on Saturn’s moon Titan. In 2012, it became the first spacecraft to leave the bubble around our sun and enter interstellar space, sending back unique data from this new region. In 2025, the mission almost ended when its backup thrusters started failing, but engineers at NASA figured out a risky fix to wake up old thrusters. They took a chance, and it worked—the thrusters came back online, saving the mission.

Voyager 1 is expected to keep working and sending data until about 2036 as its power slowly runs out. It will travel for thousands of years, carrying a gold record with sounds and images from Earth, meant for any alien life that might find it someday. The gold record includes music, greetings in many languages, and even a map to show where Earth is.

Some compared Voyager 1’s reliability to modern devices, which often break or become outdated much faster. A few commenters talked about how careful and “over-engineered” the spacecraft was, with every part tested and made redundant. Others joked about how their phone or computer would never last that long. There was also praise for the engineers who built Voyager 1, with people saying their work is a lesson in good design and planning ahead. Some users wondered if any current technology will ever match Voyager’s record for long-term reliability. A few discussed the challenges of communicating with something so far away and the thrill of the successful thruster fix. There was also talk about the Golden Record, with people debating whether aliens could ever find or understand it. Many commenters felt inspired by the story, seeing Voyager 1 as proof of human curiosity and what good engineering can achieve.

## C++26 is done ISO C++ standards meeting, Trip Report

- 原文链接: [C++26 is done ISO C++ standards meeting, Trip Report](https://herbsutter.com/2026/03/29/c26-is-done-trip-report-march-2026-iso-c-standards-meeting-london-croydon-uk/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47565365)

C++26 is finished and will soon be the new official standard for the C++ programming language. The article explains what happened at the final ISO C++ standards meeting and highlights the biggest new features coming in C++26.

The article says C++26 brings four main changes. First, C++ now has “reflection.” This means the language can look at its own code while compiling, helping with code generation and metaprogramming. This is seen as the biggest change since templates. Second, memory safety is better. Reading uninitialized variables is no longer “undefined behavior,” and the standard library is now safer by default, thanks to changes tested at big companies like Google and Apple. These safety changes fixed many real bugs and did not make programs slower. Third, C++26 adds “contracts.” These are new ways to check if a function gets the right inputs and returns the right outputs, helping catch mistakes early. Some experts worried about contracts, but most agreed they should be included. Fourth, there is a new async/concurrency model called std::execution, also known as Sender/Receiver. This helps write programs that do many things at once, in a safer and more organized way. The author warns that std::execution is powerful, but still needs better documentation and helper libraries.

The article says adoption of C++26 will be fast. Many developers want these features, and compilers like GCC and Clang already support most of them. It also says the next version, C++29, will work even more on safety, with new ideas like “profiles” to make safe code even easier.

In the Hacker News comments, some users are excited about reflection and think it will change how people write C++. Others are happy about the focus on memory safety, saying it is long overdue. Some people are glad that the new safety features do not slow down programs much. A few users are worried that contracts are still controversial and hope the feature will be easy to use. Some think std::execution will be useful, but agree it needs better guides and examples. There are comments from people who work at big tech companies, sharing stories about how safety changes have already helped them. A few users wish C++ would focus more on simplicity, saying the language keeps getting bigger and harder to learn. Others are just happy to see steady progress and praise the standards committee for listening to feedback. Some people ask about how fast compilers will support all the new features, and others discuss which features they will try first in their own projects.

## Pretext: TypeScript library for multiline text measurement and layout

- 原文链接: [Pretext: TypeScript library for multiline text measurement and layout](https://github.com/chenglou/pretext)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47556290)

Pretext is a TypeScript and JavaScript library that lets you measure and lay out multi-line text without needing to use the browser’s DOM. The main idea is to make text measurement fast, accurate, and not slow down your web app by skipping expensive layout steps in the browser.

The library works by using the browser’s font engine to measure text, but it does all the calculations itself. This way, you don’t need to call things like `getBoundingClientRect`, which can be slow and cause layout reflows. Pretext has two main uses: First, you can quickly measure how tall a paragraph will be for a certain width and font, without touching the DOM. This is great for virtualization, special layouts, and making sure text fits well, even before you render it. Second, you can fully control how text is laid out, line by line, so you can do things like custom wrapping, flow text around images, or render text to canvas, SVG, or even on the server.

The API has functions like `prepare` (does the main setup work), `layout` (finds out the text height and line count), and more advanced ones like `prepareWithSegments` and `layoutWithLines` for people who want to control every line. There are also tools to walk through lines or even change the width for each line as you lay it out. This fits many use cases, from simple text boxes to complex graphics.

Pretext supports any language, emojis, mixed writing directions, and special browser quirks. It’s fast: the `prepare` step is quick, and the `layout` step is even faster (almost instant for a batch of texts). It helps with things like keeping scroll positions stable, checking if text will overflow, and building custom UI layouts where the browser’s normal CSS is not enough.

The library does not try to be a full font rendering engine. It works best with normal word wrapping and line breaks, and asks you to avoid using system fonts like `system-ui` on macOS for precise results. You can also clear its cache or set the locale if your app needs to change languages or fonts often.

In the Hacker News comments, some developers were excited about being able to measure text outside the DOM, saying this solves real problems with performance in web apps and helps with virtualized lists or chat apps. Others pointed out that handling all languages and complex scripts is hard, so they are curious how Pretext deals with things like Arabic, emojis, or mixed text. Some users liked that you can render to canvas or SVG, making it good for games and custom graphics. A few wondered about the limits of measurement accuracy, especially since browsers can behave differently with fonts and rendering. People also discussed how this could help with server-side rendering or PDF generation, where you can’t use the browser’s layout engine. Some compared Pretext with other text measurement tools and noted that the API looks simple and useful. There were questions about memory usage and cache size, but the option to clear the cache was seen as helpful. Overall, most comments were positive, saying this library fills a gap and can save developers a lot of time and trouble.

## Show HN: Crazierl – An Erlang Operating System

- 原文链接: [Show HN: Crazierl – An Erlang Operating System](https://crazierl.org/demo/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47567049)

Crazierl is a new operating system made in Erlang, and you can try it in your web browser. The demo lets people connect their browsers together in a cluster, so they can chat or run commands as a group.

The system runs in an emulated PC inside your browser, thanks to the v86 project. You just need to open a link, share a hashtag, and enable the “dist” checkbox to join friends in the same cluster. When connected, you can use commands like `chat:start().` to talk with others.

But there are many warnings. The connection between nodes is not secure—it’s in plain text, and anyone on the network could take control if they connect. The demo uses a public relay server and does not encrypt data. The creators strongly say not to use this over open internet, and not to type passwords or private information. If someone bad connects, they could control your node or even your browser. Also, the system may not follow new laws about age or safety.

The main point is to play and learn with friends, not to use it for serious work or with strangers. It’s a fun experiment to show Erlang working as a whole operating system, not just as a language.

In the comments, some people are excited to see Erlang used like this, calling it a cool idea. Others point out the big security problems, saying they would never use it outside a safe, private network. A few ask technical questions about the emulator speed and how the OS works inside a browser. Some users wish for better security or encryption before they can trust it more. A few people like the easy way to join clusters and chat, while others worry about people not reading the warnings. Some see it as a great learning tool for Erlang, and others say it’s not practical yet, but very interesting to try.

## More on Version Control

- 原文链接: [More on Version Control](https://bramcohen.com/p/more-on-version-control)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47566332)

This article talks about new ideas for version control systems, especially how to make actions like squash and rebase safer and keep more history. The author explains that by choosing an earlier ancestor as the “primary” when squashing commits, you keep more information about what really happened.

Git is easy to use and reliable, but it throws away a lot of the real history when you squash or rebase. This can be confusing later. The author’s method tries to always keep the full story, so you know who changed what and when, even after these actions. This way, you avoid problems where changes are lost or authorship is mixed up.

The new approach means you have to “commit to diffs” when you make a commit, which is safer but needs careful coding. The author built a simple demo to show it works, and says the core should stay small and well-tested. Some other systems are too hard to understand, but this one is meant to be clear.

People sometimes find the words “left” and “right” confusing in merge tools. The author says you can use better names, like the branch or who made the change. He also mentions that the algorithm for merging changes, called “anchoring” in CRDTs, has been invented by different groups, but his version adds something called “generation counting” to make it fully structural, with no need for commit IDs.

There is a small detail: if two people delete the same thing, should that show as a conflict? The author thinks most people are fine with just removing it, but it could be a setting you can choose. Another technical point is about merging: sometimes, if two branches change the same lines in different ways, the result might look new, but the system still handles it well most of the time.

At the end, the author talks about “artisanal code,” meaning code written and checked by real people, not just AI. He thinks human-written code is safer, but using AI to help with writing text is less risky, though it might sound more boring.

In the comments, some people are excited about keeping more history and making version control safer. They agree that losing authorship or history in Git is a problem, especially for big teams. Others say that Git’s simple design is why it works so well, and they worry that making things more complex could cause new issues.

Some users like that the new system is easier to follow and that the technical ideas are well explained. A few point out that making the core simple and well-tested is important, because bad merges can cause big headaches. Others mention that tools with too many options can be hard for new users to learn.

There is debate about what should count as a conflict, with some saying they want more control, and others happy to let the system decide. A few people discuss CRDTs and are happy to see new research being used in version control, but ask about performance and real-world use.

Some commenters like the idea of “artisanal code” and agree it makes software safer. Others think using AI for code is not always bad, but it needs good review. Overall, people are glad to see new ideas in version control and hope for better tools in the future.
