Our first story is about a new rule affecting the U.S. Census Bureau. The Bureau will no longer be allowed to use “noise infusion”—that is, adding randomness—to protect privacy in its published data. In the past, the Census used many tricks to hide private details, such as not showing small counts, making data less precise, swapping data between records, and adding random noise. From 1990 to 2010, swapping was the main tool, but people still found ways to uncover private information. So for the 2020 Census, they picked “differential privacy,” which adds noise in a careful way. This method was not perfect—some users didn’t like the less exact numbers—but it was better than the other options.

Now, with noise banned, the Census can only use things like coarsening and suppression. These methods do not work well for big and detailed datasets. If you use them too much, the data becomes almost useless, especially for small or minority groups. If you don’t use them enough, privacy is in danger. The article argues that banning noise could mean future Census data will either not be safe or not be helpful. Almost all privacy methods add some randomness, so this rule might break more than expected.

The writer wonders why this rule was made. Is it to help political tricks, like gerrymandering, or to hide unfair differences in the population? Or maybe people just wanted to avoid the hard problem of privacy versus usefulness. In the comments, many people worry this change will make it easier to find private info in Census data. Some say it will help bad actors, like those trying to gerrymander voting districts. Others think the rule will force the Census to give out weak data, making it hard for researchers and planners. Some point out that almost all statistics use randomness, so the rule is confusing and hard to enforce. Some comments say the real issue is politics. There’s sadness too, as important privacy research, like differential privacy, is now ignored. Most commenters agree that the ban is a step backward for both privacy and good data.

Our seventh story is about Exif, the metadata inside image files like photos from your phone or camera. Exif stores details like when a photo was taken, camera settings, GPS, and orientation. In JPEG files, Exif sits in a special part and looks like an old TIFF file. Exif is optional—some images have it, some do not, and it can be fake or stripped out. The most common tags are date, camera, lens, exposure, GPS, and especially orientation.

Orientation is important because cameras often set the orientation tag instead of rotating the pixels, and viewers then rotate the picture for you. If you edit images, you should fix the orientation and clear the tag. Tools like exiftool help read, change, or remove Exif data. Exif is not the only metadata—there is also XMP, IPTC, color profiles, and new standards like C2PA. Each file type may store metadata differently. When you upload or download images, do not trust that metadata is gone unless you check or strip it yourself.

In the comments, many people say exiftool is their tool of choice for metadata. Some share stories where wrong Exif data caused rotated or upside-down images. A few warn that Exif can leak private data, like location, if not removed. Others use Exif to learn how a photo was taken or to sort thousands of photos by date. Some point out that not all viewers respect orientation tags, which causes confusion. A few discuss how metadata can be faked or misused. There are also debates about how much metadata should be kept or stripped. Some share fun tricks like hiding jokes in Exif fields. Overall, Exif is seen as boring but useful, and many wish metadata standards were simpler or more secure.

Our eighth story is about how Intel made the fast math adder inside the 8087 floating-point chip, released in 1980. The adder is 69 bits wide and helps the 8087 do hard math like square roots and logarithms. Each floating-point number is split into exponent and fraction. The adder works on the fraction, and if needed, copies the exponent. To make it fast, Intel broke the adder into 4-bit blocks and used a trick called the Manchester carry chain. Normally, the “carry” moves slowly from bit to bit, but the Manchester chain lets it move quickly, almost as fast as a wire.

The chain uses three ideas: Generate, Propagate, and Delete. Each 4-bit block checks if the carry can move through, and uses “carry-skip” to move quickly to the next block. The adder was built with NMOS transistors, which are fast at pulling signals low, but slow to pull them high, so the chip precharges carry lines before each addition. The adder also handles rounding and negative numbers, making it a bit larger than needed. The logic is packed tightly, and the adder is used for other math like multiplication and division.

In the comments, many people were impressed by how much Intel did with so few transistors. Some said the Manchester carry chain is smart for its time. Others noted that newer chips use even faster adders but need more hardware. Some enjoyed the detailed chip photos and the deep technical look. There were questions about NMOS design and why Intel made certain choices. Some remembered using the 8087 and called it a big step forward. A few debated if the chip’s design could be improved today, and some shared links to other articles about old chips. Overall, readers praised the clear explanation and learned more about how math works inside a chip.
