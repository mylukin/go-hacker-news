First, a new website called “In the Weights” lets you check if large language models—like GPT or Claude—know about a person or topic. You can ask the site if models “recognize” you, and it will send your question to many models, collect their answers, and show how strongly each one knows your input. The goal is to help people see what’s stored inside these models, especially as more people use AI instead of web search. It’s useful for privacy, curiosity, or checking how well-known something is in the AI world. The creator says they want to show what traces are “in the weights” of LLMs as the internet changes, and they welcome feedback.

Commenters are curious about how answer clustering works, and some worry about privacy—what if your name is inside these models? Some see it as a fun way to explore AI, while others wonder if the site could reveal bias or mistakes. There are debates about trusting model answers, and some want more technical details. Most people like the experiment, but privacy risks are a big topic.

Next, Noam Shazeer, a top AI researcher and co-author of the “Attention Is All You Need” transformer paper, is leaving Google to join OpenAI. Shazeer has been a key figure in modern AI, writing Google’s spell corrector as a new hire and leading work on transformers and mixture-of-experts models. He left Google for Character.AI, returned after a huge talent deal, and now moves to OpenAI—seen as a big win for them and a loss for Google.

The comments are lively. Some praise Shazeer as a true leader, while others discuss whether Google’s culture is driving talent away. Many say money isn’t the main reason at this level; top people want the most exciting work. There are jokes about Silicon Valley drama and debates about employee loyalty. Some say companies are not loyal, so workers should move freely; others think leaving so soon after big deals is unfair. People wonder why Google keeps falling behind in AI, despite huge resources, and debate if the AI field is becoming more open or still controlled by a few big players. There’s some drama in the comments, but most agree this is part of ongoing tech “talent wars.”

On the open source front, a developer found 10,000 GitHub repositories spreading Trojan malware. Attackers copied real projects, updated the README to add a link to a zip file with infected .exe files. Virus scanners often missed these unless the actual file was uploaded. The developer wrote a script to find these repos based on update patterns and commit copying. GitHub was slow to respond until the news went public, then started deleting the bad repos.

People are worried. Some think the attacks target bots that auto-install dependencies, starting new infection chains. Others share stories of getting malware from GitHub, and warn that even password managers are at risk if your machine is infected. There’s debate on how best to protect credentials—hardware keys like Yubikey are one answer, but not always easy to manage. Many say GitHub should scan uploads or use simple rules to stop fake repos, but others admit the scale is huge. Some lose trust in open source, since few check code or binaries, and attackers use fake stars and new accounts to look trustworthy. Many feel powerless, but agree that sharing and scanning tools help. There’s frustration at how easy it is to use trusted platforms for malware, and that big companies don’t do enough to protect users.

In privacy news, Elkjop, a large electronics chain in the Nordics, was fined €1.8 million for breaking GDPR rules. A customer received many marketing emails and found the only way to stop them was to leave the loyalty club and lose benefits. This is illegal under GDPR: you must be able to refuse marketing without losing membership. The customer complained and used various GDPR rights, leading to a five-year investigation. Norway’s data authority fined Elkjop for not allowing free choice and for using customer data for extra ads without consent.

Commenters are glad the fine happened, but surprised it took so long and that authorities didn’t keep the complainant updated. Some wonder how much money Elkjop made during those years, and others wish this kind of privacy law existed in the US. There’s support for the fight, but also concern that big companies still profit, even with fines.

Turning to energy, Switzerland’s parliament voted to lift the ban on new nuclear power plants, but the final decision will be made by a national vote. Supporters say nuclear is needed for steady, clean energy in winter, when solar and hydro can’t provide enough, and that new technology is safer. Opponents worry about cost, danger, and nuclear waste. They argue renewables are growing fast and are cheaper, but supporters say renewables alone can’t cover all needs.

Commenters debate nuclear waste—some say it’s small and manageable, others say it’s dangerous for thousands of years. France’s nuclear program is seen by some as a success, but only possible with strong government backing. There’s discussion about the high cost and slow speed of building nuclear plants in Europe, compared to China or Korea, and whether renewables really can replace all fossil fuels. Many say politics and public opinion, not just science, will decide the future. Each country’s needs are different, so there’s no one answer.

Next, Ubiquiti announced a new ZFS-based enterprise NAS called ENAS. It uses 8 ARM Neoverse N2 cores, 64GB ECC memory, supports up to 16 drives, and uses UniFi for easy management—no recurring software fees. It offers open drive support, secure access with role permissions, and will soon support backups across sites and work with Proxmox or VMware. The main selling points are no vendor lock-in, easy management, and strong security.

Users are happy to see a ZFS NAS from Ubiquiti, since ZFS is known for data safety. Some like that data can be kept local and offline. There’s praise for open drive support, and comparisons to Synology and QNAP, whose systems are seen as more locked down. Some warn DIY options like TrueNAS are cheaper but more complex, while ENAS is easier for businesses. There’s debate about the CPU—some want more power, others think ARM is enough. Some worry Ubiquiti could add subscriptions or limits later, but many trust the company’s history. Overall, ENAS is seen as a good choice for small and medium businesses, while power users may still prefer building their own systems.

Now, a look into American Express’s payment system, which uses a “cell-based architecture.” The system is split into small, independent cells, each with its own services and data. If one cell fails, others keep working. Data is copied into each cell, and a central router sends payments to the right cell. If a cell fails during a payment, the router moves the work to another cell and starts over. This keeps failures small and avoids double payments. Logging and monitoring are done inside each cell first, then collected later, so if a tool breaks, only that cell is affected. The design keeps payments fast and always online.

There’s only one comment so far, but readers may wonder how this compares to other big payment systems, and if the extra work of many cells is worth the safety and uptime. Clear boundaries help, but keeping data in sync can be hard. Still, for payments, safety is the top goal.

Next, an article on moving from GNU Stow to Chezmoi for dotfile management. Stow uses symlinks from a git repo to your home directory, but it gets messy on many machines and doesn’t handle scripts or system settings. Chezmoi keeps all your files in a source directory as real files, can handle templates and permissions, and makes it easy to apply changes across machines with one command. It also runs scripts for setup tasks.

Commenters share their own dotfile stories. Some are happy with Stow but see why Chezmoi or Nix might be better for many machines. Others mention tools like yadm, mise, or just using git, rsync, or Makefiles. Some love Chezmoi’s balance of features and ease, but say it takes getting used to. There’s debate about Nix—some find it too complex, others say it’s worth learning for full control. In the end, everyone has their own way, and Chezmoi gets praise for making things easier.

In AI news, the US government told Anthropic to cut off SK Telecom, a major Korean telco, from its newest AI, Claude Mythos, due to concerns about China links and AI safety. SK Telecom had invested $100 million and partnered with Anthropic, but the US ordered access removed. The move is part of a bigger US plan to control who can use top AI models, especially those seen as security risks. There’s also talk of industry pressure and problems between Anthropic and Amazon playing a role.

Commenters say this is a warning for foreign companies: investing in US tech can be risky if the rules change suddenly. Some say it’s better to build your own models instead of depending on US companies. Others think the story is overhyped by the media. There’s debate about how hiding model “thinking steps” affects AI safety, and whether strong human skills are being ignored in favor of AI. Some think Anthropic’s policy moves are just business strategy to protect its lead.

Next, hospitals and universities are running late-stage drug trials for much less money by repurposing old, generic drugs for new uses. This is possible because the drugs are already well-studied, so trials are less risky and cheaper. Doctors and scientists are motivated by helping patients, not just patents or profits. When a drug’s patent ends, companies lose interest, but universities can find new uses and make treatments cheaper for everyone.

Commenters share stories of repurposed drugs, like using Avastin for eye disease to save money, but note safety worries. Some say this gives hope for rare diseases, but big companies still work on new drugs too. There’s a lot of talk about the US healthcare system—insurance companies sometimes prefer expensive drugs because of profit rules, and the system makes generics harder to use. Some want easier approval for new uses of old drugs, but others worry about efficiency. There are debates about capitalism, patents, and drug pricing, with many agreeing repurposing drugs is good, but the system is slow and often unfair to patients.
