First, let’s talk about Kimi K3, a new open AI model with 2.8 trillion parameters. Kimi K3 is the biggest open AI model so far and can handle not just text but also images and videos, all with a very long context window. It’s built using special attention methods and a mixture-of-experts system, which means it has many expert “sub-models” that work together for better performance and easier scaling.

Kimi K3 is especially good at coding, even with big and complex codebases. It can use screenshots, optimize GPU kernels, and even write a GPU compiler from scratch called MiniTriton, which performs as well as other top tools. Kimi K3 can also design 3D games, create digital assets, and even design computer chips on its own. For science tasks, the model reads research papers, checks formulas, writes code, and builds dashboards—work that would normally take a human weeks to finish.

For knowledge work, Kimi K3 can make interactive reports, visualizations, and research presentations. It handles financial and scientific data, edits videos, and explains its own technology with motion graphics. The architecture uses new attention methods and a mixture-of-experts design for better efficiency and stability. While big jobs need many GPUs, Kimi K3 can run on modern hardware with features to keep costs lower. Technical details and the full open-source release are planned for 2026.

Kimi K3 is available on desktop and mobile apps, command-line tools, and an API, with prices and discounts listed. There’s also an enterprise version for organizations needing privacy and management. Benchmarks show Kimi K3 is strong against other models, sometimes winning, and sometimes just behind closed models like Claude Fable 5 or GPT 5.6 Sol.

The article mentions some weaknesses: sometimes Kimi K3 is too proactive, acting without enough user input, and switching models mid-session can lower quality. It’s not quite as polished as the top closed models, but still very competitive.

Now, let’s look back at history. Microsoft has open-sourced Comic Chat, its old chat program that turned conversations into comic panels. First released in 1996, Comic Chat is also known for introducing the Comic Sans font.

Back in the 1990s, most chats were just plain text. Comic Chat made things fun by showing messages as comic strips with cartoon characters showing emotions. It was built by Microsoft Research, with comic art by Jim Woodring, and was released with Internet Explorer 3. The team even wrote a research paper on how the program matched messages to comic poses and layouts.

Microsoft has now released the code on GitHub, with updates to help it run on modern systems and connect to today’s IRC servers. It’s not a finished product, but it is a starting point for hobbyists and fans.

On Hacker News, many people remember Comic Chat fondly, calling it ahead of its time. Some are surprised to learn about the origin of Comic Sans. Others are happy to see Microsoft open-sourcing old software and curious about how Comic Chat understood emotions. Some share stories from their youth, and others hope for modern versions. People like the idea of saving and sharing creative old software, even if just for fun or nostalgia.

Next, we have a story about Decoy Font, a special font that hides what you type from AI programs. This font shows a fake letter up close, but the real, hidden letter appears when you look from far away. It works by mixing two sets of shapes for each letter, using a “hybrid image” technique. Up close, your eyes and AI see the decoy, but from afar, the blur blends to show the real message.

Most AI models, like ChatGPT and Gemini, only see the decoy message in screenshots. People can see both messages, but computers get confused. The font is free and easy to use, based on DejaVu Sans Mono. There’s also a playground online to test it.

Decoy Font is just one of several “anti-AI fonts.” Another from the same group is Ghost Font, which hides messages by moving them in an animation. Decoy Font is easier to use, as you just type with it.

But this is not perfect. Smarter AI models or people who know the trick can still read the hidden message. Still, it can slow down simple AI tools for now. The creator hopes to expand support for more languages, like Chinese.

In the comments, people are impressed by the clever idea and the optical illusion. Some say it’s a temporary fix, as AI will get better. Others worry it’s hard for humans with poor eyesight and for people using screen readers. Some suggest using the font in puzzles or games, while others point out the start of a “cat and mouse” game between font makers and AI researchers. Many agree it’s fun to see creative ways to protect data in the age of AI.

Now for some science news. A group of scientists found that a rocky planet outside our solar system is losing helium from its atmosphere. This planet is in the habitable zone, meaning it could have liquid water.

The planet is close to Earth, making it easier to study. Scientists used a telescope to see infrared light from helium, which showed the planet’s atmosphere is leaking helium into space. The planet orbits a small, cool star and is slightly bigger than Earth. Finding helium escaping is important; it means the planet still has some atmosphere, but it’s slowly losing it. If it loses too much, it might not support life. Studying this helps scientists guess how long the atmosphere will last.

In the comments, people are excited about learning if other planets can keep their air and maybe have life. Others point out losing atmosphere is bad for life. There are questions about other gases, how the star’s light affects the air, and whether other planets also lose their atmospheres. Many hope new telescopes will let us watch more planets like this. Most agree this is a good step for learning about worlds outside our solar system.

Another story is about building a tool to detect if a story was written by a large language model like ChatGPT, using older machine learning methods. The author wanted to see if AI fiction could be detected easily.

They first tried text perplexity, but it was unreliable and expensive. So, they used classic machine learning: collecting thousands of human and AI stories, splitting them into sentences, and training a Linear SVC model using TF-IDF features. This simple method gave about 85% accuracy at detecting AI-written sentences.

They trained seven different models (one for each LLM) and used majority voting. They built a web demo that runs in the browser. Tests showed the detector works well, with low false positives for old, human-written stories, and it could flag about a third of popular online fanfics as likely AI-generated.

In the comments, people are impressed such a simple method works well. Some say AI writing is still easy to spot statistically. Others note the limits, as LLMs get better and users try to trick detectors. Some worry about bias and false accusations. Many like the open, browser-based demo and the transparency. There’s debate about whether we should even try to detect AI text, and ethical questions about AI in creative spaces. Most enjoyed the technical breakdown and want to try building similar tools themselves.

Finally, we look at privacy issues in period tracker apps. The Mozilla Foundation checked six popular apps, finding that some share users’ private health data with other companies.

Some apps keep data safe, but others share details like pregnancy status, moods, or symptoms with companies like Google, Meta, TikTok, and others. Stardust, for example, sends data to a partner not listed in its privacy policy. Spot On doesn’t share data, but if you use some features, it opens a website that shares details. Most apps send some user info to ad companies, and sometimes you can’t stop this tracking.

Euki is the most private app, keeping data only on your phone and offering a “decoy” mode. Flo and Clue do not share health data, but still keep it on their servers. Mozilla says app history matters, as some apps had privacy scandals in the past.

In the comments, people are shocked by how much data these apps share. Some say they never trust apps with health data, and others use pen and paper instead. Developers argue it’s possible to build good apps that don’t share data. There’s debate about server storage, legal risks, and calls for better privacy laws. Many are glad Mozilla did this research, but wish more people knew about the risks.
