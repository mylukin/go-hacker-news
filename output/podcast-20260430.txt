Hello everyone, this is the 2026-04-30 episode of Hacker News Daily Podcast. Today, we bring you key stories on privacy, security, open source, and the future of technology.

First, Rivian has made a big move for car owners who care about privacy. Now, you can turn off all internet connections in your Rivian vehicle. This means your car will not send any data outside, but you will lose features like navigation, lane keeping, and software updates. In Canada, you can do this with a menu setting. In other countries, you need to ask Rivian service to turn off the eSIM during a visit. However, if you pay for extra Rivian services, you must cancel them yourself—turning off data does not stop your subscriptions. The article notes that many Rivian features need an internet connection, so you might lose new functions and safety fixes. There are also links to learn more about privacy and how your driving data is used.

On Hacker News, some users were glad Rivian gave this choice, saying it is rare for car owners to control their data. Others worry that turning off data could make cars less safe, or question if all data really stops flowing. Some wish more car companies offered this. A few point out that older cars did not have this issue at all, since they had no internet. The discussion also touched on privacy laws in different countries and hopes for a simple offline mode for all smart devices.

Next, we look at LinkedIn’s hidden browser extension scanning. LinkedIn checks for over 6,000 Chrome extensions each time you visit, building a detailed profile that links your browser to your real name, job, and company. This scanning is secret, not mentioned in LinkedIn’s privacy policy, and does not ask for your consent. It works by looking for files from certain extensions and sends encrypted results to LinkedIn’s servers. Because LinkedIn knows your real-world identity, this extension data can be linked to other data about you and even your employer. Now, there is a criminal investigation in Germany, as LinkedIn may be breaking European law.

Hacker News users were shocked by how detailed and hidden this is. Many called it a big privacy violation, especially since people trust LinkedIn with their work data. Some said browser makers like Chrome should block this, and suggested using browsers like Firefox. There was technical advice on blocking these scans, but also worry that defenses may not be enough. Some felt only strict laws will stop such tracking, and that privacy policies are often too vague. Developers worry this could hurt trust in browser extensions. The overall mood was a mix of anger, worry, and calls for change.

Now, a story about mass surveillance. Mark Klein, a retired AT&T technician, gave evidence to the Electronic Frontier Foundation about secret NSA spying. He showed that the NSA copied internet traffic by tapping into AT&T fiber cables in San Francisco after 9/11. Klein found a special room in the AT&T building, with internet cables split so all data was copied to the NSA. He gave documents and diagrams to prove it, leading to a lawsuit against the government’s mass surveillance.

On Hacker News, many praised Mark Klein as a hero. Some said it shows how hard it is to protect privacy, and that mass surveillance is a problem in many countries. There were technical discussions on how simple but powerful the method was. Some worry that not much has changed, and laws are even weaker now. Others debated if any mass surveillance is needed, but most agreed that spying on everyone is wrong. There was talk about the risks whistleblowers face, and questions about how many other secret rooms may still exist.

Moving to open source security, a popular Python package—‘lightning’—was attacked with malware in versions 2.6.2 and 2.6.3. The malware stole secrets like passwords and cloud keys, and tried to spread by infecting GitHub and npm projects. It worked on AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud, and could even run again when you opened your project in tools like VS Code. If you used the bad versions, you should look for strange files and rotate your secrets right away. Signs of attack include odd commit messages or new repos with “Dune” themes.

Hacker News users were surprised at how wide and clever the attack was. Many said it is scary how easy it is for malware to enter through trusted libraries. Some discussed the rising risk of supply chain attacks and the need for better checks in package managers. There was debate about developer tools being too powerful, and advice on scanning for malware. Most agreed that open source needs stronger protections.

Revenue was $111.2 billion, up 17% from last year, and earnings per share rose by 22%. iPhone 17 sales were very strong, and services like the App Store and iCloud also grew. Apple announced a higher dividend and a $100 billion stock buyback program. The company said it is still growing in every region, but faces global risks like supply chain issues.

On Hacker News, users were impressed by Apple’s growth, especially in a mature market. Some discussed the huge buyback and its effect on stock price. Others questioned if Apple can keep growing, since many markets are already full. There was talk about services revenue and new products. Some criticized Apple’s press releases for being too positive, while others focused on Apple’s strong ecosystem and possible future regulation. The usual debate continued: is Apple still an innovator, or just good at selling?

Now, a story about learning and building. One developer wrote about making a Game Boy emulator in F#. They wanted to learn how computers work, so they first made a CHIP-8 emulator, then spent months building “Fame Boy,” a full Game Boy emulator with sound for desktop and the web. The design uses F#’s type system to prevent bugs, and mixes functional and mutable code for speed. Testing was done with help from AI, which also helped find bugs and speed up the code.

The article covers tricky parts like sound, graphics, and browser problems. The author used Fable to run the emulator in browsers and tuned the code for better speed. On Hacker News, readers praised the clear explanation, use of F#, and honest talk about using AI. Some shared their own emulator projects, others asked technical questions, and many said the story inspired them to try similar projects or learn F#. The open-source code and web demo were also popular.

Next, a strange issue with Claude Code, an AI coding tool. A developer named Theo posted that if your commit message mentions “OpenClaw” in a JSON file, Claude will block you or charge extra. He showed proof, but there is no clear answer from Claude’s makers. Some think “OpenClaw” might be a banned project or keyword, but it is not clear if this is a bug or a security feature.

Hacker News users worry about privacy, since Claude Code seems to read commit messages. Some fear this will hurt trust, while others joke about finding more banned words. A few defend Claude, saying there might be safety or legal reasons. Many call for more openness about such rules, and say hidden keyword triggers are unfair. Overall, people want answers and better transparency from AI services.

In security news, a serious Linux bug called “CopyFail” lets local users become root. The bug started in 2017 and was just fixed in new kernels, but many old “longterm” kernels are still not fixed. Some distributions like Gentoo made their own workarounds, but these are not perfect. The Linux community did not share news of the bug early, so some developers and users had no warning before it became public.

Hacker News users worry that many servers run old kernels and are now open to attack. Some say the Linux security process is broken, while others defend the developers, noting how hard backporting fixes can be. There is talk about AI-generated code causing more bugs, and calls for better rules on sharing security news. Most agree that Linux security is tough and clear communication is needed.

Turning to energy, Belgium has decided to keep its nuclear power plants open, and may even build new ones. The government plans to take control of the plants with ENGIE, and hopes to reach a deal by October. Belgium gets much of its power from gas imports, and has not built enough renewables to replace nuclear. The move ends years of debate and reverses a plan to close all nuclear plants by 2025.

Hacker News commenters were split. Some support keeping nuclear for stable, cheap energy, while others worry about the cost and safety of old reactors. There is debate about nationalizing the plants, building new ones, or investing in renewables. Some mention that energy policy often changes with governments and world events. Many note that nuclear is still important for Belgium, and joke about the country changing its mind often. Some say many countries are rethinking nuclear as energy needs grow.

Finally, we look at oil refineries. The article explains how crude oil is turned into gasoline, diesel, and plastics. Even with more wind and solar, the world still uses over 100 million barrels of oil daily. Refineries first separate the oil by heating, then use steps like cracking and reforming to make useful products. The story visits a real refinery in California and explains the huge size and complexity of these systems.

On Hacker News, people praised the clear explanations and diagrams. Some were surprised by the scale, while others raised safety and environmental concerns. There was debate about the future—how refineries will change as we use more electric cars and renewables. Some pointed out it is hard to replace all the products made from oil, not just fuel. There were stories about living near a refinery, and questions about technical details like how catalysts are cleaned. Overall, readers respected the technical challenge and were curious about how refineries will adapt in the future.
