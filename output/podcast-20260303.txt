First, Intel has launched its new 18A chip process, starting with a powerful 288-core Xeon processor for data centers. This is a big moment for Intel, as they are working to catch up with TSMC and AMD in the race to make smaller, faster chips. The 18A process means the inside parts are even smaller and more efficient. This allows more cores—more “brains”—in each chip. The new Xeon is made for huge data centers that run cloud services and big websites. Intel says it’s faster and uses less power, which saves money for companies with lots of servers.

The article notes that Intel has had trouble before, often falling behind rivals. This new 18A process is their chance to show they can still lead in chip tech. They use new tools, like RibbonFETs and PowerVia, to make the chips even better. Intel also hopes this process will bring more customers to use their factories, not just their own chips—a business called “foundry.”

Next, there’s an inspiring video from Physics Girl, Dianna Cowern, who returned after three years with a new science video. She explains how scientists can take a picture of the sun at night using the Super-Kamiokande detector in Japan. This giant tank of pure water deep underground can “see” neutrinos—tiny particles from the sun that pass through almost everything, even the Earth. When a neutrino hits a water molecule, it makes a tiny flash of light called Cherenkov radiation. Sensors catch the flash, and by putting many flashes together, scientists know where the neutrinos—and the sun—are, even at night.

Dianna also talks about why it’s so hard to catch neutrinos and how the Super-Kamiokande helps us learn about the sun and the universe. Many people in the comments are happy to see Dianna back, feeling inspired by her story, and grateful for her simple way of explaining hard science. Some share their own stories about illness and recovery, and others thank her for making science fun. There is a strong sense of support and encouragement in the community.

Now, let’s talk about payment fees, like those charged by Visa or Mastercard. The article uses examples from Ecuador, where QR code payments between bank accounts are becoming popular and have almost no fee. This is different from the US or UK, where card companies take a fee from every payment. The article explains that these fees, even if they look small—like 4%—can take a big part of a small business’s profit, especially after taxes. This can add up for shops, cafés, and taxi drivers.

QR code systems like Deuna in Ecuador, or Pix in Brazil, let people pay quickly with very low fees, and money moves instantly. This is changing how people pay, and card companies now have to compete. In the comments, some people say they never realized how much card fees hurt small businesses. Others explain why some shops offer discounts for cash or set card minimums. Some warn that new systems might not have as much protection against fraud. There’s debate about whether governments should limit card fees or let the market decide. Some worry big banks could end up controlling these new systems, but most agree these changes are good for small shops.

Moving on, there’s an article explaining CRDTs—Conflict-free Replicated Data Types. CRDTs help computers keep shared data in sync, even when users are offline or working at different times. There are two types: state-based, which send the whole data state to others and merge it, and operation-based, which just send the actions taken. The article focuses on state-based CRDTs, describing how they use a value, a state, and a merge function that always works the same way.

The article gives a simple example: the Last Write Wins Register, which always keeps the newest value using a timestamp. There’s also the Last Write Wins Map, which is like a dictionary with many values, each with its own register. Deletes are marked with a “tombstone” so everyone agrees on what’s removed. The code examples show how to set, get, delete, and merge data.

Hacker News users liked the clear, simple explanation. Some liked the interactive examples, while others asked about data getting large due to tombstones, or how to clean up old data. Some said operation-based CRDTs are smaller but harder to build. Others shared that CRDTs are used in real apps, like collaborative editors, and discussed tricky edge cases. Some asked for more advanced examples or shared links to open-source CRDT libraries. Most agree CRDTs are a useful tool for collaborative software.
