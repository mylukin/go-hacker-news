## Jelly UI: Soft-body physics for native HTML form controls

- 原文链接: [Jelly UI: Soft-body physics for native HTML form controls](https://jelly-ui.com/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48981620)

Jelly UI is a library that brings soft, bouncy, jelly-like effects to standard HTML form controls using Web Components. It has no dependencies and gives developers many custom elements they can use by adding just one script tag.

The library includes over 40 elements, such as buttons, checkboxes, sliders, switches, inputs, cards, tabs, and more. Each control looks and feels like soft jelly, moving or wobbling when you click, drag, or use the keyboard. For example, buttons pop in and out, sliders squish as you drag, and inputs ripple when you type. Everything is built for real accessibility—using native HTML elements under the hood, with ARIA roles, keyboard support, and screen reader-friendly labels. Jelly UI supports dark mode, right-to-left languages, and color choices that meet accessibility standards (WCAG AA). The API lets you change the look with themes, accents, and sizes. There are ready-made controls for common cases, like OTP input, radio groups, progress bars, alerts, and toasts. The elements are easy to use; you just add the custom tags in your HTML and set options as attributes. The documentation shows lots of examples and code snippets.

In the comments, some people are excited about how fun and tactile the UI feels. They say it’s refreshing to see form controls that look different from the usual flat or material style. Others praise that it’s dependency-free and uses native web standards. Some developers like the focus on accessibility and RTL support. A few users joke about wanting to “squish” all their buttons now. On the other hand, some are worried about performance, especially if you use many jelly elements on a page. A few point out that too much animation can be distracting or tiring for users, and ask if there’s a way to turn down the effects. Some raise questions about browser compatibility and whether it works well with frameworks like React or Vue. One comment mentions that using custom elements can sometimes break form submissions or styling, but others reply that Jelly UI keeps real form behavior. Overall, most agree it’s a creative and well-documented project, and they’re interested to see how it grows.

## Human mathematicians are being outcounterexampled

- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48983382)

This article talks about how AI is now better than humans at finding counterexamples in math. The author shares stories from the last few months where AI found mistakes or new answers in famous math problems.

First, AI disproved a famous math idea called Erdős’ Unit Distance conjecture. People checked the proof and said it was right. A company then used another AI tool to turn the AI’s proof into Lean code, which is a way to check if a math proof is really correct. The hard part was that some parts of the proof used old, very complex math that is still hard for people to write in Lean. But soon, another AI tool called Sol made a full Lean version by itself, writing over a million lines of code in just weeks—something humans took years to do.

Next, at a math workshop, the author and friends used several AI tools to help with math problems. AI found a mistake in a math paper before any person did. Later, the AI found a counterexample to a 60-year-old question by Grothendieck, which surprised the author. The proof was turned into Lean code and checked quickly. The author realized that AI is now very good at finding counterexamples and is much faster than humans.

The article also talks about the changing feelings of human mathematicians. Some are a bit sad or in denial because problems they worked on for years were solved by AI in days. Others, like the author, now think that using AI tools is a must for serious math work. At big schools, students pay for AI access, and some schools even give it for free.

Finally, the AI found a counterexample to the Jacobian Conjecture, an old problem that was open for 100 years. People quickly wrote the proof in Lean and checked it easily. The author thinks the next step is for humans to really understand what these AI-found examples mean, not just know that they exist.

People on Hacker News have strong opinions about this. Some are amazed and excited that AI can help solve problems humans struggled with for so long. They think it will speed up math and science. A few users worry that people might stop understanding the deep meaning behind proofs, relying only on AI. Some think AI-generated proofs are too big for humans to check, so it is hard to trust them fully. Others feel sad for human mathematicians, who may feel left behind. A few point out that AI could find mistakes in math used in software, which might help make programs safer. Some users are hopeful that working with AI will help people learn more, not less. There are also jokes about how soon people will say “AI solved math” while humans just watch. But many agree: this is a big change for math and for anyone who loves solving hard problems.

## Hacker wipes Romania's land registry database

- 原文链接: [Hacker wipes Romania's land registry database](https://news.risky.biz/risky-bulletin-hacker-wipes-romanias-entire-land-registry-database/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48978605)

A hacker broke into Romania’s land registry agency and deleted the country’s whole land database after trying, and failing, to get money from them. Because of this, all real-estate deals in Romania had to stop, as notaries couldn’t do their work and people couldn’t get land papers or proof they owned property.

The hacker got in using real login details, learned how the systems worked inside, and then erased both the main data and backups when the agency didn’t pay. Email servers of the land registry were also taken down. The attack became public on July 14, and the next day some of the stolen files—like worker passwords, internal documents, and IT system details—were put up for sale on a hacking forum.

Officials quickly restored the agency’s website and said they were rebuilding everything from the ground up. It turns out there was an offline backup, so not all data was lost forever, but the damage was still very big. The hacker, known online as “ByteToBreach”, has also attacked other governments before, like Sweden’s e-government portal. A security company named the hacker as Zakaria Mahdjoub from Algeria, which may help police catch him.

Romania is not alone—other countries like Poland, Slovakia, Greece, Morocco, Russia, and Ukraine have also had their land registries hacked in the last few years. These attacks often stop important services and cause chaos for normal people and businesses.

In the Hacker News comments, many people were shocked that such important national data could be wiped out by one person. Some said using only digital systems, without strong backups, is too risky for things like land ownership. A few suggested that all countries should keep paper records or have backups in very safe places that are not connected to the internet.

Some commenters talked about how the hacker got in using real logins, so better password security and two-factor authentication could help. Others worried about insider threats, where someone inside the agency gives away logins or helps hackers. There was also talk about how hackers now often delete backups first, so offline (physical) backups are more important than ever.

People argued about whether governments should pay hackers or not. Some said paying just encourages more attacks. Others wondered if restoring the system from backup would cause problems, as some property records might be out-of-date or lost forever, leading to legal fights.

Finally, some users wondered if this will push more governments to focus on cybersecurity and to treat land data with the same care as money in a bank. Others said the lesson is clear: always have good, offline backups and test them often.

## Agent swarms and the new model economics

- 原文链接: [Agent swarms and the new model economics](https://cursor.com/blog/agent-swarm-model-economics)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48982535)

This article talks about using many AI agents working together—called an agent swarm—to build complex software, like a web browser or a database, from scratch. The team wanted to see if a group of agents, each with a clear job, could work better and faster than one big AI model doing everything alone.

The main idea is to split big tasks into a tree shape—breaking a big goal into smaller pieces. Planner agents decide how to split the work and worker agents do each small task. The planners use very smart (but expensive) AI models, while workers use cheaper, faster models. This setup lets the swarm handle large projects, like making a database from a long manual, without getting lost or repeating itself.

A big problem was that, with so many agents, normal tools like Git couldn’t keep up. They built a new version control system to handle thousands of changes per second. They also found new problems: sometimes two planners worked on the same thing differently (“split-brain”), or agents fought over the same files, or big files got too large and slow. To fix these, they made special rules—like having neutral agents solve merge conflicts, and giving agents a way to break up big files.

They let agents keep notes for each other, making a “Field Guide” that helps future agents not repeat old mistakes. Review agents check work in different ways to catch errors early. All these tricks made the swarm more organized and efficient.

The team ran tests building SQLite in Rust, using only the documentation, and tried different mixes of smart and cheap models. The newer swarm system worked better every time. Even with cheaper models, it could finish the work and save a lot of money. Using the best models for planning and cheaper ones for work gave almost the same quality as using the best for everything, but at a fraction of the cost.

Looking at the code, the new system made smaller, cleaner codebases with fewer repeated parts. Most money was spent on planning, so using smart models only when needed saved a lot. The article says that in the future, the main hard part will be writing good specs—clear descriptions of what you want—because the swarm can do the rest if given good instructions.

In the Hacker News comments, some readers were excited about the results and thought this could change how software is made. Others wondered if agent swarms would really work for all types of projects, or just for tasks where the work can be split up easily. A few worried about errors that could pile up if agents misunderstand the spec or each other. Some people liked the idea of the Field Guide, saying it’s like good documentation, but others thought keeping it up-to-date could be hard.

A few commenters brought up costs, pointing out that using top AI models is still very expensive, and asked if smaller teams could afford it. Some wanted to see more real-world examples, not just lab tests. Others were curious about the new version control system and asked if it could help human teams, too. There was debate about whether splitting planning and doing is always the best way, or if sometimes mixing roles works better.

Lastly, some readers wondered what jobs humans would do if the swarm can build software from only specs. Others thought human review will always be needed, at least for now. Many said this is an exciting area to watch, but it’s early days and there are still many open questions.

## How we measured AI writing across arXiv, and where the measurement breaks

- 原文链接: [How we measured AI writing across arXiv, and where the measurement breaks](https://unslop.run/blog/measuring-ai-writing-on-arxiv)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48981206)

This article looks at how much AI writing appears in new research papers on arXiv, a popular science website. The authors tested 12,750 papers and found that about one-third of recent submissions look like they were written by machines.

They used a special detector, tuned for academic writing. To avoid mistakes, they set the detector so that it only flagged 0.4% of papers written before ChatGPT existed (these are almost surely human-written). This way, any big jump in flagged papers after 2022 would likely be real, not just a detector error.

They checked papers from 10 fields—like computer science, biology, physics, and math—between 2023 and mid-2026. They used the first version of each paper and looked at the whole text, not just the summary, since AI writing shows up more in the main body.

Their results show a jump in flagged papers right after ChatGPT came out. In computer science, about 65% of new papers look machine-written. In math, the number is much lower, just 0.7%. Other fields, like biology and engineering, fall in between, with 50–56% flagged. They explain that low numbers in math might not mean no AI use—it could be the detector misses AI writing in math papers, which often have less normal text.

There are some limits to their method. The control group (older papers) is small for each field, so the numbers are rough. Also, the detector might not find all AI writing, especially if authors use new tools or write in ways the detector does not understand well. So, the real share of AI-written text could be even higher.

A flagged paper does not prove it was written by a bot; it just means the writing style looks machine-like. It could be from heavy AI help, or just unusual writing.

Now, let’s look at what people said in the comments. Some readers are surprised by how high the numbers are, especially in computer science. Others think the detector might not be perfect, and that some “machine-like” writing is just normal, formal academic style. A few point out that academic writing has always sounded robotic, even before AI tools, so maybe the detector is just picking up on that.

Some users say it’s not clear if AI is being used for whole papers or just for grammar and editing help. Others wonder if using AI tools is a problem, since it can help non-native English speakers write better. There are worries that more AI writing might make research less clear or more formulaic.

A few commenters like the open approach of the study, especially how it tries to avoid false alarms. Others suggest better ways to measure AI writing, like looking for specific patterns or using different tools. Some users are curious to try the free detector on their own writing.

In summary, the article shows a big rise in machine-like writing in research papers, but measuring it exactly is tricky. People have mixed feelings—some see it as a useful tool, others worry about what it means for science writing.

## Who's Afraid of Chinese Models?

- 原文链接: [Who's Afraid of Chinese Models?](https://stratechery.com/2026/whos-afraid-of-chinese-models/)
- HN链接: [Hacker News讨论](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48977128)

This article looks at the rise of strong open AI models from China, like Kimi K3, and asks if U.S. tech companies and policymakers should be worried. The main point is that Chinese AI models are catching up fast, but fears about them may be exaggerated.

The author explains how AI models are not as “free” as people think. You can download and use these models, but running them costs money. Every time you use an AI model to answer a question or perform a task, it uses computer power (GPUs), which costs real money. This cost is called COGS—cost of goods sold—and it’s different from the up-front R&D (research and development) cost of creating a model. For AI companies, running many user requests means high COGS, even if the model itself is open.

The article explains that not all AI tokens (the pieces of text AI models use to process and generate language) are equal. Some models use more tokens to get to the right answer, so they cost more to run, even if the price per token looks lower. This means “intelligence” is becoming a commodity, like oil or wheat, but it’s not perfectly the same everywhere. The companies that can deliver the same results at lower costs will win as the market grows.

Right now, big U.S. companies like OpenAI and Anthropic have an advantage because they have the best models and control much of the supply. But Chinese models are getting better quickly, and China is pushing for openness. The Chinese government wants to make its models open to help its own tech and robotics industries and to prevent the U.S. from dominating AI.

One big reason Chinese labs are catching up is “distillation.” This means they use the outputs of frontier models (often from the U.S.) to train their own models faster and cheaper. It’s a shortcut, and the article says it’s hard to stop. The author thinks the U.S. should actually make this legal for American companies, so they don’t fall behind.

The article also warns about cybersecurity. U.S. companies are sometimes blocked from using the best American AI models for security reasons, so they end up using Chinese models for important tasks—like defending against hackers. This could be dangerous, and the author says the U.S. should change its rules to let defenders use the best models, not just rely on China.

In the Hacker News comments, some people agree that COGS is the real issue for AI, and they like the clear breakdown of costs. Others point out that China’s focus on open models could help the whole tech community by spreading innovation faster. Some worry about the security risks of relying on Chinese AI, saying the U.S. should be careful about where its models come from.

A few commenters are skeptical about the idea that “intelligence” will become a simple commodity. They argue that the best models will always have some edge, and users will prefer quality over price in many cases. Some think the author is too relaxed about distillation, saying it’s unfair to U.S. companies who spend billions on training.

There are also debates about whether open models really help small companies, or if big players will always control the market because of scale and resources. Some readers say the story is more about geopolitics than technology—China and the U.S. are both trying to make sure they lead the next wave of AI.

A few comments suggest that making training data “fair use” could be legally tricky, but might help balance competition. Others worry that if both sides copy each other too much, innovation could slow down. Several readers like the idea of loosening restrictions for cybersecurity use, but others warn about risks if dangerous AI tools become too open.

Overall, the comments show that people see both opportunities and dangers in China’s rise in AI, and they don’t all agree on how the U.S. should respond. Some want more openness, others want stronger protections, but most agree it’s a fast-changing situation.
